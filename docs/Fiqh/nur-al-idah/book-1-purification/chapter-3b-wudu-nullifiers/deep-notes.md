# Chapter 3b — Deep Notes · Wudū (Part 2): The Status of the Ablution

**Nur al-Idah · Book I: Purification · Hasan Shurunbulali (Hanafi)**
*This file is Ramsha's personal learning material. Read **Part A** first to understand the chapter yourself, then **Part B** when you're ready to teach it to your classmates.*

*Reading guide: technical explanations are in English; key takeaways and counter-strategies are flagged with **Yaad rakho:** boxes in Roman Urdu. Skim the Urdu boxes for a fast revision pass.*

*Source constraint: every ruling, hadith, and verse below is drawn **only** from Nur al-Idah (book pp. 56–64). Nothing has been imported from outside fiqh sources or general knowledge.*

> **Where this sits in the Wudū chapter.** Chapter 3 (Wudū) is split in two:
> - **Chapter 3a** — *Performing wudū*: the opening, the 4 pillars, the conditions, the 18 sunnas, the 14 etiquettes, the 6 disliked acts. Everything you **do** to make wudū, arranged as a four-tier quality gradient. **(Teach this first.)**
> - **Chapter 3b (this file)** — *The status of wudū*: the 3 types of wudū, the 12 nullifiers (what breaks it), and the 10 non-nullifiers (what does **not** break it). This is the **legal life** of a wudū once you have it.
>
> 3a answered *"how do I make wudū?"* This chapter answers two new questions: *"what is a wudū **for** — when is it farḍ, when wājib, when just recommended?"* and *"once I have it, **what breaks it and what doesn't**?"* Teach 3b only once the cohort can already perform wudū from 3a.

# Part A · Understanding the chapter

In Chapter 3a you learned to *build* a wudū. This chapter is about the wudū **after** it is built — its legal life. Imam Shurunbulali organises that life around **three questions**, and the whole chapter is just those three answered in order:

| § | The question | Arabic heading | What it covers |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **How much does a given wudū *weigh*?** | *Aqsām al-wuḍūʾ* (Categories) | The **3 types**: farḍ · wājib · mandūb |
| 2 | **What ends a wudū?** | *Nawāqiḍ al-wuḍūʾ* (Nullifications) | The **12 nullifiers** |
| 3 | **What looks like it should end a wudū but doesn't?** | *Mā lā yanquḍ al-wuḍūʾ* | The **10 non-nullifiers** |

Two structural ideas hold the chapter together — grab these first and the lists become easy:

1. **A wudū is not always the "same" wudū.** Sometimes it is *obligatory* (you cannot pray without it), sometimes *wājib* (for ṭawāf), sometimes just *recommended* (a beautiful extra). Same action, three different legal weights, depending on **what you are about to do with it**. That is §1.
2. **Sections 2 and 3 are a mirror.** Almost every nullifier in §2 has a near-twin in §3 that does **not** break wudū — flowing blood breaks it, *non*-flowing blood doesn't; a mouthful of vomit breaks it, *less* than a mouthful doesn't; relaxed sleep breaks it, *firmly-seated* sleep doesn't. The book is teaching you to read the **principle behind each case**, not memorise a flat list. Once you see the principle, the "non-nullifier" list practically writes itself.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh chapter wudū *karna* nahin — wudū ke *qaanooni darje* aur uski *zindagi* sikhaata hai. Teen sawaal: (1) is wudū ka **wazan** kya hai — farḍ, wājib, ya mandūb? (2) wudū **kis cheez se toot-ta** hai (12)? (3) kya cheez todne *jaisi lagti* hai magar **nahin todti** (10)? Aur sab se bada nukta: §2 aur §3 ek **aaina** hain — har todne wali cheez ka ek "na-todne wala" jod hai. Usool samjho, list khud yaad ho jaayegi.

Work through the three sections in order.

## 1 · The three types of wudū (aqsām al-wuḍūʾ)

The book opens: *"Wudu is divided into three types."* This is a ranking by **legal weight** — and the weight depends entirely on the act the wudū is *for*.

### Type 1 · Farḍ (obligatory)

> *"The first type is that which is obligatory upon one in a state of minor impurity who wishes to pray, even if the prayer is a voluntary one, or is a funeral prayer, for indeed it is still a prayer even though it is not a complete one. It is obligatory for the prostration of recital and for one who wishes to touch the Quran, even if it is only one verse that is written on a paper, dirham or wall…"*

So wudū is **farḍ** (you literally cannot do the act without it) for **three things**:
- **Prayer** — *any* prayer, including a **voluntary (nafl)** prayer and even the **funeral prayer** (which has no rukūʿ/sujūd — yet *"it is still a prayer"*).
- **The prostration of recital** (*sajdat al-tilāwa*) — the sajda you make on reciting a verse of prostration.
- **Touching the Qur'an** — even a single verse written on paper, a coin (dirham), or a wall.

The book grounds the touching-rule in the Qur'an itself:

> *"None shall touch except those who are clean."* (al-Wāqiʿa 56:79)

### Type 2 · Wājib (necessary)

> *"The second type is that which is wajib for the tawaf of the ka'bah, as the Prophet ﷺ said: [The tawaf around the ka'bah is like prayer except that you may speak, and whoever speaks should not speak unless it is good]."* (at-Tirmidhī 960)

For **ṭawāf** (circling the Kaʿba), wudū is **wājib** — one notch below farḍ. The proof is the Prophet's ﷺ statement that *ṭawāf is like prayer*; since prayer needs wudū, so does ṭawāf — but because ṭawāf is "like" prayer rather than *being* prayer, the rank is wājib, not farḍ.

> **Why farḍ for prayer but only wājib for ṭawāf?** In Ḥanafī uṣūl, *farḍ* rests on definitive proof (the Qur'an itself, al-Māʾidah 5:6 for prayer's wudū) while *wājib* rests on strong-but-less-definitive proof (here, a hadith likening ṭawāf to prayer). You don't need to teach this distinction to the cohort, but you should understand *why* the book ranks them differently — it is not arbitrary.

### Type 3 · Mandūb (recommended)

> *"The third type is that which is recommended on different occasions…"*

This is the long list — wudū you don't *have* to make, but which is rewarded and beloved. Don't teach it as a flat list of twenty items; **group it**. Every occasion in the book falls into one of five baskets:

**Basket A — To stay in a constant state of purity:**
- To **refresh an existing wudū** (already in wudū, renew it for reward and closeness to God) — *but the second wudū should be performed in a different place*; repeating worship in the same spot with nothing between is deemed wastage and is disliked.
- Before **sleep**, so one sleeps in a state of wudū; and on **waking** from sleep.

**Basket B — After a fault (wudū wipes small sins):**
- After **backbiting, lying, and slander** (*"tales spreading corruption upon the earth"*).
- After **any mistake, including the oration of evil poetry** — *"wudu expiates small sins."* The Prophet ﷺ said there is no one who sins, then performs wudū and prays seeking forgiveness, except that Allah forgives him — and recited: *"And those who, having done an act of indecency or wronged their own souls, remember Allah and ask for forgiveness for their sins."* (Āl ʿImrān 3:135)
- After **laughing loudly outside of prayer** (loud laughter *in* prayer breaks wudū — §2 #11 — so doing it outside resembles that, and wudū is desirable).
- In a state of **anger** — *"Anger is from Satan… and you extinguish fire with water, so if any of you becomes angry, he should perform wudu."* (Abū Dāwūd 4766)

**Basket C — Around worship, recitation, and knowledge:**
- For the **time of every prayer**, whether already pure or not (Anas reported the Prophet ﷺ did wudū for every prayer — at-Tirmidhī 58; and *"For a man who performs ablution in a state of ablution, ten virtuous deeds will be recorded."* — Abū Dāwūd 62).
- To **recite Qur'an from memory** (touching the Qur'an is *compulsory* — Type 1 — but reciting from memory is recommended).
- For **reading and narrating hadith**, and for the **studying of sacred knowledge** (out of honour for it).
- For the **adhān, iqāma, and the sermon (khuṭba)** — Friday or otherwise.

**Basket D — Sacred contact and places:**
- To **touch the books of Islamic law** (not the books of Qur'anic interpretation).
- **Prior to the major bath (ghusl)** — Aʿishah reported the Prophet ﷺ, washing from janāba, would wash his hands then do wudū *"like the wudu for prayer."* (Muslim 316)
- When a person in **major impurity (junub)** wishes to **eat, sleep, or repeat intercourse** (Muslim 307; and *"If anyone approached his spouse and then wanted to repeat intercourse, he should perform wudu between them."* — Ahmad 3/21).
- After **washing or carrying a dead person** — *"Whoever washed a deceased should observe ghusl, and whoever carried him should perform wudu."* (Abū Dāwūd 3161)
- When **visiting the Prophet ﷺ**, and when **standing on ʿArafah** and **running between Ṣafā and Marwah** in hajj (out of esteem).

**Basket E — After a "disputed" act (to exit the scholars' disagreement):**
- After **eating camel meat** — *"Make wudu from it."* (at-Tirmidhī 81). *Note: the Ḥanafī view is that this is recommended (to be free of the disagreement), whereas Imam Aḥmad held it a requirement.*
- After **any act the scholars dispute** — e.g. **touching a woman** (the Ḥanafīs say touching a woman does *not* break wudū, but making wudū anyway frees you of the disagreement), and **touching one's private part with the palm** (also doesn't break wudū for Ḥanafīs, but Shāfiʿī, Mālik, and Aḥmad require wudū, so it is preferred).

Notice how Basket E *quietly previews* §3: touching a woman and touching one's own genitals will reappear in the **non-nullifiers** list. The book is telling you in advance: *these don't break wudū — but making wudū anyway is a beautiful caution.*

**Yaad rakho:** Teen darje — **(1) Farḍ**: namaz (har namaz, nafl aur janaza bhi), sajda-e-tilāwat, Qur'an ko chhoona ("paak ke siwa koi na chhuye" — al-Wāqiʿa 56:79). **(2) Wājib**: sirf **ṭawāf** ke liye (Nabi ﷺ: "ṭawāf namaz jaisa hai" — at-Tirmidhī 960). **(3) Mandūb**: lambi list — paanch tokriyon mein baant lo: (A) hamesha bā-wuzu rehna, (B) kisi galti ke baad (wudū chhote gunah mitaata hai), (C) ibādat/tilāwat/ʿilm ke liye, (D) muqaddas chhuaao aur jaghein, (E) ikhtilāfi kaam ke baad (oont ka gosht, aurat ko chhoona). Farḍ chhoote to amal hi nahin hota; mandūb chhoote to bas zyada sawaab haath se gaya.

### The big insight you must take from §1

The same physical act — washing the limbs — carries **three different legal weights** depending on **what it unlocks**. For prayer and touching the muṣḥaf it is the *non-negotiable key* (farḍ). For ṭawāf it is *necessary* (wājib). For everything in the long third list it is a *beautiful recommendation* (mandūb) — and the heart of that list is one idea: **a believer tries to live in a constant state of wudū**, renewing it around worship, after faults, and out of reverence. Wudū in this chapter stops being a one-time gate and becomes a *way of being*.

## 2 · The twelve nullifiers (nawāqiḍ al-wuḍūʾ)

The book's heading is exact: *"Twelve things nullify wudu, and they are…"* Don't drown the cohort in twelve loose items — they fall into **four natural families**.

### Family A — Things that exit the body (#1–3)

1. **Anything that exits from the front or back passage** (urine, stool, wind, etc.) — *"except the air which comes out from the front passage (of a female), and this is the most sound view."* That single exception is the only exit-from-the-passages that does *not* break wudū.
2. **Giving birth without seeing blood.** Abū Yūsuf and Muḥammad: this isn't classed as *nifās* (postnatal bleeding requires actual blood), but wudū still breaks *"due to the wetness and dampness."* (Abū Ḥanīfa: as a precaution she takes the major bath.) The book notes it is rare for a birth to occur without blood — but if it does, wudū breaks.
3. **The flowing of an impurity — blood, pus — from anywhere *other* than the two passages.** The key word is **flowing**: it must *move past* the point where it emerged. (Shāfiʿī disagrees: for him nothing outside the two passages nullifies. The Ḥanafī proof: the Prophet ﷺ said *"Ablution is from all types of flowing blood."* — al-ʿAynī 1/262.)

**The hinge of Family A — and of the whole chapter — is the word *flowing*.** Blood that merely appears on the skin but does not run does **not** break wudū (you'll meet it as non-nullifier #1). This single distinction — *kharaja* (it came out and flowed) vs *ẓahara* (it merely appeared) — separates half of §2 from half of §3.

### Family B — Vomiting (#4–6)

4. **Vomiting a mouthful** of food, water, blood clots, or bile breaks wudū. The book's definition of "a mouthful": *"the mouth cannot be shut except with difficulty."* (Less than that — §3 #6 — does not break it.) The Prophet ﷺ vomited and then performed wudū (at-Tirmidhī 87), and the *"seven things"* hadith lists *"das'a (a spurt of vomit) which is a mouthful"* among the breakers of wudū.
5. **Several small vomits from the same cause are counted as one occurrence.** If together they add up to a mouthful, wudū is nullified (Imam Muḥammad; Abū Yūsuf adds the "same place" condition). *Mucus that descends from the head is **not** a nullifier — it is pure — by agreement; the fatwa is on this.*
6. **Saliva overshadowed by blood** (from an injury in the mouth) breaks wudū. If the spit is red or yellow, blood has overshadowed it → wudū void. (Equal-mix = void; light red = even; strong red = dominant. Blood that descends from the head invalidates even if minimal, since it flows.)

**Yaad rakho (A & B):** Family A — jism se kuch **nikle**: (1) aage/peeche ke raaste se kuch bhi (sirf aurat ki aagey ki hawa mustasna), (2) bila-khoon wiladat, (3) kisi aur jagah se khoon/peep ka **behna**. Family B — qai (vomit): (4) **muh-bhar** qai (muh band karna mushkil ho), (5) thodi-thodi qai jamaa ho kar muh-bhar = ek baar, (6) **thook jis par khoon ghaalib** ho. Sab ka markaz ek lafz: **"behna" (flowing)** — sirf zaahir hona kaafi nahin.

### Family C — Loss of control: sleep & consciousness (#7–10)

7. **Sleeping in a posture where the buttocks are not firmly set on the ground** (e.g. dozing while seated but slumped) breaks wudū. *Marāqī al-Falāḥ* explains the Ḥanafī logic precisely: **sleep itself is not impurity** — wudū breaks because the sleeper *can no longer guarantee* he is free of what breaks wudū (relaxed joints can release wind). So: **light sleep where one still hears people talking = wudū intact.** In the Ḥanafī school sleep breaks wudū in **three postures**: lying on the **side, back, or hip** (the joints relax). Sleeping **seated upright** with buttocks firmly placed, or **standing / bowing / in a prostration pose** — wudū remains sound (*unless the body collapses*). Proof: *"Wudu is not imposed except on the one who sleeps lying down."*
8. **The buttocks lifting off the floor while asleep** (before one wakes) breaks wudū — *even if one does not fall over* — because at that instant the seal is broken in a state of unawareness.
9/10. **Losing consciousness (fainting), temporary insanity, and drunkenness** all break wudū. (These are a *deeper* loss of control than sleep — so they break wudū even in postures where sleep wouldn't.)

### Family D — Two special prayer-related cases (#11–12)

11. **Loud laughter by a mature, conscious person *inside a prayer that has bowing and prostration*** breaks both the prayer **and** the wudū — even if done deliberately, even if done forgetfully. ("Loud / audible" = heard by those around you.) Two refinements: (a) **smiling does not affect wudū or prayer**; (b) a prayer with **no** rukūʿ/sujūd — the **funeral prayer** and the **recital prostration** — has audible laughter break the *prayer* but **not the wudū** (no full prayer-form to "resemble").
12. **A man rubbing his erect penis against a vagina (or like contact) without a screen** between them breaks wudū. The point is *rubbing*, not penetration (penetration → the major bath, ghusl). The book is explicit that *"such acts are offensive and forbidden in Islam; the jurist's role is merely to provide a legal answer should such a scenario ever occur."*

**Yaad rakho (C & D):** Family C — **control ka jaana**: (7) aisi neend jisme **chuthad zameen par jamey na ho** (karwat/peeth/kuolhe par leT kar — jod dheele ho jaate hain); halki neend jisme aawazein sunaai dein = wudū qaaim. (8) neend mein **chuthad utha** (giro chahe nahin). (9/10) be-hoshi, junoon, nasha. Family D — namaz ke do khaas masle: (11) namaz (jisme rukūʿ-sujūd ho) mein **buland qahqaha** — namaz bhi wudū bhi toot jaata hai (muskurana nahin); janaza/sajda-e-tilāwat mein qahqaha sirf namaz todta hai, wudū nahin. (12) bila-aDh (screen) ragad. Asal usool: **be-ikhtiyaari** ya **raasti ka khulna**.

### The big insight you must take from §2

The twelve nullifiers are not random. Strip away the detail and you find **two underlying principles**:
1. **Something impure exits or flows** (Families A & B — the two passages, flowing blood/pus, a mouthful of vomit, blood-overshadowed saliva). The operative word is always **flowing**, not merely appearing.
2. **The person loses reliable control of the body** (Family C — relaxed sleep, fainting, insanity, drunkenness; and arguably the elevated-buttocks case). Here wudū breaks not because something *came out*, but because the body can no longer *guarantee* nothing did.

Family D (#11–12) are the two odd specifics that sit slightly apart — laughter in prayer and the rubbing case — and they're best taught as named exceptions rather than forced into the two principles. If the cohort leaves with **"flow / exit"** and **"loss of control"** as the two big keys, they can reason their way to almost every ruling in the chapter.

## 3 · The ten things that do NOT nullify wudū (mā lā yanquḍ al-wuḍūʾ)

The book's heading: *"There are ten things that do not nullify wudu."* This is the **mirror** of §2 — and that is exactly how you should teach it. Almost every item here is the *near-twin* of a nullifier, sitting just on the other side of the line.

1. **Blood that appears but does not flow** onto another part of the skin. *(Twin of nullifier #3 — the line is "flowing".)*
2. **A piece of flesh or a scab separating without blood flowing** from its place. The flesh/skin is pure; no purification needed as long as there is no oozing. The book's example: a *"Madina blood vessel"* — *rashta* in Persian — a polyp, a hanging piece of flesh, a pimple or blister; if it comes off without flowing blood, wudū stays sound.
3. **A worm exiting a wound, or from the ear or nose** does not break wudū (its dampness is minimal, not filthy). **But** if a worm exits through the **anus**, wudū *is* nullified — *only* the two passages break it, never other locations. *(Twin of nullifier #1 — exits, but not from the two passages.)*
4. **Touching one's own penis, anus, or a woman's genitalia** does not break wudū. This was the position of great Companions (ʿUmar, ʿAlī, Ibn Masʿūd, Ibn ʿAbbās, Zayd ibn Thābit) and Tābiʿīn. The proof: a man asked the Prophet ﷺ about touching his genitals in prayer, and he replied *"Is it not a body part from you?"* (at-Tirmidhī — Tirmidhī called it the best and soundest hadith on the subject). The other hadith — *"Whoever touches his genitals is to observe wudu"* — is read by the scholars as *wudū* in its **linguistic sense** (i.e. *wash the hands*), not the ritual ablution. *(Recall: making wudū here anyway is the recommended caution of §1, Basket E.)*
5. **Touching a woman** does not break wudū. Aʿishah reported *"the Prophet ﷺ would fondle (and kiss) some of his wives and then pray without making wudū."* (at-Tirmidhī and an-Nasāʾī 165). *(Twin of the disputed act in §1, Basket E.)*
6. **Vomiting less than a mouthful** is not a nullifier. *(Twin of nullifier #4 — under the threshold.)*
7. **Vomiting phlegm / saliva** does not break wudū *even if it is more than a mouthful*, because it hasn't mixed with any filth, and saliva is pure. *(Twin of nullifier #4 — wrong substance, not impure.)*
8. **Swaying while asleep where there is merely a *possibility* of falling** does not break wudū — if one is **seated upright with the buttocks firmly on the ground**, the swaying doesn't matter. The Companions used to wait for ʿishāʾ until their heads *drooped (sank)*, then prayed without renewing wudū. (Abū Dāwūd 200) *(Twin of nullifier #7.)*
9. **Sleeping in a firm posture** is not a nullifier — even leaning on a support such that one would fall if it were removed; this is the soundest view in Abū Ḥanīfa's school (and it pairs with #8). *(Twin of nullifier #7.)*
10. **Sleeping during prayer in the sunna posture** does not break wudū — even if one sleeps during bowing or prostration — *provided the posture stays as the sunna prescribes.* If the posture collapses (e.g. the arm folds onto the rib/ground), wudū is void. Proof: *"Ablution is not compulsory on one who sleeps sitting, standing, or prostrating until he falls on his side."* The same rule applies outside prayer. *(Twin of nullifier #7.)*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh list §2 ka **aaina** hai. Har "todne wali" cheez ka ek "na-todne wala" jod: behta khoon todta hai ↔ **na-behta khoon nahin** (#1); aage/peeche se nikle to todta hai ↔ **kaan/naak/zakhm se keeda nahin** (#3, magar peeche se ho to todta hai); muh-bhar qai todti hai ↔ **kam qai (#6) aur balgham (#7) nahin**; dheeli neend todti hai ↔ **seedhi/jami neend aur sunnat-waali namaz ki neend nahin** (#8-10); aur **apne aap ko ya aurat ko chhoona** wudū nahin todta (#4, #5). Usool yaad rakho — list khud yaad ho jaayegi.

### The big insight you must take from §3

This section exists to **stop two opposite errors**: the *over-scrupulous* learner who thinks every drop of blood, every doze, every touch breaks wudū and re-does it ten times a day; and the *careless* learner who waves away real nullifiers. The cure is the **principle behind the line**:
- **Blood / impurity:** breaks wudū only when it **flows**, not when it merely appears.
- **Vomit:** breaks wudū only at a **mouthful**, and only if it's actual stomach matter (not pure phlegm).
- **Sleep:** breaks wudū only when the posture **relaxes the joints** (lying down / slumped), not when the body stays **firmly seated or in the sunna prayer-posture**.
- **Touching:** one's own body or a woman **never** breaks wudū (for the Ḥanafīs).
- **Exits:** break wudū only from the **two passages** — never a worm from the ear, never a scab from the skin.

Teach §2 and §3 **together as a mirror**, never as two separate lists to be memorised in isolation. A learner who grasps the five principles above can place a brand-new situation correctly without ever having been told the specific ruling.

## The big picture — what Chapter 3b is silently teaching

Step back from the three lists and you can see the single lesson underneath them:

1. **Purity is a *state*, not an *event*.** §1 reframes wudū from a one-time gate into a condition a believer tries to *live inside* — renewing it around prayer, after faults, before sleep, out of reverence. *(Tahārat ek **haalat** hai, ek lamha nahin.)*
2. **The Sharīʿah draws lines on *principles*, not on appearances.** Flowing vs merely-appearing blood; a mouthful vs less; relaxed vs firm sleep. The law is precise *because* it is principled — and that precision is a **mercy**: it stops needless anxiety and needless repetition.
3. **Fiqh answers even the uncomfortable question.** The book's own note on the rubbing case — *"such acts are forbidden; the jurist's role is to provide a legal answer should the scenario occur"* — models how a faqīh thinks: he maps every possibility, without endorsing the sin. This is worth pointing out to the cohort as a window into *how* fiqh works.
4. **Knowing what does *not* break wudū is as important as knowing what does.** Half this chapter is reassurance. A religion that tells you precisely when you are *still pure* is protecting you from waswasa (obsessive doubt).

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh chapter sirf "wudū kab toot-ta hai" nahin sikhaata — yeh sikhaata hai ke **paaki ek haalat hai jisme momin rehne ki koshish karta hai**, aur Sharīʿat **usool** par lakeer khinchti hai, dikhaawe par nahin. Aur aadhi kitaab **tasalli** hai: yeh jaan-na ke kya cheez wudū **nahin** todti, waswase se bachaata hai.

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

You now understand Chapter 3b. This section is how to **deliver** it to your classmates — the same adult, mostly Urdu-speaking cohort from 3a, all of whom now (after 3a) know how to *perform* wudū. This chapter is where they most often carry **inherited folk-rulings** that are simply wrong — so your job here is as much *correction* as teaching.

---

## A note on the cohort's starting point

Two things to expect. First, everyone already has *opinions* about what breaks wudū — much of it absorbed from family, not from a book. You will hear *"khoon nikla to wudū toot gaya"* (any blood) or *"aurat ko haath laga to wudū gaya"* (touching a woman) — both **wrong** in the Ḥanafī text you're teaching. Second, this chapter is a magnet for **waswasa** (obsessive doubt): the over-scrupulous learner who re-does wudū constantly. The structure of your lesson — **the mirror, and the principle behind each line** — is the cure for both. Teach the *why*, not the list.

**Yaad rakho:** Cohort ke paas pehle se "ghar ki" raayein hain — bahut si **ghalat**. Aur yeh chapter waswase ka adda hai. Dono ka ilaaj: **aaina (todta ↔ nahin todta)** aur **har lakeer ke peeche ka usool**. List nahin — *kyun* parhao.

## The five landmines for Urdu-speaking adult learners

### Landmine 1 · "Any blood breaks wudū" — the flowing rule

The single most common folk-ruling: *"khoon ka ek qatra bhi nikle to wudū toot jaata hai."* The Ḥanafī text says **only flowing blood** breaks wudū. A pin-prick of blood that beads on the skin but does not run does **not** break it (non-nullifier #1).

**Counter-strategy:** Put the two twins side by side on the board — nullifier #3 (*flowing* blood/pus from a wound) and non-nullifier #1 (blood that *appears but doesn't flow*). The word that decides it is **"behna" (to flow / run past the spot)**. Demonstrate: a drop that stays put = wudū intact; a drop that runs down = wudū broken. Give the textual anchor: *"Ablution is from all types of **flowing** blood"* — the hadith itself says *flowing*.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Hamari kitaab (Nur al-Idah) kehti hai ke wudū sirf **behne wale** khoon se toot-ta hai — har khoon se nahin. Agar zakhm se khoon nikla magar wahin ruka raha, behaa nahin — to wudū **qaaim** hai. Hadith ke alfaaz bhi yahi hain: 'wudū har **behne wale** khoon se hai.' To pin ki nok jitna khoon jo sirf upar aaya magar baha nahin — us se wudū nahin toot-ta. Yeh kitaab ka faisla hai, ghar ki riwayat nahin."*

### Landmine 2 · "Touching a woman breaks wudū" — the Shāfiʿī confusion

Many learners — especially those exposed to Shāfiʿī rulings — believe that skin-contact with a (marriageable) woman breaks wudū. In the **Ḥanafī** text, **touching a woman does not break wudū at all** (non-nullifier #5: the Prophet ﷺ *"would fondle and kiss some of his wives and then pray without making wudū"*). The *rubbing* case in nullifier #12 is a completely different, extreme scenario — don't let them be confused.

**Counter-strategy:** State the Ḥanafī ruling plainly, give the Aʿishah hadith, and then *acknowledge the other school* honestly (this is where §1's Basket E pays off): *"The Shāfiʿīs hold it does break wudū — so our book even says it's recommended to make wudū anyway, to be free of the disagreement. But in our school, it does not break it."* That respects their inherited view while correcting the ruling. **Crucially, separate it from nullifier #12** — #12 is *rubbing of the private parts without a barrier*, not ordinary touch.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Ḥanafī mazhab mein **aurat ko chhoona wudū nahin todta** — bilkul nahin. Daleel: Hazrat Aʿishah ؓ farmaati hain ke Nabi ﷺ apni azwaaj ko pyaar/bosa dete, phir bina naya wudū kiye namaz padhte. Haan, Shāfiʿī mazhab mein toot-ta hai — isi liye hamari kitaab kehti hai ke ikhtilāf se bachne ke liye wudū kar lena **behtar** hai. Magar todta nahin. Aur yeh us '#12 wali ragad' se bilkul alag baat hai — woh ek intihaai aur mamnūʿ surat hai, aam chhuaao nahin."*

### Landmine 3 · "I dozed off, so my wudū is gone" — the posture rule

Learners panic that *any* drowsiness breaks wudū. The Ḥanafī rule is about **posture**, not sleep itself: wudū breaks only when the joints relax — **lying on the side, back, or hip**, or slumping so the buttocks lift/aren't firm. **Firmly-seated sleep, and a light doze where you still hear voices, keep the wudū.**

**Counter-strategy:** Teach the *Marāqī al-Falāḥ* reasoning, it's a "lightbulb" for adults: **sleep is not itself impurity — the worry is that a relaxed body silently releases wind.** So the test is "were your joints relaxed?" Lying down = yes (broken). Sitting firm = no (intact). Give the Companions' example (non-nullifier #8): they dozed waiting for ʿishāʾ *until their heads sank*, then prayed without wudū. That single image fixes the whole rule.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Neend khud na-paaki nahin hai — asal khatra yeh hai ke jism dheela ho kar chupke se hawa khaarij kar de. Isi liye sawaal yeh hai: 'tumhare **jod dheele** hue ya nahin?' Karwat/peeth/kuolhe par leT gaye — jod dheele — wudū gaya. **Seedhe baithe, chuthad jamey hue** ungh aayi — jod qaaim — wudū qaaim. Sahaba ؓ ʿishāʾ ka intezaar karte hue üngte the, sar jhuk jaata, phir bina wudū namaz padhte. Halki ungh, jisme aawazein sunaai dein — wudū nahin toot-ta."*

### Landmine 4 · The vomit threshold — "a mouthful"

Learners either think any vomit breaks wudū, or have no idea where the line is. The book is precise: **a mouthful** — defined as *"the mouth cannot be shut except with difficulty."* Less than that does not break it (non-nullifier #6); and **phlegm/saliva never breaks it** even if copious (non-nullifier #7), because it isn't impure.

**Counter-strategy:** Give the concrete definition (mouth can't close easily = a mouthful = broken). Then the two reliefs: (a) a *small* vomit is under the line; (b) bringing up *phlegm* — even a lot — is pure and never breaks wudū. Add the "same cause" rule for completeness: several small vomits from *one* cause are added together; if they reach a mouthful, broken.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Qai se wudū tab toot-ta hai jab woh **muh-bhar** ho — yaani itni ke muh band karna mushkil ho jaaye. Us se **kam** qai? Wudū **qaaim** (non-nullifier #6). Aur sirf **balgham/thook** — chahe zyada bhi ho — kabhi wudū nahin todta, kyunki woh paak hai (#7). Haan, agar ek hi sabab se thodi-thodi qai baar baar aaye aur mila kar muh-bhar ban jaaye, to toot jaayega."*

### Landmine 5 · The over-scrupulous re-doer (waswasa)

The most pastoral landmine: the sincere classmate who re-makes wudū at the faintest doubt — a possible drop of blood, a moment's drowsiness, having touched their spouse. This chapter, taught as a *list of dangers*, makes them worse. Taught as a *mirror with principles*, it sets them free.

**Counter-strategy:** Spend real time on §3 (the non-nullifiers) — frame it as the **good news half** of the chapter. Hammer the principle that **certainty is not lifted by doubt**: a wudū you *know* you had is not broken by a *maybe*. Walk them through the twins so they can self-diagnose: *did the blood actually flow? was it a full mouthful? were my joints actually relaxed?* If the honest answer is "no / not sure," the earlier wudū **stands**.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Aadha chapter **khushi ki khabar** hai — yeh batata hai ke kya cheez wudū **nahin** todti. Usool yaad rakho: **yaqeen, shak se nahin jaata.** Jo wudū tumhe yaqeeni tha, woh ek 'shaayad' se nahin toot-ta. Apne aap se poocho: khoon **waqai baha**? Qai **poora muh-bhar** thi? Mere **jod waqai dheele** hue? Agar jawaab 'nahin' ya 'pata nahin' — to pehla wudū **barqaraar** hai. Baar baar wudū dohraana zaroori nahin — yeh waswasa hai, deen nahin."*

## Suggested 75-minute class plan

*Class shuru karne se pehle:* board ko **do columns** mein baanT do — baayein **"TOOTTA HAI" (12)**, daayein **"NAHIN TOOTTA" (10)**. Yeh poore chapter ka naqsha hai. Pehle teen darjon (§1) ko upar ek alag patti mein likho. Phir har nullifier ke saamne uska "twin" non-nullifier rakhte jao — taa ke cohort **aaina** dekhe.

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–6 min | Recap 3a in 90 seconds (the four-tier gradient) · frame 3b: *"ab humein wudū **karna** aata hai — aaj uski **zindagi**: kab farḍ, aur kis se toot-ta/nahin toot-ta"* |
| 6–18 min | **§1 · The 3 types** — farḍ (prayer incl. nafl/janāza, sajda-tilāwat, touching Qur'an + al-Wāqiʿa 56:79) · wājib (ṭawāf) · mandūb (the 5 baskets — teach the baskets, not 20 items) |
| 18–22 min | Draw the **two-column mirror** board (TOOTTA / NAHIN TOOTTA) — the map for the rest of class |
| 22–40 min | **§2 · The 12 nullifiers in 4 families** — A exits · B vomit · C sleep/consciousness · D laughter-in-prayer & rubbing. Land the master-key word **"flowing"** and the **"loss of control"** principle |
| 40–55 min | **§3 · The 10 non-nullifiers as twins** — place each opposite its nullifier. Frame as the *good-news half*. Hit Landmines 1–4 here (blood, touching a woman, dozing, vomit threshold) |
| 55–63 min | **Waswasa relief (Landmine 5)** — *"yaqeen shak se nahin jaata"*; walk the self-diagnosis questions |
| 63–72 min | **Mirror quiz** — call out a scenario (*"a drop of blood that didn't run", "dozed off sitting upright", "touched my wife", "a mouthful of vomit"*); class shouts TOOTTA or NAHIN |
| 72–75 min | Q&A · assign handout exercises · preview Chapter 4 (Ghusl — the major bath) |

**Yaad rakho:** Is chapter ka **dil** do-column aaina hai (TOOTTA ↔ NAHIN). 12 nullifiers ko **4 families** mein, 3 types ko **5 baskets** mein parhao — flat list cohort ko dubo deti hai. Sab se zyada pastoral waqt §3 + waswasa relief ko do — yahi log waapas aate hain.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 3b

- ❌ **Ghusl** (the major bath) — that's **Chapter 4**. You will *brush past* ghusl here (it's mentioned: penetration → ghusl in #12; wudū before ghusl in §1; janāba in §1) — but do **not** teach when ghusl is obligatory, its farḍ, or its method. If asked: *"Ghusl agla chapter hai (Chapter 4) — wahan tafseel se aayega."*
- ❌ **The detailed fiqh of *nifās* / postnatal bleeding** — nullifier #2 mentions birth-without-blood, but the full rules of nifās and hayd are **Chapter 7**. Only say what the book says here: *birth without blood still breaks wudū.*
- ❌ **The "excused person" (*maʿdhūr*)** rules in depth — mentioned in 3a (adab #11); not this chapter.
- ❌ **Re-teaching how to perform wudū** (the pillars, sunnas) — that was **3a**. A 90-second recap is enough; don't re-run it.
- ❌ **Cross-madhhab uṣūl** (why farḍ vs wājib rests on different proofs) — you understand it (Part A), but it will overwhelm the cohort. Teach *that* prayer-wudū is farḍ and ṭawāf-wudū is wājib; skip *why* unless asked.
- ❌ **Graphic detail on nullifier #12** — state it at the level the book does (rubbing without a screen breaks wudū; the book itself notes the act is forbidden and is only addressed for legal completeness) and move on. Don't dwell.

**Yaad rakho:** 3b sirf **wudū ka darja + todna/na-todna** hai. **Ghusl, nifās/hayd ki tafseel, wudū dobaara sikhana — sab baad mein ya pehle ho chuka.** #12 ko kitaab ke level par bayaan kar ke aage baD jao.

## Self-check before teaching

Before class, make sure you can:

- [ ] State the **3 types** with one example each — farḍ (prayer / sajda-tilāwa / touching Qur'an), wājib (ṭawāf), mandūb (any one basket) — and quote **al-Wāqiʿa 56:79** for touching the Qur'an.
- [ ] Recite the **12 nullifiers in their 4 families** without notes.
- [ ] Explain the **master-key word "flowing"** and use it to separate nullifier #3 from non-nullifier #1.
- [ ] Explain the **sleep-posture principle** (sleep isn't impurity; relaxed joints are the worry) and name the three breaking postures (side / back / hip).
- [ ] State the **vomit threshold** ("a mouthful = mouth won't shut easily") and its two reliefs (sub-mouthful; phlegm).
- [ ] Correct the **two big folk-rulings** confidently: *any* blood (→ only flowing) and *touching a woman* (→ doesn't break it in the Ḥanafī school), and keep #12 separate.
- [ ] Walk the **waswasa self-diagnosis** ("certainty isn't lifted by doubt").

If yes to all seven — *tum tayyar ho.*

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. **Can they place a new scenario in the right column?** *(Koi nayi soorat bolo — TOOTTA ya NAHIN — sahih column mein daal sakte hain?)* This is the single test of whether the mirror landed.
2. **Did the "flowing blood" correction stick?** *(Behne wala khoon todta hai — sirf upar aaya khoon nahin — yeh samjhe?)*
3. **Did the "touching a woman" correction stick — without confusing it with #12?** *(Aurat ko chhoona wudū nahin todta; aur woh #12 wali ragad se alag hai — dono samjhe?)*
4. **Do they apply the sleep-posture rule (not "any doze")?** *(Dheeli neend ↔ jami/seedhi neend ka farq pakad rahe hain?)*
5. **Has the waswasa eased — are they no longer re-doing wudū at every doubt?** *(Shak par baar baar wudū dohraana band hua?)*

**Yaad rakho:** Agar 2+ classmates ab bhi har shak par wudū dohraate hain, ya "har khoon / aurat ko chhoona todta hai" par atke hain — to Chapter 4 (Ghusl) se pehle 5-minute ka **aaina-board refresher** lo (TOOTTA ↔ NAHIN). Yeh chapter tab kaamyaab hai jab cohort **kam** wudū dohraaye, zyada nahin — kyunki ab woh **usool** jaante hain, sirf list nahin.
