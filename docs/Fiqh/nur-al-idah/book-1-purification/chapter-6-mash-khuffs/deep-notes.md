# Chapter 6 — Deep Notes · Mash on the Khuffs (wiping over leather socks)

**Nur al-Idah · Book I: Purification · Hasan Shurunbulali (Hanafi)**
*This file is Ramsha's personal learning material. Read **Part A** first to understand the chapter yourself, then **Part B** when you're ready to teach it to your classmates.*

*Reading guide: technical explanations are in English; key takeaways and counter-strategies are flagged with **Yaad rakho:** boxes in Roman Urdu. Skim the Urdu boxes for a fast revision pass.*

*Source constraint: every ruling, hadith, and verse below is drawn **only** from Nur al-Idah (book pp. 86–94). Nothing has been imported from outside fiqh sources or general knowledge.*

> **Where this sits in Book I.** In wudū (Ch 3) one of the four pillars was to **wash the feet up to and including the ankles**. This chapter is the **concession (rukhṣa)** on that single pillar: under conditions, you may **wipe over your leather socks (khuffs)** instead of washing the feet — for a fixed time. The book then turns to a *second* kind of wiping that looks similar but works on opposite rules: **wiping over a splint or bandage (jabīra)**. The whole chapter is best taught as **two wipings side by side** — the khuff (a *timed* concession) and the jabīra (a *necessity* wiping with no clock). Teach it only after the cohort is solid on wudū (3a/3b) — mash-on-khuffs is defined *against* the washing of the feet.

---

# Part A · Understanding the chapter

This is the first **rukhṣa** (legal concession) in Book I — a place where the Sharīʿah makes an obligation *easier* under real-world pressure. Washing the feet is a pillar of wudū; but a traveller with leather socks, or anyone for whom removing footwear repeatedly is a burden, may simply **wipe the top of the socks** and move on. The institution is so firmly established that the book quotes Imām Abū Ḥanīfa as fearing that *"one who denies its permissibility has committed disbelief"* — because the reports reach us by **tawātur** (mass transmission). So this is not a fringe permission: it is *"the heart of the sunna."*

The chapter answers **six plain questions** about the khuff, and then sets a **seventh topic** — the bandage — beside it as a contrast:

| § | The question | What it covers |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **What is the basic ruling?** | Valid for men & women, **minor** impurity only, even on thick non-leather |
| 2 | **When am I *allowed* to wipe?** | The **7 conditions** the khuffs must meet |
| 3 | **For how long?** | The **clock** — 24h resident / 72h traveller, when it starts, and changing status |
| 4 | **What exactly do I wipe, and how?** | The **farḍ** (3 fingers, on top, once) + the **sunna** method |
| 5 | **What *ends* the wiping?** | The **4 nullifiers** |
| 6 | **What may I *not* wipe on?** | Turban, cap, veil, gloves |
| 7 | **The other wiping — the bandage (jabīra)** | The **necessity** wiping, taught as the **mirror/contrast** of the khuff |

Before the details, grab **three master-keys**. Almost every ruling in the chapter turns on one of them:

1. **Rukhṣa, not a shortcut.** Wiping the khuff *replaces* washing the feet — it is a **concession** the Sharīʿah grants because removing footgear repeatedly is a hardship. It is established by **tawātur** (*"the heart of the sunna"*; Abū Ḥanīfa: denying it is near-disbelief). But like every concession it comes fenced with **conditions** and a **time limit**. Mercy, not laxity.
2. **The clock (a switch with a timer).** The permission runs on a **clock**: **24 hours** for a resident, **72 hours** for a traveller. The clock **starts at the first time your wudū breaks *after* you put the socks on** — not when you wear them. And it **switches off** when the time runs out *or* the sock comes off. (This is the "switch" device from tayammum, now with a timer attached.)
3. **Two wipings, opposite timers.** The chapter teaches **two** wipings. The **khuff** is a *timed concession*: you must put it on *in wudū*, meet 7 conditions, and it lasts only 24/72h. The **jabīra** (splint/bandage) is a *necessity wiping*: **no clock at all** — it lasts as long as the injury does, you needn't be in wudū when you apply it, and it doesn't break if the bandage slips off. The book draws this contrast again and again — it is the spine of the second half.

**Yaad rakho:** Teen chaabiyaan. (1) **Rukhṣa** — khuff par mash paaon dhone ka *badal/aasaani* hai (mozay baar baar utaarna mushkil); yeh **tawātur** se saabit hai (*"sunnat ka dil"*; Abū Ḥanīfa: inkaar kufr ke qareeb) — magar har rukhṣa ki tarah **shartein** aur ek **waqt ki had** ke saath. (2) **Ghadi (timer-waala switch)** — **muqeem 24 ghante, musafir 72 ghante**; ghadi **mozay pehan-ne ke baad pehli baar wudū tootne** se shuru hoti hai (pehan-ne se nahin), aur **waqt khatam ya moza utarte** hi band. (3) **Do mash, ulte timer** — **khuff** = *waqt-waala* (wudū mein pehno, 7 shartein, 24/72 ghante) ; **jabīra** (patti/khapacchi) = *zaroorat-waala* (koi waqt nahin, zakhm theek hone tak, wudū mein baandhna shart nahin, gir jaaye to nahin tootta).

## 1 · The basic ruling — who, what state, what material

The book opens the legal section plainly: *"It is valid for both men and woman to wipe the khuf (footgear) to obtain cleanliness from the state of **minor** impurity, even if the wiping is on something **thick other than leather**, and it is the same if the sole is made of leather or not."*

Three things to lock in:

- **Men *and* women** — the same ruling for both.
- **Minor impurity only.** Footnote: *"One is **not** permitted to wipe his footgear to acquire cleanliness from **major** impurity."* So if you are in a state requiring **ghusl** (janāba), the khuffs cannot help you — you must remove them and bathe. The concession is for **wudū** only.
- **Not just leather.** Thick non-leather socks count too. The book's footnote (Marāqī al-Falāḥ) gives examples: *jukh* (a thick cloth) and *kirbās* (a thick type of cotton). The two requirements (per Muḥammad and Abū Yūsuf, and the fatwā): they must **hold to the feet without being tied**, and **must not absorb water through to the skin**.

**Yaad rakho:** Buniyaadi hukm: **mard aur aurat** dono, sirf **chhoti na-paaki (wudū)** ke liye — **bari na-paaki (janāba/ghusl) mein nahin** (moze utaaro, ghusl karo). Sirf charmay nahin; **mota kapra** (jukh/kirbās) bhi chalega — agar bina baandhe paaon par **tikay** aur **khaal tak paani na pahunchne de**.

## 2 · When am I allowed to wipe — the 7 conditions

The book: *"**Seven** conditions are required to validate the wiping."* These describe what the *footgear itself* must be like before wiping is even an option.

1. **Worn after washing the feet (in wudū).** You must put the khuffs on while in a state of complete purity. The book allows a subtlety: *"It is permissible to wear the khuf even before completing wudū, provided the person completes the wudū before the occurrence of anything that invalidates it."* The example: if you wash your feet, put the khuffs on, *then* break wind **before finishing the rest of wudū**, the wiping is ruined — you must redo the process. (So the feet must be washed *and* the wudū intact when the socks go on.)
2. **They must cover the feet up to (and including) the ankles.** A low-cut sock that leaves the ankle bare does not qualify.
3. **You must be able to walk continuously in them.** *"It is not permissible to wipe on footgear that have been made from glass, wood or steel."* The footnote sets the measure: able to walk a distance of **one farsakh** (*"three miles and more"*) — so footgear that would tear with walking, or are too heavy to walk in (metal), are out.
4. **Each khuff must be free from a large tear** — *"a tear the size equivalent to three of the smallest toes."* If the tear is **less** than that, wiping is still permitted (the Ḥanafī basis: socks usually have small tears and removing them would be a hardship). Two sub-rules: tears **on one khuff** are added together (if the *sum* is under the measure, no harm); tears on **two different khuffs** are **not combined** — *"each khuff is judged on its own."*
5. **They must hold to the feet without being tied.** They stay up by themselves, not by laces or a strap.
6. **They must prevent water from reaching the skin.** Precisely: they must prevent *"the amount of water present on the hand at the time of wiping"* from reaching the skin. They needn't be fully waterproof — just block *that* amount.
7. **The front part of the foot must be present** — *"the length being equivalent to three of the smallest fingers of the hand."* If someone is **missing the front of his foot**, he *cannot* wipe even if the heel is present — *"because the compulsory place to wipe is absent."* He must wash the foot instead. (The footnote clarifies: the length means the **width** of three of the smallest fingers.)

**Yaad rakho:** **7 shartein** (khuff par mash kab durust): (1) **wudū mein** (paaon dho kar) pehne — wudū poora hone se pehle wudū toot gaya to mash baatil. (2) **takhnon samet** paaon dhaank de. (3) inmein **musalsal chalna** mumkin ho (sheeshe/lakdi/lohe ke nahin; ~ek farsakh = teen meel). (4) har moza **tein chhoti ungliyon ke baraabar bade sūraakh se khaali** ho (kam ho to chalega; ek moze ke surākh judte hain, do mozon ke nahin). (5) bina **baandhe** paaon par tikay. (6) **khaal tak paani na pahunchne de** (jitna haath par hota hai utna)। (7) **paaon ka agla hissa maujood** ho (teen chhoti ungliyon ke baraabar) — agla hissa na ho to edi hone par bhi mash nahin, dhona laazim.

## 3 · For how long — the clock, and changing status

This is master-key 2 made concrete. The book: *"A resident (non-traveller) is permitted to wipe his footgear for **one day and one night** (i.e. 24 hours) whereas the traveller is permitted **three days and three nights** (i.e. 72 hours), and this period begins **when a person breaks his wudū after wearing the footgear**."*

Two precise points people get wrong:

- **The clock starts at the first ḥadath *after* wearing — not at wearing.** The footnote example: *"if a person makes wudū at 8:00 am and then wears his footgear, and then at 12:00 pm he breaks his wudū, his duration therefore begins from 12:00 pm."* So the hours you spend in wudū *after* putting the socks on don't count against the clock — it only starts ticking when wudū first breaks.
- **The proof:** ʿAlī (may Allah be pleased with him) said that *"the Messenger of Allah ((God bless him and grant him peace)) had appointed three days and three nights as the period when a traveller is permitted to observe wiping, and one day and one night as the period for the non-traveller."*

**Changing status** (the book handles both directions):

- **Resident → traveller** before the term ends: *"he is to complete the period permitted for a traveller, which is three days and three nights."* (Footnote example: starts wiping 10 am Monday, becomes a traveller at 8 pm — he may keep the footgear until **Wednesday 10 am**, a total of 72 hours.)
- **Traveller → resident:** *"if a traveller becomes a resident, and had already wiped for one day and one night, then the wiping is **nullified** because he is now a resident; though if he had **not** wiped for one day and one night, he is to **complete** this term"* (the 24-hour resident term).

**Yaad rakho:** **Ghadi:** muqeem **24 ghante**, musafir **72 ghante** — aur ghadi **mozay pehan-ne ke baad jab pehli baar wudū tootta hai** tab shuru hoti hai (pehan-ne se nahin; misaal: 8 baje wudū + pehna, 12 baje toota → 12 se shuru). Daleel: Sayyiduna ʿAlī ki hadith. **Haalat badalna:** muqeem → musafir = musafir ki muddat (72) poori karo; musafir → muqeem = agar 24 ghante mash kar chuka to mash **khatam** (ab moze utaaro), aur agar 24 ghante poore nahin huay to **24 tak** mukammal kar lo.

## 4 · What exactly do I wipe, and how — the farḍ and its sunna

The book separates the **obligatory portion** from the **sunna method**.

**The farḍ (the obligatory amount):** *"It is obligatory to wipe the area equivalent to the **three smallest fingers of the hand** over the **top part** of each foot."* Three details follow:

- It need not be done with the hand — *"if this area becomes wet even with a cloth or by pouring water over the required area, then it is permissible"* — **though the sunna is to use the hand.**
- It is done **once only**, over the top — *"it is not valid to wipe **beneath** the khuff, nor the **back** or **side** of it."*
- *"It is **not** sunna to repeat the wiping."* (Unlike washing in wudū, where three times is sunna.)

**The sunna method:** *"It is sunna to begin the wiping **from the toes of the feet up to the shin** with **spread fingers**."* The basis is the report (Tabarani 2/30) that the Prophet ﷺ passed a man wiping his khuff, took him by the hand, and said *"Verily, my Lord ordered me to wipe like this"* — showing him **from the front of the khuff toward the leg, once,** with the fingers spread.

**Yaad rakho:** **Farḍ:** har paaon ke **upar wale hisse** par **teen chhoti ungliyon** ke baraabar mash — **ek baar**, **upar** hi (neeche/peeche/baghal nahin)। Haath zaroori nahin (kapde/paani se bhi ho jaaye) magar **haath se karna sunnat**; **dohraana sunnat nahin**. **Sunnat tareeqa:** **ungliyon ke siron se pinḍli ki taraf**, **ungliyaan khuli** rakh kar (Tabarani 2/30 — "mere Rabb ne aise mash ka hukm diya")।

## 5 · What ends the wiping — the 4 nullifiers

The book: *"There are **four** things which annul wiping."*

1. **Everything that nullifies wudū** nullifies the wiping too. If you break wind, *"his wudū is void as well as the wiping over the footgear."* He must **make wudū again and wipe** over the footgear — but *"he is **not** required to take off the footgear and wash his feet."* (This is the *normal* case — you just renew wudū and re-wipe.)
2. **Removal of the khuff** invalidates the **wiping** (not the wudū) — *"the same rule applies even if the **majority of the foot** is revealed,"* because once the foot is exposed the (minor) impurity reaches it and it now needs washing. And *"the removal of the khuff on **one** foot requires the removal of the other and the washing of **both**."*
3. **The majority of one foot becoming wet** with water (*"over half the foot"*) ruins the wiping.
4. **The expiry of the period** (24h/72h) nullifies the wiping — *"provided one does not fear he will lose his leg due to intense cold"* (in which case he may keep the footgear on until it is safe to remove them). And *"if the period expires while one is praying, the prayer is void."*

**The crucial mercy clause at the end:** *"The last three points (namely, point 2, 3, and 4) require **only the washing of the feet**, not wudū."* So in cases 2–4 your *wudū* is still intact — you only need to **wash the two feet** (not redo the whole wudū). Only case 1 (a genuine ḥadath) requires a fresh wudū.

**Yaad rakho:** **4 naaqis:** (1) jo cheez **wudū** ko tore (hawa waghaira) — wudū + mash dono baatil; **dobara wudū + mash** karo (moze utaarne/paaon dhone ki zaroorat nahin)। (2) **moza utaarna** (aadha paaon bhi khul jaaye); ek moza utra to doosra bhi utaaro aur **dono paaon dho**. (3) **aadhe se zyada paaon par paani** lag jaana. (4) **muddat khatam** hona (sakht sardi mein jaan ka khauf ho to mehfooz hone tak rakh sakte ho; namaz ke darmiyaan muddat khatam ho to namaz baatil)। **Aham:** **aakhri teen** (2,3,4) mein **sirf paaon dhona** kaafi — naya wudū nahin (sirf #1 mein naya wudū)।

## 6 · What may I *not* wipe on — turban, cap, veil, gloves

The book's heading: *"That Which Is **Not** Permitted To Wipe On."*

- **The turban.** Wiping on the turban in place of wiping the head is **not** allowed, *"because it may prevent water from penetrating the turban and entering upon the head, in which case the area required to be wiped (i.e. a quarter) was not performed."* Yes, the Prophet ﷺ was reported to wipe his turban — *"but what is ascertained from this is that the wetness would reach his honourable head."* Jābir ibn ʿAbdullāh, asked about wiping over the turban, said: *"No, not without the water touching the hair."* (Muwaṭṭaʾ of Imām Muḥammad 52)
- **The cap, veil, or gloves.** Also **not** permitted — *"because there is **no hardship** in taking off these things."* (And *no hardship* = no concession — back to master-key 1.) Nāfiʿ reported he saw Ṣafiyya bint Abī ʿUbayd perform wudū: *"she pulled off her headscarf then wiped her head."* (Muwaṭṭaʾ of Imām Muḥammad 53) Imām Muḥammad: the **headscarf** should not be wiped over, nor the turban — *"this is the verdict of Abū Ḥanīfa and our fuqahā in general."*

The deep point: the khuff concession exists because removing footgear repeatedly *is* a hardship. A turban, cap, veil, or gloves are **easy to remove**, so there is no need for a concession — the head/hands must be wiped/washed directly.

**Yaad rakho:** **Jin par mash nahin:** **pagdi (ʿimāma)** — paani sar tak nahin pahunchta to laazmi (chauthai) mash reh jaata hai (Nabī ﷺ ne pagdi par mash kiya magar paani sar tak pahunchta tha; Jābir: "nahin, jab tak paani baalon tak na pahunche" — Muwaṭṭaʾ Imām Muḥammad 52)। **Topi, naqaab/dupatta, dastaane** — bhi nahin, kyunki inka **utaarna mushkil nahin** (Ṣafiyya ne dupatta utaar kar sar par mash kiya — Muwaṭṭaʾ Imām Muḥammad 53)। Usool: jahaan utaarna aasaan, wahaan **rukhṣa nahin**.

## 7 · The other wiping — the splint / bandage (jabīra)

The book now sets a **second** kind of wiping beside the khuff. A *jabīra* is, per the footnote, *"two supports made of wood or palm branches… wrapped with cotton material over the broken limb"* — and *"everything that is placed upon a part of the body which requires it, such as bandages, leather or a brace."* (So: splints, casts, dressings, medical bandages.)

**The basic ruling.** *"If blood is drawn from a limb, or one suffers an injury or broken limb,"* and applies a splint/bandage, and *"is unable to wash or wipe the limb, then it is necessary [**wājib**] to wipe the **majority** of what is used to support the limb."* There is a madhhab nuance the book records: is it **wājib** or **farḍ**? **Abū Ḥanīfa** holds it is **wājib** (not farḍ) — *"and this is the correct view"* — so much so that if one neglects the wiping where there is **no** possibility of harm, *"it is valid, and the prayer is not deemed void."* (The two companions, Shāfiʿī, and the majority say farḍ.) **The amount:** Abū Ḥanīfa — *"it is sufficient to wipe the **majority** of the bandage"*; Shāfiʿī — the **entirety**, with water.

**The practical procedure** (in order of harm):
- If untying the bandage and washing beneath would **harm** the injury → **wipe over the bandage** (the visible/majority area) without removing it.
- If untying would **not** harm → **undo it, wash** the unharmed area, **wipe** the injury; but if **wiping the injury itself** would harm → **leave it**.
- **Restriction:** wiping over a support is granted by **necessity**, so it is *"restricted according to its measure"* — the bandage must **not extend beyond** the affected area; if it does, you undo it and wash the healthy skin behind it (if no harm follows).

**The contrast with the khuff — this is the heart of the second half.** The book itself lists the differences; here they are side by side:

| | **Khuff** (footgear) | **Jabīra** (splint/bandage) |
|---|---|---|
| **Time limit** | Yes — 24h / 72h | **None** — wipe as long as the injury lasts |
| **Ends when** | The clock runs out / the sock comes off | The **injury heals** |
| **Must be applied in wudū?** | **Yes** — worn in a state of purity | **No** — not a stipulation |
| **Wipe one foot, wash the other?** | Not permitted | **Permitted** |
| **If it falls/slips off (before the wound heals)?** | Wiping nullified | Wiping **not** nullified (standing reason) |
| **Intention required?** | Not a stipulation (some scholars said it is) | Not required (agreed) |

The book's own line: *"Wiping over the bandage or splint is like **washing** the area beneath it as long as the cause is present. There is **no time limit**… though this is contrary to the wiping of the footgear, which has a duration of twenty-four hours for a resident."*

**Two more rules the book gives:**
- **Changing a bandage** is permitted, and you needn't re-wipe the new one if the old was wiped (*"though it is preferable to do so"*). A new bandage placed **on top** of the old → no need to wipe the top one.
- **Eye / nail / medicine cases** (p. 94): *"If one's eye is infected and the physician orders him not to wash it, or one has broken his nail and has applied medicine upon the injury… the removal of which is harmful, then it is permissible to wipe over the medicine. If however, it will cause harm to wipe over the medicine, then it is permitted to **omit** the wiping."* (The same necessity logic: do what you can; if even wiping harms, it falls away.)
- **No intention needed:** *"One is not required to form an intention for wiping on the footgear, splint, and head."* For the **splint and head** this is agreed; for the **khuff** intention *"is not a stipulation, although some scholars have stated that it is."*

**Yaad rakho:** **Jabīra (patti/khapacchi/plaster):** zakhm/toot/khoon nikalne par jab **dhona ya zakhm par mash mumkin nahin** → patti ke **zyada hisse** par mash **wājib** (Abū Ḥanīfa: wājib, farḍ nahin — bila-nuqsaan chhoot jaaye to namaz nahin tootti)। **Khuff se ulta:** **koi waqt nahin** (zakhm theek hone tak), wudū mein baandhna **shart nahin**, ek paaon par mash + doosra dho sakte ho, patti **gir jaaye to mash nahin tootta**, **niyyat zaroori nahin**. Patti badalna jaayiz (naye par dobara mash zaroori nahin, behtar hai)। **Aankh/naakhun par dawa** par bhi mash; agar mash bhi nuqsaan de to **chhod do**.

## The big picture — what Chapter 6 is silently teaching

1. **The Sharīʿah builds in concessions (rukhṣa).** Worship is not meant to break you. Where an obligation becomes a recurring hardship — peeling off wet socks five times a day — the Law provides a lighter path. The very firmness of the khuff-permission (tawātur, *"the heart of the sunna"*) says: this ease is **not** a weakening of the dīn; it is part of it. *(Sharīʿat mushkil mein **aasaani** deti hai — yeh deen ki kamzori nahin, deen ka hissa hai.)*
2. **Every concession is fenced and timed.** Mercy is not a loophole. The khuff comes with 7 conditions and a clock; the moment the *reason* changes (the sock comes off, the time ends), the concession ends. *(Har rukhṣa ki had hoti hai — sabab badla to rukhṣa khatam.)*
3. **Match the relief to the need.** The two wipings prove the principle: the khuff (a *convenience* hardship) gets a **time limit**; the jabīra (a *medical* necessity) gets **no limit** — because the need itself has no clock; it lasts until the wound heals. *(Zaroorat jitni, aasaani utni — khuff ko waqt ki had, jabīra ko nahin.)*
4. **No hardship → no concession.** The turban/cap/gloves are excluded for exactly one reason: they are **easy to remove**. The concession tracks the hardship, not our preference. *(Jahaan mushkil nahin, wahaan rukhṣa nahin.)*

**Yaad rakho:** Pura chapter: Sharīʿat **rukhṣa** (aasaani) deti hai, magar **shart aur waqt** ke saath. **Khuff** = waqt-waali aasaani (24/72 ghante); **jabīra** = zaroorat-waali (zakhm theek hone tak)। Aur **jahaan utaarna aasaan** (pagdi/topi/dastaane) wahaan rukhṣa nahin.

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

You now understand Chapter 6. This section is how to **deliver** it to the same adult, mostly Urdu-speaking cohort — who already know wudū (3a/3b), ghusl (4), and tayammum (5). Mash-on-khuffs is a **practical, everyday** chapter (everyone owns socks) but it is **rule-dense** — the clock and the conditions trip people up. Your main jobs are: (1) anchor the **rukhṣa + clock** idea so the conditions make sense, (2) kill the two big myths (the clock starts when you wear them; one tear ruins it), and (3) keep the **khuff ↔ jabīra contrast table** on the board the whole second half.

---

## A note on the cohort's starting point

Two things to expect. First, **almost everyone "knows" mash on socks** but does it loosely — many think the 24 hours run from when they *put the socks on* (it's from the first ḥadath after), and many think *any* sock qualifies (it must be leather-like, hold without tying, cover the ankle, walkable). Second, the **bandage half feels academic** to a healthy cohort — so don't drown it in the madhhab dispute (wājib vs farḍ); teach it as **"the other wiping, with opposite rules,"** using the contrast table.

**Yaad rakho:** Sab "mozon par mash" jaante hain magar dheele tareeqe se — **ghadi pehan-ne se nahin, pehli ḥadath se** shuru hoti hai; aur **har moza nahin** chalta (charma-jaisa, bina baandhe tikay, takhne dhaake, chalne-laayak)। Jabīra ko **"doosra mash, ulte usool"** ke taur par contrast table se parhao — wājib/farḍ ki behes mein mat phaso.

---

## The five landmines for Urdu-speaking adult learners

### Landmine 1 · "24 ghante mozay pehan-ne se" — the clock starts at wearing

The single most common error. People think the 24/72 hours begin the moment the socks go on. The book is explicit: the clock starts at the **first time wudū breaks *after* wearing.**

**Counter-strategy:** Walk the footnote example on the board with clock-faces: wudū 8 am, socks on 8 am, wudū breaks at 12 pm → the clock starts at **12 pm**, runs to 12 pm next day (resident). The hours in wudū after wearing are "free."

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Ghadi **mozay pehan-ne se nahin chalti** — jab tum mozay pehan ne ke **baad pehli baar bē-wuzū** hote ho, **tab** se. Misaal: 8 baje wudū kiya aur mozay pehne, 12 baje wudū toota — to 24 ghante **12 baje se** shuru, agle din 12 baje tak. Beech ke chaar ghante (jab tak wuzū saalim raha) shumaar nahin."*

### Landmine 2 · "Mozay pehan kar wudū kar lo" — wearing, then doing wudū

A close cousin of condition 1. Some put on socks first and *then* make wudū, thinking they can wipe. But the feet must be **washed and the wudū complete** before the socks go on (or at least the wudū must be completed-without-breaking after wearing).

**Counter-strategy:** State the rule and the book's example: wash feet → wear socks → *if wudū breaks before you finish the rest of wudū*, the wiping is ruined; redo it. Simplest safe habit: **complete wudū first, then wear the socks.**

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Mozay **wudū mein** (paaon dho kar) pehno. Agar paaon dho kar mozay pehan liye magar **baaqi wudū poora hone se pehle** wudū toot gaya — to mash baatil, dobara karo. Aasaan tareeqa: **pehle poora wudū, phir mozay**."*

### Landmine 3 · "Thoda sa surākh ho to mash nahin" — any tear ruins it

People assume any hole disqualifies the sock. The book is the opposite: a tear ruins it **only if it is the size of three of the smallest toes or more**; smaller tears are fine (that's the whole Ḥanafī ease — socks usually have small tears).

**Counter-strategy:** Give the measure (three small toes) and the two sub-rules: small tears on **one** sock add up; tears on **two different** socks are **not** combined (each sock judged alone).

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Har surākh mash nahin tor-ta. Sirf **teen chhoti ungliyon (paaon ki)** ke baraabar ya us se bada surākh. Chhote surākh maaf — ek hi moze ke chhote surākh **jodte** hain, magar **do alag mozon** ke surākh nahin jodte (har moza apni jagah)。"*

### Landmine 4 · "Moza utaara to wudū toot gaya" — confusing removal with ḥadath

When the sock comes off (or the time expires, or the foot gets wet), people think they must redo the **whole wudū**. But the book says cases 2, 3, 4 require **only washing the feet** — the wudū itself is intact.

**Counter-strategy:** Separate the **two columns** clearly: *#1 (a real ḥadath like wind)* → wudū broke → **redo wudū** (and re-wipe). *#2–4 (sock off / foot wet / time up)* → wudū is fine → **just wash the two feet.**

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Moza utarna ya muddat khatam hona **wudū** ko nahin tor-ta — **sirf paaon dho lo** (poora wudū dobara nahin)। Haan, agar **hawa** waghaira se wudū toota (naaqis #1), to **poora wudū** dobara. Yaad rakho: aakhri teen soorton mein sirf **paaon dhona**."*

### Landmine 5 · "Patti par bhi 24 ghante" — putting the khuff's clock on the bandage

After learning the khuff clock, learners wrongly apply 24/72 hours to the **bandage** too. The jabīra has **no** time limit — it lasts until the **wound heals**.

**Counter-strategy:** This is what the **contrast table** is for. Hammer one line: *khuff = waqt ki had; jabīra = koi had nahin (zakhm theek hone tak).* And the related contrasts (apply-in-wudū, falling off, one-foot wipe).

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"**Patti/plaster par koi 24 ghante nahin.** Khuff ki ghadi patti par mat lagao — jabīra **zakhm theek hone tak** chalti hai. Aur patti **wudū mein** baandhna shart nahin, **gir jaaye to mash nahin tootta**, aur ek paaon par patti + doosra dho sakte ho. Khuff iske **ulat** hai."*

## Suggested 70-minute class plan

*Class shuru karne se pehle:* board par do cheezein taiyaar rakho — (a) ek **ghadi (clock) diagram** dikhata hua ke 24/72 ghante **pehli ḥadath se** shuru, aur (b) do-column ka **khuff ↔ jabīra contrast table** (waqt · wudū mein pehno · gir jaaye · ek paaon)। Yeh poore chapter ka naqsha hai.

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–6 min | Bridge: wudū mein paaon **dhona** farḍ tha; yeh us par **rukhṣa** hai — mozon par **mash**. Legitimacy: tawātur, *"sunnat ka dil"* (Abū Ḥanīfa: inkaar kufr ke qareeb) |
| 6–14 min | The **three master-keys** — **rukhṣa** (aasaani, magar shart+waqt) · the **clock** (24/72, pehli ḥadath se) · **two wipings, opposite timers** (khuff vs jabīra). Draw the clock + the contrast table |
| 14–22 min | **§1 basic ruling** (mard/aurat, sirf chhoti na-paaki, mota kapra bhi) + **§2 the 7 conditions** — group them: *kaise pehno* (wudū mein) · *kaisa moza* (takhna-dhaak, chalne-laayak, surākh-se-khaali, bina-baandhe, paani-rok, agla-hissa). Land Landmines 2 & 3 |
| 22–32 min | **§3 the clock** — 24/72, starts at first ḥadath (footnote example on clock-faces), changing status. Land Landmine 1 |
| 32–40 min | **§4 what & how** — farḍ (3 fingers, **top**, **once**) + sunna (toes→shin, spread fingers, Tabarani 2/30) |
| 40–50 min | **§5 the 4 nullifiers** — wudū-breaker / sock-off / foot-wet / time-up; **last three → just wash the feet.** Land Landmine 4 |
| 50–56 min | **§6 what you may NOT wipe on** — turban/cap/veil/gloves (no hardship → no concession) |
| 56–66 min | **§7 the jabīra** — necessity wiping; run the **contrast table** line by line (no clock, apply-not-in-wudū, falls-off-OK, one-foot, no-intention). Land Landmine 5. Eye/nail/medicine case |
| 66–70 min | Q&A · assign handout exercises · preview Chapter 7 (Ḥayḍ & Nifās) |

**Yaad rakho:** Do board-tools: **ghadi** (24/72, pehli ḥadath se) aur **contrast table** (khuff ↔ jabīra)। Pehle **teen chaabiyaan**, phir sab aasaan. Sab se ahem teen jagah: **ghadi kab shuru** (L1), **aakhri teen mein sirf paaon dhona** (L4), aur **jabīra ki koi muddat nahin** (L5)।

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 6

- ❌ **The metres of "a farsakh / three miles"** and the tear measured in millimetres. Teach *"chalne-laayak"* and *"teen chhoti ungliyon jitna surākh"* — the numbers are footnotes, not class points.
- ❌ **The wājib-vs-farḍ dispute on the jabīra as a debate.** State the Ḥanafī ruling (wājib, per Abū Ḥanīfa) in one line; do not run the Shāfiʿī/companions proofs (Uhud/Khaybar, qiyas, khabar āhād/ẓannī vs qaṭʿī) as a lecture — that's Part-A depth for *you*.
- ❌ **Re-teaching the wudū nullifiers** — §5 nullifier #1 only *references* the 3a/3b list ("jo wudū ko tore"); a one-line reminder is enough.
- ❌ **Ḥayḍ / nifās detail** — it begins right after this in the book but it is **Chapter 7**. The only thing this chapter says is that the khuff is for **minor** impurity, *not* major (janāba/ghusl). If asked about the female-specific rulings: *"Chapter 7 mein tafseel."*
- ❌ **Najāsa (filth) detail** — that is **Chapter 8**. Don't pre-teach.
- ❌ **Turning the Shāfiʿī contrasts into comparative fiqh** — name *that* they differ (one tayammum per prayer, the bandage being farḍ, falling-off invalidating) only so the cohort recognises it, then return to the Ḥanafī rule.

**Yaad rakho:** 6 sirf **do mash** hai — khuff (kab pehno, kitni der, kya/kaise, kab khatam, kis par nahin) aur jabīra (zaroorat-waali, koi waqt nahin)। **Farsakh ke meel, wājib/farḍ behes, hayd/nifas tafseel (Ch 7), najāsat (Ch 8)** — yeh sab chhod do. Saaf qaida parhao, footnote nahin.

## Self-check before teaching

Before class, make sure you can:

- [ ] State the **three master-keys** (rukhṣa / the clock / two-wipings-opposite-timers) and explain how each decides cases.
- [ ] Explain **when the clock starts** (first ḥadath after wearing) with the 8 am/12 pm example, and the **24h / 72h** terms.
- [ ] List the **7 conditions** (grouped: how you wear them + what the sock must be like).
- [ ] State the **farḍ** (3 fingers, top of each foot, once) and the **sunna** method (toes→shin, spread fingers).
- [ ] Name the **4 nullifiers**, and that the **last three need only washing the feet**.
- [ ] State **what you may not wipe on** (turban/cap/veil/gloves) and **why** (no hardship → no concession).
- [ ] Run the **khuff ↔ jabīra contrast table** from memory (time / apply-in-wudū / falls-off / one-foot / intention).
- [ ] State the **changing-status** rules (resident→traveller = 72h; traveller→resident = nullified if already wiped 24h, else complete 24h).

If yes to all eight — *tum tayyar ho.*

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. **Do they know the clock starts at the first ḥadath after wearing, not at wearing?** *(Ghadi pehli ḥadath se — yeh sab se bada test.)*
2. **Do they know the khuff is for minor impurity only — not janāba?** *(Janāba mein moze utaaro, ghusl karo?)*
3. **Do they know the last three nullifiers need only washing the feet, not a fresh wudū?**
4. **Can they state what the sock must be like (ankle, walkable, no large tear, holds without tying)?**
5. **Do they grasp that the jabīra has no time limit (lasts till the wound heals) — opposite to the khuff?**

**Yaad rakho:** Agar 2+ classmates abhi bhi **ghadi pehan-ne se** ginte hain, ya **patti par 24 ghante** lagaate hain — to Chapter 7 se pehle 5-minute ka **ghadi + contrast-table** refresher lo. Yeh chapter tab kaamyaab hai jab cohort **rukhṣa + ghadi** ko samjhe aur **khuff ↔ jabīra** ka farq pakad le.
