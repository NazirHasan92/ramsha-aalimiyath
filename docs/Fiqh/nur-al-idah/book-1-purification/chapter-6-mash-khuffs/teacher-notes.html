🤝 A chapter about ease — keep the tone practical

This is Book I's first rukhṣa (legal concession): a place where the Law makes an obligation lighter under real-world pressure. The institution is so firm that the book records Abū Ḥanīfa fearing that "one who denies its permissibility has committed disbelief" — it reaches us by tawātur and is "the heart of the sunna." Teach it as ease that is part of the dīn, fenced with conditions and a clock — not as a loophole.

📖 Source constraint

Every ruling and every hadith below is drawn only from Nur al-Idah (book pp. 86–94). Nothing has been added from outside sources. (This chapter cites no Qur'an verse — mash on khuffs is established by the sunna/tawātur, not by a verse; the "Connect to the Sources" section is therefore hadith-only, and left as the book leaves it.) If a classmate asks a question whose answer is not in this chapter, say: "Yeh is hisse mein nahin hai — Chapter 7 (Ḥayḍ o Nifās) ya Chapter 8 (Najāsat) mein khulega."

★ Master Overview — نَقْشَةُ الْبَاب

One bird's-eye map. In wudū (Ch 3) washing the feet was a pillar; this chapter is the concession on it — wiping over leather socks (khuffs) for a fixed time. The book then sets a second wiping beside it — the splint/bandage (jabīra) — which runs on opposite rules. Six questions govern the khuff (ruling · 7 conditions · the clock · what & how · 4 nullifiers · what's excluded), and the jabīra is its mirror.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Explain wiping the khuff as a rukhṣa (concession) on washing the feet, established by tawātur — for minor impurity only, not janāba.
  2. Apply the three master-keys — rukhṣa, the clock, and two wipings with opposite timers — to decide cases.
  3. List the 7 conditions the footgear must meet before wiping is valid.
  4. State the duration (24h resident / 72h traveller) and, crucially, when the clock starts (the first ḥadath after wearing), plus the changing-status rules.
  5. Name the farḍ (area of three fingers, top of each foot, once) and the sunna method.
  6. Name the 4 nullifiers and know that the last three need only washing the feet, not a fresh wudū.
  7. State what may not be wiped on (turban/cap/veil/gloves) and why (no hardship → no concession).
  8. Run the khuff ↔ jabīra contrast — the bandage is a necessity wiping with no time limit.

0 · From Washing the Feet to Wiping the Socks — the 3 master-keys

Open by linking back to the wudū pillar, then hand the cohort the three keys that decide almost every ruling before any list.

Where this fits: the bridge. In wudū, washing the feet to the ankles was a pillar. This chapter lets you wipe leather socks instead — under conditions, for a fixed time.

💡 The 60-second bridge

"Wudū mein paaon dhona farḍ tha. Lekin mozay baar baar utaarna mushkil — to Sharīʿat ne aasaani di: mozon ke upar mash, dhone ke bajaaye." The headline is mercy with a fence: this ease is so firmly established (tawātur) that Abū Ḥanīfa feared denying it is near-disbelief — it is "the heart of the sunna" — yet it comes with 7 conditions and a clock.

⚠️ Teach these THREE master-keys first — everything hangs on them

1 · Rukhṣa (ease, but fenced): wiping the khuff replaces washing the feet — a concession for a real hardship (peeling off wet socks repeatedly). Like every rukhṣa it is fenced with conditions and a time limit. Mercy, not laxity.
2 · The clock: the permission runs on a timer — 24 hours resident, 72 hours traveller — and the clock starts at the first time wudū breaks after you put the socks on (not when you wear them), and switches off when the time ends or the sock comes off.
3 · Two wipings, opposite timers: the khuff is a timed concession (wear it in wudū, 7 conditions, 24/72h); the jabīra (bandage) is a necessity wiping with no clock — it lasts until the wound heals.

Mash on the khuffs — definition & legitimacy

Meaning
Wiping (with wet hands) over khuffs — ankle-high leather socks (the footnote: "ankle-high leather socks that are worn inside the shoes") — instead of washing the feet in wudū. (Khuffayn is the dual of khuf.)
How established
"established by the Prophet's sayings and actions… sanctioned in the 9th year of Hijra." It reaches us by tawātur (mass transmission), so Abū Ḥanīfa said "one who denies its permissibility has committed disbelief."
From the book
Ibn Khuzayma and Ibn Habban relate that the Prophet ﷺ "permitted those travelling to wipe for three days and three nights, and for the non-traveller — one day and one night, provided one purified himself and then wore the khuf." Ḥasan al-Baṣrī: "it is the heart of the sunna."
Roman Urdu
Khuff par mash = wudū mein paaon dhone ke bajaaye charmay mozon par geele haath se mash. Yeh tawātur se saabit (Abū Ḥanīfa: inkaar kufr ke qareeb; "sunnat ka dil")। Musafir 3 din-raat, muqeem 1 din-raat (Ibn Khuzayma o Ibn Habban)।

1 · The Basic Ruling — Who, What State, What Material

The ruling — minor impurity, men & women, leather or not

From the book
"It is valid for both men and woman to wipe the khuf (footgear) to obtain cleanliness from the state of minor impurity, even if the wiping is on something thick other than leather, and it is the same if the sole is made of leather or not."
★ Minor only
The footnote: "One is not permitted to wipe his footgear to acquire cleanliness from major impurity." So in janāba you must remove the socks and make ghusl — the concession is for wudū alone.
Thick non-leather
Examples (Marāqī al-Falāḥ): jukh (a thick cloth) and kirbās (a thick cotton). They must "hold to the feet without being tied and they must not absorb water to the skin" (Muḥammad & Abū Yūsuf; the fatwā follows this).
Roman Urdu
Mard o aurat dono; sirf chhoti na-paaki (wudū) ke liye — bari na-paaki (janāba) mein nahin (moze utaaro, ghusl karo)। Charmay ke alaawa mota kapra (jukh/kirbās) bhi — agar bina baandhe tikay aur khaal tak paani na pahunchne de.

2 · The 7 Conditions That Permit the Wiping (شُرُوطُ جَوَازِ الْمَسْح)

The book: "Seven conditions are required to validate the wiping." They describe what the footgear itself must be like. Teach them in two groups — how you wear them and what the sock must be.

Where this fits: the gateway. Wiping is only an option once all seven are met.

Group A · How you wear them (condition 1)

From the book
"The first requires that they be worn after washing the feet. It is permissible to wear the khuf even before completing wudū, provided the person completes the wudū before the occurrence of anything that invalidates it."
The example
"If a person begins wudū by washing his feet, wears the khuf, and then breaks wind before the completion of wudū, the wiping of the khuf is ruined and one must re-do the process." Safe habit: complete wudū first, then wear the socks.
Roman Urdu
(1) Mozay paaon dho kar (wudū mein) pehno; agar baaqi wudū poora hone se pehle wudū toot gaya to mash baatil — dobara karo. Aasaan: pehle poora wudū, phir mozay।

Group B · What the sock must be (conditions 2–7)

From the book
(2) cover the feet "up to (and including) the ankles." (3) "one must be able to continuously walk in them" — not glass/wood/steel (footnote: a farsakh, ≈ three miles). (4) "each khuf be free from a tear the size equivalent to three of the smallest toes" (less than that → permitted). (5) "hold to the feet without being tied." (6) "prevent water from reaching the skin" (the amount on the hand at wiping). (7) "the front part of the foot must be physically present… equivalent to three of the smallest fingers of the hand" — if the front is missing, wash the foot instead (the heel alone is not enough).
★ The tear sub-rules
Tears on one khuff are added together (if the sum is under the measure, no harm). Tears on two different khuffs are not combined — "each khuf is judged on its own."
Roman Urdu
(2) takhnon samet dhaake. (3) chalne-laayak (sheeshe/lakdi/lohe ke nahin)। (4) teen chhoti ungliyon jitne bade surākh se khaali (kam ho to chalega)। (5) bina baandhe tikay। (6) khaal tak paani na pahunchne de। (7) paaon ka agla hissa maujood (teen chhoti ungliyon ke baraabar)। Surākh: ek moze ke judte hain, do mozon ke nahin।

💡 Teaching tip — group, don't list

Don't recite "1 to 7." Split the board in two: kaise pehno (condition 1 — in wudū) and kaisa moza chahiye (2–7 — ankle-covering, walkable, no big tear, holds without tying, water-blocking, front-present). The cohort remembers two buckets, not seven items.

Duration, and when the clock starts

From the book
"A resident (non-traveller) is permitted to wipe his footgear for one day and one night (i.e. 24 hours) whereas the traveller is permitted three days and three nights (i.e. 72 hours), and this period begins when a person breaks his wudū after wearing the footgear."
★ The example
"If a person makes wudū at 8:00 am and then wears his footgear, and then at 12:00 pm he breaks his wudū, his duration therefore begins from 12:00 pm." The wudū-time after wearing does not count against the clock.
The proof
ʿAlī (RA): the Messenger of Allah ﷺ "had appointed three days and three nights as the period when a traveller is permitted to observe wiping, and one day and one night as the period for the non-traveller."
Roman Urdu
Muqeem 24 ghante, musafir 72 ghante। Ghadi mozay pehan-ne ke baad pehli baar wudū tootne se shuru (pehan-ne se nahin)। Misaal: 8 baje wudū+pehna, 12 baje toota → 12 se 24/72 ginо. Daleel: Sayyiduna ʿAlī ki hadith।

Changing one's status (resident ↔ traveller)

Resident → traveller
"If a resident begins to wipe and then travels before completing his term, then he is to complete the period permitted for a traveller, which is three days and three nights." (Footnote example: starts 10 am Monday, becomes a traveller at 8 pm → may keep them until Wednesday 10 am = 72 hours.)
Traveller → resident
"If a traveller becomes a resident, and had already wiped for one day and one night, then the wiping is nullified because he is now a resident; though if he had not wiped for one day and one night, he is to complete this term" (the 24-hour term).
Roman Urdu
Muqeem→musafir: 72 poore karo. Musafir→muqeem: agar 24 ghante mash kar chuka → mash khatam (moze utaaro); agar 24 poore nahin → 24 tak mukammal karo.

⚠️ The #1 error — the clock does NOT start at wearing

Most learners count the 24/72 hours from the moment the socks go on. It starts at the first ḥadath after wearing. Draw the 8 am / 12 pm clock-faces on the board (Landmine 1).

The farḍ — the obligatory amount

From the book
"It is obligatory to wipe the area equivalent to the three smallest fingers of the hand over the top part of each foot… the wiping of the footgear is to be done once only over the top part of each khuf. It is not valid to wipe beneath the khuf, nor the back or side of it; and it is not sunna to repeat the wiping."
If it gets wet otherwise
"If this area becomes wet even with a cloth or by pouring water over the required area, then it is permissible. Though the sunna is to use the hand."
Roman Urdu
Farḍ: har paaon ke upar teen chhoti ungliyon ke baraabar — ek baar, sirf upar (neeche/peeche/baghal nahin), dohraana sunnat nahin। Haath se karna sunnat (kapde/paani se bhi durust)।

The sunna method

From the book
"It is sunna to begin the wiping from the toes of the feet up to the shin with spread fingers." The basis (Tabarani 2/30): the Prophet ﷺ took a man's hand and said "Verily, my Lord ordered me to wipe like this," showing him from the front of the khuf toward the leg, once, with the fingers spread.
Roman Urdu
Sunnat tareeqa: ungliyon ke siron se pinḍli ki taraf, ungliyaan khuli rakh kar (Tabarani 2/30 — "mere Rabb ne aise mash ka hukm diya")।

5 · The 4 Nullifiers of the Wiping (نَوَاقِضُ الْمَسْح)

The book: "There are four things which annul wiping." The mercy is in the footnote — three of the four need only the feet washed, not a fresh wudū.

Where this fits: the off-switch. A valid wiping lasts until one of four things ends it.

The four — and the "only wash the feet" rule

From the book
(1) "Wiping is nullified by all things which nullify wudū" — break wind → wudū and wiping void → make wudū again and wipe (not required to remove the footgear and wash the feet). (2) "The removal of the khuf invalidates the wiping, not wudū… even if the majority of the foot is revealed" — removing one requires removing the other and washing both. (3) "When the majority of one of the feet becomes wet with water, the wiping is ruined" (over half). (4) "When the period for wiping expires, the wiping is nullified".
★ The mercy clause
"The last three points (namely, point 2, 3, and 4) require only, the washing of the feet, not wudū." So in cases 2–4 the wudū is intact — wash the two feet only. Only case 1 (a real ḥadath) needs a fresh wudū.
The cold exception
On (4): nullified "provided one does not fear he will lose his leg due to intense cold" — if he does, he may keep them on until it is safe. "If the period expires while one is praying, the prayer is void."
Roman Urdu
4 naaqis: (1) jo wudū ko tore → dobara wudū + mash (moze utaarne ki zaroorat nahin)। (2) moza utaarna (aadha paaon bhi khule; ek utra to doosra bhi, dono paaon dho)। (3) aadhe se zyada paaon par paani। (4) muddat khatam (sakht sardi mein chhoot; namaz ke darmiyaan khatam → namaz baatil)। 2,3,4 → sirf paaon dhona (naya wudū nahin)।

The concession tracks the hardship. Where there is no hardship in removing the covering, there is no concession.

Where this fits: the limit of the concession — the head and hands have no khuff-like permission.

The turban

From the book
"It is unlawful to wipe on the turban in exchange for wiping the head, because it may prevent water from penetrating the turban and entering upon the head, in which case the area required to be wiped (i.e. a quarter) was not performed." The Prophet ﷺ did wipe his turban "but… the wetness would reach his honourable head."
The hadith
Jābir ibn ʿAbdullāh, asked about wiping over the turban: "No, not without the water touching the hair." (Muwaṭṭaʾ of Imām Muḥammad 52)
Roman Urdu
Pagdi par mash nahin — paani sar tak nahin pahunchta to laazmi (chauthai) mash reh jaata hai (Jābir: "nahin, jab tak paani baalon tak na pahunche" — Muwaṭṭaʾ Imām Muḥammad 52)।

The cap, veil, gloves

From the book
"It is not permitted over the cap, veil or gloves because there is no hardship in taking off these things." Nāfiʿ saw Ṣafiyya bint Abī ʿUbayd "pull off her headscarf then wipe her head." (Muwaṭṭaʾ of Imām Muḥammad 53) Imām Muḥammad: the headscarf is not wiped over, nor the turban — "the verdict of Abū Ḥanīfa and our fuqahā in general."
The principle
Footgear earns a concession because removing it repeatedly is a hardship. A cap, veil, or gloves come off easily → no need for a concession → the head/hands are wiped/washed directly.
Roman Urdu
Topi, naqaab/dupatta, dastaane — bhi nahin, kyunki inka utaarna mushkil nahin (Ṣafiyya ne dupatta utaar kar sar par mash kiya — Muwaṭṭaʾ Imām Muḥammad 53)। Usool: jahaan mushkil nahin, wahaan rukhṣa nahin।

The ruling and the procedure

From the book
"If blood is drawn from a limb, or one suffers an injury or broken limb, after which he applies a splint or bandage, and… is unable to wash or wipe the limb, then it is necessary [wājib] to wipe the majority of what is used to support the limb." Abū Ḥanīfa: wājib (not farḍ) — "this is the correct view" — so if neglected where there is no harm, "it is valid, and the prayer is not deemed void." The amount: the majority of the bandage (Shāfiʿī: the entirety).
Procedure (by harm)
Untying would harm → wipe over the bandage. Untying would not harm → undo, wash the healthy area, wipe the injury; but if wiping the injury would harm → leave it. The bandage "must not go beyond the affected area" — granted by necessity, restricted to its measure.
What jabīra includes
The footnote: splints of wood/palm wrapped in cotton, and "everything that is placed upon a part of the body which requires it, such as bandages, leather or a brace." Also (p. 94): a medicine on an infected eye or a broken nail the doctor says not to wash — wipe over it; if even wiping harms, omit it.
Roman Urdu

★ Khuff ↔ Jabīra — the contrast (the book's own list)

Time limit
Khuff: yes — 24h / 72h. Jabīra: "there is no time limit… wiped as long as there is a standing reason" — until the wound heals.
Applied in wudū? · one limb?
Khuff: must be worn in a state of purity; cannot wipe one + wash the other. Jabīra: "not a stipulation to tie the splint in a state of purification"; "to wipe the bandage on one foot and wash the other foot is permissible."
Falls off? · intention?
Khuff: removal nullifies. Jabīra: "not nullified if the bandage falls off before the wound heals." Changing a bandage is allowed (re-wiping the new one preferable, not required). No intention is required for wiping the khuff, splint, or head.

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. "The 24 hours start when I put the socks on"

No — the clock starts at the first time wudū breaks after wearing. Wudū at 8 am, socks on, wudū breaks at 12 pm → the clock starts at 12 pm (resident: to 12 pm next day). The wudū-time after wearing is free.

2. Wearing the socks, then making wudū

The feet must be washed and the wudū intact when the socks go on. If you break wind before completing the rest of the wudū, the wiping is ruined. Safe habit: complete wudū first, then wear the socks.

3. "Any tear ruins it"

A tear disqualifies the sock only if it is the size of three of the smallest toes or more. Smaller tears are fine. Tears on one sock add up; tears on two different socks are not combined.

4. "If the sock comes off / time is up, I must redo wudū"

Cases 2, 3, 4 (sock removed / foot wet / time expired) need only washing the feet — the wudū is intact. Only case 1 (a real ḥadath like wind) needs a fresh wudū.

5. "The bandage also lasts 24 hours"

No — the jabīra has no time limit; it lasts until the wound heals. It also need not be applied in wudū, doesn't break if it falls off, and you may wipe one foot's bandage while washing the other. The khuff is the opposite.

6. Wiping the khuff for major impurity

The concession is for minor impurity (wudū) only. In janāba you must remove the socks and make ghusl — wiping does not lift major impurity.

📿 Connect to the Sources

This chapter cites no Qur'an verse — mash on khuffs is established by the sunna and tawātur. Every hadith below is quoted by Nur al-Idah itself, at the book's own level of attribution (nothing added). Use 1–2 in class to anchor the rulings.

The permission to wipe (Ibn Khuzayma & Ibn Habban)

The book relates from Ibn Khuzayma and Ibn Habban that the Prophet ﷺ "permitted those travelling to wipe for three days and three nights, and for the non-traveller — one day and one night, provided one purified himself and then wore the khuf."

The basis for the whole institution and the two durations (24h / 72h). (The book names the two collectors without a hadith number — kept exactly so.)

"The heart of the sunna" (Ḥasan al-Baṣrī)

Ḥasan al-Baṣrī: "I was told by seventy Companions that the Prophet wiped his khuf, and it is the heart of the sunna."

Why the wiping is no fringe view — it reaches us by mass transmission (tawātur), so Abū Ḥanīfa feared that denying its permissibility is near-disbelief.

The two durations (ʿAlī, may Allah be pleased with him)

ʿAlī reported that the Messenger of Allah ﷺ "had appointed three days and three nights as the period when a traveller is permitted to observe wiping, and one day and one night as the period for the non-traveller."

The proof for the 72-hour (traveller) and 24-hour (resident) terms.

The sunna method (Tabarani 2/30)

The Prophet ﷺ passed a man wiping his khuf, took him by the hand and said "Verily, my Lord ordered me to wipe like this," showing him from the front of the khuf toward the leg, once, with the fingers spread.

The basis for the masnūn method — from the toes up to the shin, fingers spread.

No wiping on the turban (Muwaṭṭaʾ of Imām Muḥammad 52)

Jābir ibn ʿAbdullāh, asked about wiping over the turban, said: "No, not without the water touching the hair."

The basis for excluding the turban — the wiping of the head requires water to reach the head itself.
