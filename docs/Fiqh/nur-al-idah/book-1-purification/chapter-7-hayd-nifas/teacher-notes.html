🤲 A sensitive chapter — hold a calm, clinical, dignified register

This is the most intimate chapter in Book I. Match the book's own clinical, legal tone: name menstruation, intercourse, and "the area between the navel and the knees" plainly — vagueness helps no one (a married woman needs to know exactly when she may pray again or be approached) — and stop at the book's level. Frame it from the first minute as mercy and obedience, never exclusion or shame. Teach the classification, the 8 forbidden things, the make-up rule and the istiḥāḍa rule to the whole room; handle the intercourse-timing detail and the navel-to-knee rule briefly, expanding only on a direct (ideally private) question.

📖 Source constraint

Every ruling, verse and hadith below is drawn only from Nur al-Idah (book pp. 94–106). Nothing has been added from outside sources. The chapter cites three Qur'an verses (al-Wāqiʿa 56:79, al-Baqarah 2:222, al-Nisā 4:43) — included exactly where the book cites them, in the book's English wording. If a classmate asks something not in this chapter, say: "Yeh is hisse mein nahin hai — Chapter 8 (Najāsat) mein khulega."

One bird's-eye map. Ghusl (Ch 4) named ḥayḍ ending and nifās ending as causes of the major bath — and deferred the detail to here. The chapter names three bloods that exit the female, and its whole engine is telling them apart by the day-count. From that classification flow the 8 forbidden things (ḥayḍ & nifās) and the opposite ruling for istiḥāḍa (the excused / maʿdhūr).

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Name the three bloods (ḥayḍ · nifās · istiḥāḍa) and classify by the day-count: ḥayḍ 3–10, nifās max 40, anything outside = istiḥāḍa.
  2. State who has ḥayḍ (bāligha · not ill/pregnant · under 55) and the pillar (blood appears outside).
  3. Apply the colour rule — within the window, anything but pure white is menses (wait for the white discharge).
  4. State the nifās rule (max 40, no minimum) and define istiḥāḍa by exclusion, plus the 15 pure days minimum.
  5. List the 8 things forbidden in ḥayḍ/nifās with their key proofs.
  6. Explain the nested lists (8 ⊃ janāba 5 ⊃ ḥadath 3) and the make-up rule (fasts yes, prayers no).
  7. State the two intercourse-resumption cases (stopped at max → now; stopped early → purify first).
  8. Explain istiḥāḍa = maʿdhūr (a permanent nosebleed → keep worshipping, wudū per prayer-time) and the three conditions (start / continue / end).

0 · From Ghusl to the Bloods — the 3 master-keys

Open by linking back to ghusl, then hand the cohort the three keys that decide almost every ruling before any list. Set the dignified, clinical tone in the first sentence.

Where this fits: the bridge. Ghusl named ḥayḍ/nifās ending as a cause of the major bath; this chapter is the detail — what these bloods are and what they change.

💡 The 60-second bridge

"Ghusl ke chapter mein hum ne kaha tha: ḥayḍ aur nifās ke khatam hone par ghusl farḍ. Aaj uski tafseel — yeh khoon kya hain, kitne din, aur ibadat mein kya badalta hai." Frame it as raħmat aur nizaam (mercy and order) — the book calls this topic "one of the most important issues" because a vast amount of fiqh (divorce, lineage, worship) turns on it.

⚠️ Teach these THREE master-keys first — everything hangs on them

1 · The numbers decide: every bleeding is read against a day-window — ḥayḍ 3–10 (avg 5), nifās max 40 (no minimum), and anything outside the window is istiḥāḍa. The day-count, not the feeling, classifies the blood.
2 · Anything-but-white = menses (within the days): red, yellow, muddy, green, black — all count as ḥayḍ until the pure-white discharge (ṭuhr) appears. Colour-change is not the signal; white is.
3 · The great reversal: ḥayḍ & nifās STOP worship (8 forbidden things); istiḥāḍa does NOT — it is like a permanent nosebleed, the woman keeps praying/fasting and just renews wudū each prayer-time (the maʿdhūr rule).

Why this chapter is load-bearing

From the book
The topic of menstrual periods "is one of the most important issues based on the fact that a vast amount of rules are derived from it such as divorce, lineage, the permissibility of intercourse, prayer, fasting, the recitation of Quran, iʿtikāf, the entering of a mosque, ṭawāf around the kaʿbah and maturity."
Teaching note
The book sketches the three kinds of divorce (ḥasan/aḥsan/bidʿī) only to show how tightly divorce is tied to the cycle. That is background — do not teach the divorce rules (they are a different chapter).
Register
Clinical, dignified, exact. Frame as mercy + obedience. Whole-room vs. ask-me-privately: see the warning callout at the top.
Roman Urdu
Yeh chapter buniyaadi hai — talaaq, nasab, ibadat sab is par. Talaaq ki 3 qismein sirf misaal ke taur par; unke ahkaam mat parhao. Lehja saaf aur ba-waqaar; raħmat aur farmaanbardaari ke taur par pesh karo.

The three types — and istiḥāḍa by exclusion

From the book
"The types of blood that exit from the female are menstrual periods [ḥayḍ], postnatal bleeding [nifās] and chronic vaginal discharge [istiḥāḍa]."
★ Istiḥāḍa defined
"Istiḥāḍa is the discharge of blood that stops in less than three days or blood in excess of ten days of menstrual bleeding. Equally, the excess of blood after forty days of postnatal bleeding is regarded as istiḥāḍa."
The teaching move
You never identify istiḥāḍa directly — you classify by numbers, rule out ḥayḍ/nifās, and what is left over is istiḥāḍa.
Roman Urdu
Teen khoon: ḥayḍ (3–10 din), nifās (max 40, min koi nahin), istiḥāḍa (jo bach jaaye)। Istiḥāḍa ko ginti se khaarij karke pehchaano — seedha nahin.

Who has ḥayḍ, and the pillar

From the book
"Menstrual bleeding is blood that discharges (forcefully) from the uterus of a female who has attained puberty, who is not ill or pregnant and has not reached the age of menopause (which is fifty-five years old; and the fatwa issued is based on this view)."
Maturity / the pillar
On Ibn ʿĀbidīn's note: "no girl becomes mature prior to the age of nine." Blood from a girl under nine or a menopausal woman = istiḥāḍa ("the strongest view"). The pillar: "the emergence of blood from the uterus… the appearance of blood to the immediate part outside the vagina. If the blood remains in the inner part… it is not deemed menses" (the fatwā view).
Recommendation
From Sharḥ al-Wiqāya: it is recommended to use cotton wool / a pad / old cloth during menses; it is disliked to place something in the internal area (as with a tampon).
Roman Urdu
Ḥayḍ kis ko: bāligha (9 ke baad), na beemar na haamla, 55 se kam. Is se bahar ka khoon = istiḥāḍa. Rukn: khoon ka bahar zaahir hona. Rui/pad jaayiz; andar rakhna makrooh.

The clock and the colours

From the book
"The minimum days of menstrual periods are three days and three nights… (seventy-two astronomical hours). The average is five days and the maximum is ten days… (or 240 hours)." "It is not conditional for the blood to continue throughout… the discontinuation for an hour or two or more does not invalidate." Short of the minimum even by a small margin = istiḥāḍa.
The proof
The Prophet ﷺ: "The minimum period for ḥayḍ in the case of a virgin girl or deflowered women is three days and its accompanying nights, while the maximum is ten days" (Darquṭnī; al-Zaylaʿī, vol I, 191) — contrary to Imām Shāfiʿī (one day & night).
★ The colours
"The red, yellow and dark coloured fluid… is menstruation, until she sees pure white." ʿĀʾishah "deemed whatever was other than pure white as menses." Characteristic: red but closer to black, with a foul smell.
Roman Urdu
Min 3 din-raat (72 ghante), औsat 5, max 10 din (240 ghante)। Beech mein ruk jaaye to reset nahin; 72 se thoda kam bhi = istiḥāḍa. Rang: safed ke alaawa sab ḥayḍ jab tak safed (ṭuhr) na dikhe (ʿĀʾishah)।

💡 Anchor on the white discharge

The most useful single sentence for the cohort: "Period khatam tab nahin jab rang badle — balki jab safed paani dikhe." This kills the "brown/yellow means it's over" error (Landmine 2).

Nifās — postnatal bleeding

From the book
"Postnatal bleeding (nifās) is blood that discharges after giving birth. The maximum days of postnatal bleeding is forty days. There is no fixed limit on the minimum — the bleeding may stop at any time before the forty days."
The proof
Anas (RA): the Prophet ﷺ "had prescribed forty days for women in the state of nifās, except if she is purified before this" (Ibn Mājah 649). Blood past forty days = istiḥāḍa (Abū Ḥanīfa; Shāfiʿī: sixty).
★ The asymmetry
Ḥayḍ has both a floor (3) and a ceiling (10); nifās has only a ceiling (40) — a woman may bleed briefly or not at all after birth.
Roman Urdu
Nifās: paidaish ke baad — max 40 din, min koi nahin (kabhi bhi ruk sakta hai; Anas — Ibn Mājah 649)। 40 ke baad = istiḥāḍa. Ḥayḍ ki do haddein, nifās ki sirf chhat (40)।

Istiḥāḍa overflow & the pure days

The habit (ʿāda) principle
When blood overflows the maximum, the woman is returned to her known habit: "her regular days are that which she sees last," established even after one occurrence. If a usual 5 becomes 7 (still ≤10), the new number is her habit; if she bleeds past 10 with a known habit of 7, she falls back on 7 and the surplus is istiḥāḍa.
Pure days
"The minimum days of purification that separates two menstrual periods is fifteen days. There is no time limit on the maximum" (it may extend a year or two).
Don't drill the cases
Teach the principle (overflow → fall back on habit; surplus = istiḥāḍa). The worked arithmetic (half-the-sun-risen, the Ikhtiyār "ten each month / nineteen pure" view, the just-pubescent 10/15) is Part-A depth — see deep-notes.
Roman Urdu
Khoon max se barh jaaye → aurat ko uski purani aadat (ʿāda) par lauTa do, zyaadati istiḥāḍa. Do periods ke beech kam-se-kam 15 din paaki (zyada ki had nahin)। Tafseeli hisaab mat parhao.

4 · The 8 Things Forbidden During Ḥayḍ & Nifās (مَا يَحْرُمُ بِالْحَيْضِ وَالنِّفَاس)

The book: "Eight things are forbidden for a woman during menses or postnatal bleeding." Ḥayḍ and nifās share the same list. Teach them in four pairs, not as a flat list.

Where this fits: the consequence of being in ḥayḍ/nifās — what is lifted from her worship and marriage.

Pair 1 · Worship-acts — prayer & fasting

From the book
(1) Prayer — the Prophet ﷺ: "When the menstruation begins, abandon prayer, and when it is finished take a bath and pray" (Abū Dāwūd 286). (2) Fasting, obligatory or optional, is unlawful.
★ Make-up rule
Prayer is not made up; fasting is (see Concept 5). Don't let the cohort assume prayers are owed.
In class
Whole-room. This is the most-used pair — every woman needs it weekly.
Roman Urdu
(1) Namaz chhoR do ("ḥayḍ aaye to namaz chhoRo, khatam ho to ghusl kar ke parho" — Abū Dāwūd 286)। (2) Roza (farḍ ya nafl) na-jaayiz. Namaz qaza nahin, roza qaza hai।

Pair 2 · The Qur'an — reciting & touching

From the book
(3) Reciting a verse — from memory or muṣḥaf. Ibn ʿUmar: "The woman in the state of menses is not to recite anything of the Qur'an, neither the sexually impure" (Tirmidhī 131, Ibn Mājah 596). (4) Touching the Qur'an — unless with a separate covering: "No-one can touch it, except those who are pure" (al-Wāqiʿa 56:79); "No one besides the person in a state of purification is to touch the Qur'an."
★ The exception (reciting)
Words said as duʿā / praise / dhikr — tasbīḥ, takbīr, tahlīl, "innā lillāhi…", Bismillāh, "O Allah forgive me" — are permitted (consensus on dhikr & ṣalawāt). Mālikīs also permit reciting/touching for learning & teaching (she cannot bathe to lift the state).
Detail
One may turn pages with a pen/stick; a tafsīr book that is mostly commentary is treated differently from the muṣḥaf.
Roman Urdu
(3) Quran ki aayat parhna (zabani/muṣḥaf se) — magar duʿā/tasbīḥ/dhikr jaayiz; seekhne-sikhaane ki ijaazat (Mālikī)। (4) Quran chhoona bila-ghilaaf nahin (al-Wāqiʿa 56:79)।

Pair 3 · Sacred places — mosque & ṭawāf

From the book
(5) Entering a mosque — Umm Salama: "The masjid is lawful neither for a man in a state of janāba nor a menstruating female" (Ibn Mājah 645); nifās takes the same ruling. (6) Ṭawāf — even if performed: ʿĀʾishah was told "Perform that which the pilgrim of ḥajj performs, though do not make ṭawāf until you become clean" (Bukhārī 305).
Don't teach the ṭawāf expiation
The book adds a sacrifice (cow/camel/sheep) for ṭawāf done in impurity — that is ḥajj detail. Name only that ṭawāf is forbidden in this state.
Supporting
The Prophet ﷺ: "Ṭawāf around the House is like prayer, except that you may speak. So whoever speaks should speak about that which is good" (Tirmidhī, Chapter of Ḥajj 960) — why purity is conditional to ṭawāf.
Roman Urdu
(5) Masjid mein daakhil hona (Ibn Mājah 645)। (6) Ṭawāf (Bukhārī 305 — "paak hone tak ṭawāf na karo")। Ṭawāf ka kaffāra (ḥajj ki tafseel) mat parhao.

Pair 4 · Intimacy — intercourse & the navel↔knee area

From the book
(7) Sexual intercourse is unlawful with a menstruating or postnatally-bleeding woman. (8) Sexual enjoyment of the area between the navel and the knees — the ḥadīth (Zaid ibn Aslam): the Prophet ﷺ said "She should put on her izār (the cloth wrapping the lower half), then your concern is with her upper half" (Muwaṭṭaʾ of Imām Muḥammad 75). ʿĀʾishah's easier view: avoid the place of blood.
★ Register / in class
State both plainly and briefly; do not elaborate in the full room. Offer to answer details one-to-one (ideally women-to-women). See Concept 5 for when intercourse may resume.
Why the navel↔knee rule
It draws a clear protective boundary — the izār covers exactly the menses-region, and enjoyment above it is permitted.
Roman Urdu
(7) Jimāʿ na-jaayiz. (8) Naaf se ghutne tak lutf na-jaayiz (izār pehna kar upar wala hissa jaayiz — Muwaṭṭaʾ Muḥammad 75)। Lehja saaf aur mukhtasar; tafseel sirf poochhne par, alag se.

5 · The Two Smaller Lists — Janāba (5) & Ḥadath (3) (الْجَنَابَة · الْحَدَث)

After the eight, the book sets two shorter forbidden-lists beside it — and they are nested subsets of the eight. Teach them as shrinking circles: 8 ⊃ 5 ⊃ 3.

Where this fits: the rank of impurities — the worse the impurity, the more it shuts off. Plus the make-up rule and when intercourse resumes.

Janāba (5) and ḥadath (3) — the shrinking circles

Janāba — from the book
"Five things are forbidden in a state of major impurity": (1) prayer (al-Nisā 4:43), (2) reciting the Qur'an (Ibn Mājah 596), (3) touching the Qur'an without a covering (al-Wāqiʿa 56:79), (4) entering a mosque, (5) ṭawāf. (Janāba = "distance" — kept distant from worship until ghusl.)
Ḥadath — from the book
"Three things are forbidden in the state of minor impurity": prayer, ṭawāf, and touching the Qur'an without a covering.
★ Why janāba allows fasting
The eight minus fasting, intercourse, and the navel↔knee rule = the five. The junub can bathe and lift the state; the menstruating woman cannot remove her state at will — so fasting (and more) is lifted from her.
Roman Urdu
Janāba = 5 haraam (namaz, Quran parhna/chhoona, masjid, ṭawāf); ḥadath = 3 (namaz, ṭawāf, Quran chhoona)। 8 ⊃ 5 ⊃ 3. Janāba mein roza jaayiz kyunki ghusl se haalat uth sakti hai.

(a) Make up the fasts, not the prayers

From the book
"A woman with menstrual periods or postnatal bleeding must repeat the days of fasting she missed, not prayer." ʿĀʾishah: "We were encountered with it… thereupon we were ordered to make up fasting, but not ordered to make up prayer" (Muslim 335).
The reason
Prayer recurs five times daily — a week's makeup would be "great difficulty" → eased. Fasting is once a year → manageable → made up.
Recommended
She is encouraged to keep her routine alive: make wudū at each prayer-time, sit on her prayer-mat and do dhikr (Radd al-Muḥtār).
Roman Urdu
Roze ki qaza haan, namaz ki nahin (Muslim 335)। Wajah: namaz 5 baar roz (mushkil → maaf); roza saal mein ek baar (mumkin → qaza)।

(b) When may intercourse resume? (al-Baqarah 2:222)

From the book
The verse-word يَطْهُرْنَ (al-Baqarah 2:222 — "Do not approach them until they are pure") has two readings, and the book uses both for two cases.
Stopped at the MAXIMUM
Ḥayḍ reaches day 10 / nifās day 40 → intercourse permitted immediately, even without ghusl (blood beyond the max is no longer ḥayḍ/nifās).
Stopped EARLY (regular days)
Under the ceiling → not permitted until one of three: (i) she makes ghusl, (ii) unable → tayammum + prays, or (iii) a full prayer-time lapses over her (with time enough to have bathed).
Roman Urdu
Khoon max par ruka (10/40) → foran, bina ghusl bhi. Max se pehle (apni aadat) ruka → ghusl (ya tayammum+namaz, ya ek namaz ka waqt guzar jaaye) ke baad. (Full room mein mukhtasar; tafseel poochhne par.)

6 · Istiḥāḍa & the Excused (الِاسْتِحَاضَة وَحُكْمُهَا · الْمَعْذُور)

The chapter's reversal, generalised. Istiḥāḍa does not stop worship — and the rule extends to anyone with a chronic leak (the maʿdhūr).

Where this fits: the payoff — the same blood that (mis-classified) would suspend all worship instead barely interrupts it.

Istiḥāḍa is no excuse from worship

From the book
"The blood of istiḥāḍa is like that of a permanent nose bleed. It does not prevent prayer, fasting, or intercourse. The one suffering… is required to perform ablution at the time of each obligatory prayer." The Prophet ﷺ: "The woman with extended bleeding is to perform wudū for each prayer" (Ibn Mājah).
With that wudū
She may pray "as many obligatory and optional prayers as she likes" — and blood flowing during the prayer does not break it.
The general category
The same applies to anyone with a chronic condition (ʿudhr): intermittent urine drops, a perpetual nosebleed, constant wind, a wound that won't stop. Maʿdhūr = one whose ailment owns a complete prayer-time so he cannot keep wudū long enough to pray.
Roman Urdu
Istiḥāḍa ibadat nahin rokta — "musalsal naak ke khoon" jaisa. Bas har farḍ namaz ke waqt naya wudū (Ibn Mājah); ek wudū se jitni chaaho parho; namaz ke darmiyaan khoon se namaz nahin tootti. Yahi maʿdhūr ka aam usool.

The three conditions + when the wudū breaks

Start (deemed excused)
"One is not deemed excused until he suffers from the illness for a full prayer time in which there is no let-up" enough to make wudū and pray. (The ailment must own a whole prayer-time once.)
Continue / End
Remain: "the illness must occur in every prayer time after it has been established — even if only once." End: "the absence of the illness for a complete prayer time" → no longer excused.
★ When the wudū breaks
"The ablution of those who are excused is nullified only when the time of the obligatory prayer ends." Riders: a different non-chronic ḥadath (e.g. a fresh cut) breaks it normally; fajr-time ends at sunrise, not ẓuhr.
Roman Urdu
3 shartein — shuru: poora ek namaz ka waqt bina mohlat; jaari: har waqt mein kam-az-kam ek baar; khatam: poore waqt bilkul na aaye. Maʿdhūr ka wudū waqt khatam hone par tootta (alag ḥadath bhi tor de; fajr ka waqt tulūʿ par)।

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Calling istiḥāḍa "a period" (the classification error)

Bleeding 12 days does not mean 12 days of ḥayḍ — only up to 10 is ḥayḍ; day 11–12 is istiḥāḍa, during which she must pray (those prayers are owed). Likewise, spotting under 3 days is not ḥayḍ — it is istiḥāḍa, and she must pray. Classify by the numbers, not by feeling.

2. "Coloured discharge means it's over"

Within the days, every colour except pure white is still menses. Wait for the white discharge (ṭuhr) — ʿĀʾishah: "Do not be hasty until you see a white discharge."

3. "She makes up the missed prayers too"

No — fasts are made up, prayers are not. Prayers recur five times daily (a week's makeup = great hardship → forgiven); fasting is once a year (made up). (Muslim 335.)

4. "When the blood stops, intercourse is fine right away"

Only if it stopped at the maximum (10 / 40). If it stopped early (her regular days), intercourse waits until she makes ghusl (or tayammum+prays, or a full prayer-time lapses).

5. Putting ḥayḍ's "stop all worship" onto istiḥāḍa

Istiḥāḍa is the opposite — she prays and fasts, renewing wudū each prayer-time (a permanent nosebleed). The maʿdhūr rule.

6. Thinking the under-9 girl or the post-55 woman menstruates

Blood from a girl under nine or a woman past 55 (menopause, per the fatwā) is treated as istiḥāḍa, not ḥayḍ — "the strongest view."

📿 Connect to the Sources

Every verse and hadith below is quoted by Nur al-Idah itself, at the book's own level of attribution (nothing added). The verses are given in the book's English wording (no Arabic muṣḥaf text was added). Use a few in class to anchor the rulings.

"No-one can touch it, except those who are pure" (al-Wāqiʿa 56:79)

The book cites this verse for the rule that one in ḥayḍ/janāba may not touch the Qur'an without a separate covering. (Book footnote: "Al-Waqi'ah 79". The book quotes it in English; the Arabic text has not been added.)

"Do not approach them until they are pure" (al-Baqarah 2:222)

The verse on resuming intercourse — its word يَطْهُرْنَ has two readings, grounding the two cases (stopped at the max vs. stopped early). (Book footnote: "Al-Baqarah 222".)

The prayer-in-janāba verse (al-Nisā 4:43)

Cited for the first thing forbidden in janāba — prayer. (Book footnote: "Nisa 43".)

"Abandon prayer… then bathe and pray" (Abū Dāwūd 286)

The Prophet ﷺ: "When the menstruation begins, abandon prayer, and when it is finished take a bath and pray." The proof that prayer is dropped during ḥayḍ and resumes after ghusl.

"Not to recite anything of the Qur'an" (Tirmidhī 131, Ibn Mājah 596)

Ibn ʿUmar: "The woman in the state of menses is not to recite anything of the Qur'an, neither the sexually impure." The basis for forbidding recitation (with the dhikr/duʿā exception).

The masjid hadith (Ibn Mājah 645)

Umm Salama: "The masjid is lawful neither for a man in a state of janāba nor a menstruating female." The basis for forbidding entry to the mosque.

"Do not make ṭawāf until you become clean" (Bukhārī 305)

ʿĀʾishah was told: "Perform that which the pilgrim of ḥajj performs, though do not make ṭawāf until you become clean." The basis for forbidding ṭawāf in this state.

Nifās is forty days (Anas — Ibn Mājah 649)

The Prophet ﷺ "had prescribed forty days for women in the state of nifās, except if she is purified before this." The proof for the 40-day maximum (and no minimum).

Make up fasting, not prayer (Muslim 335)

ʿĀʾishah: "We were encountered with it… thereupon we were ordered to make up fasting, but not ordered to make up prayer."

"Wait for the white discharge" (Muwaṭṭaʾ of Imām Mālik 99)

Women would send ʿĀʾishah cotton cloths with a yellowness on them, asking about prayer; she said "Do not be hasty until you see a white discharge" — meaning purity from menses.
