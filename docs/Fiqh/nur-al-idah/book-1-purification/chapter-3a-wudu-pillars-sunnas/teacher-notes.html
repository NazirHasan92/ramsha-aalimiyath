🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter (Part 1), the student will be able to:

  1. Recite the four pillars (farāʾiḍ) in order, with each limb's boundary, and define what "washing" means.
  2. Explain that wiping a quarter of the head is the farḍ (whole head = sunna), and that the head is wiped, not washed.
  3. Separate the conditions that obligate wudu (person + moment) from the conditions that validate it (the act).
  4. Apply the sub-rules of fulfilment — thick vs sparse beard, tight ring, barriers, the pin-hole/heel danger-zones.
  5. Recall the 18 sunnas in six groups, and explain why niyyah and tartīb are sunna (not farḍ) in the Ḥanafī school.
  6. Distinguish adab (reward-only) from sunna, and list the 6 makrūhāt (leading with isrāf and taqtīr).
  7. Place any given action of wudu into its correct tier — farḍ, sunna, adab, or makrūh.

0 · The Opening — حَدِيثٌ وَآيَة

Begin every class here. Wudu is grounded in a hadith of mercy and a Qur'anic command — and the four pillars are read straight out of the verse.

Where this fits: the emotional and textual entry point. Before any rule, show the cohort why wudu matters and where it comes from.

🕊️ The opening hadith — sins falling away (Mālik, an-Nasāī, Ibn Mājah)

The Prophet ﷺ said: "When a person performs ablution and rinses his mouth, his wrong deeds fall from it. As he rinses his nose, his wrong deeds fall from it. When he washes his face, his wrong deeds fall from it until they fall from beneath his eyelashes… When he washes his feet, his wrong deeds fall from him until they fall from beneath his toenails. Then his walking to the masjid and his prayer give him extra reward."

Teaching frame: wudu is not "putting water on the body" — it is gunaahon ka jhadna. Open the heart before opening the rulebook.

📖 The Qur'anic basis — al-Māʾidah 5:6

"O you who believe! When you prepare for prayer wash your faces and your hands (and arms) to the elbows; Rub your head (with water) and (wash) your feet to the ankles."

The four pillars are in the verse, in order: wash face · wash arms to elbows · rub (wipe) head · wash feet to ankles. Show the class that the jurists did not invent the pillars — they unpacked the āyah.

💡 Teaching tip

Write al-Māʾidah 5:6 on the board and underline the four actions as you read. Then say: "Yeh chaaron arkān seedha Qur'an se aaye hain." When you reach Concept 1, you are simply pointing back at words already on the board.

1 · The Four Pillars — أَرْكَانُ الْوُضُوء (farāʾiḍ)

The non-negotiable core. Miss any one — even a pin-hole of one — and there is no wudu. Exactly four.

Where this fits: the floor of the four-tier gradient. Everything in Concepts 4–6 is refinement on top of these four.

Pillar 1 · Wash the face

From the book
"The first of these is to wash the face… its limit length wise, is from the start of the top of the forehead (typically from the hairline) to the bottom of the chin. And breadth wise, its limit is the area between the two ear lobes. The definition of washing is that at least a couple of drops should flow from the limb."
Boundary
Length: hairline → bottom of chin. Breadth: earlobe → earlobe.
"Washing" defined
At least a couple of drops must flow from the limb. Merely wetting the skin is not washing.
Roman Urdu
Chehra: baalon ki jadh se thodi (chin) tak, ek kaan ki lau se doosri tak. "Dhona" = paani baha — sirf geela karna nahin.

Pillars 2 & 3 · Wash arms (with elbows) & feet (with ankles)

From the book
"The second is to wash the hands with the arms (and elbows) once. The third is to wash the feet with the ankles…"
Key point
The elbows and ankles are included — they are part of what is washed, not a line you stop before.
The heel warning
The Prophet ﷺ saw dry heels and said: "Woe to the dry heels from Hellfire. Perform ablution in full." (Abū Dāwūd 97). The heel is the most-missed spot.
Roman Urdu
Haath + bāzu kuhniyon samet, paaon takhno samet. Edi sab se zyada chhootti hai — "sukhi edi ke liye jahannam".

Pillar 4 · Wipe a quarter of the head (masḥ)

From the book
"The fourth pillar is to wipe a quarter of the head as the Prophet ﷺ wiped the front of his head."
Two corrections
(1) The farḍ is one quarter — wiping the whole head is a sunna (Concept 4, #10). (2) The head is wiped (masḥ), not washed — a wet hand passing over it, no flowing water.
Roman Urdu
Sar ka farḍ sirf chauthayi ka masah — pura sar sunna hai. Aur sar dhoya nahin jaata, masah hota hai: geela haath pher do.

⚠️ The pillars ARE the floor — the validity test is unforgiving

A single dry pin-hole on a farḍ limb (heel, between toes/fingers, under a ring) means the wudu is deficient there. Drill the four pillars until the cohort recites them in order without notes — this is the irreducible minimum the whole chapter is built on.

2 · Conditions — شُرُوط · Obligate vs Validate

Two different question-words. Wujūb = "is this person even required to make wudu now?" (person + moment). Ṣiḥḥa = "did the wudu they made actually count?" (the act).

Where this fits: after the pillars tell you what wudu is, the conditions tell you when it is demanded and when it succeeds. Keep the two columns separate.

The reason for wudu (al-sabab)

From the book
"Its reason is to make lawful those actions that cannot be valid except with wudu. This is its ruling pertaining to this life, while in the hereafter; it is the attainment of rewards."
The framing
Wudu is a key, not an end — this life: it makes prayer (etc.) lawful; the hereafter: reward.
Roman Urdu
Wudu maqsad nahin, chaabi hai — namaz ko jaiz banata hai (duniya), sawaab deta hai (aakhirat).

Conditions that OBLIGATE (shurūṭ al-wujūb)

From the book
"The conditions that render wudu obligatory are sanity, maturity, and Islam… Additionally, one must have the ability to use a sufficient amount of water… Being in a state of minor ritual impurity obligates wudu… the absence of menstrual periods and postnatal bleeding requires the performance of ablution… When there is inadequate time left for the obligatory prayer, the wudu must be made immediately."
Maturity
Reached by a wet dream, or — failing that — at age fifteen (view of the two disciples, also Shāfiʿī & Aḥmad; another view: eighteen, from Ibn ʿAbbās). Fatwa = fifteen. "The pen has been lifted from three…" (Ibn Mājah 2041).
Roman Urdu
Kis par wudu farḍ hota hai: ʿaql, bulūgh, Islam · paani ki istitāʿat · ḥadath / hayd-nifās ka na hona · waqt ka tang hona.

Conditions that VALIDATE (shurūṭ al-ṣiḥḥa) — exactly three

From the book
"1. Pure water is to reach the complete outer skin, and if the area of a pin hole remains in which water does not touch… wudu is not valid. 2. The things that are incompatible with wudu must cease before commencing… 3. One must remove objects of dirt that prevent water from reaching the body, such as wax or fatty grease… nail polish, paint, or glue."
The pin-hole rule
A single dry pin-prick on a farḍ limb invalidates the whole wudu. This is the strict edge of validity.
Nail polish
A film over the nail stops water reaching the skin → wudu invalid. (It is a validity issue, never an obligation issue.)
Roman Urdu
Wudu sahih kab hota hai: paani pure khaal tak (sui ki nok jitni sukhi jagah bhi nahin) · maniʿ haalat khatam ho · har rukāwat (nail polish, mom, glue) hati ho.

💡 Teaching tip — two columns, one example

Draw two columns: OBLIGATE (person + moment) and VALIDATE (the act). Then ask: "Nail polish konse column mein aati hai?" Answer: VALIDATE — it never changes whether you must make wudu, only whether the wudu you made counts. One good example fixes the whole distinction.

3 · Fulfilling the Rulings — the fine print of "reaching the skin"

Every rule here is one principle applied: water must reach the skin. The body's obstacles — hair, nails, rings, wounds — each get a ruling.

Where this fits: the detailed application of validity-condition #1 (water reaches the skin) and #3 (remove barriers). Practical and high-impact — this is where the cohort's daily wudu improves.

The beard — thick vs sparse

From the book
"It is obligatory to wash the visible part of a thick beard… A thick beard is one that covers the skin… the obligation passes from the skin to the beard. It is obligatory for water to reach the skin of a person with a small beard, one of the reasons being that it is easy to wash and poses no hardship."
Thick beard
Hides the skin → wash the visible (outer) part; obligation moves from skin to hair surface. (The book wrote wājib here but means farḍ/obligatory, per Marāqī al-Falāḥ.)
Sparse beard
Skin visible through it → water must reach the skin beneath (it's easy, no hardship).
Roman Urdu
Ghani daadhi: oopri hissa dhona (baal khaal ki jagah le leta hai). Halki daadhi: neeche khaal tak paani lazmi.

Obstructions — nails, fingers, ring

From the book
"If the fingers are tightly held together… or the finger nails have grown over the finger tips… or if there is something which prevents water from the skin such as dough, it becomes obligatory to wash it after removing the obstruction… It is necessary to move around the tight ring during wudu… and when the Prophet ﷺ performed wudu, he moved his ring." (Ibn Mājah 449)
What blocks (must clear)
Tightly-held fingers, nails grown over the tips, dough under nails — remove, then wash beneath.
What does NOT block
Insect droppings, the dung of fleas, and the like — washing is valid even with them present.
Roman Urdu
Tight angoothi: ghumao (Nabi ﷺ ne ghumayi). Nakhun ke neeche aata / lamba nakhun: rukāwat hata kar dhona farḍ. Makkhi ki beeT waghaira: rukāwat nahin (wudu sahih).

Hardship & no re-doing

From the book
"If the washing of cuts on one's feet would cause harm or pain, it is permitted to pass water over the medicine that is placed on the cuts. One is not required to repeat the wiping on the place of the hair after it has been shaved, and neither is one required to repeat the washing after cutting his nails or moustache."
Wound
If washing a cut harms you, pass water over the medicine on it — don't injure yourself.
No re-doing
Already wiped your head then shaved? No re-wipe. Cut nails/moustache after wudu? No re-wash.
Roman Urdu
Zakhm par dhona takleef de: paani dawaa ke oopar se gujaar do. Masah ke baad baal mundaye / nakhun kaate: dobaara karne ki zaroorat nahin.

4 · The Eighteen Sunnas — سُنَنُ الْوُضُوء

Tier two: the Prophet's confirmed practice. Omit and the wudu is valid but deficient. Don't teach 18 loose items — teach six groups.

Where this fits: the climb from valid (the 4 pillars) toward excellent. The Prophet ﷺ made wudu this way and called the threefold wash "the wudu of the Prophets before me."

Groups A–C · Openers · Mouth & nose · Hidden places

A · Openers (3)
(1) Wash the hands to the wrists first (Muslim 252). (2) Tasmiyah — "Bismillāh…" (Abū Dāwūd 101: "no wudu for one who does not mention the name of Allah on it"). (3) Siwāk, even with the finger (Muslim 487).
B · Mouth & nose (3)
(4) Rinse the mouth ×3 (Abū Dāwūd 144). (5) Draw water into the nose ×3 with fresh handfuls. (6) Go deeper (mubālagha) — but not when fasting.
C · Hidden places (2)
(7) Comb the fingers through a large beard (Abū Yūsuf: sunna; Abū Ḥanīfah: permissible). (8) Interlace the fingers (and toes) — at-Tirmidhī 39; Ṭabarānī 22/64.
Roman Urdu
A: haath dhona, Bismillah, miswāk. B: kulli ×3, naak ×3, gehraai (roze mein nahin). C: daadhi ka khilāl, ungliyon ka khilāl.

Groups D–E · Three & head · Quality of washing

D · Three & head (3)
(9) Wash each limb ×3 (Ibn Mājah 420 — once = obligation, thrice = "this is my wudu, the wudu of the Prophets before me"). (10) Wipe the entire head (Bukhārī 185). (11) Wipe the ears, even with leftover head-water (Abū Dāwūd 134: "The ears are from the head").
E · Quality (2)
(12) Rub the limbs with water (dalk). (13) Wash the limbs successively — muwālāt — without pausing long enough for the previous limb to dry.
Note on #10
Wiping the whole head is sunna #10 — the quarter was the farḍ (Concept 1). This is exactly where students conflate the two.
Roman Urdu
D: har aʿzaa teen baar, pura sar (sunna), kaan. E: malna (dalk), lagaataar (muwālāt). Yaad rakho: pura sar = sunna, chauthayi = farḍ.

Group F · The "last four" — and the niyyah rule

From the book
"The intention of wudu is sunna. Though Mālik and Shāfiʿī said that it is compulsory… Our argument is the words of Allah [When you prepare for prayer wash your faces…] and there is no mention of intention." And: "It has been said that the last four mentioned acts (15, 16, 17, 18) are recommended (mandūb), and not sunna."
The four
(14) Niyyah (intention). (15) Tartīb (the verse's order). (16/17) Begin with the right; start from fingertips/toes & front of head. (18) Wipe the neck (not the throat).
★ The key ruling
Niyyah and tartīb are sunna — not farḍ — in the Ḥanafī school (muwālāt too is a sunna — #13 — but the book argues the rank explicitly only for niyyah and tartīb). A wudu is technically valid without them (e.g. falling into a river); but you should never drop them on purpose.
Roman Urdu
F: niyyah, tartīb, daahne se shuru, gardan. Ḥanafī mein niyyah/tartīb SUNNA hain — farḍ nahin (muwālāt bhi sunna hai — #13). Iska matlab chhodna nahin — sirf darja samajhna.

⚠️ The doctrinal heart of this chapter

Most learners believe niyyah (and often tartīb) is obligatory. In the Ḥanafī school they are sunna. Nur al-Idah's argument: al-Māʾidah 5:6 commands the washing with no mention of intention, and water is itself a purifier (unlike the soil of tayammum, where intention is farḍ). Teach the rank carefully — never let it become "so I'll skip the niyyah."

💡 Teaching tip

Teach the 18 sunnas in their six groups on the board (A–F), not as a numbered list of 18. The cohort will remember "openers / mouth-nose / hidden places / three-and-head / quality / last-four" far longer than "sunna number 13."

5 · The Fourteen Etiquettes — آدَابُ الْوُضُوء (mandūb)

Tier three: pure bonus. The book defines adab as "what the Prophet performed once or twice" — rewarded if done, no blame if omitted.

Where this fits: above sunna, below "must". Keep these clearly separate from the 18 sunnas, or the cohort will start treating everything as obligatory.

What "adab" means — and the full list

From the book
"Adab… is considered as that which the Prophet performed once or twice. This is also called nafl, mustahab, mandub and tatawwuʿ. The one who undertakes its performance is rewarded though it is not blameworthy for its omission."
The 14 (book order)
1 sit elevated · 2 face qibla · 3 no seeking help · 4 no worldly talk · 5 unite heart+tongue · 6 transmitted supplications · 7 Bismillah at each limb · 8 little finger in ears · 9 move the loose ring · 10 gargle/nose with right hand, mucus with left · 11 wudu before prayer-time (not the excused) · 12 two shahādahs standing · 13 drink leftover water standing · 14 the "tawwābīn" du'a.
Adab vs sunna
Sunna = the Prophet kept to it; adab = he did it once or twice. Both rewarded — but omitting adab carries truly no blame.
Roman Urdu
Adab = Nabi ﷺ ne ek-do baar kiya (sunna = hamesha). Karne par sawaab, na karne par koi gunaah nahin. In 14 ko 18 sunna se alag rakho.

The two closing du'as (adab 12–14)

Two shahādahs (Abū Dāwūd 169)
"If anyone performs ablution and performs it well, and when he finishes ablution, he utters the words 'I bear witness that there is no god except Allah and I bear witness that Muhammad is His servant and Messenger', all the eight doors of Paradise will be opened for him…"
Drink the leftover (Bukhārī 1637)
To drink from the remaining wudu water while standing, facing the qibla — the Prophet ﷺ "drank standing from the excess of wudu, the water of Zamzam."
The closing du'a
"Allāhumma ijʿalnī min at-tawwābīn wa-jʿalnī min al-mutaṭahhirīn" — "O Allah, make me of those who repent and of those who purify themselves."
Roman Urdu
Wudu ke baad: shahādatain (Jannat ke aathon darwaze khulte hain), bacha hua paani khade ho kar peena, aur "tawwābīn" waali du'a.

💡 Teaching tip — the spirit of adab

Frame the 14 adab as turning wudu into a moment of dhikr and du'a: face the qibla, Bismillah on each limb, finish with the two shahādahs and the tawwābīn du'a. Don't drill them as a checklist — present them as the beauty layer. "Adab woh hai jo wudu ko ek du'a bana de."

6 · The Six Disliked Acts — مَكْرُوهَاتُ الْوُضُوء

The top tier: what to avoid. These do not invalidate wudu, but they are disliked and cost reward. Exactly six.

Where this fits: the mirror of the adab — adab adds to a valid wudu, makrūhāt subtract from it. Together they complete the picture of an excellent wudu.

The two-sided water rule (makrūh 1 & 2)

From the book
"To waste water is disliked… the Prophet ﷺ [said to Saʿd] 'What is this wastage, O Saʿd?'… 'Is there wastage in wudu?' The Prophet ﷺ said 'Yes, even if you were at a flowing river.' It is disliked to use very little water… that it nearly becomes like wiping. 'The best of matters is the middle course.'"
Both extremes
Too much (isrāf) and too little (taqtīr) are both makrūh. Islam wants the middle path even in how much water you use.
Source
Isrāf: Ibn Mājah 425. Taqtīr / "middle course": al-Bayhaqī 261.
Roman Urdu
Paani ka israaf ("behte darya par bhi") aur taqtīr (bohot kam) — dono makrūh. Beech ka raasta.

Manner — makrūh 3 to 6

From the book
"To slap water on the face is disliked for the reason that the face has honour… 'Do not hit the face.' To speak words of general speech… is disliked as it entails distraction from supplication. To seek assistance from others without a reason is disliked… To wipe the head three times using new water each time is disliked, because wiping is only performed once."
3 · Slap the face
Apply water gently — "the face has honour" (ʿAbd ar-Razzāq 9/333).
4 · Worldly talk
Mirror of adab #4. Distracts from supplication.
5 · Help without need · 6 · Head ×3 fresh water
ʿUmar moved to pour for the Prophet ﷺ, who said "Stop, O ʿUmar — I do not need anyone to help me with my prayers." · Wiping the head is done once, not thrice with new water.
Roman Urdu
Chehre par paani maarna (izzat-e-chehra), duniyaawi baat, bila-zaroorat madad, sar ka masah teen baar naye paani se — sab makrūh.

⚠️ Makrūh ≠ invalid

None of these six break the wudu — the wudu is still valid. They are disliked and cost reward. Keep the cohort precise: a makrūh act means "avoid it," not "redo your wudu." Only a missing farḍ forces a redo.

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. "Niyyah is farḍ" — the intention misconception

In the Ḥanafī school the intention is a sunna, not a farḍ. The book's argument: al-Māʾidah 5:6 commands the washing with no mention of intention, and water is itself a purifier (unlike the soil of tayammum, where intention is farḍ). Teach the rank carefully — never let it become "so I can skip the niyyah."

2. "The whole head must be wiped" — head confusion

The farḍ is a quarter of the head; the whole head is a sunna (#10). And the head is wiped (masḥ), not washed — wet hand, no flowing water. Two corrections in one.

3. Treating every sunna as make-or-break

The opposite over-correction: panicking that a wudu where one forgot the siwāk or rinsed only once is "invalid." A missed sunna = valid wudu, less reward. A missed farḍ = redo. The four-tier gradient is the cure.

4. Collapsing "obligate" and "validate" into one list

They answer two different questions: wujūb = "is this person required to make wudu now?"; ṣiḥḥa = "did the wudu they made count?" Nail polish lives under validate, never under obligate.

5. The dry heel / pin-hole

A single dry pin-hole on a farḍ limb invalidates the wudu. Most-missed spots: heels ("Woe to the dry heels"), between toes/fingers, under a ring. Induce attention, not guilt.

6. Forgetting the fasting caveat on rinsing

Going deeper (mubālagha) when rinsing the mouth and nose is sunna #6 — but not when fasting (water could go down the throat). Don't drop the caveat.

📿 Connect to the Qur'an & Hadith

Every text below is quoted by Nur al-Idah itself in this chapter. Use 1–2 in class to anchor the rulings in the sources.

al-Māʾidah 5:6 — the foundation verse

"O you who believe! When you prepare for prayer wash your faces and your hands (and arms) to the elbows; Rub your head (with water) and (wash) your feet to the ankles."

The four pillars are read straight out of this āyah. It is also the Ḥanafī proof that niyyah and tartīb are sunna (the verse commands only the washing).

The opening hadith — sins falling away (Mālik, an-Nasāī, Ibn Mājah)

The Prophet ﷺ said: "When a person performs ablution and rinses his mouth, his wrong deeds fall from it… When he washes his feet, his wrong deeds fall from him until they fall from beneath his toenails. Then his walking to the masjid and his prayer give him extra reward."

The mercy-frame for the whole chapter: wudu is the falling-away of sins.

"Woe to the dry heels" (Abū Dāwūd 97)

The Prophet ﷺ saw people whose heels were dry and said: "Woe to the dry heels from Hellfire. Perform ablution in full."

The textual basis for the validity-rule that no farḍ limb may be left dry — the heel above all.

The threefold wash (Ibn Mājah 420)

The Prophet ﷺ performed each act of ablution once and said "This is the obligatory requisite of ablution"; then twice, "the ablution who God grants a double reward"; then thrice, "This is my wudu, the wudu of the Prophets before me."

The basis for sunna #9 — and a clean illustration of farḍ (once) vs sunna (thrice).

"No wudu without the name of Allah" (Abū Dāwūd 101)

The Prophet ﷺ said: "There is no prayer for one with no wudu, and there is no wudu for one who does not mention the name of Allah on it."

The basis for the tasmiyah (sunna #2) — read by the Ḥanafīs as a denial of perfection, not of validity.

The two shahādahs after wudu (Abū Dāwūd 169)

The Prophet ﷺ said: "If anyone performs ablution and performs it well, and when he finishes… he utters the words 'I bear witness that there is no god except Allah and… Muhammad is His servant and Messenger', all the eight doors of Paradise will be opened for him."

The basis for adab #12 — the two declarations of faith after wudu.

"Even at a flowing river" (Ibn Mājah 425)

The Prophet ﷺ said to Saʿd, who was making wudu: "What is this wastage, O Saʿd?" Saʿd asked, "Is there wastage in wudu?" He replied: "Yes, even if you were at a flowing river."

The basis for makrūh #1 (isrāf) — waste is disliked even where water is endless.
