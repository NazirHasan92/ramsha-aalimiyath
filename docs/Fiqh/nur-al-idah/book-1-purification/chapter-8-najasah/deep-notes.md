# Chapter 8 — Deep Notes · an-Najāsa (filth) & its purification

**Nur al-Idah · Book I: Purification · Hasan Shurunbulali (Hanafi)**
*This file is Ramsha's personal learning material. Read **Part A** first to understand the chapter yourself, then **Part B** when you're ready to teach it to your classmates.*

*Reading guide: technical explanations are in English; key takeaways and counter-strategies are flagged with **Yaad rakho:** boxes in Roman Urdu. Skim the Urdu boxes for a fast revision pass.*

*Source constraint: every ruling, hadith, and verse below is drawn **only** from Nur al-Idah (book pp. 107–114). Nothing has been imported from outside fiqh sources or general knowledge.*

> **This is the LAST chapter of Book I (Purification).** Every chapter before it asked *"how do I make MYSELF pure?"* — wudū, ghusl, tayammum, wiping, the bloods. This chapter flips the question: *"what makes things AROUND me impure, and how do I clean THEM?"* — my clothes, my body's surface, the ground, a sword, a leather hide. So this is the natural closing chapter: it cleans up everything the other chapters didn't cover. After this, Book I is complete and the cohort moves to **Book II (Prayer)** — for which a *pure body, pure clothes, and a pure place* are conditions. So najāsa is literally the bridge into prayer.

> **Where this sits in Book I.** The earlier chapters kept naming filthy things in passing — Ch 2 (istinja) measured the excused filth by a *dirham*; Ch 3b said *"flowing"* blood breaks wudū; Ch 7 (ḥayḍ) produced impure menstrual blood. This chapter gathers all of that into **one system**: it names the two categories of impurity, sorts real filth into **heavy and light**, fixes **how much is excused**, and lists **how each surface is purified**. It reuses two devices you already taught: the **dirham measure** (from istinja) and the **heavy-vs-light comparison table** (the contrast-table device from Ch 6's khuff↔jabīra).

# Part A · Understanding the chapter

The book opens by splitting **najāsa** (filth/impurity) into **two categories** — and this split is the whole architecture of purity in Islam, so make sure you hold it firmly:

- **Real / true filth — `najāsa ḥaqīqiyya`, also called `khubth`** (meaning "repugnant" or "foul"). This is filth that is **a physical substance** — something whose *essence* the law regards as impure: *"urine, wine, flowing blood, stool, pus and vomit that is a mouthful."* You can point at it; it sits on your clothes or skin. **This chapter is about this category.**
- **Legal filth — `najāsa ḥukmiyya`, also called `ḥadath`.** This is **not a substance** — it is *"an attribute that the law applies upon unclean limbs."* It is an invisible legal *state*: minor impurity (lifted by wudū) or major impurity (lifted by ghusl). **You already covered this** — every chapter from wudū to ḥayḍ was about ḥadath. This chapter only names it to contrast it.

So the one-line map: **two filths — one you can see and wash off (khubth, this chapter), one you cannot see and lift with wudū/ghusl (ḥadath, the previous chapters).**

Once you are inside **real filth (khubth)**, the book immediately makes the move that drives the entire rest of the chapter: real filth is itself **two kinds — heavy (`ghalīẓa`) and light (`khafīfa`).** This heavy/light split is the **engine**, because it decides *how much filth is excused* (a dirham vs a quarter) — and that is the question every real-life case turns on. So the chapter has three master-keys:

1. **Two categories, then the heavy/light split is the engine.** Najāsa = real (khubth) + legal (ḥadath). Inside real filth, everything is sorted **heavy vs light** — and that classification is what decides the excused amount.

2. **The excused amount — a *dirham* for heavy, a *quarter* for light.** The Sharīʿa does not demand the impossible: *"it is not possible to avoid a little impurity and it is therefore excused."* So a small amount is pardoned, and the threshold depends on the heavy/light classification:
   - **Heavy filth (ghalīẓa):** excused **up to the size of a dirham** (the same large-coin measure you taught in istinja, Ch 2). More than a dirham → **not** excused (prayer invalid).
   - **Light filth (khafīfa):** excused if it afflicts **less than a quarter** of the garment or body. **Reaching a quarter** (or more) → **not** excused.
   - *(Two tiny extras the book adds: pin-head **specks of urine** are excused — you cannot guard against them; and the "quarter" is measured against the **minimum clothing a person can pray in**, i.e. the area from the navel to below the knees.)*

3. **The traces decide — colour, smell, taste.** Filth is *present* when its **trace** (colour / smell / taste) is present, and **gone when the trace is gone.** This single idea runs through three places: (a) **purification** = removing the substance until no trace remains; (b) the **wet/dry transfer** rules — a clean thing only becomes impure if a *trace* actually moves onto it; and (c) **molecular transformation (istiḥāla)** — when filth's very essence changes (wine → vinegar, a dead body → salt, dung → ash), it becomes **pure**, because the filthy thing *no longer exists*.

**Yaad rakho:** Teen chaabiyaan. (1) **Do najāsatein** — **ḥaqīqiyya (khubth)** = nazar aane wali **gandagi** (peshaab, sharab, behta khoon, paakhana…) — yahi chapter; aur **ḥukmiyya (ḥadath)** = na-nazar aane wali **haalat** (wudū/ghusl waali — pichle chapters). Khubth ke andar sab kuchh **bhaari (ghalīẓa) vs halki (khafīfa)** mein bant-ta hai — yahi **engine** hai. (2) **Maaf kitni?** — bhaari: **dirham ke baraabar tak** (wahi sikka jo istinja mein); halki: **kapde/jism ke chौthaai se kam** (chौthaai tak pohnch jaaye to maaf nahin); peshaab ke **suई ke sar jaise chhinte** maaf. (3) **Asar (nishaan) faisla karta hai** — rang/boo/maza: jab tak nishaan hai gandagi hai, nishaan gaya to paak; aur jab gandagi ki **asliyat hi badal jaaye** (sharab → sirka) to **paak** (istiḥāla).

## 1 · The two categories — real (khubth) vs legal (ḥadath)

The book's first sentence: *"The chapter of najasa is divided into two categories. The first is the real or true najasa [najasa ḥaqīqiyya], which is referred to as khubth (meaning repugnant or foul). This is known as all things whose essence or substance is legally regarded impure and filthy, such as urine, wine, flowing blood, stool, pus and vomit that is a mouthful. The second type is the legal najasa [najasa ḥukmiyya], referred to as ḥadath, which is an attribute that the law applies upon unclean limbs. That is, it is either minor or major impurity."*

Two clean distinctions to hold:
- **Substance vs state.** Khubth is a *thing* (you can see/touch it). Ḥadath is a *legal attribute* — an invisible condition on the limbs.
- **Different removers.** Khubth is removed by **washing off the substance** (this chapter). Ḥadath is removed by **wudū** (minor) or **ghusl** (major) — the previous chapters.

This is why a person can be in *two* kinds of impurity at once — e.g. someone who broke wudū (ḥadath) **and** has blood on the sleeve (khubth) must both **make wudū** *and* **wash the sleeve**; each impurity has its own cure.

**Yaad rakho:** **Khubth** = cheez (gandagi) — **dho** kar saaf; **ḥadath** = haalat — **wudū/ghusl** se saaf. Dono ek saath ho sakti hain; har ek ka apna ilaaj. Yeh chapter sirf **khubth** ka hai.

## 2 · Heavy vs light filth — the engine, with examples

Real filth splits into **heavy (ghalīẓa)** and **light (khafīfa)**. The book gives a list for each; learn them as a **contrast table** (the Ch 6 khuff↔jabīra device), because the cohort will constantly ask "is *this* heavy or light?"

**Examples of HEAVY filth (ghalīẓa)** — from the book: *"alcohol, flowing blood, the meat of a dead animal and its hide (skin), the urine of animals whose meat is unlawful such as a human, fox or wolf, the dung of a dog as well as the dung of a predatory animal and its saliva, the droppings of a chicken, duck or swan, and whatever discharges from a person's body which nullifies wudu, such as flowing blood, sperm, madhī (pre-cum), menstrual period, postnatal bleeding and vomit that is a mouthful."*

A few of these need a note:
- **"Flowing" blood** has a precise meaning the book gives in a footnote: *blood that flows from its original place to another location which then requires purification.* This connects straight back to Ch 3b's master-key — blood is najāsa **when it flows**, not when it merely appears. The little trace of blood that **remains on meat** (in the veins, liver, spleen) is **pure** (it didn't flow from its place). The **blood of a flea, louse or gnat is pure** even in a large amount; the **blood of a fish** and the **blood of a martyr** are also **pure**. *(The proof the book cites for poured-out blood being impure: [or blood poured forth] — Qur'an 6:145.)*
- **"The meat of a dead animal and its hide"** is heavy — but the *hide* can later be **purified by tanning** (see Concept 6); the meat cannot.
- **Madhī, sperm, menstrual/postnatal blood** — these were already named in Ghusl (Ch 4) and Ḥayḍ (Ch 7); here they are simply listed as heavy filth.

**Examples of LIGHT filth (khafīfa)** — from the book: *"the urine of a horse… the urine of an animal whose meat is lawful to eat such as a sheep, and the droppings of birds that are not lawful to eat such as a falcon, hawk and eagle."* (On the horse: its meat is lawful to eat though disliked; Imam Muḥammad held its meat **pure**.)

The book then resolves the apparent puzzles with **two tables** at the end of the chapter — teach the tables, they pre-empt the questions:

- **Table 1 — droppings of birds (faecal matter from birds):**
  - **Non-edible birds** (e.g. eagle, hawk): if they **fly** (eagle, hawk) → **LIGHT**; if they do **not** fly → **HEAVY**.
  - **Edible birds** (e.g. pigeon, sparrow, chicken): if they **fly** (pigeon, sparrow) → **EXCUSED**; if they do **not** fly (turkey, chicken) → **HEAVY**.
- **Table 2 — urine of animals:**
  - **Predatory animals** (fox, wolf, lion) → **HEAVY**.
  - **Non-predatory animals** (sheep) → **LIGHT**.

There is one more sub-rule the book gives on these bird-droppings (a *necessity* allowance): even if a garment is soiled by the **droppings of birds whose meat is unlawful** to the extent that the soiled area **exceeds the size of a dirham**, **prayer is permitted in it** — because such droppings fall from the air and one cannot guard against them. *(The imams differ: Imam Muḥammad held it is **not permissible**, since there is no necessity — these birds are absent from human habitations; Abū Yūsuf held that since they drop from the air and you cannot take preventative measures, the **necessity is established** [so prayer is permitted]. This difference is **Part-A depth** — teach only that there is a difference, don't run the arguments.)*

**Yaad rakho:** **Bhaari (ghalīẓa):** sharab, **behta** khoon, murda jaanwar ka gosht-o-khaal, haraam-gosht jaanwar ka peshaab (insaan/lomri/bheriya), kutte ka aur darinde ka gobar + uska laar, murgi/batakh/hans ki beeT, aur jo bhi jism se nikal kar wudū toRe (khoon, mani, madhī, ḥayḍ/nifās, mooh-bhar qai). **Halki (khafīfa):** ghode ka peshaab, halaal-gosht jaanwar (bakri) ka peshaab, na-khaaye jaane wale **uRne wale** parindon (baaz/shikra/uqaab) ki beeT. **"Behta" khoon** = jo apni jagah se beh kar doosri jagah jaaye (gosht ki rag/jigar ka khoon, pissoo/machhli/shaheed ka khoon **paak**). Do table yaad: parindon ki beeT (Table 1), peshaab (Table 2).

## 3 · The degree of filth that is excused

This is the most-asked practical question: *"My clothes have a little najāsa on them — can I still pray?"* The book's answer turns on the heavy/light classification and a measured allowance, because **a little impurity is impossible to avoid and is therefore pardoned.**

**Heavy filth — excused up to the size of a dirham.** *"Regarding heavy filth, the size of a dirham is excused. The reason this is exempt is because it is not possible to avoid a little impurity and it is therefore excused. And the estimation of this unavoidable impurity is up to the size of a dirham. If however, the amount exceeds, it is not exempt."* So: **≤ a dirham → excused** (pray); **more than a dirham → not excused** (wash it first). This is the **same dirham** you established in istinja (Ch 2) — a large coin, roughly the size of the hollow of the palm — so the cohort already knows the measure. *(The three imams differ: Imam Zafar and Imam Shāfiʿī say any impurity is impure whether more or less; the Ḥanafī position is that a little impurity is unavoidable and is pardoned.)*

**Light filth — excused if less than a quarter.** *"Light filth which has afflicted less than one quarter of one's dress or body, is excused, though if it reaches one quarter, it is not."* So the threshold for light filth is **a quarter of the garment/body**: under a quarter → excused; **reaching** a quarter → not.
- *Which "quarter"?* The book answers a natural question — *a quarter of which garment?* — with the principle that the measure is **the minimum clothing a person can pray in**, i.e. *"that which covers his private area (i.e. that which is from the navel to the bottom of the knees)."* So if light filth lands on a sleeve, trousers, or hem, and that amount **equals a quarter of the minimum prayer-clothing or more**, it is not excused; if less, it is pardoned.

**The pin-head specks of urine.** *"The specks of urine such as that of the head of a pin are excused even if they come onto one's dress and body. The reason… is because one cannot prevent this most of the time."* A tiny splash-back you cannot guard against is forgiven.

> A note on what "excused" means: it means the **prayer is valid** despite the small filth — *not* that the filth is now pure. You should still remove it when you reasonably can; the excuse is a mercy for the unavoidable, not a licence to leave filth on deliberately.

**Yaad rakho:** **Bhaari:** **dirham ke baraabar tak maaf** (us se zyada → dho kar parho)। Wahi dirham jo istinja mein paRha (baRa sikka, hatheli ke gaddhe jitna)। **Halki:** **chौthaai se kam maaf**, chौthaai tak pohnch jaaye to nahin। "Chौthaai" = **namaz ke liye sab se kam libaas** (naaf se ghutne ke neeche tak) ka chौthaai। Peshaab ke **suई-sar jaise chhinte maaf** (bach nahin sakte)। "Maaf" ka matlab: **namaz ho jaayegi**, gandagi paak nahin hui — mauqa mile to saaf karo.

## 4 · The wet/dry transfer rules — does the filth actually move?

The book now handles a very practical family of cases: a clean thing touches (or lies near) a filthy thing — **is the clean thing now impure?** The single deciding principle is master-key 3: **a clean thing becomes impure only if a *trace* of filth (colour, smell, taste) actually transfers onto it.** No trace → still pure. The book gives four scenarios:

1. **A bed / soil already stained by filth, that gets wet.** *"If a bed or soil that has previously been stained by filth becomes wet because of the sweat of one who slept on it or wet feet, and as a result the trace of filth appears on the body or feet, then both are regarded as impure. If the trace of filth does not appear on the body or feet, then they are not regarded as impure."* The traces = **colour, smell and taste.** (Example: a mattress once stained with urine; you sleep on it and sweat — if urine's trace shows on your body, you and the mattress are impure; if not, you're clean.)

2. **A dry clean cloth wrapped inside a damp filthy cloth (→ this is Table 3).** *"A dry clean cloth does not become impure when wrapped in a filthy damp cloth which does not drip when it is squeezed, because the actual substance of the filth cannot separate onto the clean one since nothing drips from it if it is pressed."* The substance can't migrate without dripping, so the dry clean cloth stays clean. **Table 3** gives the three outcomes:
   - If **both would NOT drip** when wrung → the clean cloth **remains pure**.
   - If the **filthy cloth WOULD drip** when squeezed → the clean cloth is **deemed filthy IF a trace becomes evident** (if not, then not).
   - If **water can be squeezed out of both** → **both are deemed filthy.**

3. **A wet clean cloth spread on dry unclean soil.** *"A wet cloth does not become impure if it is spread out on dry unclean soil, such as dried urine, and the earth has become damp through the wetness of the cloth."* (Someone urinated on the ground, it dried, an hour later you spread a wet jumper on it — the jumper stays pure **unless** some trace of filth appears on it.)

4. **A cloth struck by a wind that blew over filth.** *"A cloth does not become impure if it has been struck by a wind that has blown over some filth, unless the filth becomes visible on the clothes."* Wind carrying a filthy smell does **not** make your clothes impure — only **visible** filth does.

The thread through all four: **impurity needs a real transfer of substance/trace. A smell carried on the wind, dryness with no dripping, dampness with no trace — none of these transmit filth.**

**Yaad rakho:** Usool: **gandagi tab lagti hai jab uska nishaan (rang/boo/maza) waqai muntaqil ho.** Char surtein: (1) pehle se gande **bistar/zameen** par paseena/geele paaon se nishaan **zaahir ho to dono na-paak**, warna nahin; (2) **sookha saaf kapda** geele gande kapde mein lipta ho jo nichoRne par **na Tapke → saaf rehta** (Table 3: dono na Tapkein → paak; gandaa Tapke → nishaan aaye to na-paak; dono se paani nichuRe → dono na-paak); (3) **geela saaf kapda sookhi gandi zameen** par phailao → paak jab tak nishaan na aaye; (4) **hawa** jo gandagi par se guzri → kapda paak jab tak gandagi **nazar** na aaye. Boo se na-paaki nahin lagti — **nazar aane wali** gandagi se lagti hai.

## 5 · Purifying the filth — visible vs invisible, and the surfaces

Now the core "how do I clean it?" section. The book first splits by whether the filth is **visible or invisible**, then runs through the special surfaces.

**Visible filth (has a body you can see).** *"Visible filth (even if it is heavy) is purified by removing the actual filth itself even if this is achieved by washing it once."* The rule is about **removing the substance**, not counting washes: as soon as the visible filth (and its body) is gone, the thing is pure — even one wash suffices (the correct view, per Abū Ḥanīfa). *"If its removal is difficult, there is no harm if some trace remains, such as colour or smell"* — e.g. a blood-stain whose colour won't fully come out after the substance is gone. And you may use **soap or other than water** to lift a stubborn stain. **Summary: washing is not a stipulation once the substance is removed.**

**Invisible filth (no visible body — e.g. dried urine you can't see).** *"If the filth is invisible, it is to be washed three times and wrung after each wash. Washing the invisible filth three times is obligatory."* So for filth with no body to remove, the rule becomes a **count: 3 washes, wring after each.** *(Contrast Imam Shāfiʿī: wash seven times, one with soil, for the filth of the dog. The book records this as the contrast; the Ḥanafī rule is three.)*

**Liquid purifiers.** *"Filth is purified from the body and clothes with water or any liquid that removes filth, such as vinegar and rose water."* So najāsa is not water-only — **any cleansing liquid** (vinegar, rose water) that lifts the filth works. *(This is different from ḥadath, which needs water/earth specifically — another khubth-vs-ḥadath contrast.)*

**The special surfaces** — the book lists things that are purified **without ordinary washing**:
- **Footgear (khuff) and the like** — purified by **rubbing on the ground/soil** *if the filth is in solid (dried) form*, even if it is still moist. (Proof: the Prophet ﷺ — *"If anyone stepped on something of harm with his khuff, then the purification of that is soil"*; and *"If one arrives at the mosque then he should observe his khuff; if he sees upon his khuff something of harm or filth, then he should wipe (rub) them and pray with them"* — Abū Dāwūd 385 and 650.) *(Al-Hidāyah: if the filth is moist/wet, not permitted until the boot is washed; Abū Yūsuf: if you rub until no effect remains, it is pure. A juristic difference — note it, don't drill it.)*
- **Swords and metal objects** — purified by **wiping with soil or a cloth**, because filth cannot penetrate a smooth surface. *(The Companions would kill a disbeliever in battle, wipe their swords, and pray.)*
- **The ground** — purified by **drying**: *"When the traces of filth disappear from the ground, and the ground becomes dry, prayer is permitted on that very ground."* (Proof: *"Any ground which is dry, then verily it is pure"* — Darquṭnī 1/125.) **But** — a sharp limit — *you may pray on it, yet you may **not** perform **tayammum** from it,* because tayammum needs ground that is pure **and purifying for other things**, and dried-out ground is pure-in-itself but not purifying (Qur'an, al-Māʾida 6: *"Perform tayammum with clean sand or earth"*). *(This is a lovely callback to Ch 5.)*
- **Trees, plants and standing grass** — likewise become **pure from filth upon drying.**
- **Molecular transformation (istiḥāla)** — *"Filth is purified when its essence molecularly transforms into something else, such as a pig becoming salt, dust or soap or fire has turned it into ash. Another example is wine turning into vinegar."* When the filthy substance's very **essence** changes into a new thing, it becomes **pure** — because the filthy thing no longer exists (master-key 3 again). *(Wine → vinegar is the headline example.)*
- **Sperm (manī)** — *"Sperm which has become dry is purified by scratching it off the clothes and body, even if it is the discharge of a female. And wet sperm is purified by washing it."* (Proof: *"Wash it if it is wet and rub it if it is dry."*) *(Imam Shāfiʿī held sperm itself is pure; Abū Ḥanīfa's proof that it is filth: the Prophet ﷺ — "The dress is washed due to five things…" — among them he mentioned manī.)*

**Yaad rakho:** **Nazar aane wali gandagi:** sirf **asli cheez haTao** — ek baar dhone se bhi (gintī shart nahin); zid'di nishaan (rang/boo) reh jaaye to harj nahin; saabun/koi bhi cheez chal jaayegi. **Na-nazar aane wali (sookha peshaab):** **3 baar dho** kar har baar **nichoRo** (3 farḍ)। **Maaye (liquids):** paani ya koi bhi saaf karne wala maaya (sirka, gulaab-jal)। Khaas satahein: **khuff** — sookhi gandagi zameen par **ragaR** kar (Abū Dāwūd 385 o 650); **talwaar/dhaatu** — mitti/kapde se **poonchh** kar; **zameen/darakht/ghaas** — **sookh** kar paak (par us se **tayammum nahin** — Darquṭnī 1/125, al-Māʾida 6); **istiḥāla** — asliyat badal jaaye (sharab → sirka, murda → namak) to **paak**; **mani** — sookha **khurach** do, geela **dho** do.

## 6 · Purifying the hide of a dead animal (tanning) + the pure parts

The chapter's final section (a separate *faṣl*) handles a special, very practical case: **dead animals.** The meat of a dead animal is heavy filth (Concept 2) and stays filth — but its **hide (skin)** can be reclaimed.

**Tanning purifies the hide.** *"The hide (skin) of a dead animal (including that of the dog) is purified via true tanning."* Two routes:
- **True (real) tanning** — using **karaḍ** (pods of the mimosa plant), salt, orange-peel extracts, etc., to remove the foul smell and dampness and stop the hide rotting.
- **Legal tanning** — something that does the same job, e.g. **cleansing the hide with soil** or **placing it in the sun.**

Proof: the Prophet ﷺ — *"Hide which has been tanned is pure"* (At-Tirmidhī, the Book of Purification — *The Tanned Hide of a Dead Animal* — 1728).

**Two exclusions — tanning does NOT purify:**
- **The hide of a pig** — never becomes pure by any means of tanning.
- **The skin of a human** — not purified, *due to its dignity.*

**Slaughter compensates for tanning.** *"The hide of an animal whose meat is unlawful is purified if it is slaughtered in Islamic Law, and this is the correct view which the fatwa coincides with"* (excluding the pig). So if you **slaughter** an animal in the Sharʿī way — even one whose **meat is unlawful** — its **skin** is rendered pure **without** needing tanning, *"the slaughtering in effect compensates for the tanning"* (the meat, though, remains unlawful).

**The parts of the animal that blood does not flow through are pure.** *"All other parts of the animal which blood does not flow through is regarded as pure, such as the hair and feathers… including the horns, hoofs and bones that contain no fatty grease."* So **hair, feathers, horns, hoofs, dry bones** = **pure** (this is the same "no flowing blood → no najāsa" principle from Concept 2). **However, the nerves are impure** according to the soundest view.

**Musk and civet are pure.** *"Musk is pure, like the musk gland, and it is lawful to eat"* — the musk (and the pod/gland that contains it), found in the deer, gazelle and antelope, is pure and even edible. **Civet** (a strong-smelling substance from the civet cat) is **also pure**, and one **may pray while perfumed with it** — because it has undergone **transformation into an aroma** (the same istiḥāla logic — *something that has undergone transformation is regarded as pure*).

**Yaad rakho:** **Murda jaanwar ka gosht** = bhaari gandagi (rehti hai), magar **khaal** wapas paak ho sakti hai: **dabaaghat (tanning)** se — asli (karaḍ/namak/santare ke chhilke) ya hukmī (mitti se saaf, dhoop mein) — *"jo khaal dabaaghat ho gayi woh paak"* (Tirmidhī 1728)। **Magar:** **suar ki khaal** kabhi paak nahin, aur **insaan ki khaal** uski **izzat** ki wajah se nahin. **Zibah dabaaghat ka kaam de deta hai** — haraam-gosht jaanwar bhi Sharʿī tareeqe se zibah ho to khaal paak (gosht haraam hi rahega)। **Jin hisson mein khoon nahin behta woh paak:** baal, par, seeng, khur, sookhi haddi — **magar pat-the (nerves) na-paak**. **Kasturi (musk) aur zabaad (civet) paak** (istiḥāla — khushboo ban gaye); kasturi khaana bhi jaayiz.

## The big picture — what Chapter 8 is silently teaching

1. **Purity has two halves, and this chapter is the outer half.** Wudū/ghusl cleaned *you* (ḥadath); this chapter cleans *your world* (khubth) — clothes, body-surface, ground, tools, hides. Book I is only complete when **both** halves are covered, because prayer (Book II) requires a pure **person, garment, and place** all three. *(Paaki ke do hisse — andar [ḥadath] aur baahar [khubth]; yeh chapter baahar wala.)*
2. **The Law measures, it does not crush.** It does not demand a germ-free perfection — it **excuses the unavoidable** (a dirham of heavy, a quarter of light, a pin-head of urine). The excused-amount machinery is *mercy with a measure.* *(Sharīʿat **naapti** hai, kuchalti nahin — jo bach na sake woh maaf.)*
3. **Filth is about the trace, not the memory.** A thing is impure while its **trace** (colour/smell/taste) is present and pure when the trace is gone — and when the substance's very essence transforms, the filth simply ceases to exist (istiḥāla). Purity is a **present-tense** fact about the object, not a permanent stain. *(Gandagi **nishaan** ka naam hai, yaad ka nahin; asliyat badli to gandagi khatam.)*
4. **Classification first, ruling second — again.** Just like Ḥayḍ's "classify the blood," here you **classify the filth** (real/legal → heavy/light) *before* you can say how much is excused or how to clean it. Almost every real-life mistake is a wrong classification. *(Pehle gandagi ki **pehchaan** [haqiqi/hukmī → bhaari/halki], phir hukm.)*

**Yaad rakho:** Poora chapter: **do najāsatein** (khubth dho kar, ḥadath wudū/ghusl se); khubth ke andar **bhaari vs halki** — yahi engine; **maaf** = bhaari dirham-tak, halki chौthaai-se-kam; **purification** = nishaan haTao (nazar-aane: ek baar; na-nazar: 3 baar) + khaas satahein (khuff ragaR, zameen sookh, istiḥāla, mani khurach/dho); **murda khaal** dabaaghat/zibah se paak (suar/insaan nahin). Sab **Allah ka nizaam aur raħmat** — naap ke saath.

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

You now understand Chapter 8. This section is how to **deliver** it to the same adult, mostly Urdu-speaking cohort — who have now completed the whole of personal purity (wudū 3a/3b, ghusl 4, tayammum 5, mash 6, the bloods 7). This is the **closing** chapter of Book I, and it is the most **practical, daily-life** chapter of all — every classmate deals with stains on clothes, a baby's urine, a question about leather, "can I pray in this?" So pitch it as the **most usable** chapter they've learned. Your three jobs: (1) drill the **classification** (real/legal → heavy/light) because every ruling hangs off it; (2) make the **excused amounts** (dirham / quarter) concrete and memorable; (3) keep the long **purification list** from overwhelming them — group it.

---

## A note on register and audience — read before you plan the class

A couple of the heavy-filth examples are intimate (madhī, sperm, menstrual blood) and one purification rule is about sperm. These were **already** met in Ghusl (Ch 4) and Ḥayḍ (Ch 7), so the cohort is not meeting them cold. Keep the **same calm, clinical register** you used there: name them plainly *as items on a filth-list*, state the sperm-purification rule once (dry → scratch off, wet → wash), and move on. Do **not** dwell or elaborate — here they are just entries in a list, not the topic. Everything else in this chapter is non-sensitive, everyday material.

**Yaad rakho:** Mani/madhī/ḥayḍ ka khoon — yeh **pehle se** Ch 4 aur Ch 7 mein aa chuke; yahan sirf **list ke item** hain. Lehja wahi **saaf, mukhtasar**: mani ka hukm (sookha khurach, geela dho) ek baar keh kar aage baRho. Baaqi poora chapter **rozmarra** ka, ghair-hassaas hai.

## The five landmines for this cohort

### Landmine 1 · Confusing khubth (filth on me) with ḥadath (my state)

The cohort has spent six chapters on **ḥadath** (wudū/ghusl), so they may try to "make wudū" to remove a **blood stain**, or think washing a stain lifts their **ḥadath**. The two are different impurities with different cures.

**Counter-strategy:** Draw the two-column split on the board from minute one: **khubth = a thing → wash it off; ḥadath = a state → wudū/ghusl.** Give the combined example (broke wudū *and* blood on sleeve → do **both**).

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Do tarah ki na-paaki. **Khubth** — nazar aane wali **gandagi** (daag) — usay **dho** do. **Ḥadath** — andar ki **haalat** — uske liye **wudū/ghusl**. Daag dhone se wudū nahin hota, aur wudū se daag saaf nahin hota. Dono alag — dono ka apna ilaaj."*

### Landmine 2 · "All najāsa is the same — any amount breaks the prayer"

Learners assume any speck of filth invalidates prayer. The Ḥanafī school **excuses** measured amounts — and the amount depends on **heavy vs light.**

**Counter-strategy:** Anchor the two thresholds with the dirham they already know: **heavy → up to a dirham excused; light → less than a quarter excused.** Plus pin-head urine specks. Stress that "excused" = *the prayer is valid* (still clean it when you can).

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Har zarra namaz nahin toRta. **Bhaari** gandagi **dirham ke baraabar tak maaf** (wahi sikka jo istinja mein); **halki** **chौthaai se kam tak maaf**. Peshaab ke **suई-sar jaise chhinte** bhi maaf. 'Maaf' ka matlab **namaz ho jaayegi** — phir bhi mauqe par saaf kar lo."*

### Landmine 3 · Getting heavy vs light backwards (esp. birds and urine)

The heavy/light list is counter-intuitive: a **chicken's** droppings are **heavy**, but a flying **pigeon's** are **excused**; a **sheep's** urine is **light**, a **wolf's** is **heavy**. Learners guess instead of using the rule.

**Counter-strategy:** Put **Table 1 (birds)** and **Table 2 (urine)** on the board and let the rule do the work: birds → *edible + flying = excused; non-flying = heavy; non-edible flying = light*; urine → *predatory = heavy, non-predatory = light.* Drill 4–5 quick "heavy or light?" calls.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Andaaza mat lagao — **table** dekho. Parinde: **khaaye jaane wale + uRne wale** (kabootar) ki beeT **maaf**; **na-uRne wale** (murgi) **bhaari**; na-khaaye jaane wale **uRne wale** (baaz) **halki**. Peshaab: **darinda** (bheriya) **bhaari**, **ghair-darinda** (bakri) **halki**."*

### Landmine 4 · "Filth needs water" / "you must wash 7 times"

Two myths: that only water purifies, and the Shāfiʿī seven-washes (with soil for a dog) is the rule. Neither is the Ḥanafī position.

**Counter-strategy:** Two clean facts: (1) **any cleansing liquid** purifies khubth — water, vinegar, rose water; (2) **visible** filth → remove the substance (even **one** wash); **invisible** filth → **three** washes, wring each time. Seven-times is the *other* school.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Do baatein theek karo: (1) khubth ko **koi bhi saaf karne wala maaya** uthaata hai — paani, sirka, gulaab-jal. (2) **Nazar aane** wali gandagi: asli cheez haTao (ek baar bhi kaafi); **na-nazar aane** wali: **3 baar dho** kar nichoRo. Saat baar wala doosre maslak ka hai."*

### Landmine 5 · "A dead animal's skin / a leather item is always impure"

After hearing "the meat and hide of a dead animal are heavy filth," learners write off all leather. But **tanning purifies the hide** (and slaughter compensates for tanning) — only the **pig** and the **human** are excluded.

**Counter-strategy:** Teach the reclaim clearly: **dead-animal hide → tanning makes it pure** (real or legal tanning); **slaughter** of even an unlawful-meat animal purifies its skin; **EXCEPT pig and human.** Add the "no flowing blood → pure" parts (hair, feathers, horn, hoof, dry bone) and musk/civet.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Murde ki **khaal** hamesha gandi nahin — **dabaaghat** (tanning) se **paak** ho jaati hai (Tirmidhī 1728); Sharʿī **zibah** bhi khaal ko paak kar deta hai. **Sirf suar aur insaan** ki khaal mustasnā. Aur jin hisson mein khoon nahin behta — baal, par, seeng, khur, sookhi haddi — woh **paak** (pat-the na-paak). Kasturi aur civet bhi paak."*

## Suggested 70-minute class plan

*Class shuru karne se pehle:* board par do cheezein taiyaar rakho — (a) the **two-column split** (khubth → wash · ḥadath → wudū/ghusl) and (b) the **heavy↔light contrast table** with the excused amounts (dirham / quarter). Open with one sentence of **framing**: *"Yeh Book I ka **aakhri** chapter hai — aur sab se **rozmarra** ka: kapdon, jism, zameen ki paaki."*

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–6 min | Framing + bridge: ab tak hum ne **apni** paaki seekhi (ḥadath); aaj **cheezon** ki paaki (khubth). This closes Book I and opens the door to Prayer (pure body + clothes + place). |
| 6–14 min | The **three master-keys** — *two categories (the heavy/light split is the engine)* · *the excused amount (dirham / quarter)* · *the traces decide (colour/smell/taste)*. Draw the two-column split + the contrast table. |
| 14–22 min | **§1–2 The two categories + heavy vs light** — khubth vs ḥadath; then the heavy and light example-lists; put **Table 1 (birds)** and **Table 2 (urine)** on the board. Land Landmines 1 & 3. |
| 22–32 min | **§3 The degree excused** — heavy = dirham, light = quarter (of the minimum prayer-clothing), pin-head urine specks. Drill "can I pray in this?" cases. Land Landmine 2. |

Sab se ahem teen jagah: **classification** (L1 + L3), **excused amount** (L2), aur **dabaaghat/khaal** (L5)। Lambi purification-list ko **group** karke parhao.

## What you should NOT teach (to the full room) in Chapter 8

- ❌ **The three-imam juristic arguments** — Imam Muḥammad vs Abū Yūsuf on bird-droppings necessity; Imam Zafar/Shāfiʿī "any amount is impure" vs the Ḥanafī excuse; the Shāfiʿī seven-washes; the moist-khuff Al-Hidāyah/Abū Yūsuf difference. Teach the **Ḥanafī ruling**; mention only *that* the schools differ. The arguments are Part-A depth for *you*.
- ❌ **The fine print of "which quarter"** beyond the one principle (a quarter of the minimum prayer-clothing = navel-to-knee). Don't run the worked sleeve/trouser arithmetic.
- ❌ **Elaborating the intimate items** (madhī, sperm) — state the sperm-purification rule once (dry → scratch, wet → wash) and move on; they are list-items here, not the topic (already covered in Ch 4/7).
- ❌ **Pre-teaching Prayer (Book II)** — najāsa is a *condition* for prayer, but the conditions/pillars of prayer themselves are the **next book**. Say only "a pure body, dress and place are needed for prayer — that's where we go next."
- ❌ **Over-detailing istiḥāla cases** — the headline (wine → vinegar; essence changes → pure) is enough. Don't open a chemistry discussion.
- ❌ **The footnote source-texts as a topic** (Al-Hidāyah, Maraky al-Falah page numbers) — these are the book's citations; you don't teach them, you just rely on them.

**Yaad rakho:** Chhod do: **teen-imam ke dalaail** (sirf itna keh do ke ikhtilāf hai), **chौthaai ka tafseeli hisaab**, **mani/madhī ki tafseel** (ek line bas), **namaz ke ahkaam** (woh agla book), **istiḥāla ki chemistry**, aur **footnote ki kitaabon ke hawaale**. Usool parhao, footnote nahin.

## Self-check before teaching

Before class, make sure you can:

- [ ] State the **two categories** (khubth = real/substance → wash; ḥadath = legal/state → wudū-ghusl) and give the combined example.
- [ ] Explain why the **heavy/light split is the engine** (it sets the excused amount).
- [ ] Give 3 examples each of **heavy** and **light** filth from the book, and read **Tables 1 & 2** (birds, urine) correctly.
- [ ] State the **excused amounts**: heavy = **up to a dirham**; light = **less than a quarter** of the minimum prayer-clothing; pin-head urine specks excused.
- [ ] Run the **four wet/dry transfer scenarios** and **Table 3** (dry cloth in damp filthy cloth) using the "trace must transfer" rule.
- [ ] State the **visible vs invisible** purification rule (remove substance, even one wash · vs · 3 washes + wring) and that **any cleansing liquid** works.
- [ ] List the **special surfaces** (khuff rub · sword wipe · ground/plants dry · istiḥāla · sperm) and the **ground-but-not-tayammum** nuance.
- [ ] Explain **tanning** purifies the dead-animal hide (pig & human excluded; slaughter compensates), the **no-flowing-blood pure parts**, and **musk/civet**.

If yes to all eight — *tum tayyar ho.*

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. **Can they separate khubth from ḥadath?** *(Blood on the sleeve — wudū ya dhulai? Combined case?)* — the foundational test.
2. **Do they apply the right excused amount?** *(Heavy → dirham; light → quarter; can I pray in this?)*
3. **Can they use the tables instead of guessing?** *(Chicken vs pigeon droppings; sheep vs wolf urine — heavy or light?)*
4. **Do they know visible = remove substance (one wash ok), invisible = 3 washes?** And that vinegar/rose-water work?
5. **Do they know a tanned/slaughtered hide is pure (except pig & human)?**

**Yaad rakho:** Agar 2+ classmates abhi bhi **daag ke liye wudū** karte hon, ya **har zarra ko namaz-tor** samajhte hon, ya **murde ki har khaal** ko gandi maanein — to Book II shuru karne se pehle 5-minute ka **classification + excused-amount + tanning** refresher lo. Yeh chapter (aur poora Book I) tab kaamyaab hai jab cohort **gandagi ko classify** kar le, **naap ke saath maaf** ko samjhe, aur paaki ko **rozmarra ka aasaan nizaam** maane — sakhti ya wuswuse ka zariya nahin.
