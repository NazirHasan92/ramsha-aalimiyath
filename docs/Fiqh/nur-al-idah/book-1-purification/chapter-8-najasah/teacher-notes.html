🏁 The LAST chapter of Book I — and the most everyday

Every chapter before this asked "how do I purify myself?" (ḥadath — wudū, ghusl, tayammum, the bloods). This one flips it: "what makes the things around me impure, and how do I clean them?" (khubth — clothes, body-surface, ground, tools, hides). It is the most practical, daily-life chapter of Book I, and it closes the book: a pure body, dress and place are the gateway into Prayer (Book II).

📖 Source constraint

Every ruling, verse and hadith below is drawn only from Nur al-Idah (book pp. 107–114). Nothing has been added from outside sources. The chapter cites two Qur'an verses (al-Anʿām 6:145 on poured-out blood, in a footnote; al-Māʾida 6 on tayammum) — included exactly where the book cites them, in the book's English wording. If a classmate asks about the conditions of prayer, say: "Woh agla book hai — Book II (Namaz)."

★ Master Overview — نَقْشَةُ الْبَاب

One bird's-eye map. Najāsa splits into real filth (khubth — a substance) and legal filth (ḥadath — a state). This chapter is about khubth, which is sorted heavy vs light — the engine that sets how much is excused (a dirham / a quarter). Then: how to purify each surface, ending with the dead-animal hide (tanning).

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Distinguish the two categories of najāsa — khubth (real/substance → washed off) and ḥadath (legal/state → wudū/ghusl).
  2. Sort real filth into heavy (ghalīẓa) and light (khafīfa), with examples, and read Tables 1 & 2 (bird droppings, urine).
  3. State the excused amounts: heavy = up to a dirham; light = less than a quarter of the minimum prayer-clothing; pin-head urine specks excused.
  4. Apply the wet/dry transfer rule (a clean thing is impure only if a trace moves onto it) and read Table 3.
  5. Purify filth: visible (remove the substance, even one wash) vs invisible (3 washes + wring); name the liquid purifiers.
  6. List the special surfaces (khuff · sword · ground/plants · istiḥāla · sperm) and the ground-but-not-tayammum nuance.
  7. Explain how the hide of a dead animal is purified (tanning; slaughter compensates; pig & human excluded) and which parts are pure (hair/feathers/horn/hoof/bone; musk & civet).

0 · From "Purify Myself" to "Purify My World" — the 3 master-keys

Open by linking back to all of personal purity, then hand the cohort the three keys that decide almost every ruling before any list.

Where this fits: the bridge that closes Book I. Wudū/ghusl cleaned you (ḥadath); this chapter cleans your world (khubth) — clothes, body-surface, ground, tools, hides.

💡 The 60-second bridge

"Ab tak hum ne seekha apni paaki — wudū, ghusl, tayammum, ḥayḍ/nifās. Yeh sab ḥadath tha (andar ki haalat). Aaj cheezon ki paaki — khubth: kapde, jism par lagi gandagi, zameen, talwaar, khaal. Yeh Book I ka aakhri chapter hai — aur sab se rozmarra ka." Frame it as the gateway to Prayer (Book II), which needs a pure body, dress and place.

⚠️ Teach these THREE master-keys first — everything hangs on them

1 · Two categories, and the heavy/light split is the engine: najāsa = khubth (real, a substance — this chapter) + ḥadath (legal, a state — earlier chapters). Inside khubth, everything is sorted heavy vs light, and that classification sets the excused amount.
2 · The excused amount — a dirham for heavy, a quarter for light: the Law excuses the unavoidable. Heavy → up to the size of a dirham (the istinja coin); light → less than a quarter of the garment/body. Pin-head urine specks are excused too.
3 · The traces decide — colour, smell, taste: filth is present while its trace is present and gone when the trace is gone. This drives purification, the wet/dry transfer rules, and istiḥāla (essence transforms → pure).

Why this chapter closes Book I

The two halves of purity
Purity has two halves: ḥadath (the invisible state on the limbs — lifted by wudū/ghusl, Ch 1–7) and khubth (visible filth on things — washed off, this chapter). Book I is complete only when both are covered.
The gateway to prayer
Prayer (Book II) requires a pure person, garment and place — all three. So najāsa is literally the bridge into prayer; this is why it is the closing chapter of Purification.
Reused devices
The dirham measure (from istinja, Ch 2) and the heavy↔light comparison table (the khuff↔jabīra contrast device, Ch 6) — both carry this chapter.
Roman Urdu
Paaki ke do hisse: ḥadath (andar — wudū/ghusl) aur khubth (baahar — dhulai)। Yeh chapter khubth ka — Book I ka aakhri, aur namaz (Book II) ka darwaaza (paak jism + kapde + jagah)। Dirham aur bhaari↔halki table dobaara kaam aayenge.

1 · The Two Categories — Khubth vs Ḥadath (أَقْسَامُ النَّجَاسَة)

The book's opening split, and the architecture of all purity: a substance you can see and wash off vs a legal state you lift with wudū/ghusl.

Where this fits: the first division. Najāsa is either real (khubth — this chapter) or legal (ḥadath — the earlier chapters).

The two najāsas defined

From the book
"The chapter of najasa is divided into two categories. The first is the real or true najasa [najasa ḥaqīqiyya], which is referred to as khubth (meaning repugnant or foul). This is known as all things whose essence or substance is legally regarded impure and filthy, such as urine, wine, flowing blood, stool, pus and vomit that is a mouthful."
The second type
"The second type is the legal najasa [najasa ḥukmiyya], referred to as ḥadath, which is an attribute that the law applies upon unclean limbs. That is, it is either minor or major impurity."
★ The two distinctions
Substance vs state: khubth is a thing; ḥadath is an invisible legal attribute. Different cures: khubth → wash off the substance (this chapter); ḥadath → wudū/ghusl (earlier chapters). One can be in both at once (broke wudū + blood on sleeve → do both).
Roman Urdu
Khubth = nazar aane wali gandagi (peshaab, sharab, behta khoon, paakhana, peep, mooh-bhar qai) — dho kar saaf. Ḥadath = na-nazar aane wali haalat — wudū/ghusl se. Dono ek saath ho sakti hain; har ek ka apna ilaaj. Yeh chapter sirf khubth.

2 · Heavy vs Light Filth — the Engine (النَّجَاسَةُ الْغَلِيظَةُ وَالْخَفِيفَة)

Inside khubth, everything is heavy (ghalīẓa) or light (khafīfa). Teach it as a contrast table (the Ch 6 device) — the cohort will keep asking "is this heavy or light?"

Where this fits: the engine. This classification decides the excused amount (Concept 3).

"Flowing" blood — and what blood is pure

From the book
Heavy filth includes "flowing blood" — the book's footnote: blood that flows from its original place to another location which then requires purification. (Connects to Ch 3b: blood is najāsa when it flows.)
What stays pure
The little trace of blood remaining on meat (in the veins, liver, spleen) is pure (it didn't flow from its place); the blood of a flea, louse, gnat is pure even in a large amount; the blood of a fish and the blood of a martyr are pure. (Proof for poured-out blood being impure: [or blood poured forth] — al-Anʿām 6:145.)
On the horse
Horse urine is light; its meat is lawful though disliked, and Imam Muḥammad held its meat pure.
Roman Urdu
"Behta" khoon = jo apni jagah se beh kar doosri jagah jaaye. Gosht ki rag/jigar ka khoon, pissoo/machhli/shaheed ka khoon — paak. Ghode ka peshaab halki (gosht halaal magar makrooh)।

Tables 1 & 2 — bird droppings & urine (pre-empt the questions)

Table 1 · droppings of birds
Non-edible birds (eagle, hawk): if they fly → LIGHT; if non-flying → HEAVY. Edible birds (pigeon, sparrow, chicken): if they fly (pigeon, sparrow) → EXCUSED; if non-flying (turkey, chicken) → HEAVY.
Table 2 · urine
Predatory animals (fox, wolf, lion) → HEAVY. Non-predatory animals (sheep) → LIGHT.
The bird-dropping & the dirham
Even if a garment is soiled by the droppings of birds whose meat is unlawful to the extent the area exceeds a dirham, prayer is permitted in it (a necessity allowance — they fall from the air and can't be guarded against). (Imam Muḥammad held it is not permissible — no necessity; Abū Yūsuf, that the necessity is established. A juristic difference — Part-A depth; teach only that they differ.)
Roman Urdu
Table 1: parinde — khaaye-jaane + uRne (kabootar) = maaf; na-uRne (murgi) = bhaari; na-khaaye-jaane uRne wale (baaz) = halki. Table 2: peshaab — darinda (bheriya) = bhaari, ghair-darinda (bakri) = halki. Andaaza nahin — table dekho.

3 · The Degree of Filth That Is Excused (مَا يُعْفَى عَنْهُ مِنَ الْأَنْجَاس)

The most-asked practical question — "can I pray in this?" The Law excuses the unavoidable, by a measure that depends on heavy/light.

Where this fits: the payoff of the heavy/light engine — the threshold that decides whether a stained garment is prayable.

The two thresholds + the specks

Heavy — from the book
"Regarding heavy filth, the size of a dirham is excused… it is not possible to avoid a little impurity and it is therefore excused. And the estimation of this unavoidable impurity is up to the size of a dirham. If however, the amount exceeds, it is not exempt." The same dirham as istinja (Ch 2).
Light — from the book
"Light filth which has afflicted less than one quarter of one's dress or body, is excused, though if it reaches one quarter, it is not."
★ Quarter of which garment?
The measure is the minimum clothing one can pray in — "that which is from the navel to the bottom of the knees." So light filth on a sleeve/trousers/hem that equals a quarter of that minimum (or more) is not excused; less is pardoned. Also: "the specks of urine such as that of the head of a pin are excused."
Roman Urdu
Bhaari: dirham ke baraabar tak maaf (wahi istinja waala sikka); us se zyada → dho kar parho. Halki: chौthaai se kam maaf; chौthaai tak pohnche to nahin. "Chौthaai" = namaz ke sab se kam libaas (naaf se ghutne ke neeche) ka chौthaai. Peshaab ke suई-sar jaise chhinte maaf.

💡 "Excused" ≠ "pure"

Excused means the prayer is valid despite the small filth — not that the filth has become pure. Remove it when you reasonably can; the excuse is mercy for the unavoidable, not a licence to leave filth deliberately.

4 · The Wet/Dry Transfer Rules — Does the Filth Actually Move? (انْتِقَالُ النَّجَاسَة)

A clean thing touches (or lies near) a filthy thing — is it now impure? One rule decides: a clean thing is impure only if a trace of filth (colour/smell/taste) actually transfers onto it.

Where this fits: master-key 3 in action — the trace must really move. Smell-on-the-wind and no-drip dampness do not transmit filth.

The four scenarios

Bed / soil (from the book)
"If a bed or soil that has previously been stained by filth becomes wet because of the sweat of one who slept on it or wet feet, and as a result the trace of filth appears on the body or feet, then both are regarded as impure. If the trace… does not appear… then they are not regarded as impure." The traces = colour, smell, taste.
Wet on dry soil · wind
Wet cloth on dry unclean soil (e.g. dried urine, earth dampened by the cloth) → pure unless a trace appears. A wind that blew over filth striking a cloth → pure unless the filth becomes visible (a carried smell does not make it impure).
Table 3 · dry clean cloth in a damp filthy cloth
Both would NOT drip when wrung → clean cloth remains pure. Filthy cloth WOULD drip → clean cloth is filthy IF a trace becomes evident (if not, then not). Water squeezes from both → both are filthy.
Roman Urdu
Usool: gandagi tab lagti hai jab uska nishaan waqai muntaqil ho. (1) gande bistar par paseena → nishaan aaye to na-paak; (2) sookha saaf kapda jo gande mein lipta par na Tapke → saaf (Table 3); (3) geela kapda sookhi gandi zameen par → paak jab tak nishaan na aaye; (4) hawa → kapda paak jab tak gandagi nazar na aaye.

5 · Purifying the Filth — Visible/Invisible & the Surfaces (بِمَ تَطْهُرُ النَّجَاسَة)

Where this fits: from classifying filth to removing it. The principle throughout: remove the trace; when the essence transforms, the filth is simply gone.

Visible vs invisible + liquid purifiers

Visible — from the book
"Visible filth (even if it is heavy) is purified by removing the actual filth itself even if this is achieved by washing it once." The rule is removing the substance, not counting washes; if removal is hard, no harm if a colour/smell trace remains; soap or other-than-water may be used. "Washing is not a stipulation after the substance has been removed."
Invisible — from the book
"If the filth is invisible, it is to be washed three times and wrung after each wash. Washing the invisible filth three times is obligatory." (Contrast Shāfiʿī: seven times, one with soil, for the dog's filth.)
Liquid purifiers
"Filth is purified from the body and clothes with water or any liquid that removes filth, such as vinegar and rose water." (Unlike ḥadath, which needs water/earth — a khubth-vs-ḥadath contrast.)

Nazar aane wali: asli cheez haTao (ek baar bhi kaafi; gintī shart nahin); zid'di rang/boo reh jaaye to harj nahin; saabun chalega. Na-nazar aane wali: 3 baar dho kar nichoRo (farḍ)। Maaye: paani ya koi bhi saaf karne wala (sirka, gulaab-jal)।

The special surfaces

Khuff & ground
Footgear (khuff) — purified by rubbing on the ground/soil if the filth is in solid (dried) form, even if moist (Abū Dāwūd 385 and 650). The ground — purified by drying: "Any ground which is dry, then verily it is pure" (Darquṭnī 1/125) — you may pray on it, but you may not make tayammum from it (al-Māʾida 6: it is pure-in-itself but not purifying). Trees, plants & grass likewise become pure on drying.
Swords & metal
Swords and metal objects — purified by wiping with soil or a cloth (filth cannot penetrate). The Companions killed in battle, wiped their swords, and prayed.
★ Istiḥāla & sperm
Molecular transformation (istiḥāla) — when the essence transforms, it is pure: a pig becoming salt, dust/soap/fire turning it to ash, wine turning into vinegar. Sperm (manī) — dry → scratch it off (even a female's discharge); wet → wash it ("Wash it if it is wet and rub it if it is dry"). (Shāfiʿī held sperm pure; Abū Ḥanīfa's proof it is filth: "The dress is washed due to five things…" — among them manī.)
Roman Urdu
Khuff: sookhi gandagi zameen par ragaR kar (Abū Dāwūd 385 o 650)। Zameen/darakht/ghaas: sookh kar paak — par us se tayammum nahin (Darquṭnī 1/125, al-Māʾida 6)। Talwaar: mitti/kapde se poonchh kar. Istiḥāla: asliyat badle (sharab→sirka) to paak. Mani: sookha khurach, geela dho.

The closing faṣl. The meat of a dead animal stays filth — but its hide can be reclaimed by tanning, and the "no flowing blood → pure" parts come along too.

Where this fits: the practical close — leather, hide, and which animal parts are pure. Same "no flowing blood → no najāsa" principle as Concept 2.

Tanning, slaughter, and the pure parts

Tanning — from the book
"The hide (skin) of a dead animal (including that of the dog) is purified via true tanning." True tanning: karaḍ (mimosa pods), salt, orange-peel extracts. Legal tanning: cleansing with soil, or placing in the sun. Proof: "Hide which has been tanned is pure" (At-Tirmidhī, the Book of Purification 1728).
★ The two exclusions
Tanning does not purify the hide of a pig (never), nor the skin of a human (due to its dignity).
Slaughter compensates · the pure parts · musk & civet
"The hide of an animal whose meat is unlawful is purified if it is slaughtered in accordance with Islamic Law" (excluding the pig) — "the slaughtering in effect compensates for the tanning" (skin pure, meat still unlawful). Parts with no flowing blood are pure: hair, feathers, horns, hoofs, dry bones — but the nerves are impure (soundest view). Musk (and its gland) is pure and lawful to eat; civet is pure and one may pray perfumed in it (it underwent transformation into an aroma — istiḥāla).
Roman Urdu
Khaal: dabaaghat (tanning) se paak — asli (karaḍ/namak) ya hukmī (mitti/dhoop); "jo khaal dabaaghat ho woh paak" (Tirmidhī 1728)। Sirf suar aur insaan mustasnā. Zibah bhi khaal paak kar deta hai (gosht haraam rahega)। Khoon-na-behne wale hisse (baal/par/seeng/khur/sookhi haddi) paak (pat-the na-paak)। Kasturi o civet paak.

💡 This closes Book I

With najāsa covered, the cohort has both halves of purity — the self (ḥadath) and the world (khubth). They are now ready for Book II — the Chapter of Prayer (aṣ-Ṣalāh), for which a pure body, dress and place are conditions.

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Confusing khubth (filth on me) with ḥadath (my state)

You don't make wudū to remove a blood stain, and washing a stain doesn't lift your ḥadath. Khubth = a substance → wash it off; ḥadath = a state → wudū/ghusl. If you broke wudū and have blood on your sleeve, do both.

2. "Any amount of filth breaks the prayer"

The Ḥanafī school excuses measured amounts: heavy → up to a dirham; light → less than a quarter of the minimum prayer-clothing; pin-head urine specks. "Excused" means the prayer is valid (still clean it when you can).

3. Getting heavy vs light backwards (birds & urine)

A chicken's droppings are heavy, but a flying pigeon's are excused; a sheep's urine is light, a wolf's is heavy. Use Table 1 (birds) and Table 2 (urine) — don't guess.

4. "Filth needs water" / "you must wash 7 times"

Neither is Ḥanafī. Any cleansing liquid (water, vinegar, rose water) purifies khubth. Visible filth → remove the substance (even one wash); invisible filth → three washes + wring. Seven-times is the other school.

5. "A dead animal's skin / any leather is always impure"

Tanning purifies the hide (real or legal), and slaughter of even an unlawful-meat animal purifies its skin — except the pig and the human. Hair, feathers, horn, hoof, dry bone are pure too (nerves are not).

6. Thinking dried-out ground that's prayable can also be used for tayammum

Dried ground is pure-in-itself (you may pray on it) but not purifying for other things — so you may not make tayammum from it (al-Māʾida 6).

📿 Connect to the Sources

Every verse and hadith below is quoted by Nur al-Idah itself, at the book's own level of attribution (nothing added). The verses are given in the book's English wording (no Arabic muṣḥaf text was added). Use a few in class to anchor the rulings.

"…or blood poured forth" (al-Anʿām 6:145)

The book cites this verse (in a footnote on "flowing blood") for the rule that blood which pours forth from a slaughtered animal is impure — while the little trace left in the meat, and the blood of a flea, fish or martyr, is pure. (Book footnote: "Quran 6:145".)

"Perform tayammum with clean sand or earth" (al-Māʾida 6)

Cited for the limit on the dried ground: it is pure enough to pray on, but you may not make tayammum from it, because tayammum needs ground that is pure and purifying for other things. (Book footnote: "Al-Maida 6".)

The khuff hadiths (Abū Dāwūd 385 and 650)

The Prophet ﷺ: "If anyone stepped on something of harm with his khuff, then the purification of that is soil"; and: "If one arrives at the mosque… if he sees upon his khuff something of harm or filth, then he should wipe (rub) them and pray with them." The basis for purifying footgear by rubbing on the ground.

"Any ground which is dry, then verily it is pure" (Darquṭnī 1/125)

The proof that the ground is purified by drying — once the traces of filth (colour, smell) are gone and it is dry, prayer is permitted on that very ground.

"Wash it if it is wet and rub it if it is dry"

The Prophet ﷺ on sperm (manī): dry sperm is scratched off, wet sperm is washed. (Abū Ḥanīfa's proof that sperm is filth: "The dress is washed due to five things…" — among them he mentioned manī.)

"Hide which has been tanned is pure" (At-Tirmidhī 1728)

At-Tirmidhī, the Book of Purification (The Tanned Hide of a Dead Animal). The proof that tanning purifies the hide of a dead animal — except the pig (and the human, due to dignity).
