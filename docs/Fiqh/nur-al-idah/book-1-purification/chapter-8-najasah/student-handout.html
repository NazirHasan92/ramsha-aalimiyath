Chapter 8 — an-Najāsa (filth) & its purification · Student Handout

Nur al-Idah · Book I: Purification · The two kinds of filth, heavy vs light, how much is excused, and how to clean each surface · the last chapter of Book I

👋 Welcome — What you'll learn today

Every chapter so far has answered "how do I purify myself?" — wudū, ghusl, tayammum, the bloods. Today we flip the question: "what makes the things around me impure — my clothes, my body's surface, the ground, leather — and how do I clean them?" This is the most everyday chapter of all, and it completes Book I (Purification). We answer:

  1. The two kinds of filth — khubth (a substance) vs ḥadath (a state).
  2. Heavy vs light filth — and the bird/urine tables.
  3. How much is excused — the dirham and the quarter.
  4. The wet/dry transfer rules — when does filth actually move?
  5. How to purify filth — visible vs invisible, and the special surfaces.
  6. The hide of a dead animal — tanning, and which parts are pure.

The big idea: purity has two halves — your self (ḥadath, the earlier chapters) and your world (khubth, today). Once you have both, you are ready for Prayer. (Paaki ke do hisse — andar aur baahar.)

Source: everything here comes from one book — Nur al-Idah by Imam Hasan Shurunbulali (the standard introductory Hanafi text), the Chapter on Filth and its Purification (book pp. 107–114). No outside sources have been added.

The three keys to the whole chapter

Before the lists, learn these three. Almost every ruling turns on one of them:

KeyWhat it means
1 · Two categories — heavy/light is the engineFilth is either khubth (a real substance — washed off) or ḥadath (a legal state — wudū/ghusl). Inside khubth, everything is sorted heavy vs light, and that decides how much is excused.
2 · The excused amountThe Law excuses the unavoidable: heavy filth up to a dirham (a large coin), light filth under a quarter of your clothing/body. Pin-head urine specks are excused too.
3 · The traces decideFilth is present while its trace (colour, smell, taste) is present, and gone when the trace is gone — and when its very essence transforms (wine → vinegar), it becomes pure.

Urdu mein: Teen chaabiyaan: (1) do najāsatein (khubth = dho kar, ḥadath = wudū/ghusl); khubth ke andar bhaari vs halki — yahi engine; (2) maaf — bhaari dirham tak, halki chौthaai se kam; (3) nishaan (rang/boo/maza) faisla karta hai — asliyat badle to paak.

Rule 1 · The two kinds of filth

KindWhat it isHow it is removed
Khubth (real / true filth)A substance you can see/touch — urine, wine, flowing blood, stool, pus, a mouthful of vomitWash off the substance (this chapter)
Ḥadath (legal filth)An invisible state on the limbs — minor or major impurityWudū (minor) / Ghusl (major) (earlier chapters)

You can be in both at once — e.g. you broke wudū and there is blood on your sleeve. Then you must make wudū and wash the sleeve: each impurity has its own cure.

Urdu mein: Khubth = gandagi (daag) → dho do. Ḥadath = haalat → wudū/ghusl. Daag dhone se wudū nahin hota, wudū se daag saaf nahin hota — dono alag.

Rule 2 · Heavy vs light filth

Heavy (ghalīẓa)Light (khafīfa)
Alcohol · flowing blood · the meat & hide of a dead animal · the urine of animals whose meat is unlawful (human, fox, wolf) · the dung & saliva of a dog or a predator · the droppings of a chicken, duck or swan · anything from the body that nullifies wudū (blood, sperm, madhī, menses, postnatal blood, a mouthful of vomit) The urine of a horse · the urine of a lawful-meat animal (sheep) · the droppings of non-edible birds that fly (falcon, hawk, eagle)

💡 "Flowing" blood — and the blood that stays pure

"Flowing" blood means blood that leaves its place and moves elsewhere. So the trace of blood left in meat (veins, liver, spleen), and the blood of a flea, fish or martyr, is pure.

Two tables in the book settle the tricky cases (use them — don't guess):

Non-edible birds (eagle, hawk)that fly (eagle, hawk)Light
non-flyingHeavy

non-flying (turkey, chicken)Heavy

Predatory animals (fox, wolf, lion)Heavy

Urdu mein: Bhaari: sharab, behta khoon, murda gosht-o-khaal, haraam-gosht ka peshaab, kutte/darinde ka gobar. Halki: ghode ka peshaab, bakri ka peshaab, uRne wale na-khaaye parindon ki beeT. Table dekho, andaaza mat lagao.

Rule 3 · How much filth is excused?

The Law does not demand the impossible — a little filth is unavoidable, so it is pardoned. The allowance depends on heavy vs light:

FilthExcused if…Not excused if…
Heavyup to the size of a dirham (a large coin)it exceeds a dirham
Lightless than a quarter of the clothing/bodyit reaches a quarter or more
Urine speckstiny pin-head splashes — always excused—

"A quarter of which garment?" — a quarter of the minimum clothing you can pray in, i.e. what covers the private area (from the navel to below the knees).

⚠️ "Excused" does not mean "pure"

Excused means your prayer is valid despite the small filth — not that the filth has become clean. Remove it when you reasonably can.

Urdu mein: Bhaari: dirham tak maaf (wahi istinja waala sikka). Halki: chौthaai se kam maaf. Peshaab ke suई-sar jaise chhinte maaf. "Maaf" = namaz ho jaayegi — phir bhi saaf kar lo.

Rule 4 · The wet/dry transfer rules

A clean thing touches (or lies near) a filthy thing — is it now impure? Only if a trace of filth (colour, smell, taste) actually moves onto it. Four cases:

SituationThe ruling
A stained bed/soil gets wet (your sweat or wet feet)Impure only if a trace appears on the body/feet; otherwise both stay clean.
A dry clean cloth wrapped in a damp filthy cloth that does not dripThe clean cloth stays clean (the filth can't migrate without dripping). (See Table 3.)
A wet clean cloth spread on dry unclean soilPure unless a trace of filth appears on it.
A cloth struck by wind that blew over filthPure unless the filth becomes visible on it (a smell alone does not make it impure).
Table 3 · A dry clean cloth wrapped inside a damp filthy cloth
If both would not drip when wrungthe clean cloth remains pure
If the filthy cloth would drip when squeezedthe clean cloth is filthy if a trace becomes evident (if not, then not)
If water can be squeezed out of bothboth are deemed filthy

Urdu mein: Gandagi tab lagti hai jab nishaan waqai muntaqil ho. Boo se nahin — nazar aane wali gandagi se. Sookha kapda jo na Tapke = saaf (Table 3).

Rule 5 · How to purify filth

First, is the filth visible or invisible?

FilthHow to purify it
Visible (has a body — e.g. blood)Remove the actual substance — even one wash is enough. If a colour/smell trace is hard to remove, no harm if it remains. (Soap or another liquid may be used.)
Invisible (no body — e.g. dried urine)Wash three times and wring after each wash (obligatory).

What purifies? Water — or any liquid that removes filth, such as vinegar or rose water.

Special surfaces — purified without ordinary washing:

SurfaceHow it is purified
Footgear (khuff)Rub on the ground/soil if the filth is solid (dried), even if moist (Abū Dāwūd 385 and 650)
Swords / metalWipe with soil or a cloth (filth can't penetrate)
The ground, trees, grassBy drying — then you may pray on it (but you may not make tayammum from it)
Anything transformed (istiḥāla)If its essence changes — wine → vinegar, a dead body → salt, → ash — it becomes pure
Sperm (manī)Dry → scratch it off · wet → wash it

Urdu mein: Nazar aane wali: asli cheez haTao (ek baar bhi). Na-nazar aane wali: 3 baar dho kar nichoRo. Paani ya koi bhi saaf maaya (sirka, gulaab-jal). Khuff ragaR, zameen sookh, sharab→sirka paak, mani sookha khurach / geela dho.

Rule 6 · The hide of a dead animal (tanning)

The meat of a dead animal is heavy filth and stays filth — but its hide can be reclaimed:

  • Tanning purifies the hide (including a dog's): true tanning (mimosa pods, salt, orange-peel) or legal tanning (cleansing with soil, or placing in the sun). ("Hide which has been tanned is pure" — At-Tirmidhī 1728.)
  • EXCEPT the hide of a pig (never pure) and the skin of a human (due to its dignity).
  • Slaughter in the Sharʿī way also purifies the skin — even of an animal whose meat is unlawful (the meat stays unlawful; the slaughter "compensates for the tanning").
  • Parts with no flowing blood are pure: hair, feathers, horns, hoofs, dry bones — but the nerves are impure.
  • Musk (and its gland) is pure and lawful to eat; civet is pure, and you may pray perfumed in it.

Urdu mein: Khaal dabaaghat (tanning) se paak; Sharʿī zibah bhi paak kar deta hai. Sirf suar aur insaan mustasnā. Baal/par/seeng/khur/sookhi haddi paak (pat-the na-paak). Kasturi o civet paak.
