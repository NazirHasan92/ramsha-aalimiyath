Chapter 4 — Student Handout

"For Muhammad" · "Whose?" · Iḍāfa (possession)

👋 Welcome — What you'll learn today

By the end of this lesson you will be able to:

  1. Use the preposition لِـ (for / belongs to) — attached to the next word.
  2. Apply the spelling rule: لِـ + الْ = لِلْـ (the alif of "al-" disappears).
  3. Recognise that names (Muhammad, Ali, etc.) are definite by themselves but still take tanween.
  4. Ask "Whose ___?" using لِمَنْ.
  5. Build a basic iḍāfa — two nouns expressing possession (كِتَابُ مُحَمَّدٍ = "Muhammad's book").
  6. Know 6 common Arabic names + 5 family terms.

Time needed: about one hour of class + 30 minutes of self-practice.

Rule 1 · لِـ means "for / belongs to"

Last week you learned 4 prepositions (فِي · عَلَى · إِلَى · مِنْ). This week we add a 5th: لِـ (li-).

لِمُحَمَّدٍ

li-Muḥammadin · "for Muhammad" / "Muhammad's" · Muhammad ke liye / Muhammad ka

💡 Two important things

  1. لِـ is a single letter — it's attached to the next word. NO space. (Different from فِي, عَلَى etc., which stand alone.)
  2. The noun after لِـ takes kasra — exactly like all the other prepositions from Ch 3. مُحَمَّدٌ → لِمُحَمَّدٍ.

With names — read aloud:

Citing formAfter لِـMeaning
مُحَمَّدٌلِمُحَمَّدٍli-Muḥammadin — for Muhammad
عَلِيٌّلِعَلِيٍّli-ʿAliyyin — for Ali
حَامِدٌلِحَامِدٍli-Ḥāmidin — for Hamid
عَبَّاسٌلِعَبَّاسٍli-ʿAbbāsin — for Abbas
يَاسِرٌلِيَاسِرٍli-Yāsirin — for Yasir
خَالِدٌلِخَالِدٍli-Khālidin — for Khalid

Rule 2 · The big spelling rule — لِـ + الْ = لِلْـ

This is the trickiest spelling rule of the chapter. When لِـ meets a word starting with الـ ("the"), the alif of الـ disappears in writing.

لِـ + الْوَلَدُ → لِلْوَلَدِ

li- + al-waladu → lil-waladi · "for the boy" · "ladke ke liye / ladke ka"

⚠️ The alif drops — but the meaning stays

The boy is still "the boy" (definite). Only the visible alif of الـ disappears in writing. The ل of الـ remains — silent, with sukoon. So you write two ل letters back-to-back: لِلْـ.

Roman: "`لِـ` + `الـ` = `لِلْـ` — alif gum, sirf do لَامs back-to-back. Yeh classical Arabic ka writing rule hai — saari kitabon mein aisa hi hai, Qur'an mein bhi (`الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ` mein bhi)."

With definite (الـ) nouns — read aloud:

Definite formAfter لِـ (alif dropped)Meaning
الْوَلَدُلِلْوَلَدِlil-waladi — for the boy
الْبَيْتُلِلْبَيْتِlil-bayti — for the house
الْكِتَابُلِلْكِتَابِlil-kitaabi — for the book
الْمَسْجِدُلِلْمَسْجِدِlil-masjidi — for the mosque
الْإِمَامُلِلْإِمَامِlil-imaami — for the imam

💡 Notice — names vs الـ-nouns sound different

  • With name: لِمُحَمَّدٍ — "li-Muḥammad-IN" (kasra-tanween, doubling stays).
  • With الـ-noun: لِلْوَلَدِ — "lil-walad-I" (single kasra, no doubling).

Rule 3 · Names take tanween (even though they're definite)

A surprise rule. In Ch 1 you learned: with الـ = definite, without الـ = indefinite (with tanween). But names break this pattern.

⚠️ The surprise — three routes to definiteness

A noun can be definite (specific) in three ways:

  1. With الـ — الْوَلَدُ (the boy) — Ch 1.
  2. Being a name — مُحَمَّدٌ (Muhammad) — Ch 4. Names are already definite — no الـ needed. But they still take tanween!
  3. Being in an iḍāfa — كِتَابُ مُحَمَّدٍ (Muhammad's book) — Ch 4 (Rule 5 below).

Roman: "Naam (`مُحَمَّد`, `عَلِيّ` etc.) khud-ba-khud khaas hote hain — `الـ` nahin chahiye. Par tanween phir bhi lagi hai (`مُحَمَّدٌ`). Yeh classical Arabic ka usool hai."

Important: never write الـ on a name. الْمُحَمَّدُ is wrong. مُحَمَّدٌ is correct.

After لِـ, names take kasra-tanween — same as any indefinite-shaped noun:

مُحَمَّدٌ → لِمُحَمَّدٍli-Muḥammadin — "for Muhammad"
عَلِيٌّ → لِعَلِيٍّli-ʿAliyyin — "for Ali" (note: shadda preserved)
حَامِدٌ → لِحَامِدٍli-Ḥāmidin — "for Hamid"

Rule 4 · لِمَنْ ؟ — "Whose? / For whom?"

To ask "Whose ___?", put لِمَنْ (li-man) at the start of a question. It's لِـ (Ch 4) glued to مَنْ (Ch 2's "who?").

لِمَنْ هَذَا الْكِتَابُ ؟

li-man haadhal-kitaabu? · "Whose book is this?" · Yeh kitaab kis ki hai?

The answer uses لِـ:

هَذَا الْكِتَابُ لِمُحَمَّدٍ

haadhal-kitaabu li-Muḥammadin
"This book is Muhammad's" · "Yeh kitaab Muhammad ki hai"

⚠️ Three-way contrast — be careful

Three little words that look similar. Don't mix them up:

مَنْman"who?"Ch 2 — asking about a person
مِنْmin"from"Ch 3 — a preposition
لِمَنْli-man"whose?"Ch 4 — about possession

First two have same letters (م + ن), differ in vowel. Third has لِـ in front — clearly different.

More examples:

لِمَنْ هَذَا الْقَلَمُ ؟هَذَا الْقَلَمُ لِلْوَلَدِWhose pen is this? — This pen is the boy's.
لِمَنْ هَذَا الْبَيْتُ ؟هَذَا الْبَيْتُ لِحَامِدٍWhose house is this? — This house is Hamid's.
لِمَنْ هَذِهِ السَّيَّارَةُ ؟هَذِهِ السَّيَّارَةُ لِلْإِمَامِWhose car is this? — This car is the imam's.

Rule 5 · Iḍāfa — possessing without a preposition

The biggest idea in the chapter. Iḍāfa (إِضَافَة) is a way to express possession by gluing two nouns together — without any preposition.

كِتَابُ مُحَمَّدٍ

kitaabu Muḥammadin · "Muhammad's book" / "the book of Muhammad" · Muhammad ki kitaab

💡 Word order — flipped from English/Urdu

Arabic puts the owned thing first, then the owner. Literally: "book + Muhammad".

  • English: "Muhammad's book" (owner first)
  • Urdu: "Muhammad ki kitaab" (owner first)
  • Arabic: كِتَابُ مُحَمَّدٍ (owned first)

Urdu's Arabic-borrowed phrase "kitaab-e-Muhammad" uses the Arabic order — and the "-e-" is literally the kasra you'll see on the second noun in Arabic!

⚠️ The 3 pakka rules of iḍāfa

  1. First noun: NO الـ. Ever. Even though the meaning is "the book of Muhammad" (definite), don't write الْكِتَابُ. Just كِتَابُ.
  2. First noun: NO tanween. Just a single ending vowel (single ḍamma in most Ch 4 examples).
  3. Second noun: in kasra. Yes — kasra without a preposition! This is new. The second noun is always in jarr in an iḍāfa.

Roman: "Teen rules: (1) pehle lafz par `الـ` nahin, (2) pehle lafz par tanween nahin, (3) doosra lafz hamesha zer mein."

💡 Two ways to say "X's Y" in Chapter 4

Iḍāfa and لِـ can both express possession. Sometimes they overlap:

كِتَابُ مُحَمَّدٍMuhammad's book (iḍāfa — direct possession)
هَذَا الْكِتَابُ لِمُحَمَّدٍThis book is Muhammad's (لِـ — assigning possession)

Both correct. Iḍāfa joins them tightly into one phrase; لِـ makes a full sentence.

✏️ Exercises

Exercise 1 · Add لِـ to each noun, applying the rules

For names: kasra-tanween. For الـ-nouns: drop the alif, single kasra. Write the full form.

  1. مُحَمَّدٌ → (e.g. li-Muḥammadin)
  2. حَامِدٌ →
  3. عَلِيٌّ →
  4. الْوَلَدُ → (alif drops!)
  5. الْبَيْتُ →
  6. الْكِتَابُ →
  7. يَاسِرٌ →
  8. الْإِمَامُ →

Exercise 2 · Build the iḍāfa

Combine the two nouns into an iḍāfa. Drop الـ and tanween from the first noun (single ḍamma at end). Put the second noun in kasra.

  1. book + Muhammad → كِتَابٌ + مُحَمَّدٌ → (e.g. kitaabu Muḥammadin)
  2. house + Ali → بَيْتٌ + عَلِيٌّ →
  3. pen + Hamid → قَلَمٌ + حَامِدٌ →
  4. son + Yasir → ابْنٌ + يَاسِرٌ →
  5. paternal uncle + Khalid → عَمٌّ + خَالِدٌ →
  6. book + the boy → كِتَابٌ + الْوَلَدُ →
  7. car + the imam → سَيَّارَةٌ + الْإِمَامُ →
  8. pen + the teacher → قَلَمٌ + الْمُدَرِّسُ →

Exercise 3 · مَنْ / مِنْ / لِمَنْ — which fits?

For each blank, choose the right word.

  1. "Who is this?" — هَذَا ؟
  2. "Whose pen is this?" — هَذَا الْقَلَمُ ؟
  3. "He is from the market." — هُوَ السُّوقِ
  4. "Whose house is this?" — هَذَا الْبَيْتُ ؟
  5. "Who is the imam?" — الْإِمَامُ ؟
  6. "This is from the mosque." — هَذَا الْمَسْجِدِ

Exercise 4 · Find the mistake — circle it and write the correction
