🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Use the four prepositions فِي · عَلَى · مِنْ · إِلَى (in, on, from, to) correctly.
  2. Apply the kasra rule: the noun after a preposition ends in ـِ (al-baytu → fī l-bayti).
  3. Say the مِنَ الْـ smoothing — mina l-hindi, not min al-hindi.
  4. Ask and answer أَيْنَ ؟ (where?) and مِنْ أَيْنَ ؟ (where from?).
  5. Use ذَهَبَ (went) with إِلَى and خَرَجَ (went out) with مِنْ.
  6. Recognise and use the 15 new words — places, countries, and the two verbs — and read the lesson's names with correct endings.

1 · The Four Prepositions — حُرُوف الجَرّ

The small words that locate a noun: in, on, from, to. The lesson's own box (p.26) names them as حُرُوف الجَرّ — "the pulling words".

Where this fits: Lesson 3 made nouns definite (al-baytu). Lesson 4 puts a small word in front of them to say where — and that word changes the ending (Concept 2).

The four prepositions of place

Meaning
فِي (in), عَلَىٰ (on), مِنْ (from), إِلَىٰ (to). Each sits before a noun to locate it.
Roman Urdu
في = mein, على = par, من = se, إلى = tak / ki taraf. Chaar chhote lafz jo ism se pehle aate hain.
Important / Rule
These are حُرُوف الجَرّ — literally "pulling words". They pull the next noun into a kasra ending (Concept 2). عَلَىٰ and إِلَىٰ end in alif maqṣūrah (ـى) — pronounced as a long "aa": ʿalā, ilā (not "-ay").
Example
فِي الْبَيْتِ · عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ · مِنَ الْهِنْدِ · إِلَى الْمَسْجِدِ fī l-bayti (in the house) · ʿalā l-maktabi (on the desk) · mina l-hindi (from India) · ilā l-masjidi (to the mosque)

💡 Teaching tip

Anchor each preposition to a physical gesture: فِي — point inside a box; عَلَىٰ — rest a hand on top; مِنْ — pull away from; إِلَىٰ — point toward. Gestures fix the four meanings before any ending-talk.

2 · The Kasra Rule — الجَرّ (the chapter's hammer)

The single most important rule of Lesson 4: the noun after a preposition changes its ending to kasra (zer). Repeat it in every drill.

Where this fits: Lesson 3 was all ḍamma (al-baytu). Lesson 4 introduces the student's second ending — the kasra — and the preposition is what triggers it.

al-baytu · ـُ (ḍamma)"]:::main P["+ فِي

fī l-bayti · ـِ (kasra)"]:::ex N --> P --> R classDef main fill:#cffafe,stroke:#0e7490,color:#0c4a6e,stroke-width:2px,font-weight:bold; classDef sub fill:#fef3c7,stroke:#b45309,color:#7c2d12; classDef ex fill:#ffe4e6,stroke:#be123c,color:#881337,stroke-width:2px,font-weight:bold;

Preposition → kasra on the next noun

Meaning
After any of the four prepositions, the noun's last vowel becomes a kasra (ـِ). On a definite noun: ـُ → ـِ.
Roman Urdu
Preposition ke baad ism ka aakhri harkat zer (kasra) ban jaata hai. Pesh (ـُ) se zer (ـِ). al-baytu → fī l-baytI.
Important / Rule
Teach the effect, not a case-name. (The formal term for this state arrives much later — don't name it.) When مِنْ meets الـ, its ن takes a fatḥa: said "mina-l" — مِنَ الْبَيْتِ.
Example
الْمَكْتَبُ → عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ al-maktabu → ʿalā l-maktabi · "the desk → on the desk" · pesh se zer

The مِنَ الْـ smoothing

Meaning
مِنْ + الـ is pronounced "mina-l" — the ن borrows a fatḥa to glide into the الـ.
Roman Urdu
مِنْ ke baad الـ aaye to "min-al" nahin — "mina-l". ن par halki zabar.
Important / Rule
Teach as a memorised sound, not a rule. (Same phonology the book names later — don't name it now.)
Example
مِنَ الْيَابَانِ · مِنَ الصِّينِ · مِنَ الْهِنْدِ mina l-yābāni · mina ṣ-ṣīni · mina l-hindi — "from Japan / China / India"

⚠️ This is THE rule of the chapter — drill it relentlessly

Out of Lesson-3 habit, students keep the ḍamma: fī l-baytu. Catch it every time. Write the noun in ḍamma, add the preposition, and recolour the ending to a kasra as you say it. Keep a wall-rule: "preposition aaya → ending par zer."

3 · أَيْنَ ؟ & مِنْ أَيْنَ ؟ — Asking About Place

Two question words — "where?" and "where from?" — both answered with a preposition phrase. Every question re-drills the kasra.

Where this fits: the prepositions and the kasra need a reason to appear in conversation. أَيْنَ supplies it — the answer is always a preposition phrase.

where from? (origin)"]:::main W --> WE["هُوَ فِي الْغُرْفَةِ
'he is in the room'"]:::ex WF --> WFE["أَنَا مِنَ الْيَابَانِ
'I am from Japan'"]:::ex classDef root fill:#064e3b,stroke:#064e3b,color:#fff,stroke-width:2px,font-weight:bold; classDef main fill:#cffafe,stroke:#0e7490,color:#0c4a6e,stroke-width:2px,font-weight:bold; classDef ex fill:#ffe4e6,stroke:#be123c,color:#881337;

أَيْنَ ؟ — "where (is)?"

Meaning
Asks for a location. Answered with فِي / عَلَىٰ + a noun in kasra.
Roman Urdu
أَيْنَ ؟ = "kahaan (hai)?" Jawab في/على + zer wale ism se.
Important / Rule
The answer reuses هُوَ/هِيَ as ready-made "he/it / she/it" words (they appear on the page). Don't build a pronoun table — that's a later lesson.
Example
أَيْنَ الْكِتَابُ ؟ — هُوَ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ. ayna l-kitābu? — huwa ʿalā l-maktabi · "Where is the book? — It is on the desk." · "Kitaab kahaan hai? — Mez par."

مِنْ أَيْنَ ؟ — "from where?"

Meaning
Asks for origin — where someone is from. Answered with مِنْ + a country/place in kasra.
Roman Urdu
مِنْ أَيْنَ ؟ = "kahaan se?" Jawab مِنْ + mulk/jagah (zer ke saath).
Important / Rule
Watch مَنْ (who) vs مِنْ (from) — same letters, different vowel. See Common Mistakes.
Example
مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتَ ؟ — أَنَا مِنَ الْيَابَانِ. min ayna anta? — anā mina l-yābāni · "Where are you from? — I am from Japan." · "Tum kahaan se ho? — Main Japan se."

💡 Map round

Bring a simple world map. Point to a country, ask "min ayna … ?", and have a student answer "huwa mina ṣ-ṣīni / mina l-hindi …". The lesson itself uses Japan, China, India, and the Philippines — start there.

4 · The Verbs ذَهَبَ / خَرَجَ — and Countries

The student's first two verbs — taught as fixed words, not conjugated. Each partners a preposition: ذَهَبَ … إِلَى / خَرَجَ … مِنْ.

Where this fits: the verbs give the kasra a second home (motion to / from a place), and they introduce the country vocabulary used with مِنْ / إِلَى.

The two verbs and their prepositions

Meaning
ذَهَبَ = "he went" (pairs with إِلَى — went to). خَرَجَ = "he went out / left" (pairs with مِنْ — out from).
Roman Urdu
ذَهَبَ = "(woh) gaya" → إِلَى ke saath. خَرَجَ = "(woh) bāhar niklā" → مِنْ ke saath.
Important / Rule
Fixed words — do NOT conjugate. Only "he went / he went out" for now; no "she/I/you" forms. The noun after the preposition still takes kasra.
Example
خَرَجَ الْمُدَرِّسُ مِنَ الْفَصْلِ وَذَهَبَ إِلَى الْمُدِيرِ. kharaja l-mudarrisu mina l-faṣli wa-dhahaba ilā l-mudīri · "The teacher left the classroom and went to the director." · "Ustaad kamre se nikla aur director ke paas gaya."

⚠️ Don't conjugate the verbs

These are the first verbs students meet, and the temptation is to ask "how do I say she went / I went?" Defer it: "abhi sirf 'woh gaya / woh nikla' — baaki aage." Verb conjugation is far ahead in the book.

💡 The name-reading drill (p.23)

The lesson sets two columns of names to read aloud "with correct endings": boys' names take tanween (-un) — مُحَمَّدٌ، خَالِدٌ، حَامِدٌ، يَاسِرٌ، عَمَّارٌ، سَعِيدٌ، عَلِيٌّ، عَبَّاسٌ; these girls' names take a single ḍamma (-u, no tanween) — آمِنَةُ، زَيْنَبُ، فَاطِمَةُ، مَرْيَمُ، عَائِشَةُ، خَدِيجَةُ، صَفِيَّةُ، رُقَيَّةُ. Keep it observational — just read the endings as printed; don't open the underlying rule (that's a much later lesson).

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Forgetting the kasra after a preposition ("fī l-baytu")

The chapter's #1 slip. Out of Lesson-3 habit, students keep the ḍamma. Correct: fī l-baytI, not fī l-baytU. Recolour the ending to a kasra every time. "Preposition aaya → ending par zer."

2. Mixing up مَنْ (who) and مِنْ (from)

Same letters, different vowel: مَنْ (man, zabar) = "who"; مِنْ (min, zer) = "from". The lesson even writes مَنْ مِنَ الصِّينِ ؟ ("who is from China?") with both. Drill the pair with vowels in red.

3. Reading عَلَىٰ / إِلَىٰ as "-ay"

The final ـى (alif maqṣūrah) looks like a "ya" but sounds like a long "aa": ʿalā, ilā — not "ʿalay / ilay". Ear-drill before showing the spelling.

4. Dropping the مِنَ الْـ fatḥa

Students say a flat min al-bayti. It's mina l-bayti — the ن takes a little fatḥa before الـ. Drill mina l-hindi, mina ṣ-ṣīni as one fused sound.

5. Wrong verb-preposition pairing (and conjugating)

خَرَجَ goes with مِنْ (out from); ذَهَبَ goes with إِلَى (went to). Don't let students conjugate them — only "he went / he went out" for now.

📿 Connect to the Qur'an — the four prepositions you just learned

Lesson 4 teaches no Qur'anic vocabulary directly — but its four little prepositions are everywhere in the Book. Show the class that each one, followed by a noun in kasra, is exactly today's rule. This is the pay-off of the lesson.

فِي — "in" · Surah at-Tīn 95:4

لَقَدْ خَلَقْنَا الْإِنْسَانَ فِي أَحْسَنِ تَقْوِيمٍ

… fī aḥsani taqwīm — "… in the best of forms."

See it: فِي + أَحْسَنِ — the noun after فِي takes a kasra. Exactly today's rule.

عَلَىٰ — "on / over" · a recurring phrase (e.g. al-Baqarah 2:20)

إِنَّ اللَّهَ عَلَىٰ كُلِّ شَيْءٍ قَدِيرٌ

inna-llāha ʿalā kulli shayʾin qadīr — "Indeed Allah is powerful over all things."

See it: عَلَىٰ + كُلِّ — kasra on the noun after عَلَىٰ. (This exact phrase closes many āyāt.)

مِنْ — "from" · Surah an-Nās 114:6

مِنَ الْجِنَّةِ وَالنَّاسِ

mina l-jinnati wa-n-nās — "… of jinn and mankind."

See it: the مِنَ الْـ smoothing live — مِنْ takes a fatḥa (mina), and الْجِنَّةِ takes a kasra. The last āyah of the Qur'an.

إِلَىٰ — "to" · a recurring phrase (e.g. Āl ʿImrān 3:109)

وَإِلَى اللَّهِ تُرْجَعُ الْأُمُورُ

wa-ilā-llāhi turjaʿu l-umūr — "… and to Allah all matters are returned."

See it: إِلَىٰ + اللَّهِ — kasra on the noun after إِلَىٰ. (Recurs across the Qur'an.)
