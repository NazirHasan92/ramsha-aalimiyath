# Part A · Understanding the chapter

Lesson 3 gave the learner the definite article `الـ` and the describing sentence — *"the door is open"*. Everything stayed in one ending: the ḍamma (pesh). Lesson 4 cracks open a **second ending — the kasra (zer)** — and gives the learner the tool that produces it: the **preposition**.

The lesson hands over four small words and one big consequence:

1. **The four prepositions of place** — `فِي` (in), `عَلَى` (on), `مِنْ` (from), `إِلَى` (to). The lesson box on p.26 names them outright: *«فِيْ · عَلَىٰ · مِنْ · إِلَىٰ» مِنْ حُرُوفِ الجَرِّ* — "these are among the *ḥurūf al-jarr* (prepositions)".
2. **The kasra effect** — the noun *after* a preposition changes its ending to **kasra (zer)**: `الْبَيْتُ → فِي الْبَيْتِ`. This is the engine of the whole chapter.
3. **Two question words** — `أَيْنَ ؟` (where?) and `مِنْ أَيْنَ ؟` (where from?) — both answered with a preposition phrase, so every question silently re-drills the kasra.
4. **Two verbs as fixed forms** — `ذَهَبَ` (he went) and `خَرَجَ` (he went out) — your learner's first taste of a verb. They are *not* conjugated here; they appear as set words that pair naturally with `إِلَى` (went *to*) and `مِنْ` (went out *from*).
5. **Place & country vocabulary** + a **name-reading drill** — the words to talk about *where* things and people are, and *where* people are from.

Notice the architecture: the preposition is introduced first, the kasra it triggers second, and then everything else (questions, verbs, vocab) exists to *exercise* that one ending change. The kasra is the hammer of this chapter.

**Yaad rakho:** Lesson 3 ka ending sirf pesh (ḍamma) tha. Lesson 4 ek **doosra ending — zer (kasra)** laata hai, aur us ko banane wala auzaar: **preposition** (`في، على، من، إلى`). Poora sabaq isi ek tabdeeli ke ird-gird ghoomta hai: *preposition ke baad ism par zer.*

> **A note on this edition.** Some other printings of Madinah Book 1 introduce `لِـ` ("for"), `لِمَنْ` ("whose"), and the *iḍāfa* (possessive) construction around this point. **This edition's Lesson 4 does none of that.** Its four prepositions are exactly `فِي · عَلَى · مِنْ · إِلَى` — no `لِـ`, no iḍāfa. Stay with what *this* book teaches. (If you ever saw an older version of these very notes that taught `لِـ`/iḍāfa for Chapter 4 — that was the wrong lesson; this rebuild fixes it.)

## 1 · The preposition — `حَرْف الجَرّ`

A preposition is a small word that sits *before* a noun and locates it — *in* the house, *on* the desk, *from* India, *to* the mosque. Arabic groups them under one name: **حُرُوف الجَرّ** (*ḥurūf al-jarr*) — literally "the letters/words of *pulling*".

The four this lesson teaches:

| Arabic | Read it | English | Roman Urdu |
|---|---|---|---|
| `فِي` | *fī* | in | mein |
| `عَلَىٰ` | *ʿalā* | on | par |
| `مِنْ` | *min* | from | se |
| `إِلَىٰ` | *ilā* | to / towards | tak / ki taraf |

That word "pulling" is the key image. The preposition **pulls** the noun after it into a new ending — the kasra. Hold that picture; it explains Concept 2.

**Yaad rakho:** `حرف الجر` ka matlab hi "kheenchne wala lafz" hai. Yeh agle ism ko **zer (kasra)** ki taraf *kheench* leta hai. Chaar lafz: `في` (mein), `على` (par), `من` (se), `إلى` (tak). Bas yeh chaar — is sabaq mein aur koi preposition nahin.

> **Two of them end in a special alif.** `عَلَىٰ` and `إِلَىٰ` are written with **alif maqṣūrah** (ـى) — a "ya"-shaped alif that is *pronounced as a long "aa"*: *ʿalā*, *ilā* (NOT "ʿalay" / "ilay"). You don't need to name *alif maqṣūrah* for the class — just model the sound. This is a pronunciation trap worth catching early (Part B, Landmine 3).

## 2 · The kasra effect — the chapter's hammer

In Lesson 3 every noun sat in the ḍamma (pesh): `الْبَيْتُ`, `الْقَلَمُ`. The moment a preposition comes in front, the ending flips to **kasra (zer)**:

> `الْبَيْتُ` → `فِي الْبَيْتِ` · *al-baytu → fī l-bayti* · "the house → in the house"
> `الْمَكْتَبُ` → `عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ` · *al-maktabu → ʿalā l-maktabi* · "the desk → on the desk"
> `الْمَسْجِدُ` → `إِلَى الْمَسْجِدِ` · *al-masjidu → ilā l-masjidi* · "the mosque → to the mosque"
> `الْبَيْتُ` → `مِنَ الْبَيْتِ` · *al-baytu → mina l-bayti* · "the house → from the house"

The pattern is mechanical: **preposition + noun → the noun's last vowel becomes kasra.** On a definite noun (`الـ`) that means `ـُ → ـِ`. The lesson keeps everything definite, so you only ever deal with the single-kasra ending `ـِ`.

### Don't over-name it

The book calls the *words* `حُرُوف الجَرّ` (it's printed on the page, so you may use that name). But it does **not** give the noun's new state a formal case-name yet (the term *majrūr* / "the jarr case" arrives much later). So teach the *effect*, not the iʿrāb label: *"after one of these four words, the noun's ending becomes zer (kasra)."* That's all a beginner needs.

**Yaad rakho:** Lesson ka sab se bada qaida: **preposition ke baad ism ka aakhri harkat zer (kasra) ban jaata hai.** `الْبَيْتُ` → `فِي الْبَيْتِ`. Pesh se zer. Iska rasmi naam (majrūr) abhi nahin — sirf asar sikhaao: "in chaar lafz ke baad, ism par zer."

### The `مِنَ الْـ` smoothing

Look again at `مِنَ الْبَيْتِ`. The preposition is `مِنْ` (with a sukoon on the ن) — but here the ن carries a **fatḥa**: *mina*, not *min*. Why? Because `مِنْ` ends in a sukoon and `الـ` begins with a (silent) sukoon-bearing alif; Arabic dislikes two sukoons colliding, so the ن borrows a little fatḥa to glide across. You hear it constantly in the lesson:

> `مِنَ الْبَيْتِ` (*mina l-bayti*) · `مِنَ الْيَابَانِ` (*mina l-yābāni*) · `مِنَ الصِّينِ` (*mina ṣ-ṣīni*) · `مِنَ الْهِنْدِ` (*mina l-hindi*)

For class, teach it as a **memorised pair**, not a rule: *"`مِنْ` + `الـ` is said **mina-l**."* (This is the same phonological smoothing behind Lesson 3's sun-letter merge and `لِلْـ`-type elisions; the book *names* this family later as *hamzat al-waṣl* in Lesson 5 — so don't name it now, just drill the sound.)

**Yaad rakho:** `مِنْ` + `الـ` ko jodte waqt `مِنْ` ki ن par **zabar (fatḥa)** aa jaati hai: *mina-l*. Naam mat do — bas yaad karwaa do: "min-al → **mina-l**." (`مِنَ البَيْتِ`، `مِنَ الهِنْدِ`.)

## 3 · `أَيْنَ ؟` and `مِنْ أَيْنَ ؟` — asking about place

The chapter gives two question words, and both are answered with a preposition phrase — so the question *is* the kasra drill.

- **`أَيْنَ ؟`** — "where (is)?" — asks for a *location*. The answer uses `فِي` / `عَلَى`:
  > `أَيْنَ مُحَمَّدٌ ؟ — هُوَ فِي الْغُرْفَةِ.` · *ayna Muḥammadun? — huwa fī l-ghurfati.* · "Where is Muhammad? — He is in the room."
  > `أَيْنَ الْكِتَابُ ؟ — هُوَ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ.` · *ayna l-kitābu? — huwa ʿalā l-maktabi.* · "Where is the book? — It is on the desk."

- **`مِنْ أَيْنَ ؟`** — "from where? / where … from?" — asks for *origin*. The answer uses `مِنْ`:
  > `مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتَ ؟ — أَنَا مِنَ الْيَابَانِ.` · *min ayna anta? — anā mina l-yābāni.* · "Where are you from? — I am from Japan."
  > `وَمِنْ أَيْنَ حَامِدٌ ؟ — هُوَ مِنَ الْهِنْدِ.` · *wa-min ayna Ḥāmidun? — huwa mina l-hindi.* · "And where is Hamid from? — He is from India."

### A heads-up on the pronouns in these answers

The answers use `هُوَ` (he/it), `هِيَ` (she/it), `أَنَا` (I), `أَنْتَ` (you). **These pronouns appear in this lesson's dialogue, so you will use them** — but the book does *not* formally teach the pronoun system here; that is **Lesson 5's** job (`هُوَ`/`هِيَ`) and later (`أَنَا`/`أَنْتَ`). So use them as ready-made answer-words exactly as the page does — *"the answer starts with 'huwa…' / 'anā…'"* — but **don't drill the full pronoun set or build a pronoun table in this chapter.** One step at a time. (This note is for *you*; classmates just copy the answer pattern.)

**Yaad rakho:** `أَيْنَ ؟` = "kahaan?" (jagah poochho) → jawab `في`/`على` se. `مِنْ أَيْنَ ؟` = "kahaan se?" (asal/origin poochho) → jawab `مِنْ` se. Jawab mein `هو/هي/أنا/أنت` aate hain — inko ready-made lafz ki tarah istemaal karo; inka poora system Lesson 5+ mein aayega, abhi table mat banao.

## 4 · `ذَهَبَ` and `خَرَجَ` — the first verbs (as fixed words)

This is the learner's first encounter with an Arabic **verb**. Two of them:

- **`ذَهَبَ`** (*dhahaba*) — "he went" — *gaya*. Pairs with `إِلَى`: *went **to**.*
  > `ذَهَبَ إِلَى الْمُدِيرِ.` · *dhahaba ilā l-mudīri.* · "He went to the director."
- **`خَرَجَ`** (*kharaja*) — "he went out / left" — *bāhar niklā*. Pairs with `مِنْ`: *went out **from**.*
  > `خَرَجَ الْمُدَرِّسُ مِنَ الْفَصْلِ.` · *kharaja l-mudarrisu mina l-faṣli.* · "The teacher left the classroom."

Notice how cleanly they reinforce the prepositions: **`خَرَجَ` … `مِنْ`** (out *from*) and **`ذَهَبَ` … `إِلَى`** (went *to*). The lesson often chains them: *"`خَرَجَ مِنَ الْفَصْلِ وَذَهَبَ إِلَى الْمُدِيرِ`"* — "he left the classroom and went to the director."

### Treat them as vocabulary, not grammar

`ذَهَبَ`/`خَرَجَ` are past-tense, 3rd-person-masculine-singular forms. **Conjugation — changing the verb for "she/I/you/they" — is far off in the book.** Do not open it. Teach these two as fixed words that mean "he went" / "he went out", and let the learner notice the preposition partnership. If a sharp classmate asks "how do I say *she* went?", say *"yeh hum aage seekhenge"* and move on.

**Yaad rakho:** `ذَهَبَ` = "(woh) gaya" → `إِلَى` ke saath (kahin **ko/taraf**). `خَرَجَ` = "(woh) bāhar niklā" → `مِنْ` ke saath (kahin **se**). Inko sirf do lafz ki tarah sikhaao — verb ki gardaan (conjugation) abhi bilkul nahin. `خرج … من`, `ذهب … إلى` — yeh jodi yaad karwaao.

## 5 · The vocabulary — places, countries, and a name drill

### The new-words box (p.26) — 13 nouns + 2 verbs

Places: `الْمَدْرَسَةُ` (school), `الْفَصْلُ` (classroom), `الْحَمَّامُ` (bathroom), `الْمِرْحَاضُ` (toilet), `الْمَطْبَخُ` (kitchen), `الْغُرْفَةُ` (room), `الْجَامِعَةُ` (university), `السُّوقُ` (market), `الْمُدِيرُ` (the director/principal).
Countries: `الْيَابَانُ` (Japan), `الصِّينُ` (China), `الْهِنْدُ` (India), `الْفِلِبِّيْنُ` (the Philippines).
Verbs: `ذَهَبَ` (went), `خَرَجَ` (went out).

These are the words the lesson actually *teaches*. The describing sentences also reuse known nouns from earlier lessons (`الْبَيْت`, `الْكِتَاب`, `الْمَكْتَب`, `السَّرِير`, `السَّاعَة`, `الْمَسْجِد`, `الطَّالِب`, `الرَّجُل`, `التَّاجِر`, `الدُّكَّان`…). Spend new-word drill time on the box above.

> **One spelling note for you:** countries take `الـ` in the box (`الْيَابَانُ`, `الصِّينُ`, `الْهِنْدُ`) — Arabic treats most country names as inherently definite and writes them with `الـ`. Don't theorize this for class; just teach each country as one word with its `الـ`. `الْهِنْد` is a *moon-letter* `الـ` (so *al-hind*, L heard); `الصِّين` is a *sun-letter* `الـ` (so *aṣ-ṣīn*, L swallowed) — a nice live review of Lesson 3.

### The name-reading drill (p.23)

The lesson sets two columns of names to read aloud "with correct endings" (*ضبط أواخر الكلمات*):

- **Boys' names — take tanween `ـٌ`:** `مُحَمَّدٌ`، `خَالِدٌ`، `حَامِدٌ`، `يَاسِرٌ`، `عَمَّارٌ`، `سَعِيدٌ`، `عَلِيٌّ`، `عَبَّاسٌ`.
- **Girls' names — take a single ḍamma `ـُ` (no tanween):** `آمِنَةُ`، `زَيْنَبُ`، `فَاطِمَةُ`، `مَرْيَمُ`، `عَائِشَةُ`، `خَدِيجَةُ`، `صَفِيَّةُ`، `رُقَيَّةُ`.

For class, keep this purely observational: *"read each name with the ending shown — boys' names usually end in -un (tanween); these girls' names end in a single -u."* **Do not open the underlying rule.** (For *your* understanding: these girls' names are *ghayr munṣarif* — "diptote" — a category that refuses tanween. That named system is a much later lesson. Don't teach it, don't name it; just have them read the endings as printed.)

**Yaad rakho:** Naam padhne ki mashq: ladkon ke naam par aksar tanween (`-un`), in ladkiyon ke naam par sirf ek pesh (`-u`, bina tanween). Class ko bas itna: "jaisa likha hai waisa ending bolo." Andar ka qaida (*ghayr munṣarif*) abhi mat kholo — woh bahut aage hai.

## The big picture — what this chapter is silently building

Step back. Four small words, one ending, and the language suddenly has *space* in it:

1. **A second ending is now live.** Lesson 3 was all ḍamma; Lesson 4 introduces the **kasra** through prepositions. The learner's mental "case system" now has two visible slots.
2. **Sentences gain a place and a direction.** *"The book is on the desk." "He went from the classroom to the director."* The learner can now situate things and describe simple movement.
3. **Questions become a drill engine.** `أَيْنَ` and `مِنْ أَيْنَ` force a preposition phrase every time — so the kasra gets practised in every exchange.

After this lesson your classmates can answer "where is it?" and "where are you from?" — the backbone of every real beginner conversation. The Qur'anic payoff (Part B) is that the four little prepositions they just met are everywhere in the Book they recite.

**Yaad rakho:** Lesson 4 ke baad classmates "kahaan hai?" aur "kahaan se ho?" ka jawab de sakte hain — asal baat-cheet ki buniyaad. Aur jo chaar chhote lafz aaj seekhe, woh Qur'an mein har jagah hain — yahi is sabaq ka inaam (Part B).

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

Now you understand what the chapter is doing. This section is how to *deliver* it.

---

## The five landmines for Urdu-speaking adult learners

### Landmine 1 · Forgetting the kasra after a preposition ("fī l-baytu")

This is **the** mistake of the chapter. Out of Lesson-3 habit, classmates keep the ḍamma: they say *fī l-baytu* instead of *fī l-bayti*. The kasra is the whole point of the lesson, so it needs catching every single time.

**Counter-strategy:** On the board, write the bare noun in ḍamma, then add the preposition and *change the ending colour* to red as you say it: `الْبَيْتُ` → `فِي الْبَيْتِ`. Make the class say both, hearing "-u" become "-i". Keep a running rule on the wall: **"preposition aaya → ending par zer."**

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Dekho — akela `al-baytu` (pesh). Jaise hi aage `في` lagao, ending pesh se **zer** ban jaati hai: `fī l-baytI`. 'fī l-baytU' bolna ghalat hai. Yeh chaar lafz (`في، على، من، إلى`) ke baad hamesha zer."*

### Landmine 2 · `مَنْ` (who) vs `مِنْ` (from) — the twin words

Same three letters (م ن), only the vowel differs: **`مَنْ`** = *man* = "who"; **`مِنْ`** = *min* = "from". The lesson actually puts them side by side — *`مَنْ مِنَ الصِّينِ ؟`* ("who is from China?") has *both*. Classmates will read one for the other.

**Counter-strategy:** Write the pair big, vowel marks in red: `مَنْ` (zabar → "who") vs `مِنْ` (zer → "from"). Drill: "man = kaun (سوال log ke liye), min = se (jagah/asal)." Then read `مَنْ مِنَ الصِّينِ؟` together slowly.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`مَنْ` (zabar) = 'kaun' — logon ke liye sawaal. `مِنْ` (zer) = 'se' — kahin se aana. Letters wahi, sirf harkat ka farq: `man` upar (zabar), `min` neeche (zer). `مَنْ مِنَ الصِّينِ؟` = 'kaun China se hai?' — dono lafz ek hi line mein!"*

### Landmine 3 · Reading `عَلَىٰ` / `إِلَىٰ` as "-ay" instead of "-aa"

The final `ـى` (alif maqṣūrah) looks like a "ya", so classmates say *ʿalay* / *ilay*. It is pronounced as a long **"aa"**: *ʿalā*, *ilā*.

**Counter-strategy:** Ear-drill before eye. Say *ʿalā… ilā…* with a long open "aa" and have them echo, *before* they see the spelling. Then show the word and tell them: "this tail is a hidden alif — it sounds 'aa', not 'ay'."

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`عَلَىٰ` ko 'ʿalā' bolo — aakhir mein lambi 'aa', 'ʿalay' nahin. Waise hi `إِلَىٰ` = 'ilā'. Yeh `ى` chhupa hua alif hai — 'aa' ki awaaz deta hai. Pehle kaan se sun lo, phir spelling dekho."*

### Landmine 4 · The `مِنَ الْـ` fatḥa goes missing

Classmates will say a flat *min al-bayti* instead of the smooth *mina l-bayti*, dropping the little fatḥa on the ن.

**Counter-strategy:** Drill `مِنْ` + `الـ` as one fused sound — *"mina-l"* — across several countries: *mina l-yābāni, mina ṣ-ṣīni, mina l-hindi*. Don't explain the phonology; make it a memorised chant.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`مِنْ` ke baad jab `الـ` aaye, to `مِنْ` ki 'n' par halki zabar lag jaati hai: 'min-al' nahin — '**mina-l**'. `mina l-hindi`, `mina ṣ-ṣīni`. Ek hi saans mein jodo."*

### Landmine 5 · Treating `ذَهَبَ` / `خَرَجَ` like English "go" (and trying to conjugate)

Two traps: (a) pairing the wrong preposition — *"kharaja ilā"* — and (b) a curious classmate trying to make "she went / I went".

**Counter-strategy:** Glue the partnerships from minute one: **`خَرَجَ` + `مِنْ`** (out *from*), **`ذَهَبَ` + `إِلَى`** (went *to*). Draw a little doorway: come *out of* (`مِنْ`) one room, go *to* (`إِلَى`) another. For conjugation questions: defer warmly — *"abhi sirf 'woh gaya' — baaki aage."*

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`خَرَجَ` ke saath hamesha `مِنْ` (kisi jagah **se** bāhar). `ذَهَبَ` ke saath hamesha `إِلَى` (kisi jagah **ki taraf**). 'Woh kamre se nikla aur director ke paas gaya' = `خَرَجَ مِنَ الْغُرْفَةِ وَذَهَبَ إِلَى الْمُدِيرِ`. Verb ko abhi badalna nahin — sirf 'woh gaya / woh nikla'."*

## Suggested 60-minute class plan

*Class shuru karne se pehle:* board par likho — chapter ka Arabic title (`الدَّرْسُ الرَّابِعُ`), aaj ke chaar prepositions (`في · على · من · إلى`) un ke Urdu matlab ke saath, aur ek bada rule: **"preposition ke baad ism par zer."**

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–5 min | Greetings · today's four words `في·على·من·إلى` and the one big rule (kasra) |
| 5–18 min | The kasra drill: take known `الـ`-nouns, put a preposition in front, hear `ـُ → ـِ` (*al-baytu → fī l-bayti*) — whole class, then individuals |
| 18–28 min | `أَيْنَ ؟` round: point/ask "where is …?", answer with `فِي`/`عَلَى` + kasra. Drill `مِنَ الْـ` as "mina-l". |
| 28–40 min | `مِنْ أَيْنَ ؟` + countries: "where are you from?" map round — *anā mina l-yābāni / huwa mina ṣ-ṣīni*. Catch `مَنْ` vs `مِنْ`. |
| 40–50 min | The two verbs: `خَرَجَ … مِنْ` / `ذَهَبَ … إِلَى` — read the lesson's chained sentences aloud |
| 50–60 min | Qur'an payoff (the four prepositions in the Qur'an) · name-reading drill · Q&A · assign handout |

**Yaad rakho:** Kasra drill (5–18 min) is chapter ka dil hai — ise jaldi mat nipटाओ. Har activity mein wahi rule dohraao: "preposition ke baad zer." `مَنْ` vs `مِنْ` ko ek dedicated minute do — yeh sab se aam ghalti hai.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 4

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum aage dekhenge"*:

- ❌ `لِـ` ("for / belongs to"), `لِمَنْ` ("whose"), and the **iḍāfa** (noun-noun possessive) — **a later lesson**. (These were the *old* mis-built Chapter 4; they are not Lesson 4 in this edition.)
- ❌ Other prepositions — `تَحْتَ` (under) and `هُنَاكَ` (there) are **Lesson 5**; `أَمَامَ`/`خَلْفَ` (in front of / behind) are **Lesson 8**; `عَنْ`, `مَعَ`, `بِـ` are later still. Keep to the four.
- ❌ The full **pronoun system** — `هُوَ`/`هِيَ` are formally **Lesson 5**, `أَنَا`/`أَنْتَ` later. Use them as ready-made answer words (they're on this page) but don't build a pronoun table.
- ❌ **Verb conjugation** — `ذَهَبَ`/`خَرَجَ` stay as fixed "he went / he went out". No "she/I/you/they" forms.
- ❌ The formal **case name** for the noun after a preposition (*majrūr* / the jarr case) — teach the *effect* (kasra), not the iʿrāb label. Much later.
- ❌ The named **diptote** rule (*ghayr munṣarif*) behind the girls' names taking no tanween — read the endings as printed; don't open the rule.
- ❌ The feminine "this" (`هَذِهِ`) and the `ة` feminine system — **Lesson 6**.

**Yaad rakho:** Pre-loading madad nahin karti — overwhelm karti hai. Book ki tarteeb par bharosa karo. Khaaskar `لِـ`/iḍāfa bilkul mat chhuo — woh is sabaq ka hissa hi nahin (woh purani ghalti thi). Sirf chaar prepositions + zer + `أين`/`من أين` + do verbs.

## Self-check before teaching

Before the class, make sure:

- [ ] You can say all 15 new words confidently with correct endings — places, countries, and the two verbs. *(Har lafz sahi harakat ke saath.)*
- [ ] You can take any `الـ`-noun and put each of the four prepositions in front, changing the ending to kasra every time. *(Koi bhi `الـ`-ism le ke `في/على/من/إلى` ke saath zer laga sako.)*
- [ ] You can say the `مِنَ الْـ` smoothing automatically — *mina l-hindi*, not *min al-hindi*. *(min-al → mina-l bina sochay.)*
- [ ] You can explain `مَنْ` vs `مِنْ` in one Urdu line and read `مَنْ مِنَ الصِّينِ؟` correctly. *(Twin lafz ka farq aur woh line.)*
- [ ] You can chain the verbs: *kharaja mina l-faṣli wa-dhahaba ilā l-mudīri.* *(خرج…من…وذهب…إلى — ek saans mein.)*
- [ ] You can point to the four prepositions inside a Qur'anic phrase (Part B). *(Qur'an mein charon prepositions dikhaa sako.)*

If yes to all six — *tum tayar ho.*

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Are they putting the kasra on the noun after every preposition? *(`fī l-baytI`, not `fī l-baytU`?)*
2. Are they keeping `مَنْ` (who) and `مِنْ` (from) apart? *(Twin lafz to nahin mila rahe?)*
3. Are they saying `عَلَىٰ`/`إِلَىٰ` with a long "aa", not "-ay"? *(`ʿalā`/`ilā` — alif maqṣūrah?)*
4. Are they smoothing `مِنَ الْـ` to "mina-l"? *(Fatḥa to laga rahe hain?)*
5. Do they pair `خَرَجَ` with `مِنْ` and `ذَهَبَ` with `إِلَى` correctly? *(Verb-preposition jodi sahi?)*

**Yaad rakho:** Agar 3+ classmates kasra par phans rahe hain — Lesson 5 par jaane se pehle ek chhoti revision karwao. Kasra Lesson 4 ki neev hai, aur aage `تَحْتَ` jaisi aur preposition-jaisi cheezein isi par khadi hongi.
