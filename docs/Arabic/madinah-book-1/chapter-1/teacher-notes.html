🎯 What this lesson actually teaches (pp. 5–11)

Lesson 1 in this edition is deliberately small: the pointing word هَذَا, the fact that Arabic has no word for "is", the two questions مَا هَذَا؟ (what is this?) and مَنْ هَذَا؟ (who is this?), and the yes/no question أَ…؟ answered with نَعَمْ/لَا. It does NOT teach الـ ("the"), sun/moon letters, or the grammar terms Mubtada'/Khabar — those arrive in Lesson 3. Keep them out of class (see deep-notes Part A for why you, the teacher, still want to understand them).

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Point to a nearby object or person and say "This is a ___" in Arabic, using هَذَا.
  2. Build a simple two-word sentence without ever reaching for a word for "is/am/are".
  3. Ask مَا هَذَا؟ ("what is this?") about a thing and مَنْ هَذَا؟ ("who is this?") about a person, and answer each.
  4. Ask a yes/no question using أَ, and answer with نَعَمْ or لَا.
  5. Recognize, write, and use the 24 common nouns of the lesson (house, mosque, book, pen, key, dog, cat, camel, …).

هَذَا

Meaning
"This" — used when pointing to something nearby.
Roman Urdu
"Yeh" / "yeh hai" (jab kisi qareeb cheez ki taraf ishaara kar rahe hon).
Important
Two things to remember from day one:
  1. هَذَا is only for singular masculine objects (e.g. a book, a man, a pen). The feminine "this" (هَذِهِ) comes later — don't name it yet.
  2. The spelling has an alif (ا) after ه, but it is silent. Pronounce it "haa-dhaa" not "ha-aa-dhaa".
Example
هَذَا بَيْتٌ haadhaa baytun · "This is a house" · "Yeh ek ghar hai"

💡 Teaching tip

Don't explain grammar yet. Hold up a real pen, point at it, and say "haadhaa qalamun". Hold up a book: "haadhaa kitaabun". Repeat with 8–10 objects in the room. Students will absorb the pattern before they hear the word "grammar". This is exactly how Dr. Abdur Rahim teaches it — direct method, no English explanation first.

2 · Two Words, No "Is"

The biggest surprise for English/Urdu speakers — Arabic has no word for "is/am/are" in the present tense.

Where this fits: every sentence the student says today (haadhaa baytun) is two nouns sitting next to each other, joined without a verb.

The "no verb" rule

Meaning
In the present tense, Arabic does NOT use a word for "is/am/are". The two nouns simply sit next to each other.
Roman Urdu
Arabi mein "hai/hain/hoon" jaisa koi lafz nahin aata — bas dono lafz ek doosre ke pass rakh diye jaate hain. Tarjuma karte waqt hum English/Urdu mein "is/hai" daal lete hain.
Important
English: "This is a house" (3 words)
Urdu: "Yeh ek ghar hai" (4 words)
Arabic: هَذَا بَيْتٌ (only 2 words — no "is")
Example
هَذَا قَلَمٌ haadhaa qalamun · "This (is) a pen" · "Yeh ek qalam hai"

The bare noun's ending — ـٌ (-un)

Meaning
Every "a ___" noun in this lesson ends in a small doubled mark ـٌ read as -un: baytun, qalamun, kitaabun. Just teach it as "the natural ending of the word" — students copy the sound.
Roman Urdu
Har lafz ke aakhir mein chhoti si "-un" ki awaaz hai (baytun, qalamun). Abhi sirf awaaz sikhao — naam ya rule mat do. Aage ke chapter mein iska poora hisaab khulega.
Rule
Do not name this "tanween", "nominative", "rafʿ", "Mubtada'/Khabar", or "nominal sentence" in class. Those names come later. (You'll find the full picture in deep-notes Part A — for your eyes only.)
Example
بَيْتٌ · قَلَمٌ · كِتَابٌ baytun · qalamun · kitaabun — each ends in the -un sound

⚠️ Common mistake

Beginners often try to insert a word for "is" — e.g. they make up something like haadhaa huwa baytun. Stop this early. Tell them: "In Arabic, silence does the work of 'is'." The gap between the two words IS the "is".

3 · مَا هَذَا؟ / مَنْ هَذَا؟ — "What / Who is this?"

Two question words that unlock real back-and-forth — one for things, one for people.

Where this fits: once the student can say "this is a house", they can now ask about something they don't recognise — and the answer is just a هَذَا ___ sentence.

مَا — "what" (for things)

Meaning
Ask مَا هَذَا؟ (maa haadhaa?) when pointing at a thing or an animal. Answer with هَذَا ___.
Roman Urdu
Kisi cheez ya jaanwar ki taraf ishaara karke poochho: "maa haadhaa?" — jawab: "haadhaa ___."
Rule
مَا here means "what" — it is a question word, nothing to do with anything else students may have seen.
Example
مَا هَذَا؟ — هَذَا نَجْمٌ maa haadhaa? — haadhaa najmun · "What is this? — This is a star." · "Yeh kya hai? — Yeh ek sitara hai."

مَنْ — "who" (for people)

Meaning
Ask مَنْ هَذَا؟ (man haadhaa?) when pointing at a person. Answer with هَذَا ___.
Roman Urdu
Kisi insaan ki taraf ishaara karke poochho: "man haadhaa?" — jawab: "haadhaa ___."
Rule
Choose مَا vs مَنْ by the answer: thing → مَا; person → مَنْ.
Example
مَنْ هَذَا؟ — هَذَا مُدَرِّسٌ man haadhaa? — haadhaa mudarrisun · "Who is this? — This is a teacher." · "Yeh kaun hai? — Yeh ek ustad hai."

💡 Teaching tip

Use the book's own pictures (pp. 9–11): point at the picture of the man and ask man haadhaa?; point at the dog and ask maa haadhaa?. Let the class call out the answer. The thing/person split lands fastest through pictures, not explanation.

⚠️ Watch the spelling pair مَا vs مَنْ

They look almost the same. مَا ends in alif (maa, "what"); مَنْ ends in nūn with sukūn (man, "who"). Drill them as a pair so the difference is locked in from day one.

The question pattern

Meaning
Just put أَ (a-, with a fatḥa) before any statement to ask "Is...?".
Roman Urdu
Jumle ke shuru mein "a-" (alif par zabar) lagao — sawal ban jaata hai. English mein "is/are/am" jumle ke aage daalte hain — wahi kaam Arabi mein "أَ" karta hai.
Rule
Answer with نَعَمْ (yes) or لَا (no) — and always in a full sentence, naming the thing.
Example
أَهَذَا بَيْتٌ ؟ — نَعَمْ ، هَذَا بَيْتٌ
أَهَذَا مِفْتَاحٌ ؟ — لَا ، هَذَا قَلَمٌ "Is this a house? — Yes, this is a house." · "Is this a key? — No, this is a pen."

💡 Teaching tip

The book's pages 6, 8 and 10 are full of a-haadhaa ___? with "no" answers ("Is this a bed? — No, this is a chair", "Is this a dog? — No, this is a cat"). Pair students up and let them quiz each other straight from the pictures — the "no, this is ___" answer quietly re-drills all the vocab.

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Inserting "is/hai" into the sentence

Student says "haadhaa huwa baytun" (mixing in هُوَ = he/it). Correct them gently: in Arabic, the silence between the two words IS the "is". No extra word is needed.

2. Pronouncing the silent ا in هَذَا

Student says "ha-aa-dhaa" with a long pause. Correct: it's spelled with an alif but said "haadhaa" — one short "ha" then "dhaa".

3. Using مَا for a person (or مَنْ for a thing)

Student asks "maa haadhaa?" while pointing at the doctor. Correct: people take مَنْ (man haadhaa?), things and animals take مَا (maa haadhaa?).

4. Forgetting to repeat هَذَا in the answer

Student answers "na'am, baytun". The book always answers in a full sentence: "na'am, haadhaa baytun". Drill the full answer.

5. Mixing up هَذَا (masculine) vs the feminine form

Chapter 1 only uses هَذَا. If students ask "what about feminine?", say "we'll learn that in a later chapter — stay with هَذَا for now". Don't name the feminine word or overload them.

📿 Connect to the Qur'an

Adult learners stay motivated when they see Chapter 1 structures in the actual Qur'an. Each verse below shows one of today's patterns — هَذَا, مَا هَذَا, أَهَذَا. Use 1–2 in class; point only at the highlighted structure, nothing more advanced.

Surah Al-Anʿām · 6:76

قَالَ هَٰذَا رَبِّي

qāla hādhā rabbī — "He said: This is my Lord." (Prophet Ibrāhīm, looking at the star.)

Students recognise: هَذَا = "this" — exactly today's pointing word.

Surah Yūsuf · 12:31

مَا هَٰذَا بَشَرًا

mā hādhā basharan — "This is no mortal man!" (the women, on seeing Yūsuf).

Students recognise: مَا هَذَا — today's "what is this?" words, here used to exclaim about a person.

Surah Al-Furqān · 25:41

أَهَٰذَا الَّذِي بَعَثَ اللَّهُ رَسُولًا

a-hādhā lladhī baʿatha llāhu rasūlā — "Is this the one whom Allah has sent as a messenger?"

Students recognise: the question particle أَ glued to هَذَا → أَهَذَا = "is this…?" — exactly today's yes/no question.
