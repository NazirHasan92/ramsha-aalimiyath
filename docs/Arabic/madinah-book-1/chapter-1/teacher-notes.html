🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Point to a nearby object and say "This is a ___" in Arabic, using هَذَا.
  2. Build simple nominal sentences (Mubtada + Khabar) without using any verb "is/am/are".
  3. Add الـ to a noun to make it "the ___", and pronounce the result correctly.
  4. Hear the difference between sun-letter assimilation (e.g. ash-shams) and moon-letter pronunciation (e.g. al-qamar).
  5. Ask a yes/no question using أَ, and answer with نَعَمْ or لَا.
  6. Recognize, write, and use ~20 common nouns (house, mosque, book, pen, key, etc.).

هَذَا

Meaning
"This" — used when pointing to something nearby.
Roman Urdu
"Yeh" / "yeh hai" (jab kisi qareeb cheez ki taraf ishaara kar rahe hon).
Important
Two things to remember from day one:
  1. هَذَا is only for singular masculine objects (e.g. a book, a man, a pen). The feminine "this" (هَذِهِ) comes later.
  2. The spelling has an alif (ا) after ه, but it is silent. Pronounce it "haa-dhaa" not "ha-aa-dhaa".
Example
هَذَا بَيْتٌ haadhaa baytun · "This is a house" · "Yeh ek ghar hai"

💡 Teaching tip

Don't explain grammar yet. Hold up a real pen, point at it, and say "haadhaa qalamun". Hold up a book: "haadhaa kitaabun". Repeat with 8–10 objects in the room. Students will absorb the pattern before they hear the word "grammar". This is exactly how Shaykh Vaniya teaches it — direct method, no English explanation first.

2 · The Nominal Sentence — جُمْلَةٌ اِسْمِيَّةٌ

The biggest surprise for English/Urdu speakers — Arabic has no word for "is/am/are" in the present tense.

Where this fits: every sentence the student says today (haadhaa baytun) is technically a nominal sentence — two parts joined without a verb.

(the news — what we say about it)"]:::main P1 --> EX1["هَذَا
haadhaa"]:::ex P2 --> EX2["بَيْتٌ
baytun"]:::ex EX1 --> R["= هَذَا بَيْتٌ
'This (is) a house'"]:::ex EX2 --> R classDef root fill:#064e3b,stroke:#064e3b,color:#fff,stroke-width:2px,font-weight:bold; classDef main fill:#cffafe,stroke:#0e7490,color:#0c4a6e,stroke-width:2px,font-weight:bold; classDef ex fill:#ffe4e6,stroke:#be123c,color:#881337;

The two parts

Mubtada'
مُبْتَدَأٌ — literally "the one we begin with". This is the subject; what the sentence is about.
Roman
Jis ke baare mein baat ki jaa rahi hai — woh "mubtada" hai.
Khabar
خَبَرٌ — literally "news". This is the predicate; what we are saying about the mubtada.
Roman
Mubtada ke baare mein jo khabar di jaa rahi hai — woh "khabar" hai.

The "no verb" rule — لَا فِعْلَ

Rule
In the present tense, Arabic does NOT use a word for "is/am/are". The two nouns simply sit next to each other.
Roman
Arabi mein "hai/hain/hoon" jaisa koi lafz nahin aata — bas dono lafz ek doosre ke pass rakh diye jaate hain. Tarjuma karte waqt hum English/Urdu mein "is/hai" daal lete hain.
Compare
English: "This is a house" (3 words)
Urdu: "Yeh ek ghar hai" (4 words)
Arabic: هَذَا بَيْتٌ (only 2 words — no "is")
More examples
هَذَا قَلَمٌ haadhaa qalamun · "This (is) a pen" · "Yeh ek qalam hai"

⚠️ Common mistake

Beginners often try to insert a word for "is" — e.g. they make up something like haadhaa huwa baytun. Stop this early. Tell them: "In Arabic, silence does the work of 'is'." The gap between the two words IS the "is".

3 · الـ — The Definite Article ("the")

In one stroke, this turns any noun from "a thing" into "the (specific) thing".

Where this fits: after the student can say "haadhaa baytun" (this is a house), we now teach them to say "this is the house" — haadhal-baytu.

The rule of الـ

English
الـ is equivalent to English "the". Attach it to the front of any indefinite noun to make it definite.
Roman
"Al-" = "the" / "woh khaas". Kisi bhi nakira (indefinite) ism ke shuru mein lagaa do, woh maʿrifa (definite) ban jaata hai.
Tanween rule
Indefinite Arabic nouns end in tanween — a doubled short vowel: ـٌ (un), ـٍ (in), or ـً (an). When you add الـ, that doubling drops and you are left with a single short vowel: ـُ, ـِ, or ـَ.
Roman
Nakira ism ke aakhir mein "tanween" hota hai (jaise baytun, qalamun). Jab "al-" lagao, tanween hat jaata hai aur sirf ek harakat reh jaati hai (al-baytu, al-qalamu).
Compare
بَيْتٌ → الْبَيْتُ baytun → al-baytu · "a house" → "the house"

💡 Teaching tip

Draw two columns on the board: Indefinite (a/an) and Definite (the). Write each new vocab word in both forms side by side: baytun / al-baytu, qalamun / al-qalamu, kitaabun / al-kitaabu. After 6–7 words, students see the pattern themselves.

4 · Sun Letters & Moon Letters — حُرُوفُ شَمْسِيَّةٌ وَقَمَرِيَّةٌ

The pronunciation rule that confuses learners forever if it's not introduced clearly now.

Where this fits: when you add الـ to certain letters, the ل stops being pronounced — it "melts" into the next letter. This is purely a sound rule. The spelling stays the same.

The names

Why?
The names come from the test words: the moon = الْقَمَرُ (al-qamar) — the ق belongs to one group. The sun = الشَّمْسُ (ash-shams) — the ش belongs to the other.
Roman
"Qamar" (chand) ke ق par "al-" saaf bolaa jaata hai — to ق jaisi 14 letters "Qamariyya" (moon letters) hain. "Shams" (sooraj) ke ش par "al-" ki "l" gum ho jaati hai, ش double ho jaata hai — to ش jaisi 14 letters "Shamsiyya" (sun letters) hain.

Moon letters (ل is pronounced) · 14 letters

Letters
ا · ب · ج · ح · خ · ع · غ · ف · ق · ك · م · هـ · و · ي
Examples
الْبَيْتُ · الْقَمَرُ · الْكِتَابُ · الْمَسْجِدُ al-baytu, al-qamaru, al-kitaabu, al-masjidu — the "L" is fully pronounced.

Sun letters (ل goes silent, next letter doubles) · 14 letters

Letters
ت · ث · د · ذ · ر · ز · س · ش · ص · ض · ط · ظ · ل · ن
Examples
الشَّمْسُ · النَّجْمُ · السُّكَّرُ · الرَّجُلُ ash-shamsu, an-najmu, as-sukkaru, ar-rajulu — NOT "al-shams", say "ash-shams".

💡 Memory hook for adult learners

Tell them: "The Sun is loud — it eats the L." When you point at the sun (الشمس), say ash-shams; the "L" is swallowed. The Moon is gentle (القمر) — it lets the L through. After this image, students rarely forget.

⚠️ Important note

This is purely a pronunciation rule. The spelling always stays the same — you always write الـ. Whether you say the L or not depends on what comes next. Many students try to write "ash" instead of "al-sh" — correct them.

5 · أَ — The Question Particle

A single letter that turns any statement into a yes/no question. Beautifully simple.

Where this fits: students have built "this is a house" — now they will ask "is this a house?".

The question pattern

English
Just put أَ (a-, with a fatḥa) before any nominal sentence to ask "Is...?".
Roman
Jumle ke shuru mein "a-" (alif par zabar) lagao — sawal ban jaata hai. English mein "is/are/am" jumle ke aage daalte hain — wahi kaam Arabi mein "أَ" karta hai.
Examples
أَهَذَا قَلَمٌ ؟ — نَعَمْ ، هَذَا قَلَمٌ a-haadhaa qalamun? — na'am, haadhaa qalamun. · "Is this a pen? — Yes, this is a pen."
أَهَذَا بَابٌ ؟ — لَا ، هَذَا قَلَمٌ a-haadhaa baabun? — laa, haadhaa qalamun. · "Is this a door? — No, this is a pen."

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Inserting "is/hai" into the sentence

Student says "haadhaa huwa baytun" (mixing in هُوَ = he/it). Correct them gently: in Arabic, the silence between the two words IS the "is". No extra word is needed.

2. Pronouncing the silent ا in هَذَا

Student says "ha-aa-dhaa" with a long pause. Correct: it's spelled with an alif but said "haadhaa" — one short "ha" then "dhaa".

3. Forgetting the tanween drops

Student says "al-baytun" (keeps the doubling). Correct: when al- is attached, the doubling drops — al-baytu, not al-baytun.

4. Saying "al-shams" instead of "ash-shams"

Sun-letter assimilation is unfamiliar to English speakers. Drill it specifically: have them say ash-shams, an-najm, ar-rajul, as-sareer aloud, comparing to al-bayt, al-qamar, al-kitaab.

5. Mixing up هَذَا (masculine) vs هَذِهِ (feminine)

Chapter 1 only uses هَذَا. If students ask "what about feminine?", say "we'll learn that in Chapter 3 — stay with هَذَا for now". Don't overload them.

📿 Connect to the Qur'an

Adult learners stay motivated when they see Chapter 1 vocabulary in the actual Qur'an. Use 1–2 of these in class.

Surah Al-Baqarah · 2:35

uskun anta wa zawjuka al-jannah — "Dwell, you and your wife, in the Garden."

وَالشَّمْسُ تَجْرِي لِمُسْتَقَرٍّ لَّهَا

wa-ash-shamsu tajrī li-mustaqarrin lahā — "And the sun runs to its resting place."

Students recognise: الشَّمْسُ — sun letter assimilation, just learned!

Surah Al-Fatihah · 1:2

الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ رَبِّ الْعَالَمِينَ

al-ḥamdu lillāhi rabbi al-ʿālamīn — "All praise is for Allah, Lord of the worlds."

Students recognise: two الـ prefixes — both moon letters (ح, ع). The L is heard.
