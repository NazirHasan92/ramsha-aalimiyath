# Part A · Understanding the chapter

Before you can teach Chapter 1, you need to see what the chapter is *actually* doing. On the surface it teaches one word (`هَذَا`), two question words (`مَا` / `مَنْ`), and one question particle (`أَ`). But underneath, it is silently teaching the entire architecture of the Arabic nominal sentence.

**Yaad rakho:** Ch 1 dekhne mein simple hai — par andar se yeh poori Arabic ki bunyaadi grammar daal raha hai students ke dimaag mein. Tumhe samajhna zaroori hai *kyun* sab kuch is tarah hai, na ke sirf padhna hai.

Work through the four concepts below at your own pace (Concept 5 is "looking ahead" — background only). For each, ask yourself: *can I explain this to someone who has never seen Arabic before?* (*Kya main yeh kisi aise insaan ko samjha sakti hoon jisne Arabic kabhi nahin dekhi?*) If yes — move on. If not — re-read.

## 1 · هَذَا — the architecture of "pointing"

`هَذَا` is a **demonstrative pronoun** (Arabic: *ism al-ishaarah*, اسم الإشارة — literally "noun of pointing"; Roman Urdu: *ishaarah wala lafz*). It points to something near and masculine.

### The bigger system you're not seeing yet

Arabic actually has a 4-way demonstrative grid:

|   | Near (*qareeb* · قریب) | Far (*door* · دور) |
|---|---|---|
| **Masculine** (*mudhakkar* · مذکر) | `هَذَا` haadhaa | `ذَلِكَ` dhaalika — **Ch 2** |
| **Feminine** (*mu'annath* · مؤنث) | `هَذِهِ` haadhihi — later | `تِلْكَ` tilka — later |

Chapter 1 opens only **one cell** of this grid (near-masculine). The far-masculine `ذَلِكَ` arrives in Ch 2; the feminine forms come later.

**Yaad rakho:** Book chupke se sirf masculine vocab dene waali hai Ch 1 mein — yeh deliberate hai, taake "haadhaa" har lafz par fit ho jaaye. Feminine ka jhanjhat baad ke chapters mein khulta hai.

### The "silent alif" puzzle

You write `هَذَا` but say "haadhaa". Where does the long *aa* come from? Not from the final ا — the final ا is a leftover writing convention. The long *aa* actually sits *between* `ه` and `ذ`.

In fully vocalised editions you sometimes see this written as `هَـٰذَا` with a small **dagger alif** (ـٰ) above the ه. That's the historically accurate form. The big alif at the end is just how scribes wrote it centuries ago.

**Yaad rakho:** Jab board par pehli baar likho, dagger alif (`هَـٰذَا`) ke saath likho. Class ko bolo: *"Aakhir wali ا puraani writing ka leftover hai — asli 'aa' yahaan upar wali chhoti waali hai."* Phir slowly bolo: "haa-dhaa." Aakhir ki ا ko khainch mat na — woh khaali padi hai.

### Why gender matters from Day 1

Every Arabic noun is either masculine (*mudhakkar*) or feminine (*mu'annath*) — and the demonstrative *must* match. `هَذَا` only works with masculine nouns. The book quietly hands you only masculine vocab in Ch 1 so this constraint never appears as a problem. Later, when feminine nouns and `هَذِهِ` arrive, the matching habit is already in place.

**Yaad rakho:** Agar koi classmate poochhe *"feminine ke baare mein kya?"* — jawab: *"Yeh aage aayega. Abhi sirf qareeb wali, mardaana cheezein."* Aage ki cheezein early mat khol do — overwhelm hota hai. (Aur feminine ka lafz `هَذِهِ` abhi naam bhi mat lo.)

## 2 · The nominal sentence — why there is no "is"

`هَذَا بَيْتٌ` = "This is a house." But where is the word for "is"?

There isn't one. Arabic, in the present tense, doesn't use a copula (the linguistic term for "is/am/are"). Two nouns sitting next to each other = a sentence.

### The terminology (for YOU — not for the class)

This kind of sentence is called a **Jumla Ismiyya** (جُمْلَةٌ اِسْمِيَّةٌ) — a *nominal* sentence (Roman Urdu: *naam wala jumla*), because it starts with a noun (*ism*). It has two parts:

- **Mubtada'** (مُبْتَدَأٌ) — literally "the one we begin with" (*jis se shuruaat hoti hai*). The starting noun. In English: subject.
- **Khabar** (خَبَرٌ) — literally "news" (*khabar — us ke baare mein jo bataya jaa raha hai*). What we say about the Mubtada'. In English: predicate.

**These names belong to Lesson 3, not Lesson 1.** Know them so you understand the structure — but in Ch 1 do **not** put the words "Mubtada'", "Khabar", or "Jumla Ismiyya" in front of the class. Teach only the *behaviour*: two words, no "is".

**Yaad rakho (apne liye — class ko nahin):** Yeh teen naam (Mubtada' · Khabar · Jumla Ismiyya) **Lesson 3 ka maal hain**. Tum samajhne ke liye yaad rakho, par Ch 1 mein class ko sirf pattern dikhao — "do lafz, beech mein koi 'hai' nahin." Naam baad mein.

### Why this is a real conceptual jump

- English: "This **is** a house." (3 words, one is a verb)
- Urdu: "Yeh ek ghar **hai**." (4 words, one is the copula)
- Arabic: `هَذَا بَيْتٌ` (2 words. No verb.)

Both English and Urdu speakers feel an unfilled gap in the Arabic sentence. Their inner ear adds a phantom "is" or "hai". You probably did this too when you first learned it.

**Yaad rakho:** Class mein words ginwana — board par teen lines likho:

- Urdu: *yeh / ek / ghar / hai* (4)
- English: *this / is / a / house* (4)
- Arabic: *haadhaa / baytun* (**2**)

Yeh visual comparison se dimaag accept karta hai ke Arabic genuinely chhoti hai. *Silence is the 'hai'* — khaali jagah hi "hai" ka kaam karti hai.

### A preview of cases (don't teach this — but you should know it)

Both `هَذَا` and `بَيْتٌ` are technically in *rafʿ* (the nominative case). That's why the ending of `بَيْتٌ` is `ـٌ` (-un) — the nominative ending for an indefinite noun. This "-un" doubled mark is called **tanween**. The case system is invisible in Ch 1 because there's nothing to compare it to yet — but it's the silent reason the endings look the way they do. Cases get named explicitly in a later lesson.

In Ch 1, just teach the `ـٌ` (-un) ending as "the natural sound at the end of the word" — copy it, don't analyse it.

**Yaad rakho (apne liye — classmates ko nahin batana):** Haadhaa aur baytun dono "rafʿ" case mein hain, aur "-un" ko **tanween** kehte hain. Yeh "case" ka system aage khulega. Abhi class ko sirf awaaz dikhao — terminology mat lao. Agar smart classmate kyun-kyun poochhe — *"Yeh aage samjha jaayega."*

## 3 · مَا هَذَا؟ / مَنْ هَذَا؟ — the two questions

This is the heart of the lesson's *conversation*. Once you can say `هَذَا بَيْتٌ`, the book immediately teaches you to **ask** about something — and the answer is just another `هَذَا ___` sentence.

### Two question words, split by what they ask about

- **`مَا`** (*maa* — *kya*) = "what?" — used for **things and animals**. `مَا هَذَا؟` → `هَذَا نَجْمٌ` ("What is this? — This is a star.")
- **`مَنْ`** (*man* — *kaun*) = "who?" — used for **people**. `مَنْ هَذَا؟` → `هَذَا طَبِيبٌ` ("Who is this? — This is a doctor.")

The whole choice is decided by the *answer*: if the answer is a person, the question is `مَنْ`; if it's a thing or animal, the question is `مَا`. The book drills this directly with its pictures on pp. 9–11 (the man, the doctor, the merchant → `مَنْ`; the dog, the donkey, the camel, the star → `مَا`).

### The two words look almost identical — watch them

- `مَا` ends in **alif** → long *aa* → "what" (things).
- `مَنْ` ends in **nūn with sukūn** → short, closed *man* → "who" (people).

Same two-letter skeleton, one letter different. This is the lesson's one genuine "twin-word" trap.

**Yaad rakho:** `مَا` (alif, "kya") cheezon ke liye · `مَنْ` (noon, "kaun") logon ke liye. Faisla *jawab* se hota hai — agar jawab insaan hai to `مَنْ`, agar cheez/jaanwar hai to `مَا`. Dono lafz dekhne mein lagbhag ek jaise hain — ek harf ka farq hai. Isi par sab se zyaada dhyaan do.

### A subtlety for you: `مَا` here is *not* negation

Later in the book, `مَا` can also mean "not" (negation). **In Lesson 1 it only means "what".** Don't mention the other meaning to the class — and don't let it confuse you when you meet it later.

**Yaad rakho (apne liye):** `مَا` ka ek aur matlab ("nahin" — negation) aage aata hai. Ch 1 mein sirf "kya". Class ko doosra matlab mat batao.

## 4 · أَ — questions without inversion

`أَ` is the **interrogative hamza** (*hamzat al-istifham*, همزة الاستفهام — Roman Urdu: *sawal wali hamza*). One letter, glued to the front of any sentence, turns it into a yes/no question.

### Why this is simpler than English

English forms yes/no questions by *inverting* word order: "This is a house" → "Is this a house?" Urdu uses a question word ("kya"): "Yeh ghar hai?" → "Kya yeh ghar hai?"

Arabic just prefixes a letter. No inversion, no extra word. The sentence structure stays exactly the same:

- Statement: `هَذَا بَيْتٌ` (*haadhaa baytun* · *"Yeh ek ghar hai."*)
- Question: `أَهَذَا بَيْتٌ ؟` (*a-haadhaa baytun?* · *"Kya yeh ek ghar hai?"*)

**Yaad rakho:** Arabic ka sawal banana English ya Urdu se aasaan hai — bas ek harf shuru mein. Word order vahi, kuch nahin badalta. Urdu mein "kya" jo kaam karti hai, Arabic mein "أَ" us se bhi chhoti hai — bas ek alif.

### Answer particles — and the full-sentence habit

`نَعَمْ` (*naʿam* — *haan*) = yes. `لَا` (*laa* — *nahin*) = no.

The book always answers in a **full sentence**, not a fragment: `أَهَذَا سَرِيرٌ؟ — لَا، هَذَا كُرْسِيٌّ` ("Is this a bed? — No, this is a chair."). The "no" answer always names the correct thing. This is excellent because every answer silently re-drills the vocabulary.

**Yaad rakho:** Jawab hamesha poora jumla — *"naʿam, haadhaa baytun"*, sirf "naʿam" nahin. "Laa" wale jawab mein sahi cheez ka naam bhi do — *"laa, haadhaa kursiyyun"*. Isi tarah har jawab vocab dohra deta hai.

### There's also هَلْ, and بَلَى (later — for your awareness)

Arabic has a *second* yes/no particle `هَلْ`, roughly interchangeable with `أَ`, and a special "yes!" — `بَلَى` (*balaa*) — used to contradict a *negative* question. **Neither is in Lesson 1.** If a classmate asks about `هَلْ` — *"Hum woh aage dekhenge; abhi sirf أَ."*

**Yaad rakho:** Ch 1 mein sirf `أَ` + `نَعَمْ`/`لَا`. `هَلْ` aur `بَلَى` ka tumhe pata ho, par class mein mat lao.

## 5 · Looking ahead — `الـ` and sun/moon letters (LESSON 3, not now)

> ⚠️ **This whole concept is background for YOU. None of it is taught in Lesson 1.** In this edition, the definite article `الـ` ("the"), the dropping of the tanween, and the sun/moon-letter pronunciation rule are all **Lesson 3** (pp. 14–20). They are placed here only so you're not caught off guard when you build/teach Ch 3 — and so that if a classmate has seen "al-" somewhere, you can defer it confidently. **Do not put any of this in the Ch 1 class.**

### What's coming (so you recognise it)

- **`الـ` = "the".** It attaches to the front of an indefinite noun and makes it definite (`بَيْتٌ` "a house" → `الْبَيْتُ` "the house"). When it attaches, the tanween doubling drops: `ـٌ` → `ـُ`. The case (rafʿ) doesn't change — only the definiteness.
- **Sun & moon letters.** When `الـ` meets certain letters (sun letters), the `ل` goes silent and the next letter doubles (`الشَّمْس` → *ash-shams*, not *al-shams*). When it meets the others (moon letters), the `ل` is heard (`الْقَمَر` → *al-qamar*). It's a pure pronunciation rule; the spelling never changes. The physical reason: sun letters are articulated near the front of the mouth, close to where `ل` is made, so the tongue merges them; moon letters are articulated far away, so `ل` stays clear.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh sab **Lesson 3** hai — `الـ`, tanween ka girna, aur sun/moon letters. Tumhe samajh ke liye yahaan diya hai, taake Ch 3 banate waqt taiyar ho. **Ch 1 ki class mein bilkul nahin lana.** Agar koi classmate "al-" ke baare mein poochhe — *"Yeh hum Lesson 3 mein seekhenge."*

## The big picture — what this chapter is silently teaching

Now that you've worked through each concept, step back. Chapter 1 teaches one pointing word, two question words, and one question particle — but underneath, it has handed you the foundation of basic Arabic:

1. **Arabic builds sentences by juxtaposition**, not by inflecting a copula verb. (*Arabic mein dono lafz ek doosre ke paas rakh do — "hai" ki zaroorat nahin*.)
2. **Case-endings exist** — the `ـٌ` (-un / tanween) on every noun is the silent start of the case system. (*Lafz ke aakhir ki harkat case batati hai — abhi sirf awaaz, aage poora hisaab*.)
3. **Demonstratives match in gender** — `هَذَا` is masculine-only; the grid fills out over the next chapters. (*Ishaarah ka lafz gender se match karta hai*.)
4. **Questions are formed by particles and question-words**, not by word-order inversion. (*Sawal banane ke liye `أَ` ya `مَا`/`مَنْ` — word order vahi*.)
5. **Things vs people is a grammatical split** the language cares about (`مَا` vs `مَنْ`). (*Cheez aur insaan ka farq grammar mein zinda hai*.)

**Yaad rakho:** Dr. Abdur Rahim jaan-boojh kar over-explain nahin karte. Wo chahte hain ke yeh structures *absorb* hon — *analyse* na hon. Tumhare classmates yeh paanch points consciously nahin samjhenge — par Ch 1 ke baad inhe yeh paanch baatein "feel" mein aa jaayengi. Tum ko yeh poora picture maaloom hai — par class ko sirf surface dikhana hai.

## The five landmines for Urdu-speaking adult learners

### Landmine 1 · "Hai" feels missing

Urdu speakers feel a sentence is incomplete without "hai/hain". When they hear `haadhaa baytun`, their inner ear adds a phantom "hai" — and so they often try to verbalise it. Some try to use `هُوَ` (he/it) as a substitute for "is".

**Counter-strategy:** Don't just tell them "there is no 'is' in Arabic". Show them by counting words on the board:

- Urdu: *yeh / ek / ghar / hai* (4 words)
- English: *this / is / a / house* (4 words)
- Arabic: *haadhaa / baytun* (2 words)

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Dekho — Urdu mein 'hai' lafz hai, English mein 'is' hai. Arabic mein yeh kaam **khaali jagah** karti hai. Silence is the 'hai'. Dono lafz jod do, beech ki khaali jagah hi 'hai' ka kaam karti hai. Koi extra word mat daalo."*

### Landmine 2 · The silent alif in هَذَا

`هَذَا` is written **هـ + ـذ + ـا**. That alif looks long. Classmates who have just finished learning the alphabet will lengthen it: "ha-AA-dhaa". The actual pronunciation is "haadhaa" — the alif represents a long *aa* in the middle, not a separate syllable.

**Counter-strategy:** When you first write it on the board, write `هَـٰذَا` with the small **dagger alif** above the ه.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Dekho — likhne mein ا aakhir mein hai, par sunaayi 'aa' beech mein deti hai. Yeh chhoti waali alif (dagger alif) batati hai ke asli 'aa' yahaan upar hai. Aakhir wali ا ek puraani writing convention hai — usse alag se mat khaincho, woh khaali padi hai. Bolo: 'haa-dhaa' — beech mein 'aa', aakhir mein bilkul nahin."*

### Landmine 3 · مَا (things) vs مَنْ (people) mix-up

The two question words look almost the same, and Urdu's *kya/kaun* split doesn't map onto the *shape* of the Arabic words — so classmates will point at the doctor and ask *maa haadhaa?*

**Counter-strategy:** Teach them as a fixed pair from minute one, decided by the answer. Run a rapid picture drill: flash a picture, class shouts the right question word *before* the answer.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`مَا` (alif ke saath, 'kya') — cheez ya jaanwar ke liye. `مَنْ` (noon ke saath, 'kaun') — insaan ke liye. Faisla jawab se: agar jawab insaan hai to `مَنْ`, warna `مَا`. Dono lafz lagbhag ek jaise hain — sirf aakhri harf ka farq. Tasveer dekho, pehle sawaal ka lafz bolo, phir jawab."*

### Landmine 4 · Dropping or garbling the -un ending

Beginners reading the alphabet often ignore the final harakah, so they say "bayt" instead of "baytun", or randomly swap the ending. In Ch 1 every citing-form noun ends in `ـٌ` (-un).

**Counter-strategy:** Drill the ending as part of the word's "music" — never say the noun bare. Always *baytun, qalamun, kitaabun*. Don't name it "tanween" or "case"; just insist the sound is part of the word.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Lafz ke aakhir mein chhoti si 'un' ki awaaz hai — woh lafz ka hissa hai, alag cheez nahin. 'Bayt' mat bolo — 'baytun' bolo. Ise lafz ka 'sur' samjho. Naam abhi nahin — bas awaaz pakdo."*

### Landmine 5 · Answering with a fragment

When asked `أَهَذَا قَلَمٌ؟`, classmates will answer just "naʿam" or "laa". The book's habit is a full-sentence answer that names the thing.

**Counter-strategy:** Insist on the full reply: *naʿam, haadhaa qalamun* / *laa, haadhaa kitaabun*. Model it, then make them echo. The "no" answer must name the correct object.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Sirf 'haan' ya 'nahin' kaafi nahin — poora jumla bolo: 'naʿam, haadhaa qalamun'. Aur 'laa' kaho to sahi cheez ka naam bhi do: 'laa, haadhaa kitaabun'. Isi tarah har jawab tumhari vocab pakki karta hai."*

### Teacher's own discipline · Resist teaching `الـ`, sun/moon, and grammar names

You now understand `الـ`, sun/moon letters, and Mubtada'/Khabar (from Part A). **Resist the urge to share any of it.** All three are **Lesson 3** in this edition. Putting "al-", sun-letter assimilation, or "Mubtada'/Khabar" into Ch 1 overloads beginners and gets ahead of the book.

**Counter-strategy (apne liye yaad rakho):** Agar koi smart classmate "al-" ya "the" poochhe — *"Yeh hum Lesson 3 mein seekhenge — abhi sirf `هَذَا`, sawaal, aur haan/nahin."* Grammar ke naam (Mubtada'/Khabar) zabaan par mat lao. Self-discipline ki zaroorat hai yahaan — book ki tarteeb par bharosa.

## Suggested 60-minute class plan

*Class shuru karne se pehle:* board par cheezein likho — chapter ka Arabic title (`الدَّرْسُ الأَوَّلُ`), aaj ke 4 rules ki ek-line tagline, aur 10 vocab objects/pictures ki list jo tum point karne wali ho.

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–5 min | Greetings · what they'll learn today (3 sentences mein) |
| 5–18 min | Vocabulary drill: 10 objects/pictures, point + repeat + name (*haadhaa qalamun* — full -un ending) |
| 18–30 min | "Haadhaa ___" pattern drill — whole class, then individuals; insist on the -un ending |
| 30–42 min | `مَا هَذَا؟` (things) vs `مَنْ هَذَا؟` (people) — picture drill from pp. 9–11; class calls the question word, then the answer |
| 42–54 min | Question particle `أَ` — pair work: classmates ask *"a-haadhaa ___?"* and answer in **full sentences** (`نَعَمْ`/`لَا`) |
| 54–60 min | Wrap up · Q&A · assign exercises from the handout |

**Yaad rakho:** Pacing important hai — agar kisi step mein zyaada time lag jaaye, baad ke steps short kar do, par har step pe touch karna zaroori hai. Vocabulary ki nayi cheezein next class mein refresh kar sakti ho. `مَا`/`مَنْ` wala step skip mat karna — yeh lesson ka dil hai.

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum aage seekhenge"*:

- ❌ The definite article `الـ` ("the") and the tanween drop — **Lesson 3**
- ❌ Sun letters & moon letters (pronunciation rule) — **Lesson 3**

- ❌ Feminine demonstrative `هَذِهِ` — later (don't even name it)
- ❌ Far demonstrative `ذَلِكَ` ("that") — **Chapter 2**

- ❌ `مَا` as negation ("not") — later (in Ch 1 `مَا` only means "what")

**Yaad rakho:** Pre-loading madad nahin karti — yeh classmates ko overwhelm kar deti hai. Book ki sequencing par bharosa karo — woh khud apna kaam karti hai. Aage ki cheezein aage waqt par. **`مَا هَذَا؟` aur `مَنْ هَذَا؟` is lesson ka hissa hain — inhe zaroor padhao** (yeh aage waali list mein nahin).

## Self-check before teaching

Before the class, make sure:

- [ ] You can say all 24 vocab nouns confidently, each with its `ـٌ` (-un) ending. *(Har lafz "-un" ke saath confident bol sako.)*
- [ ] You can ask `مَا هَذَا؟` for a thing and `مَنْ هَذَا؟` for a person without hesitating over which one. *(Cheez par `مَا`, insaan par `مَنْ` — bina sochay.)*
- [ ] You can give 5 spontaneous *a-haadhaa ___?* questions and answer each in a full sentence. *(5 sawal aur poore jumle wale jawab khud bana sako.)*
- [ ] You can explain — in one sentence each — why the alif in `هَذَا` is silent and why Arabic needs no word for "is". *(In dono ko ek-ek line mein Urdu mein samjha sako.)*
- [ ] You can answer "what about 'the' / 'al-'?" with calm confidence: *"Lesson 3."*

If yes to all five — *tum tayar ho.*

---

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Are they inserting "is/hai" into sentences? *(Kya woh 'hai' Arabic mein bhi daal rahe hain?)*
2. Are they pronouncing the silent ا in `هَذَا` long? *(Silent alif ko lambi kar rahe hain?)*
3. Are they choosing `مَا` vs `مَنْ` correctly (thing vs person)? *(`مَا`/`مَنْ` sahi chun rahe hain?)*
4. Are they keeping the `ـٌ` (-un) ending on the nouns? *(Lafz ke aakhir ki awaaz bol rahe hain?)*
5. Are they answering yes/no questions in full sentences, naming the object? *(Poore jumle mein jawab de rahe hain?)*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh paanch points tumhe batayenge ke Ch 2 par jaana hai ya Ch 1 ke kisi hisse ko dohraana hai. Agar 3+ items mein cohort phans rahi hai — ek aur revision session karwao Ch 2 par jaane se pehle. Foundation mazboot honi chahiye.
