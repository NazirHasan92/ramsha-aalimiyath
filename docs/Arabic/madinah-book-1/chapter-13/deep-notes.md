> **📖 A note before you start — this is the LONGEST lesson in Book 1 (≈14 pages, PDF pp. 68–81), in three parts (أ، ب، ج).** Content read directly from the textbook pages. Don't be scared by its length: the whole lesson is **one idea repeated three times**. The idea is the **plural** — talking about *many* people instead of one. Once you see that one idea, all 14 pages collapse into a single picture.
>
> **The one idea: when you talk about a *group of people*, FOUR words shift to their plural form together —**
> 1. the **noun** itself goes plural (`طَالِب` → `طُلَّاب`, `طَالِبَة` → `طَالِبَات`);
> 2. the **pointer** changes — `هَذَا`/`هَذِهِ` both become **`هَؤُلَاءِ`** ("these"), and `ذَلِكَ`/`تِلْكَ` both become **`أُولَئِكَ`** ("those");
> 3. the **pronoun** changes — `هُوَ`/`هِيَ` become **`هُمْ`** (men) / **`هُنَّ`** (women), and the tail `ـهُ`/`ـهَا` becomes **`ـهُمْ`** / **`ـهُنَّ`** ("their");
> 4. the **verb** changes — `ذَهَبَ`/`ذَهَبَتْ` become **`ذَهَبُوا`** (men) / **`ذَهَبْنَ`** (women).
>
> The three parts of the lesson just walk you through this: **Part أ** does it for **men** (`هُمْ`, `ذَهَبُوا`), **Part ب** for **women** (`هُنَّ`, `ذَهَبْنَ`), and **Part ج** adds the **far** pointer `أُولَئِكَ`. The pointers `هَؤُلَاءِ`/`أُولَئِكَ` are the gift of this lesson: **one word for both genders** — the gender split you fought with in `هَذَا`/`هَذِهِ` simply vanishes in the plural.
>
> **⏱️ This is a two-sitting lesson — plan it that way.** Class 1 = the masculine plural (Part أ). Class 2 = the feminine plural (Part ب) + the far pointer `أُولَئِكَ` (Part ج). Do not try to cram all of it into one hour.

# Part A · Understanding the chapter

For twelve chapters your class has lived in the **singular** — one book, one boy, one girl, *this* one, *that* one, *he*, *she*. Every tool they own is built for talking about **one** thing at a time. Lesson 13 is where the world becomes **plural**: many students, many men, many women, "these", "those", "they". It is a big lesson because Arabic marks the plural in *several* places at once — but it is not a *hard* lesson, because every one of those places is just the singular tool you already know, switched to its plural form.

In one sentence: **Chapter 13 teaches how to talk about a group of rational beings — the plural noun (sound and broken), the plural pointers `هَؤُلَاءِ` ("these") and `أُولَئِكَ` ("those"), the plural pronouns `هُمْ`/`هُنَّ` ("they") with their tails `ـهُمْ`/`ـهُنَّ` ("their"), and the plural past verb `ذَهَبُوا`/`ذَهَبْنَ`.**

**Yaad rakho:** Lesson 13 sabse **lamba** sabaq hai (≈14 page, teen hisse: أ، ب، ج) — magar ek hi cheez hai: **jama** (plural, yani "bahut saare log"). Jab logon ke **group** ki baat karo, to char cheezein ek saath plural ban jaati hain: (1) **ism** (`طَالِب`→`طُلَّاب`); (2) **ishaarah** — `هَذَا`/`هَذِهِ` dono `هَؤُلَاءِ`, aur `ذَلِكَ`/`تِلْكَ` dono `أُولَئِكَ`; (3) **zameer** — `هُوَ`/`هِيَ` → `هُمْ` (mard)/`هُنَّ` (aurtein), aur tail `ـهُ`/`ـهَا` → `ـهُمْ`/`ـهُنَّ`; (4) **verb** — `ذَهَبَ`/`ذَهَبَتْ` → `ذَهَبُوا` (mard)/`ذَهَبْنَ` (aurtein). Part أ = mardon ki jama, Part ب = aurton ki jama, Part ج = door wala `أُولَئِكَ`. **Sabse pyaari baat:** plural mein `هَؤُلَاءِ`/`أُولَئِكَ` dono genders ke liye ek hi — `هَذَا`/`هَذِهِ` wala gender-jhagra plural mein khatam.

## The dialogues (read them first — everything grows out of them)

The lesson is built on three short conversations. Read them slowly.

### Part أ — about men (`هُمْ`, plural pointer `هَؤُلَاءِ`)

> — مَنْ هَؤُلَاءِ الْفِتْيَةُ الطِّوَالُ يَا عَلِيُّ ؟
> — هُمْ طُلَّابٌ جُدُدٌ .
> — مِنْ أَيْنَ هُمْ ؟ — هُمْ مِنْ أَمْرِيكَا .
> — أَهُمْ زُمَلَاؤُكَ ؟ — نَعَمْ . هُمْ زُمَلَائِي . هُمْ فِي فَصْلِي .
> — أَهُمْ مُجْتَهِدُونَ ؟ — نَعَمْ . هُمْ مُجْتَهِدُونَ .
> — مَا أَسْمَاؤُهُمْ ؟ — أَسْمَاؤُهُمْ : يَاسِرٌ وَزَكَرِيَّا وَمُوسَى وَعَبْدُ اللهِ .
> — وَمَنْ هَؤُلَاءِ الرِّجَالُ الْقِصَارُ ؟ — هُمْ حُجَّاجٌ .
> — مِنْ أَيْنَ هُمْ ؟ — بَعْضُهُمْ مِنَ الصِّينِ وَبَعْضُهُمْ مِنَ الْيَابَانِ .
> — أَيْنَ مُوسَى وَأَصْدِقَاؤُهُ ؟ — ذَهَبُوا إِلَى الْمَطْعَمِ .

### Part ب — about women (`هُنَّ`, tail `ـهُنَّ`, verb `ذَهَبْنَ`)

> — مَنْ هَؤُلَاءِ الْفَتَيَاتُ يَا مَرْيَمُ ؟ — هُنَّ زَمِيلَاتِي .
> — أَأَخَوَاتٌ هُنَّ ؟ — نَعَمْ ، هُنَّ أَخَوَاتٌ .
> — مَنْ أَبُوهُنَّ ؟ — أَبُوهُنَّ الشَّيْخُ بِلَالٌ . أُمُّهُنَّ أُسْتَاذَتِي .
> — أَيْنَ بَيْتُهُنَّ ؟ — بَيْتُهُنَّ قَرِيبٌ مِنَ الْمَدْرَسَةِ .

### Part ج — "those" (the far pointer `أُولَئِكَ`)

> — هَؤُلَاءِ إِخْوَتِي وَأُولَئِكَ أَصْدِقَائِي .
> — مَنْ أُولَئِكَ الرِّجَالُ الطِّوَالُ ؟ — هُمْ أَطِبَّاءُ مِنْ أَمْرِيكَا .
> — مَنْ أُولَئِكَ النِّسَاءُ ؟ — هُنَّ أُمَّهَاتُ الطَّالِبَاتِ .
> — أَأُولَئِكَ النِّسَاءُ خَالَاتُكَ يَا مَرْيَمُ ؟ — لَا . هُنَّ عَمَّاتِي .
> — هَؤُلَاءِ أَطِبَّاءُ وَأُولَئِكَ مُهَنْدِسُونَ . مَنْ أُولَئِكَ الرِّجَالُ ؟ — هُمْ وُزَرَاءُ .

Notice how *little* is genuinely new: it is the old question machinery (`مَنْ`, `مِنْ أَيْنَ`, `أَيْنَ`, prepositions + kasra, `بَعْضُ`, iḍāfa `أَسْمَاؤُهُمْ`/`أُمَّهَاتُ الطَّالِبَاتِ`) — now with everything switched to the **plural**.

**Yaad rakho:** Teeno dialogue pehle parho. Purana machinery wahi hai (`مَنْ`, `مِنْ أَيْنَ`, prepositions, iḍāfa) — bas sab kuch **plural** mein. Part أ mard, Part ب aurat, Part ج door (`أُولَئِكَ`).

## 1 · Making nouns plural — two ways: sound and broken

The first thing the plural touches is the **noun** itself. Arabic makes a plural in two very different ways, and Lesson 13 shows both:

**(a) The *sound* plural (`جَمْع سَالِم`) — just add an ending, the word stays whole.**

- **Sound *masculine* plural:** add **`ـُونَ`** — `مُدَرِّس` → `مُدَرِّسُونَ`, `مُهَنْدِس` → `مُهَنْدِسُونَ`, `فَلَّاح` → `فَلَّاحُونَ`, `مُجْتَهِد` → `مُجْتَهِدُونَ`, `مُسْلِم` → `مُسْلِمُونَ`. These tend to be the "doer / describer" words (the مُـ-pattern from `هُوَ مُهَنْدِسٌ`).
- **Sound *feminine* plural:** add **`ـَات`** — `طَالِبَة` → `طَالِبَات`, `مُدَرِّسَة` → `مُدَرِّسَات`, `طَبِيبَة` → `طَبِيبَات`, `مُسْلِمَة` → `مُسْلِمَات`, `زَوْجَة` → `زَوْجَات`, `زَمِيلَة` → `زَمِيلَات`. (The `ة` drops, `ـَات` takes its place.)

**(b) The *broken* plural (`جَمْع تَكْسِير`) — the word is re-shaped from the inside,** exactly like English *man → men*, *foot → feet*, *mouse → mice*. There is no suffix; the consonants stay but the vowels (and sometimes added letters) rearrange:

- `طَالِب` → `طُلَّاب` · `تَاجِر` → `تُجَّار` · `حَاجّ` → `حُجَّاج` · `رَجُل` → `رِجَال` · `وَلَد` → `أَوْلَاد` · `ابْن` → `أَبْنَاء` · `شَيْخ` → `شُيُوخ` · `ضَيْف` → `ضُيُوف` · `أَخ` → `إِخْوَة` · `فَتَى` → `فِتْيَة` · `رَجُل` → `رِجَال`.
- Adjectives break too: `كَبِير` → `كِبَار` · `صَغِير` → `صِغَار` · `قَصِير` → `قِصَار` · `طَوِيل` → `طِوَال` · `جَدِيد` → `جُدُد`.
- And the `ـَاء`-ending group: `صَدِيق` → `أَصْدِقَاء` · `زَمِيل` → `زُمَلَاء` · `فَقِير` → `فُقَرَاء` · `غَنِيّ` → `أَغْنِيَاء` · `طَبِيب` → `أَطِبَّاء` · `قَوِيّ` → `أَقْوِيَاء` · `عَالِم` → `عُلَمَاء` · `وَزِير` → `وُزَرَاء` · `امْرَأَة` → `نِسَاء`.

**The honest truth you must tell your class:** you **cannot reliably predict** which plural a given word takes — you **learn it with the word**, the way you memorised "men" not "mans". The book *gives* you each plural (in the exercises' bracketed answers and the word-boxes); your job is to learn the three *types* so the plurals stop looking random, and then memorise the specific ones the lesson lists.

> **For you, not the class:** the Arabic names are `جَمْع مُذَكَّر سَالِم` (sound masculine), `جَمْع مُؤَنَّث سَالِم` (sound feminine), and `جَمْع تَكْسِير` (broken). The book does **not** print these terms in Lesson 13 — it just shows the forms. So teach the *idea* ("regular ending" vs "internal reshape") and don't burden classmates with the Arabic terminology yet. Also: notice that several `ـَاء`-plurals are printed with a **single ḍamma and no tanween** (`أَصْدِقَاءُ`, `فُقَرَاءُ`, `وُزَرَاءُ`, `عُلَمَاءُ`, `نِسَاءُ`, `زُمَلَاءُ`) while the `ـٌ`-plurals do take tanween (`طُلَّابٌ`, `تُجَّارٌ`, `رِجَالٌ`). There is a rule behind that (these are *diptotes*), but it is **Lesson 14+** — for now just **read each plural exactly as the book prints it** and say nothing about the missing tanween. Do not "fix" `أَصْدِقَاءُ` to `أَصْدِقَاءٌ`.

**Yaad rakho:** Plural do tarah ke: **(a) سالم (sound)** — sirf ending lagao, lafz poora rehta hai: mard `ـُونَ` (`مُهَنْدِسُونَ`), aurat `ـَات` (`طَالِبَات`). **(b) تكسير (broken)** — lafz andar se badal jaata hai (jaise English man→men): `طَالِب`→`طُلَّاب`, `رَجُل`→`رِجَال`, `كَبِير`→`كِبَار`. **Sach yeh hai:** kaunsa plural kis lafz ka — yeh **yaad** karna parta hai, predict nahin hota (jaise "men" yaad kiya tha). Kitaab har plural deti hai; tum types samjho aur specific plurals raţţo. (`أَصْدِقَاءُ` waghairah ko jaise kitaab mein likha hai waise hi parho — tanween mat lagao; uski wajah Lesson 14+.)

## 2 · `هَؤُلَاءِ` — "these" (the plural of `هَذَا` *and* `هَذِهِ`)

You taught `هَذَا` ("this", masc, Ch 1) and `هَذِهِ` ("this", fem, Ch 6). In the **plural**, both of them collapse into **one** word:

> **`هَؤُلَاءِ`** (hāʾulāʾi) = "these" — for a group of **men or women, it makes no difference**.

> `هَذَا طَالِبٌ` ("this is a student", m) → `هَؤُلَاءِ طُلَّابٌ` ("these are students")
> `هَذِهِ طَالِبَةٌ` ("this is a student", f) → `هَؤُلَاءِ طَالِبَاتٌ` ("these are students")

This is the lesson's first gift: the gender battle you fought in the singular (`هَذَا` vs `هَذِهِ`) **disappears** in the plural. One pointer, both genders. The dialogues open with it: `مَنْ هَؤُلَاءِ الْفِتْيَةُ ؟` ("who are these youths?", men) and `مَنْ هَؤُلَاءِ الْفَتَيَاتُ ؟` ("who are these girls?", women) — same `هَؤُلَاءِ` for both.

Just like `هَذَا الْبَيْتُ` (Ch 8), `هَؤُلَاءِ` can sit in front of a definite noun: `هَؤُلَاءِ الرِّجَالُ` ("these men") is an open phrase waiting for more, while `هَؤُلَاءِ رِجَالٌ` ("these are men") is a complete sentence.

> **For you, not the class:** `هَؤُلَاءِ` is used for the plural of **rational beings** (people). The plural of *non-rational* things (books, cars) is treated grammatically as feminine singular — `هَذِهِ كُتُبٌ` — but that is a later subtlety; this whole lesson is about **people**, so stay with `هَؤُلَاءِ` for groups of people and don't open the non-rational rule.

**Yaad rakho:** `هَؤُلَاءِ` = "yeh" (bahut saare log). `هَذَا` aur `هَذِهِ` **dono** ki jagah ek hi `هَؤُلَاءِ` — mard ho ya aurat, koi farq nahin! Plural ka pehla tohfa: gender ka jhagra khatam. `هَؤُلَاءِ طُلَّابٌ` (yeh talaba), `هَؤُلَاءِ طَالِبَاتٌ` (yeh talibaat) — ek hi lafz.

## 3 · `أُولَئِكَ` — "those" (the plural of `ذَلِكَ` *and* `تِلْكَ`)

Exactly the same story for the **far** pointer. You taught `ذَلِكَ` ("that", masc, Ch 2) and `تِلْكَ` ("that", fem, Ch 7). In the plural both collapse into:

> **`أُولَئِكَ`** (ulāʾika) = "those" — again, **one word for men or women**.

> `ذَلِكَ طَالِبٌ` → `أُولَئِكَ طُلَّابٌ` ("those are students")
> `تِلْكَ طَالِبَةٌ` → `أُولَئِكَ طَالِبَاتٌ` ("those are students")

Part ج of the lesson is full of it: `مَنْ أُولَئِكَ الرِّجَالُ ؟` ("who are those men?"), `مَنْ أُولَئِكَ النِّسَاءُ ؟` ("who are those women?"), `أُولَئِكَ الطُّلَّابُ ضِعَافٌ` ("those students are weak").

### The spelling trap you must flag

`أُولَئِكَ` is written with a **`و` that is NOT pronounced**. You write أُ-و-لَ-ئِ-كَ but you say **"u-lā-ʾi-ka"** — there is no "w" sound. This silent `و` is a spelling convention (the same kind of silent letter you'll meet in `أُولُو`, `أُولَات`). Tell the class plainly: *"the `و` is written but stays silent."*

**Yaad rakho:** `أُولَئِكَ` = "woh" (bahut saare, door wale). `ذَلِكَ` aur `تِلْكَ` **dono** ki jagah ek hi `أُولَئِكَ`. **Spelling trap:** ismein `و` likha jaata hai magar **boli nahin jaati** — parho "u-lā-ʾi-ka", "w" ki awaaz nahin. (`هَؤُلَاءِ` qareeb, `أُولَئِكَ` door — `هَذَا`/`ذَلِكَ` wala qareeb/door farq plural mein bhi qaayam.)

## 4 · `هُمْ` / `هُنَّ` ("they") and the tails `ـهُمْ` / `ـهُنَّ` ("their")

The pronoun is the third thing that goes plural. In Ch 5 you taught `هُوَ` ("he/it") and `هِيَ` ("she/it"). Their plurals split by gender:

> **`هُمْ`** (hum) = "they" — for a group of **men** (or a mixed group). `هُمْ طُلَّابٌ` ("they are students"), `هُمْ مِنْ أَمْرِيكَا` ("they are from America"), `هُمْ مُجْتَهِدُونَ` ("they are hard-working").
> **`هُنَّ`** (hunna) = "they" — for a group of **women** only. `هُنَّ زَمِيلَاتِي` ("they are my colleagues"), `هُنَّ أَخَوَاتٌ` ("they are sisters").

And the **attached tail** (the "their" from Ch 10's tail family) goes plural the same way:

> **`ـهُمْ`** = "their" (of men): `أَسْمَاؤُهُمْ` ("their names"), `أَبْنَاؤُهُمْ` ("their sons"), `بَعْضُهُمْ` ("some of them").
> **`ـهُنَّ`** = "their" (of women): `أَبُوهُنَّ` ("their father"), `أُمُّهُنَّ` ("their mother"), `بَيْتُهُنَّ` ("their house").

This is just Ch 10's tail logic (`ـهُ`/`ـهَا`) scaled up to a group. Notice the lovely `بَعْضُهُمْ … وَبَعْضُهُمْ` pattern from the dialogue: `بَعْضُهُمْ مِنَ الصِّينِ وَبَعْضُهُمْ مِنَ الْيَابَانِ` ("some of them are from China and some of them from Japan").

> **For you, not the class — the deferral line:** these are the **3rd-person** plurals ("they / their"). The **2nd-person** plural — "you all" (`أَنْتُمْ`) and its tail `ـكُمْ` (`بَيْتُكُمْ`) — is **Lesson 14**, and the feminine "you all" (`أَنْتُنَّ` / `ـكُنَّ`) plus "we" (`نَحْنُ` / `ـنَا`) and the **complete pronoun table** are **Lesson 15**. So even though everyone says `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ` daily, do **not** teach `ـكُمْ` here. This lesson is `هُمْ`/`هُنَّ` and `ـهُمْ`/`ـهُنَّ` — "they / their" only.

**Yaad rakho:** `هُوَ`/`هِيَ` ka plural: **`هُمْ`** (mard / mixed group) aur **`هُنَّ`** (sirf aurtein). Tail bhi: `ـهُمْ` = "unka (mardon ka)" — `أَسْمَاؤُهُمْ`, `بَعْضُهُمْ`; `ـهُنَّ` = "unka (aurton ka)" — `أَبُوهُنَّ`, `بَيْتُهُنَّ`. Yeh Ch 10 wali tail (`ـهُ`/`ـهَا`) ka group-version hai. **Yaad:** yeh "they/their" (3rd person) hai — "tum sab" (`ـكُمْ`/`أَنْتُمْ`) Lesson 14 hai, abhi nahin (rozmarra `عَلَيْكُمْ` bhi abhi nahin).

---

## 5 · The plural past verb — `ذَهَبُوا` (men) / `ذَهَبْنَ` (women)

The fourth and last thing the plural touches is the **verb**. In Ch 12 you cracked the verb open a sliver: `ذَهَبَ` (he went) → `ذَهَبَتْ` (she went). Lesson 13 adds the two **plural** doers:

> men go: `ذَهَبَ` → **`ذَهَبُوا`** (dhahabū) — "they (m) went". Add a **`و` + a silent `ا`** (`ـُوا`). `خَرَجَ` → `خَرَجُوا`, `جَلَسَ` → `جَلَسُوا`.
> women go: `ذَهَبَتْ` → **`ذَهَبْنَ`** (dhahabna) — "they (f) went". Add **`ـْنَ`**. `خَرَجَتْ` → `خَرَجْنَ`, `جَلَسَتْ` → `جَلَسْنَ`.

From the dialogues: `ذَهَبُوا إِلَى الْمَطْعَمِ` ("they [the men] went to the restaurant"), `الطَّبِيبَاتُ خَرَجْنَ مِنَ الْمُسْتَشْفَى` ("the [female] doctors went out of the hospital"), `ذَهَبْنَ إِلَى الْمَكْتَبَةِ` ("they [the women] went to the library").

The book lays the four forms out as a perfect grid (p.81):

| singular | plural |
|---|---|
| `مُحَمَّدٌ ذَهَبَ` | `مُحَمَّدٌ وَحَامِدٌ وَعَلِيٌّ ذَهَبُوا` |
| `مَرْيَمُ ذَهَبَتْ` | `مَرْيَمُ وَآمِنَةُ وَفَاطِمَةُ ذَهَبْنَ` |

So the verb now agrees on **both** number and gender: one man `ذَهَبَ`, many men `ذَهَبُوا`; one woman `ذَهَبَتْ`, many women `ذَهَبْنَ`. The spelling trap is the **silent `ا` in `ذَهَبُوا`** — it is written but never pronounced (you say "dhahabū", not "dhahabuwa").

> **Important scope line:** you teach **only** the plural agreement on the three known past verbs (`ذَهَبَ`/`خَرَجَ`/`جَلَسَ`). You are **not** teaching the present tense, the other persons ("you all went", "we went"), or the doer's case-name. Keep it to the four-cell grid: one man / many men / one woman / many women.

**Yaad rakho:** Verb ka plural bhi: **mard** group → `ذَهَبُوا` (`ـُوا` lagao — `و` + khaamosh `ا`), **aurat** group → `ذَهَبْنَ` (`ـْنَ` lagao). Char-khaane ka grid: `ذَهَبَ` (ek mard) / `ذَهَبُوا` (kai mard) / `ذَهَبَتْ` (ek aurat) / `ذَهَبْنَ` (kai aurtein). **Spelling trap:** `ذَهَبُوا` ka `ا` likha jaata hai magar bola nahin ("dhahabū"). **Sirf itna:** yeh teen past verbs ka plural — poori conjugation abhi nahin.

## 6 · The payoff — the complete demonstrative grid

This is the chapter's centrepiece, and the book itself draws it for you (the two boxed tables, p.78 and p.81). Once `هَؤُلَاءِ` and `أُولَئِكَ` are in place, the **entire** demonstrative system of Book 1 is complete — and it fits in one little grid:

|  | **near** ("this / these") | **far** ("that / those") |
|---|---|---|
| **masc. singular** | `هَذَا` | `ذَلِكَ` |
| **fem. singular** | `هَذِهِ` | `تِلْكَ` |
| **plural (m or f)** | `هَؤُلَاءِ` | `أُولَئِكَ` |

Read it as a story your class has lived through chapter by chapter: `هَذَا` (Ch 1), `ذَلِكَ` (Ch 2), `هَذِهِ` (Ch 6), `تِلْكَ` (Ch 7) — four singular pointers, split by near/far and masc/fem. Then Lesson 13 caps the grid with **two** plurals — and the beauty is that the bottom row needs only **one** word per column, because **the plural doesn't care about gender**. Six chapters of pointing, finished in one frame.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh sabaq ka **taj** hai — poora ishaarah-system mukammal: qareeb `هَذَا`/`هَذِهِ`/`هَؤُلَاءِ`, door `ذَلِكَ`/`تِلْكَ`/`أُولَئِكَ`. Upar ki do qatarein (singular) gender se bant-ti hain, neeche wali (plural) **nahin** — ek hi lafz dono ke liye. Chha sabaq ka pointing ek choti si grid mein.

## Vocabulary spotlight — what's actually new

Three small word-boxes (pp. 73, 78, 81). Most of the lesson's "vocabulary" is really **plurals of words you already know** (`أَب`→`آبَاء`, `أُمّ`→`أُمَّهَات`, `ابْن`→`أَبْنَاء`). The genuinely new singular words are few:

| Arabic | Plural | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| `النَّاسُ` | (already collective) | people |
| `القَرْيَةُ` | — | village |
| `الْحَقْلُ` | `حُقُولٌ` | field |
| `الْمَطْعَمُ` | — | restaurant |
| `الضَّيْفُ` | `ضُيُوفٌ` | guest |
| `الشَّيْخُ` | `شُيُوخٌ` | old man / sheikh |
| `الزَّوْجُ` | `أَزْوَاجٌ` | husband |
| `الْمَرْأَةُ` (without `الـ`: `امْرَأَةٌ`) | `نِسَاءٌ` | woman |
| `الْعَالِمُ` | `عُلَمَاءُ` | scholar |
| `الْوَزِيرُ` | `وُزَرَاءُ` | minister |
| `قَرِيبٌ` | — | near |
| `ضَعِيفٌ` / `ضَعِيفَةٌ` | `ضِعَافٌ` | weak |

Plus the three school types (review/expand): `الْمَدْرَسَةُ الِابْتِدَائِيَّةُ` (primary), `الْمُتَوَسِّطَةُ` (middle), `الثَّانَوِيَّةُ` (secondary).

> **Note on `الْمَرْأَة`:** the book makes a special point (p.78): with the article it is `الْمَرْأَةُ` ("the woman"), but **without** the article the form is `امْرَأَةٌ` (with a hamza-alif at the front) — *not* `مَرْأَةٌ`. And its plural is the completely different word `نِسَاءٌ` ("women"). Flag this oddity; don't over-explain it.

**Yaad rakho:** Naya vocab thora — zyaada tar **purane lafzon ke plural** hain (`أَب`→`آبَاء`, `أُمّ`→`أُمَّهَات`). Naye singular: `النَّاس` (log), `القَرْيَة` (gaon), `الْحَقْل` (khet), `الْمَطْعَم` (restaurant), `الزَّوْج` (shauhar), `الْمَرْأَة` (aurat, plural `نِسَاء`), `الْعَالِم` (scholar), `الْوَزِير` (minister), `قَرِيب` (qareeb), `ضَعِيف` (kamzor), `قَوِيّ` (taaqatwar). `الْمَرْأَة` ki oddity: `الـ` ke baghair `امْرَأَة` (aage hamza-alif), plural `نِسَاء`.

## The big picture — what Chapter 13 silently does

Step back. Chapter 13 is the chapter where the singular world finally becomes **plural** — and it does it with total consistency. Whenever you move from one person to a group, **four things shift together**, and the shift is gendered everywhere except the pointer:

| singular | → plural (men) | → plural (women) |
|---|---|---|
| noun: `طَالِبٌ` / `طَالِبَةٌ` | `طُلَّابٌ` | `طَالِبَاتٌ` |
| pointer (near): `هَذَا` / `هَذِهِ` | `هَؤُلَاءِ` | `هَؤُلَاءِ` *(same!)* |
| pointer (far): `ذَلِكَ` / `تِلْكَ` | `أُولَئِكَ` | `أُولَئِكَ` *(same!)* |
| pronoun: `هُوَ` / `هِيَ` | `هُمْ` | `هُنَّ` |
| "their": `ـهُ` / `ـهَا` | `ـهُمْ` | `ـهُنَّ` |
| verb: `ذَهَبَ` / `ذَهَبَتْ` | `ذَهَبُوا` | `ذَهَبْنَ` |

That table **is** the lesson. Teach it as one reflex — "go plural, and match the gender, except the pointer, which doesn't care" — and the fourteen pages become a single, learnable picture. This is also the natural close of Book 1's foundational arc: after this, `أَنْتُمْ`/`نَحْنُ` (L14) and the full pronoun table (L15) just fill in the last two rows of a grid the class can now see whole.

**Yaad rakho:** Ch 13 woh sabaq hai jahan singular duniya **plural** ban-ti hai — aur bilkul consistent. Ek se group: char cheezein ek saath badalti hain (ism, pointer, zameer, verb), aur gender har jagah match hota hai **siwaye pointer ke** (`هَؤُلَاءِ`/`أُولَئِكَ` dono ke liye ek). Upar wali table = poora sabaq. Ek reflex sikhao: "plural karo, gender match karo, magar pointer ko gender ki parwah nahin."

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

Now you understand it. This is how to *deliver* it. The single most important decision is **pacing** — this is the longest lesson in the book, so **plan two sittings** and do not rush.

> **⏱️ Pacing — two sittings, firmly.**
> - **Class 1 (≈55 min) — the masculine plural (Part أ):** the men's dialogue + sound vs broken plural nouns + `هَؤُلَاءِ` + `هُمْ` / `ـهُمْ` + `ذَهَبُوا`.
> - **Class 2 (≈55 min) — the feminine plural + "those" (Parts ب & ج):** the women's dialogue + `ـَات` plurals + `هُنَّ` / `ـهُنَّ` + `ذَهَبْنَ` + the far pointer `أُولَئِكَ` + the complete demonstrative grid as the closing payoff.
>
> Trying to teach all four shifts × two genders × near/far in one hour will overwhelm Urdu-speaking adults. Split it.

## The landmines for Urdu-speaking adult learners

### Landmine 1 · Expecting a single, predictable plural ending (the broken-plural shock)

Urdu plurals are fairly regular (larka → larke, kitaab → kitaabein). So classmates expect Arabic plurals to be a tidy suffix too — and the **broken** plurals (`رَجُل` → `رِجَال`, `وَلَد` → `أَوْلَاد`) feel chaotic and unfair.

**Counter-strategy:** Disarm it with the English parallel up front. English *also* has broken plurals — *man → men, foot → feet, mouse → mice, child → children* — and nobody finds them unfair; you just **learned** them. Same here. Sort the lesson's plurals into two columns on the board: "regular ending" (`ـُونَ` / `ـَات`) vs "re-shaped" (broken), and tell them honestly: *the re-shaped ones are memorised one by one, like "men".*

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Arabic mein plural do tarah ka hai. Ek **regular** — ending lago: mard `ـُونَ` (`مُهَنْدِسُونَ`), aurat `ـَات` (`طَالِبَات`). Doosra **toota hua (broken)** — lafz andar se badal jaata hai: `رَجُل`→`رِجَال`, `وَلَد`→`أَوْلَاد`. Yeh ajeeb nahin — English mein bhi hai: man→men, foot→feet. Inko **yaad** karna parta hai, jaise 'men' yaad kiya tha. Predict mat karo, raţţo."*

### Landmine 2 · Keeping the gender split in the pointer (`هَؤُلَاءِ` / `أُولَئِكَ`)

After six chapters of drilling `هَذَا` vs `هَذِهِ`, classmates will *look* for a separate "masculine these" and "feminine these" — and feel something is missing when there isn't one.

**Counter-strategy:** Make the *absence* the headline. Put the grid on the board and physically point: top two rows split by gender, bottom row — **one box, spanning both**. Say it as relief, not as a rule: *"good news — in the plural you don't choose; `هَؤُلَاءِ` and `أُولَئِكَ` work for everyone."* Drill `هَؤُلَاءِ طُلَّابٌ` and `هَؤُلَاءِ طَالِبَاتٌ` back to back so they *hear* the same pointer on both.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Singular mein `هَذَا` (mard) / `هَذِهِ` (aurat) ka farq tha. Plural mein **woh farq khatam** — `هَؤُلَاءِ` mardon ke liye bhi, aurton ke liye bhi! `أُولَئِكَ` bhi waise hi. Khushkhabri: yahan gender choose nahin karna parta. `هَؤُلَاءِ طُلَّابٌ`, `هَؤُلَاءِ طَالِبَاتٌ` — ek hi lafz."*

### Landmine 3 · The silent letters — `و` in `أُولَئِكَ` and `ا` in `ذَهَبُوا`

Beginners read every written letter. So they will say "ul-**w**-ā-ʾika" for `أُولَئِكَ` and "dhahab-**u-wa**" for `ذَهَبُوا`, sounding out the silent `و` and `ا`.

**Counter-strategy:** Mark them on the board — draw a small ✗ or a circle over the silent `و` of `أُولَئِكَ` and the silent `ا` of `ذَهَبُوا` — and say each word slowly: "u-lā-ʾi-ka", "dha-ha-bū". This is a *read-aloud* habit, like the `هَمْزَة الْوَصْل` of Ch 5 — pure pronunciation, no rule to memorise.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`أُولَئِكَ` mein `و` **likha** hai magar **bola nahin** jaata — parho 'u-lā-ʾi-ka', 'w' ki awaaz nahin. Isi tarah `ذَهَبُوا` ka aakhri `ا` khaamosh — parho 'dhahabū'. Yeh sirf parhne ki aadat hai (jaise `هَمْزَة الْوَصْل`), koi qaida nahin."*

### Landmine 4 · The verb not matching the group (`ذَهَبَ` for many, or wrong gender)

Two slips: (a) leaving the verb singular for a plural doer (`ذَهَبَ الرِّجَالُ` instead of `ذَهَبُوا`), and (b) using the masculine plural `ذَهَبُوا` for a group of women (should be `ذَهَبْنَ`).

**Counter-strategy:** Use the book's four-cell grid as a permanent board fixture: `ذَهَبَ` (one man) | `ذَهَبُوا` (many men) | `ذَهَبَتْ` (one woman) | `ذَهَبْنَ` (many women). Drill by pointing at groups in the room: point at three male classmates — *"`ذَهَبُوا`"*; three female — *"`ذَهَبْنَ`"*. This re-uses the Ch 12 "verb matches the doer" reflex, now with number added.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Verb ko **doer** se match karo — number aur gender dono. Char-khaana: ek mard `ذَهَبَ`, kai mard `ذَهَبُوا`; ek aurat `ذَهَبَتْ`, kai aurtein `ذَهَبْنَ`. Kai logon ke liye singular `ذَهَبَ` mat chhoro, aur aurton ke group par `ذَهَبُوا` (mardana) mat lagao — unke liye `ذَهَبْنَ`."*

### Landmine 5 · The `ـهُمْ` / `ـهُنَّ` tail vs the forbidden `ـكُمْ`

Because `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ` is on everyone's lips, a classmate will reach for `ـكُمْ` ("your", plural) the moment plurals come up — but `ـكُمْ` is **Lesson 14**, not now.

**Counter-strategy:** Draw a clean line: this lesson is **"they / their"** (`هُمْ`/`هُنَّ`, `ـهُمْ`/`ـهُنَّ`) — talking *about* a group. "You all" (`ـكُمْ`) is *talking to* a group, and that's next lesson. If `عَلَيْكُمْ` comes up, acknowledge it warmly and park it: *"haan, woh agle sabaq mein."*

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Aaj sirf **'woh log / unka'** — `هُمْ`/`هُنَّ`, `ـهُمْ`/`ـهُنَّ` (group ke baare mein baat). **'Tum sab / tumhara'** (`ـكُمْ`, `أَنْتُمْ`) **agla** sabaq (Lesson 14) hai — `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ` wala `ـكُمْ` abhi nahin, chahe rozmarra bolte ho."*

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 13

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum baad ke sabaq mein dekhenge"*:

- ❌ **The 2nd-person plural `أَنْتُمْ` / `ـكُمْ`** ("you all / your", `بَيْتُكُمْ`, `عَلَيْكُمْ`) — **Lesson 14**, even though `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ` is daily speech. This lesson is "they / their" (`هُمْ`/`هُنَّ`).
- ❌ **The feminine "you all" `أَنْتُنَّ` / `ـكُنَّ`, "we" `نَحْنُ` / `ـنَا`, and the COMPLETE detached-pronoun table** — that is the **Lesson 15** payoff. Don't pre-draw the full table.
- ❌ **The diptote (`غَيْر مُنْصَرِف`) rule as a named system** — many `ـَاء`-plurals (`أَصْدِقَاءُ`, `وُزَرَاءُ`, `عُلَمَاءُ`, `نِسَاءُ`) print with no tanween for this reason, but the rule is **Lesson 14+**. Just read them as printed; say nothing about the missing tanween.
- ❌ **The dual (`الْمُثَنَّى`)** — "two students" (`طَالِبَانِ`) is a *separate* number from the plural, and it is **Lesson 16+**. Lesson 13 is **plural (3+)** only — don't mention the dual.
- ❌ **The full verb-conjugation system** — you teach only `ذَهَبُوا`/`ذَهَبْنَ` plural agreement on the three known past verbs. No present tense, no "you all went / we went", no doer's case-name.
- ❌ **The Arabic plural-type terminology** (`جَمْع مُذَكَّر سَالِم` / `مُؤَنَّث سَالِم` / `تَكْسِير`) as a memorised system — teach the *idea* (regular ending vs re-shaped); the book doesn't drill the names here.
- ❌ **The non-rational plural rule** ("plural of things = feminine singular agreement", `هَذِهِ كُتُبٌ`) — this lesson is entirely about **people**; keep `هَؤُلَاءِ` for groups of people.

**Yaad rakho:** `ـكُمْ`/`أَنْتُمْ` (tum sab) — Lesson 14. `أَنْتُنَّ`/`ـكُنَّ`/`نَحْنُ` + poori pronoun table — Lesson 15. Diptote rule — bata mat, sirf jaise likha hai parho. **Dual (do log, `طَالِبَانِ`) — Lesson 16+, bilkul mat chhero** (yeh plural se alag number hai). Poori verb conjugation mat. Plural ke Arabic naam (`سالم`/`تكسير`) raţţaane mat — sirf idea. "Cheezon ka plural" (non-rational) wala rule mat.

## Self-check before teaching

Before the class, make sure:

- [ ] You can sort any of the lesson's nouns into sound (`ـُونَ`/`ـَات`) vs broken, and you've memorised the specific broken plurals (`رِجَال`, `أَوْلَاد`, `إِخْوَة`, `نِسَاء`, `أَطِبَّاء`…).
- [ ] You can say `هَؤُلَاءِ` for *both* `هَؤُلَاءِ طُلَّابٌ` and `هَؤُلَاءِ طَالِبَاتٌ`, and `أُولَئِكَ` for both — and explain that the plural pointer ignores gender.
- [ ] You can read `أُولَئِكَ` ("u-lā-ʾi-ka", silent `و`) and `ذَهَبُوا` ("dhahabū", silent `ا`) correctly.

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Do they form the plural correctly — and treat broken plurals (`رِجَال`, `نِسَاء`) as memorised, not guessed? *(Concept 1.)*
2. Do they use one `هَؤُلَاءِ`/`أُولَئِكَ` for both genders, without hunting for a "feminine these"? *(Concepts 2–3.)*
3. Do they read the silent `و` of `أُولَئِكَ` and silent `ا` of `ذَهَبُوا` silently? *(Pronunciation.)*
4. Do they match `هُمْ` to men, `هُنَّ` to women — and the tails `ـهُمْ`/`ـهُنَّ` likewise? *(Concept 4.)*
5. Do they make the verb plural *and* gender-matched (`ذَهَبُوا` vs `ذَهَبْنَ`), not leave it singular? *(Concept 5.)*
6. Do they avoid `ـكُمْ` (the L14 leak)? *(The deferral line.)*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh sabaq Ch 1 se bana hua pointing-and-pronoun system **band** karta hai. Diagnosis: plural sahi banta hai (broken plurals raţţe hue)? `هَؤُلَاءِ`/`أُولَئِكَ` dono genders ke liye ek? Khaamosh `و`/`ا` parh rahe hain? `هُمْ`/`هُنَّ` aur `ـهُمْ`/`ـهُنَّ` gender se match? Verb plural + gender (`ذَهَبُوا`/`ذَهَبْنَ`)? `ـكُمْ` ka leak to nahin? Agla sabaq (Lesson 14): `أَنْتُمْ`/`نَحْنُ` + `ـكُمْ`/`ـنَا`. Aaj uska zikr nahin.
