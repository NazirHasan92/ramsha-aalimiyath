⏱️ The longest lesson in Book 1 — teach it in TWO sittings

Fourteen pages, three parts (أ، ب، ج). A clean split: Class 1 = the masculine plural (Part أ) — plural nouns + هَؤُلَاءِ + هُمْ + ذَهَبُوا; Class 2 = the feminine plural (Part ب) + the far pointer أُولَئِكَ (Part ج) + the complete demonstrative grid as the closing payoff. Do not cram it into one hour.

★ Master Overview — نقشۂ الدرس

One bird's-eye map: Chapter 13 is the chapter where the singular world becomes plural. The whole lesson is one idea — when you talk about a group of people, four things shift to plural together: the noun, the pointer, the pronoun, and the verb. Everything is gender-matched except the pointer, which uses one word for both.

How Ch 13 connects back — and closes the system

Every "new" thing here is an old singular tool, switched to plural:

  • The pointers (Ch 1/2/6/7) get their last row: هَذَا/هَذِهِ → هَؤُلَاءِ, ذَلِكَ/تِلْكَ → أُولَئِكَ — completing the demonstrative grid (Concept 6).
  • The pronouns (Ch 5) and tails (Ch 10): هُوَ/هِيَ → هُمْ/هُنَّ, ـهُ/ـهَا → ـهُمْ/ـهُنَّ.
  • The verb (Ch 12): ذَهَبَ/ذَهَبَتْ grows its plurals ذَهَبُوا/ذَهَبْنَ — same "match the doer" reflex, now with number.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Form the plural of a noun — recognise the sound endings (masc ـُونَ, fem ـَات) and the broken (re-shaped) plurals, and treat broken plurals as memorised.
  2. Use هَؤُلَاءِ ("these") and أُولَئِكَ ("those") for a group of either gender, and read the silent و of أُولَئِكَ.
  3. Use هُمْ (they, m) / هُنَّ (they, f) and the tails ـهُمْ / ـهُنَّ ("their").
  4. Make a past verb agree with a plural doer: ذَهَبُوا (men) / ذَهَبْنَ (women).
  5. Draw the complete demonstrative grid — all six pointers, near/far × masc/fem/plural.

1 · Making nouns plural — sound & broken

The first thing the plural touches is the noun itself. Arabic builds a plural two ways: a sound plural (add an ending, the word stays whole) or a broken plural (the word is re-shaped from the inside, like English man → men).

Where this fits: Every singular noun from Ch 1–12 now has a plural form. The lesson's exercises drill turning هَذَا طَالِبٌ into هَؤُلَاءِ طُلَّابٌ.

The sound plural (جَمْع سَالِم) — add an ending

Meaning
The word stays whole; you add a suffix. Masculine: ـُونَ (مُهَنْدِس → مُهَنْدِسُونَ, مُسْلِم → مُسْلِمُونَ, فَلَّاح → فَلَّاحُونَ). Feminine: ـَات — the ة drops, ـَات replaces it (طَالِبَة → طَالِبَات, مُسْلِمَة → مُسْلِمَات).
Roman Urdu
Lafz poora rehta hai, sirf ending: mard ـُونَ (مُهَنْدِسُونَ), aurat ـَات (طَالِبَات). "Sālim" = saalim/poora.
Rule
The ـُونَ group is mostly the مُـ-pattern "doer/describer" words (مُهَنْدِس, مُجْتَهِد, مُدَرِّس). The ـَات group is the ة-ending feminine words.
Example
هُمْ مُجْتَهِدُونَ . — هُنَّ مُسْلِمَاتٌ . hum mujtahidūn. — hunna muslimāt. · "They (m) are hard-working. — They (f) are Muslims."

The broken plural (جَمْع تَكْسِير) — re-shaped inside

Meaning
No suffix — the word's vowels (and sometimes letters) rearrange: رَجُل → رِجَال, وَلَد → أَوْلَاد, طَالِب → طُلَّاب, أَخ → إِخْوَة, امْرَأَة → نِسَاء. Adjectives too: كَبِير → كِبَار, جَدِيد → جُدُد.
Roman Urdu
Lafz andar se "toot" jaata hai (broken): رَجُل→رِجَال, وَلَد→أَوْلَاد. Bilkul English "man→men, foot→feet" jaisa.
Rule
You cannot predict which broken plural a word takes — memorise it with the word (like "men", not "mans"). The book gives each one; learn them. Read the printed harakah exactly — some ـَاء-plurals (أَصْدِقَاءُ, وُزَرَاءُ) take no tanween; just read them as printed (the reason is Lesson 14+).
Example
هَؤُلَاءِ الرِّجَالُ فَلَّاحُونَ . أَيْنَ الْأَوْلَادُ ؟ hāʾulāʾi r-rijālu fallāḥūn. ayna l-awlād? · "These men are farmers. Where are the boys?"

⚠️ Broken plurals are memorised, not guessed

Urdu plurals are fairly regular, so classmates expect a tidy suffix and find رِجَال/نِسَاء unfair. Disarm it with English: man → men, foot → feet, mouse → mice — you just learned them. Sort the lesson's words on the board into "regular ending" vs "re-shaped".

2 · هَؤُلَاءِ — "these" (the plural near pointer)

The plural of هَذَا and هَذِهِ — and here is the lesson's first gift: in the plural, the masculine/feminine split of the pointer disappears. One word for a group of men or women.

Where this fits: هَذَا (Ch 1) + هَذِهِ (Ch 6) both become هَؤُلَاءِ. The dialogues open with it for both genders: هَؤُلَاءِ الْفِتْيَةُ (youths) and هَؤُلَاءِ الْفَتَيَاتُ (girls).

هَؤُلَاءِ · "these" (near, plural)

Meaning
"these" — pointing at a group of people nearby. The plural of both هَذَا ("this", m) and هَذِهِ ("this", f).
Roman Urdu
هَؤُلَاءِ = "yeh" (bahut saare log). هَذَا aur هَذِهِ dono ki jagah ek hi lafz.
Rule
One word for both genders — the gender split of the singular pointer vanishes. Like هَذَا الْبَيْتُ (Ch 8), it can precede a definite noun: هَؤُلَاءِ الرِّجَالُ ("these men", open phrase) vs هَؤُلَاءِ رِجَالٌ ("these are men", full sentence).
Example
هَؤُلَاءِ طُلَّابٌ جُدُدٌ . — هَؤُلَاءِ زَمِيلَاتِي . hāʾulāʾi ṭullābun judud. — hāʾulāʾi zamīlātī. · "These are new students. — These are my colleagues (f)."

💡 Lead with the relief

After six chapters of هَذَا-vs-هَذِهِ, classmates will hunt for a "feminine these". Make the absence the headline: "good news — in the plural you don't choose." Drill هَؤُلَاءِ طُلَّابٌ and هَؤُلَاءِ طَالِبَاتٌ back-to-back so they hear the same pointer on both.

3 · أُولَئِكَ — "those" (the plural far pointer)

Exactly the same story for the far pointer: ذَلِكَ and تِلْكَ both become أُولَئِكَ — one word for both genders. Plus a spelling trap: a silent و.

Where this fits: ذَلِكَ (Ch 2) + تِلْكَ (Ch 7) both become أُولَئِكَ. Part ج of the lesson is built on it: أُولَئِكَ الرِّجَالُ, أُولَئِكَ النِّسَاءُ.

أُولَئِكَ · "those" (far, plural)

Meaning
"those" — pointing at a group of people further away. The plural of both ذَلِكَ ("that", m) and تِلْكَ ("that", f).
Roman Urdu
أُولَئِكَ = "woh" (bahut saare, door wale). ذَلِكَ aur تِلْكَ dono ki jagah ek hi lafz.
Rule
One word for both genders. Spelling trap: the و is written but silent — say "u-lā-ʾi-ka", not "ul-wā…". Same near/far contrast as هَذَا/ذَلِكَ: هَؤُلَاءِ near, أُولَئِكَ far.
Example
هَؤُلَاءِ إِخْوَتِي وَأُولَئِكَ أَصْدِقَائِي . hāʾulāʾi ikhwatī wa-ulāʾika aṣdiqāʾī. · "These are my brothers and those are my friends."

⚠️ The silent و

Beginners read every written letter, so they'll sound out the و of أُولَئِكَ. Mark it with a small ✗ on the board and say it slowly: "u-lā-ʾi-ka". It is a read-aloud habit (like هَمْزَة الْوَصْل, Ch 5), not a rule to memorise.

4 · هُمْ / هُنَّ ("they") & the tails ـهُمْ / ـهُنَّ ("their")

The pronoun goes plural too. هُوَ/هِيَ (Ch 5) split by gender into هُمْ (men) / هُنَّ (women), and the Ch 10 tail ـهُ/ـهَا becomes ـهُمْ/ـهُنَّ ("their").

Where this fits: هُوَ/هِيَ (Ch 5) and the attached tails (Ch 10) scaled up to a group. These are the 3rd-person plurals ("they/their") — the 2nd-person ـكُمْ ("your, plural") is Lesson 14.

هُمْ / هُنَّ · "they" + ـهُمْ / ـهُنَّ · "their"

Meaning
هُمْ = "they" (a group of men, or a mixed group). هُنَّ = "they" (women only). The attached tails: ـهُمْ = "their" (of men), ـهُنَّ = "their" (of women).
Roman Urdu
هُمْ = "woh log" (mard/mixed); هُنَّ = "woh" (sirf aurtein). Tail: ـهُمْ/ـهُنَّ = "unka". Ch 10 wali ـهُ/ـهَا ka group-version.
Rule
These are "they / their" (3rd person). The 2nd-person plural أَنْتُمْ / ـكُمْ ("you all / your") is Lesson 14 — defer it, even though عَلَيْكُمْ is daily speech.
Example
هُمْ مِنْ أَمْرِيكَا . — أَيْنَ بَيْتُهُنَّ ؟ — بَيْتُهُنَّ قَرِيبٌ مِنَ الْمَدْرَسَةِ . hum min amrīkā. — ayna baytuhunna? — baytuhunna qarībun mina l-madrasah. · "They are from America. — Where is their house? — Their house is near the school."

⚠️ Don't leak ـكُمْ

Because السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ is on everyone's lips, classmates reach for ـكُمْ the moment plurals appear. Draw the line: today is about a group (هُمْ/هُنَّ = "they/their"); talking to a group (ـكُمْ) is next lesson.

5 · The plural past verb — ذَهَبُوا / ذَهَبْنَ

The fourth and last shift. In Ch 12 the verb learned one change (ذَهَبَ → ذَهَبَتْ). Lesson 13 adds the two plural doers — men add ـُوا, women add ـْنَ — completing a tidy four-cell grid.

Where this fits: Extends Ch 12's "verb matches the doer's gender" with number. Teach only the plural agreement on the three known verbs (ذَهَبَ/خَرَجَ/جَلَسَ); full conjugation stays deferred.

ذَهَبُوا / ذَهَبْنَ · the plural past verb

Meaning
"they went". Many men: add ـُوا (a و + a silent ا) — ذَهَبُوا, خَرَجُوا, جَلَسُوا. Many women: add ـْنَ — ذَهَبْنَ, خَرَجْنَ, جَلَسْنَ.
Roman Urdu
Mard group → ذَهَبُوا (ـُوا); aurat group → ذَهَبْنَ (ـْنَ). ذَهَبُوا ka aakhri ا khaamosh ("dhahabū").
Rule
The verb now matches the doer on both number and gender. The four cells: ذَهَبَ (one man) · ذَهَبُوا (many men) · ذَهَبَتْ (one woman) · ذَهَبْنَ (many women). Only these three known verbs — no further conjugation.
Example
مُحَمَّدٌ وَحَامِدٌ وَعَلِيٌّ ذَهَبُوا . — مَرْيَمُ وَآمِنَةُ وَفَاطِمَةُ ذَهَبْنَ . muḥammadun wa-ḥāmidun wa-ʿaliyyun dhahabū. — maryamu wa-āminatu wa-fāṭimatu dhahabna. · "Muhammad, Hamid and Ali went. — Maryam, Aminah and Fatimah went."

💡 The four-cell grid is a permanent board fixture

Keep this up the whole lesson and point at real groups in the room: three male classmates → ذَهَبُوا; three female → ذَهَبْنَ.

6 · ★ The complete demonstrative grid — the payoff

This is the chapter's centrepiece, and the book draws it for you (the boxed tables, PDF pp. 78 & 81). Once هَؤُلَاءِ and أُولَئِكَ are in place, the entire demonstrative system of Book 1 is complete — six pointers in one frame. This is a grid, so it lives in a table, not a chart.

Where this fits: The close of a six-chapter arc — هَذَا (Ch 1), ذَلِكَ (Ch 2), هَذِهِ (Ch 6), تِلْكَ (Ch 7), and now both plurals. Use it as the last thing on the board in Class 2.
near — "this / these" far — "that / those"
masc. singular هَذَا hādhā · Ch 1 ذَلِكَ dhālika · Ch 2
fem. singular هَذِهِ hādhihi · Ch 6 تِلْكَ tilka · Ch 7
plural (m or f) هَؤُلَاءِ hāʾulāʾi · NEW أُولَئِكَ ulāʾika · NEW

💡 The beauty is the bottom row

The top two rows split by gender (four words). The bottom row needs only one word per column — because the plural pointer ignores gender. Six chapters of pointing, finished in one frame. Yeh poora ishaarah-system ka taj hai — neeche wali qatar mein gender ka koi farq nahin.

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Expecting a predictable plural ending

Classmates want one tidy suffix and find broken plurals (رَجُل → رِجَال, امْرَأَة → نِسَاء) unfair. Anchor with English (man → men). Broken plurals are memorised, not guessed.

2. Hunting for a "feminine these"

After هَذَا/هَذِهِ, they look for a separate plural for each gender. There isn't one: هَؤُلَاءِ and أُولَئِكَ serve both. Sell it as relief, not a rule.

3. Sounding out the silent letters

أُولَئِكَ = "u-lā-ʾi-ka" (the و is silent); ذَهَبُوا = "dhahabū" (the final ا is silent). Mark both on the board.

4. The verb not matching the group

Leaving it singular for many doers (ذَهَبَ الرِّجَالُ ✗ → ذَهَبُوا), or using the masculine ذَهَبُوا for women (✗ → ذَهَبْنَ). Use the four-cell grid.

5. Leaking ـكُمْ from daily speech

السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ makes ـكُمْ tempting, but this lesson is "they/their" (هُمْ/هُنَّ, ـهُمْ/ـهُنَّ). ـكُمْ ("your", plural) is Lesson 14.

📿 Connect to the Qur'an

The plural forms of this lesson — هَؤُلَاءِ, أُولَئِكَ, هُمْ, and the sound masculine plural ـُونَ — are everywhere in the Qur'an. Three verses your class can connect to today's forms.

Surah Al-Muʾminūn · 23:1–2 — the sound masc. plural ـُونَ + هُمْ

قَدْ أَفْلَحَ الْمُؤْمِنُونَ ۝ الَّذِينَ هُمْ فِي صَلَاتِهِمْ خَاشِعُونَ

qad aflaḥa l-muʾminūn · alladhīna hum fī ṣalātihim khāshiʿūn — "Successful indeed are the believers — those who are humble in their prayers."

Students recognise: الْمُؤْمِنُونَ and خَاشِعُونَ end in the sound masculine plural ـُونَ — exactly Concept 1 — and هُمْ is today's "they" (Concept 4). Plus the tail ـهِمْ in صَلَاتِهِمْ ("their prayer").

Surah Al-Baqarah · 2:5 — أُولَئِكَ (those) + هُمُ (they)

أُولَٰئِكَ عَلَىٰ هُدًى مِّن رَّبِّهِمْ ۖ وَأُولَٰئِكَ هُمُ الْمُفْلِحُونَ

ulāʾika ʿalā hudan min rabbihim · wa-ulāʾika humu l-mufliḥūn — "Those are upon guidance from their Lord, and those are the successful ones."

Students recognise: أُولَئِكَ = "those" (Concept 3, here twice) and هُمُ = "they" (Concept 4), plus another sound-plural الْمُفْلِحُونَ and the tail ـهِمْ ("their Lord").

Surah Al-Baqarah · 2:31 — هَؤُلَاءِ (these)

أَنبِئُونِي بِأَسْمَاءِ هَٰؤُلَاءِ إِن كُنتُمْ صَادِقِينَ

anbiʾūnī bi-asmāʾi hāʾulāʾi in kuntum ṣādiqīn — "Inform Me of the names of these, if you are truthful." (part of 2:31)

Students recognise: هَؤُلَاءِ = "these" — exactly today's plural near pointer (Concept 2) — and بِأَسْمَاءِ ("the names of") echoes the dialogue's مَا أَسْمَاؤُهُمْ ؟ ("what are their names?").
