How Ch 5 connects back

Three threads from earlier chapters tie together here:

  • The demonstrative/pronoun system extends. Ch 1's هَذَا, Ch 2's ذَلِكَ, Ch 3's هَذِهِ — all pointing words. Ch 5 adds هُوَ/هِيَ — the referring-back pronouns. Same gender-agreement principle from Ch 3 carries forward (ة-ending → feminine → هِيَ).
  • Iḍāfa from Ch 4 expands. Ch 4 gave you iḍāfa with names and الـ-nouns as the 2nd term. Ch 5 adds مَنْ as a 2nd-term option (قَلَمُ مَنْ) and quietly formalises the terminology (مُضَاف / مُضَافٌ إِلَيْهِ).
  • The kasra rule extends one more time. Ch 3 introduced kasra-after-preposition; Ch 4 extended to iḍāfa; Ch 5 adds تَحْتَ (a near-preposition) to the family.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Use هُوَ ("he/it" — masculine) and هِيَ ("she/it" — feminine) to refer back to a previously mentioned noun.
  2. Build sentences with هُوَ/هِيَ as Mubtada': "It is on the desk" = هُوَ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ.
  3. Ask "Whose ___?" using iḍāfa with مَنْ: قَلَمُ مَنْ هَذَا ؟ — and contrast with Ch 4's لِمَنْ ؟.
  4. Use تَحْتَ ("under") with kasra on the following noun, extending the Ch 3 rule.
  5. Use هُنَاكَ ("there") as a stand-alone location pointer.
  6. Recognise the formal terms مُضَاف and مُضَافٌ إِلَيْهِ for the parts of an iḍāfa.
  7. Read ابْن and اسْم correctly in connected speech (initial vowel absorbed — hamzat al-waṣl).
  8. Recognise and use new vocab: الْكَعْبَة، الرَّسُول، الِاسْم، الشَّارِع، الْمُهَنْدِس، خَدِيجَة.

1 · هُوَ & هِيَ — the first pronouns

Two new words that refer back to a noun — your classmates' first taste of pronoun referencing. The gender-matching rule from Ch 3 (ة → feminine → هَذِهِ) extends directly: ة → هِيَ.

Where this fits: Up to Ch 4 you've built sentences only with pointing words (هَذَا / ذَلِكَ / هَذِهِ) or full nouns. هُوَ/هِيَ let you continue a conversation by referring back: "Where is the book? — It is on the desk."

هُوَ · huwa · he / it (masculine)

Meaning
Refers back to a masculine noun previously mentioned. For people: "he". For objects/places that are grammatically masculine: "it".
Roman Urdu
"Woh" — magar sirf mardaana lafz ke liye. Urdu mein 'woh' har cheez ke liye chalti hai; Arabic mein har lafz ki gender pakdani padti hai.
Important
هُوَ is not "is". It is a subject pronoun (Mubtada'). The "is" of English is invisible in Arabic.
Example
أَيْنَ كِتَابُ مُحَمَّدٍ ؟ — هُوَ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ ayna kitaabu Muḥammadin? — huwa ʿala l-maktabi · "Where is Muhammad's book? — It is on the desk" · "Muhammad ki kitaab kahaan? — Woh mez par hai"

هِيَ · hiya · she / it (feminine)

Meaning
Refers back to a feminine noun. For people: "she". For objects with ة ending: "it".
Roman Urdu
"Woh" — magar sirf zanaana lafz ke liye. ة wala lafz → هِيَ.
Rule
The same ة-rule from Ch 3 applies: noun ends in ة → feminine → use هِيَ. Same family of choice as هَذِهِ.
Example
أَيْنَ حَقِيبَةُ الْمُدَرِّسِ ؟ — هِيَ تَحْتَ الْمَكْتَبِ ayna ḥaqībatu l-mudarrisi? — hiya taḥta l-maktabi · "Where is the teacher's bag? — It is under the desk" · "Ustaad ki bag kahaan? — Woh mez ke neeche hai"

The sentence pattern — هُوَ as Mubtada'

Pattern
هُوَ / هِيَ + (prepositional phrase / iḍāfa / noun).
+ Preposition
هُوَ فِي الْبَيْتِ · هِيَ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ huwa fi l-bayti · hiya ʿala l-maktabi · "He/it is in the house" · "She/it is on the desk"
+ Iḍāfa
هُوَ ابْنُ خَالِدٍ · هِيَ سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدَرِّسِ huwa-bnu Khālidin · hiya sayyāratu l-mudarrisi · "He is Khalid's son" · "It is the teacher's car"
+ Noun
هُوَ طَبِيبٌ · هِيَ مُدَرِّسَةٌ huwa ṭabībun · hiya mudarrisatun · "He is a doctor" · "She is a teacher"

💡 Teaching tip — drill noun→pronoun pairing rapid-fire

Build the gender-pair chart on the board first thing. Then go down both columns calling out the noun; classmates respond instantly with the pronoun. "كِتَاب?" — "هُوَ". "سَيَّارَة?" — "هِيَ". "مَسْجِد?" — "هُوَ". "خَدِيجَة?" — "هِيَ". Twelve pairs, 90 seconds, full class participation. The auditory pairing becomes reflex.

⚠️ Don't pre-teach other pronouns

Classmates will ask about أَنَا (I), أَنْتَ (you), نَحْنُ (we). These are Ch 6–10 territory. Stick to هُوَ/هِيَ only. Tell them: "Aaj sirf yeh do. Baqi pronouns aage ke chapters mein milenge — ek-ek karke."

⚠️ هُوَ is not "is"

English speakers will reach for هُوَ as a copula. Wrong: الْكِتَابُ هُوَ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ (double-subject — using the noun AND the pronoun together). Right: either الْكِتَابُ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ (with noun) OR هُوَ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ (with pronoun) — never both. Arabic has no "to be" verb in present tense; the pronoun replaces the noun, doesn't add a link.

2 · قَلَمُ مَنْ ؟ — a 2nd "whose?" via iḍāfa

A different way to ask "Whose ___?". Ch 4's لِمَنْ uses the preposition لِـ. Today's uses an iḍāfa with مَنْ in the second slot.

Where this fits: Ch 4 taught iḍāfa with two kinds of 2nd-noun (a name like مُحَمَّدٍ, or an الـ-noun like الْوَلَدِ). Today adds a third: مَنْ as 2nd noun. Same iḍāfa, new content in the owner slot.

قَلَمُ مَنْ ؟ · qalamu man? · "Whose pen?"

Structure
An iḍāfa — same as Ch 4 — but with مَنْ in the 2nd slot instead of a name or an الـ-noun.
Roman Urdu
"Kis ka qalam?" — iḍāfa hi hai, doosre lafz par مَنْ baith gaya hai. Wahi 3 rules: pehle par الـ nahin, tanween nahin, doosra zer mein (par مَنْ ki harakat fix hoti hai).
Example 1
قَلَمُ مَنْ هَذَا ؟ — هَذَا قَلَمُ الْمُدَرِّسِ qalamu man haadhaa? — haadhaa qalamu l-mudarrisi · "Whose pen is this? — This is the teacher's pen" · "Kis ka qalam? — Yeh ustaad ka qalam hai"
Example 2
كِتَابُ مَنْ هَذَا ؟ — هَذَا كِتَابُ مُحَمَّدٍ kitaabu man haadhaa? — haadhaa kitaabu Muḥammadin · "Whose book is this? — This is Muhammad's book"
Example 3
سَيَّارَةُ مَنْ هَذِهِ ؟ — هَذِهِ سَيَّارَةُ خَالِدٍ sayyāratu man haadhihi? — haadhihi sayyāratu Khālidin · "Whose car is this? — This is Khalid's car"

The two "whose?" doors — side by side

Ch 4 way
لِمَنْ هَذَا الْقَلَمُ ؟ — هَذَا الْقَلَمُ لِمُحَمَّدٍ li-man haadhal-qalamu? — haadhal-qalamu li-Muḥammadin
Ch 5 way
قَلَمُ مَنْ هَذَا ؟ — هَذَا قَلَمُ مُحَمَّدٍ qalamu man haadhaa? — haadhaa qalamu Muḥammadin
Meaning
Both = "Whose pen is this? — This is Muhammad's pen." Subtle nuance: لِمَنْ leans toward "to whom does it belong?"; قَلَمُ مَنْ leans toward "the pen of whom?". Practically interchangeable.
Roman
Do tareeqe, ek matlab. Pichhle hafte لِمَنْ seekha tha, aaj iḍāfa wala tareeqa seekh rahe ho. Dono sahi — class ko bolo: "Native speaker dono use karte hain. Tum bhi dono ko pehchaano, dono ko bolo."

💡 Teaching tip — Q&A in both forms back-to-back

After introducing the iḍāfa pattern, alternate Q&A between the two forms with the same physical object. Hold up a pen: "li-man haadhal-qalamu?" — get answer in لِـ-form. Hold up the same pen: "qalamu man haadhaa?" — get answer in iḍāfa form. The classmates feel both shapes for the same situation.

⚠️ Don't drop the iḍāfa rules just because the 2nd word is مَنْ

Classmates may forget that قَلَمُ مَنْ is itself an iḍāfa, and write الْقَلَمُ مَنْ (with الـ on the first noun). No. The first noun of any iḍāfa loses الـ and tanween — even when the second noun is a question word. So it's قَلَمُ مَنْ, never الْقَلَمُ مَنْ or قَلَمٌ مَنْ.

تَحْتَ · taḥta · "under"

Meaning
"Under" — used for physical location below something.
Roman Urdu
"Neeche" — bilkul Urdu wala "neeche".
Rule
Triggers kasra on the next noun — same rule as فِي, عَلَى, إِلَى, مِنْ, لِـ. Treat as another member of the family.
Roman
Aage wale ism mein zer — wahi Ch 3 ka rule. تَحْتَ ke baad lafz "ـِ" ya "ـٍ" mein.
Examples
تَحْتَ الْمَكْتَبِ · تَحْتَ السَّرِيرِ · تَحْتَ الشَّجَرَةِ · تَحْتَ الْكِتَابِ taḥta l-maktabi · taḥta s-sarīri · taḥta sh-shajarati · taḥta l-kitaabi

هُنَاكَ · hunāka · "there"

Meaning
"There" (a place away from the speaker). Stand-alone — no noun follows it.
Roman Urdu
"Wahaan / udhar". هَذَا/ذَلِكَ wali family ka location version.
Important
No kasra-trigger. هُنَاكَ ends in fatḥa always — it's a frozen adverb (mabnī). Just teach the meaning; don't analyse the ending.
Example
الْكِتَابُ هُنَاكَ · هُوَ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ هُنَاكَ al-kitaabu hunāka · huwa ʿala l-maktabi hunāka · "The book is there" · "It is on the desk there"

The 6-word kasra family (board chart for Ch 5)

All 6
فِي · عَلَى · إِلَى · مِنْ · لِـ · تَحْتَ fī · ʿalā · ilā · min · li- · taḥta
Rule
Each of these is followed by a noun in kasra. Same ending applies whether the next noun has الـ, is a name, or is indefinite.
Roman
Yeh chhe lafz — aaj se sab "kasra-rule wale lafzon ki family" mein. Class ko bolo: "Aage wala lafz hamesha zer mein — chahe naam ho, الـ wala ho, ya tanween wala."

💡 Teaching tip — link تَحْتَ to the family from minute one

When you introduce تَحْتَ, point at the board chart of the 5 existing kasra-triggers. Add تَحْتَ as the 6th member. Say: "Aaj ek aur lafz aaya jo wahi rule chalata hai — aage wala ism mein zer." Then drill 5 examples immediately. Don't leave a gap between introduction and drilling — the rule re-triggering needs reinforcement.

⚠️ Don't explain why تَحْتَ ends in fatḥa

Technically تَحْتَ is a ẓarf (adverb of place) — a noun in naṣb — and the next noun is its muḍāf ilayhi, which is why it takes kasra (it's an iḍāfa under the hood, not a true preposition-construct). This is true but deeply confusing for Ch 5. Just teach تَحْتَ as "another word that takes kasra on the next noun." If a smart classmate asks "تَحْتَ par fatḥa kyun?" — say: "Yeh ek alag kind of lafz hai, aage ke chapter mein theory milegi. Aaj sirf rule yaad rakho."

What the names mean

مُضَاف
muḍāf — literally "the added one". The first noun in an iḍāfa. The thing being owned.
Roman
"Jodne wala / owned cheez" — Ch 4 mein "pehla lafz" tha, ab uska asli naam مُضَاف.
مُضَافٌ إِلَيْهِ
muḍāf ilayhi — literally "the one added-to". The second noun. The owner.
Roman
"Jis ki taraf joda jaata hai / maalik" — Ch 4 mein "doosra lafz" tha, ab uska asli naam مُضَافٌ إِلَيْهِ.

How to use the names in class

Approach
Casual introduction. Don't drill — just label. Once you write the diagram, classmates will recognise the words when you say them, but they don't need to produce them quickly.
Roman
Drill mat karo. Sirf board par diagram dikhao, aur kabhi-kabhi bolte waqt asli naam use karo ("is muḍāf par الـ nahin") — natural absorption. Class ke akhir mein recognise kar lein, kaafi hai.
Old terminology still OK
You can continue saying "first noun / second noun" — both are acceptable. The classical terms are just an upgrade, not a replacement.

💡 Teaching tip — draw the diagram once, point to it often

Draw the سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدَرِّسِ diagram on the board with both labels. Leave it visible for the rest of class. When discussing any iḍāfa, point at the diagram: "pehla — yaani muḍāf. Doosra — yaani muḍāfun ilayhi." Repetition by physical reference, not by drill.

5 · هَمْزَة الوَصْل — the dropping connecting alif

A phonology rule with a classical name. You've been encountering it since Ch 3 (مِنَ الْـ) and Ch 4 (لِلْـ). Today the book names it and asks classmates to practise reading aloud.

Where this fits: A pronunciation rule, not a writing rule. The initial alif of الـ, اِبْن, اِسْم is a "connecting alif" — its vowel drops when something precedes it in speech. Ch 3's مِنَ الْـ and Ch 4's لِلْـ were already showing this; Ch 5 names the phenomenon.

The pronunciation pattern

At sentence start
اِبْنُ مُحَمَّدٍ فِي الْعِرَاقِ ibnu Muḥammadin fi l-ʿIrāqi · "Muhammad's son is in Iraq" · (full i at start)
Mid-sentence
خَرَجَ ابْنُ الطَّبِيبِ مِنَ الْبَيْتِ kharaja-bnu ṭ-ṭabībi mina l-bayti · "The doctor's son left the house" · (i swallowed in pronunciation)
More examples
مَا اسْمُ الْمُدَرِّسِ ؟ — اِسْمُهُ مُحَمَّدٌ ma -smu l-mudarrisi? — ismuhu Muḥammad · ("what is the teacher's name?" — i drops mid-sentence; "his name is Muhammad" — i at start, full vowel)

What classmates need to do

Reading
When reading aloud, connect ابن / اسم to the previous word smoothly. Don't break to insert a fresh i.
Writing
The alif is typically still written — only the vowel drops in speech. (Exception: لِلْـ, where the alif drops in writing too.)
Roman
Yeh sirf bolne ka rule hai. Likhne mein farq nahin (zyaada tar). Read-aloud drill mein pakdaayi karwaao — main pehle bolungi, classmates phir dohraayenge.

💡 Teaching tip — model the absorption, don't lecture

Don't explain the phonology theory in class. Just read 4 sentences aloud exaggerating the smooth connection. Then have classmates read the same sentences. If they break and insert a fresh i, gently re-model: "sun lo — kharaja-BNU, na ki kharaja IBNU." The ear teaches what the rule cannot.

⚠️ Don't drill this heavily in Ch 5

Hamzat al-waṣl is a Qur'an-recitation skill that classmates will refine over many years. In Ch 5, just point it out when reading aloud — don't make it a drill objective. Five minutes of read-aloud at the end of class is enough.

⚠️ Note on خَدِيجَةُ — no tanween!

Unlike the masculine names from Ch 4 (مُحَمَّدٌ, عَلِيٌّ) which take full tanween, خَدِيجَةُ ends in single ḍamma — no tanween. Same for other feminine names: فَاطِمَةُ, زَيْنَبُ, مَرْيَمُ. They're in the ghayr munṣarif category. Don't open this rule in class. Just teach: "feminine naam mein tanween nahin lagti — aage chapter mein wajah samjhayenge."

⚠️ Note on hamzat al-waṣl in الِاسْم and الِابْن

These words contain TWO hamzat al-waṣl alifs: one for الـ and one for the word's own initial alif (اسم, ابن). The kasra under the first alif is written explicitly (الِـ) to show the connecting nature. In speech, both alifs lose their vowels when preceded by another word. This is a subtle detail — flag it as you point out hamzat al-waṣl, but don't drill it.

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Using هُوَ for feminine nouns (gender mismatch)

Urdu's woh is genderless, so classmates default to هُوَ. They'll say "as-sayyaaratu — huwa fi sh-shaariʿi" (wrong) for "the car — it is on the street". Should be "hiya". Drill noun→pronoun pairing rapid-fire at the start of class — make the gender link reflex before sentence-building begins.

2. Confusing لِمَنْ ؟ and قَلَمُ مَنْ ؟ as different questions

Both mean "whose ___?". Classmates ask "kaunsa sahi hai?" (which one is right?). Answer: both. Drill them back-to-back with the same physical object. Show that the answer also takes two forms (لِمُحَمَّدٍ vs قَلَمُ مُحَمَّدٍ). The structural parallelism is the whole point.

3. Treating هُوَ as the verb "is"

English-influenced classmates write الْكِتَابُ هُوَ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ ("the book IS on the desk") — using هُوَ as a copula. Wrong — that's two subjects in one sentence. Either use the noun (الْكِتَابُ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ) or the pronoun (هُوَ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ) — never both. Arabic has no "to be" in present tense; the pronoun replaces the noun.

4. Forgetting kasra after تَحْتَ

Three weeks of preposition drills, one new word — classmates skip the kasra. "taḥta al-maktabu" (wrong) instead of "taḥta l-maktabi". Re-link تَحْتَ to the 5 prepositions from minute one — write all 6 on the board together. Drill 5 quick examples.

5. Putting الـ on the first noun of قَلَمُ مَنْ

Ch 4's iḍāfa rule #1 still applies — even when the second noun is a question word. Classmates may write الْقَلَمُ مَنْ هَذَا (wrong). Should be قَلَمُ مَنْ هَذَا. Treat this as an iḍāfa drill — apply all 3 Ch 4 rules.

6. Reading ابن and اسم with a fresh i in mid-sentence

When the sentence has "kharaja ibnu ṭ-ṭabībi...", classmates pronounce a clear i. Should be smooth: "kharaja-bnu ṭ-ṭabībi..." — the i dropped. Model first, then ask classmates to mimic. Don't lecture on the phonology theory.

7. Adding tanween to feminine names

By analogy with مُحَمَّدٌ, classmates may write خَدِيجَةٌ. Wrong — should be خَدِيجَةُ (single ḍamma, no tanween). Just say: "yeh khaas naam hai — tanween nahin" and move on. Full rule (ghayr munṣarif) is Ch 12+.

📿 Connect to the Qur'an

Chapter 5's grammar — pronouns, iḍāfa, the Names-of-Allah pattern — appears on almost every page of the Qur'an. Use 1–2 of these in class.

Surah Al-Ikhlāṣ · 112:1

قُلْ هُوَ اللَّهُ أَحَدٌ

qul huwa llāhu aḥad — "Say: He is Allah, [the] One."

Students recognise:

  • هُوَ — today's pronoun, used as the subject (Mubtada') of a nominal sentence about Allah Almighty.
  • The whole phrase هُوَ اللَّهُ أَحَدٌ is built without any "to be" verb — a perfect demonstration of how Arabic nominal sentences work.
  • The classical opening of the Sūrah every classmate already memorises — they will be amazed to realise they can now parse it grammatically.

Surah Al-Baqarah · 2:255 (opening of Āyat al-Kursī)

اللَّهُ لَا إِلَٰهَ إِلَّا هُوَ

Allāhu lā ilāha illā huwa — "Allah — there is no deity except Him."

Students recognise: هُوَ — today's pronoun, here referring back to اللَّهُ earlier in the same sentence. This is a textbook example of pronoun-as-reference. The most famous āyah of the Qur'an — and today's grammar opens its first line.

Surah Al-Qāriʿah · 101:11

هِيَ نَارٌ حَامِيَةٌ

hiya nārun ḥāmiyah — "It is a blazing fire."

Students recognise: هِيَ — today's feminine pronoun, referring back to هَاوِيَة ("an abyss" — feminine because of the ة) earlier in the Sūrah. Beautiful parallel to the masculine هُوَ in Al-Ikhlāṣ — both pronouns doing the same job, matched to gender.

Surah Al-Fātiḥah · 1:2 (review from Ch 4 — now with Ch 5 vocabulary)

الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ رَبِّ الْعَالَمِينَ

al-ḥamdu li-llāhi rabbi l-ʿālamīn — "All praise is for Allah, Lord of the worlds."

Students recognise: رَبِّ الْعَالَمِينَ is a classic iḍāfa — now classmates can name the parts: رَبِّ is the muḍāf; الْعَالَمِينَ is the muḍāfun ilayhi. Same verse you showed in Ch 4 — but now classmates have the formal terminology to discuss it.

The three sacred sentences from the textbook

The book itself (page 28) walks classmates through these phrases — each a perfect Ch 5 grammar showcase:

١) الْكَعْبَةُ بَيْتُ اللَّـهِ
٢) مُحَمَّدٌ صَلَّى اللَّهُ عَلَيْهِ وَسَلَّمَ رَسُولُ اللَّـهِ
٣) الْقُرْآنُ كِتَابُ اللَّـهِ

"The Ka'ba is the House of Allah. / Muhammad ﷺ is the Messenger of Allah. / The Qur'an is the Book of Allah."

Students recognise: each sentence is a nominal sentence (Ch 1) with an iḍāfa as Khabar/predicate (Ch 4). All three say "X is [iḍāfa]". The predicate iḍāfa is always two nouns + kasra on the second — perfectly demonstrated. Use these three sentences in your closing recap; classmates leave knowing they can now parse every word in Friday khutbah opening lines.
