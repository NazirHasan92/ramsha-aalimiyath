الدَّرْسُ الخَامِسُ

Chapter 5 — Student Handout

"He" & "She" · "Whose?" (a 2nd way) · "Under" · iḍāfa terminology

👋 Welcome — What you'll learn today

By the end of this lesson you will be able to:

  1. Use هُوَ (he/it) and هِيَ (she/it) to refer back to a noun.
  2. Ask "Whose ___?" using iḍāfa: قَلَمُ مَنْ هَذَا ؟ — and recognise it as a 2nd way (alongside Ch 4's لِمَنْ ؟).
  3. Use تَحْتَ ("under") and هُنَاكَ ("there") for location.
  4. Recognise the formal terms مُضَاف and مُضَافٌ إِلَيْهِ for the two parts of an iḍāfa.
  5. Read ابن and اسم smoothly in connected speech (initial vowel absorbed).
  6. Know the new vocabulary — including اللَّـه, الْكَعْبَة, الرَّسُول, خَدِيجَة.

Time needed: about one hour of class + 30 minutes of self-practice.

Rule 1 · هُوَ & هِيَ — "he/it" and "she/it"

Until now you've built sentences with pointing words (هَذَا، ذَلِكَ، هَذِهِ) or with full nouns. Today you learn two pronouns that refer back to a noun already mentioned.

هُوَ    huwa  =  he / it (masculine)
هِيَ    hiya  =  she / it (feminine)

💡 The gender rule (same as Ch 3's هَذِهِ)

Match the pronoun to the gender of the noun you're referring to:

  • If the noun is masculine (no ة) → use هُوَ.
  • If the noun ends in ة (feminine) → use هِيَ.

Roman: "Urdu mein 'woh' har cheez ke liye chalti hai. Arabic mein nahin — har lafz ki gender pakdani padti hai. `ة` wala lafz feminine → هِيَ. Baqi sab → هُوَ."

Gender pairing — read both columns aloud:

Masculine → هُوَFeminine → هِيَ
كِتَاب · قَلَم · بَيْتحَقِيبَة · سَيَّارَة · غُرْفَة
مَسْجِد · سُوق · شَارِعمَدْرَسَة · شَجَرَة · حَدِيقَة
مُحَمَّد · خَالِد · وَلَدخَدِيجَة · فَاطِمَة · مَرْيَم

⚠️ هُوَ is NOT the verb "is"

Arabic has no "to be" in present tense. هُوَ means "he/it" — it's the subject, not a link word.

  • ✅ الْكِتَابُ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ — "The book is on the desk." (subject = noun)
  • ✅ هُوَ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ — "It is on the desk." (subject = pronoun)
  • ❌ الْكِتَابُ هُوَ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ — wrong (two subjects).

Use either the noun or the pronoun, never both.

Q&A pattern — أَيْنَ + iḍāfa, answered with هُوَ/هِيَ:

QuestionAnswerMeaning
أَيْنَ كِتَابُ مُحَمَّدٍ ؟هُوَ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِWhere is Muhammad's book? — It is on the desk.
أَيْنَ حَقِيبَةُ الْمُدَرِّسِ ؟هِيَ تَحْتَ الْمَكْتَبِWhere is the teacher's bag? — It is under the desk.
أَيْنَ سَيَّارَةُ خَالِدٍ ؟هِيَ فِي الشَّارِعِWhere is Khalid's car? — It is on the street.
أَيْنَ ابْنُ يَاسِرٍ ؟هُوَ فِي الْمَدْرَسَةِWhere is Yasir's son? — He is in the school.

Rule 2 · قَلَمُ مَنْ ؟ — a 2nd way to ask "Whose?"

Last week you learned لِمَنْ هَذَا الْقَلَمُ ؟ ("Whose pen is this?") using the preposition لِـ. This week you learn a second way — using an iḍāfa with مَنْ in the second slot.

قَلَمُ مَنْ هَذَا ؟

qalamu man haadhaa? · "Whose pen is this?" · "Kis ka qalam hai yeh?"

💡 It's just an iḍāfa with مَنْ as the 2nd noun

You already know قَلَمُ مُحَمَّدٍ (Muhammad's pen) — a regular Ch 4 iḍāfa. Today's قَلَمُ مَنْ is the same structure with مَنْ ("whose?") replacing the name. All 3 iḍāfa rules from Ch 4 still apply:

  • 1st noun: NO الـ
  • 1st noun: NO tanween
  • 2nd noun (here مَنْ): a fixed shape

The two "whose?" doors — side by side:

Ch 4 way (with لِـ)Ch 5 way (iḍāfa)
لِمَنْ هَذَا الْقَلَمُ ؟قَلَمُ مَنْ هَذَا ؟
هَذَا الْقَلَمُ لِمُحَمَّدٍهَذَا قَلَمُ مُحَمَّدٍ
"This pen is Muhammad's""This is Muhammad's pen"

Both = "Whose pen is this? — Muhammad's." Both are correct Arabic. Native speakers use both.

More examples — read aloud:

كِتَابُ مَنْ هَذَا ؟هَذَا كِتَابُ حَامِدٍWhose book is this? — This is Hamid's book.
بَيْتُ مَنْ ذَلِكَ ؟ذَلِكَ بَيْتُ الْإِمَامِWhose house is that? — That is the imam's house.
سَيَّارَةُ مَنْ هَذِهِ ؟هَذِهِ سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدَرِّسِWhose car is this? — This is the teacher's car.
حَقِيبَةُ مَنْ هَذِهِ ؟هَذِهِ حَقِيبَةُ خَدِيجَةَWhose bag is this? — This is Khadijah's bag.

Rule 3 · تَحْتَ & هُنَاكَ — new location words

تَحْتَ  = "under"  ·  neeche
هُنَاكَ  = "there"  ·  wahaan / udhar

⚠️ تَحْتَ triggers kasra — same as Ch 3 prepositions

The noun after تَحْتَ takes kasra, exactly like after فِي · عَلَى · إِلَى · مِنْ · لِـ. So your kasra-trigger family is now 6 words:

فِي · عَلَى · إِلَى · مِنْ · لِـ · تَحْتَ

تَحْتَ in action:

تَحْتَ الْمَكْتَبِtaḥta l-maktabiunder the desk
تَحْتَ السَّرِيرِtaḥta s-sarīriunder the bed
تَحْتَ الشَّجَرَةِtaḥta sh-shajaratiunder the tree
تَحْتَ الْكِتَابِtaḥta l-kitaabiunder the book

💡 هُنَاكَ stands alone

Unlike تَحْتَ, هُنَاكَ doesn't take a noun after it. It just means "there" — used as the predicate of a sentence, or as an extra location-pointer.

  • الْكِتَابُ هُنَاكَ — "The book is there."
  • هُوَ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ هُنَاكَ — "It is on the desk there."

Rule 4 · مُضَاف & مُضَافٌ إِلَيْهِ — the names you didn't know you knew

Last week you learned iḍāfa using "first noun / second noun". This week, the two parts get their formal Arabic names. Same rules — just new labels.

سَيَّارَةُ     الْمُدَرِّسِ
↓            ↓
مُضَاف    مُضَافٌ إِلَيْهِ

sayyāratu l-mudarrisi · "the teacher's car"

💡 What the names mean

  • مُضَاف (muḍāf) — literally "the added one". The first noun. The thing being owned. Roman: "jodne wala / owned cheez."
  • مُضَافٌ إِلَيْهِ (muḍāfun ilayhi) — literally "the one added-to". The second noun. The owner. Roman: "jis ki taraf joda jaata hai / maalik."

You can still say "first noun / second noun" — both work. The classical names are just an upgrade.

The 3 iḍāfa rules — restated with the new names:

  1. The مُضَاف (1st noun) takes no الـ.
  2. The مُضَاف (1st noun) takes no tanween.
  3. The مُضَافٌ إِلَيْهِ (2nd noun) is in kasra.

Rule 5 · هَمْزَة الوَصْل — the connecting alif (reading rule)

You've already met the dropping initial alif twice — in Ch 3 (مِنَ الْـ: "fil-bayt", "ʿalal-maktab") and in Ch 4 (لِلْـ: alif gone in writing). Today the rule gets its name: هَمْزَة الوَصْل ("the connecting alif").

💡 The rule, in one sentence

The initial alif of الـ, اِبْن, اِسْم is a "connecting alif". Its vowel is full only at the start of speech. If anything precedes it, the vowel drops in pronunciation.

Roman: "Yeh sirf bolne ka rule hai. Likhne mein farq nahin (zyaada tar). Pehle bolo to vowel — 'ibn', 'ism'. Beech mein aaye to vowel chala jaata hai — 'kharaja-bnu', 'ma-smu'."

Compare — same word, different position:

SentenceRead asNotes
ابْنُ مُحَمَّدٍ فِي الْعِرَاقِibnu Muḥammadin fi l-ʿIrāq"Ibn" at start → full i
خَرَجَ ابْنُ الطَّبِيبِkharaja-bnu ṭ-ṭabīb"Ibn" mid-sentence → i swallowed
اسْمُ الْمُدَرِّسِ مُحَمَّدٌismu l-mudarrisi Muḥammad"Ism" at start → full i
مَا اسْمُ الْمُدَرِّسِ ؟ma-smu l-mudarris?"Ism" mid-sentence → i swallowed

This is a reading rule, not a writing rule. The alif stays in writing — only the vowel disappears in speech. Listen to your teacher's reading and mimic.

🕌 Three sacred sentences — every word is now known grammar

The book's exercises walk you through these three phrases. Each is a nominal sentence (Ch 1) with an iḍāfa as Khabar (Ch 4). Memorise them.

الْكَعْبَةُ بَيْتُ اللَّـهِ
مُحَمَّدٌ صَلَّى اللَّهُ عَلَيْهِ وَسَلَّمَ رَسُولُ اللَّـهِ
الْقُرْآنُ كِتَابُ اللَّـهِ

"The Ka'ba is the House of Allah. / Muhammad ﷺ is the Messenger of Allah. / The Qur'an is the Book of Allah."

💡 What's happening in each

All three follow the pattern: [subject noun] + [iḍāfa predicate].

  • الْكَعْبَةُ (subject) + بَيْتُ اللَّـهِ (iḍāfa: "house of Allah")
  • مُحَمَّدٌ (subject) + رَسُولُ اللَّـهِ (iḍāfa: "messenger of Allah")
  • الْقُرْآنُ (subject) + كِتَابُ اللَّـهِ (iḍāfa: "book of Allah")

In each iḍāfa: first noun (بَيْتُ / رَسُولُ / كِتَابُ) has no الـ, no tanween, single ḍamma. Second noun (اللَّـهِ) is in kasra. The pattern is perfect.
