# Part A · Understanding the chapter

If Chapter 5 was a *consolidation* chapter, Chapter 6 is a **focus** chapter. It does one thing — and does it thoroughly: it teaches your classmates how Arabic marks the **feminine**.

You've actually been *using* the feminine since Chapter 3, when `هَذِهِ` (this — feminine) appeared and you learned the rule *"noun ends in `ة` → feminine → use `هَذِهِ`"*. Chapter 6 takes that single rule and turns it into a whole working system. By the end of the lesson your classmates won't just *recognise* feminine nouns — they'll be able to *make* a word feminine, *match* an adjective or profession to a feminine subject, and *spot* the handful of sneaky words that are feminine without any `ة` at all.

In one sentence: **Chapter 6 is the gender chapter — the `ة` (taa marbuuta) is revealed as the master switch for the feminine, and your classmates learn to flip it on nouns, professions, and adjectives, while meeting feminine family words, household objects, and body parts.**

Here is what's actually in the lesson:

1. **The feminine marker `ة` (التَّأْنِيث)** — the core grammar point. Add `ة` to the end of a masculine word and it becomes feminine: `مُسْلِم → مُسْلِمَة`, `مُهَنْدِس → مُهَنْدِسَة`, `طَبِيب → طَبِيبَة`. This is the chapter's *hammer*.
2. **Feminine agreement** — when the subject is feminine, the *predicate* (a profession or an adjective) also takes `ة`: `هِيَ طَبِيبَةٌ` (she is a [female] doctor), `هِيَ جَمِيلَةٌ` (she/it is beautiful), `دَرَّاجَةٌ جَدِيدَةٌ` (a new bicycle). The demonstrative `هَذِهِ` (review from Ch 3) is the front end of this same agreement.
3. **Feminine nouns *without* `ة`** — a small, important wrinkle. A few common nouns are feminine even though they have no `ة`. In this lesson they show up as **paired body parts**: `عَيْن` (eye), `أُذُن` (ear), `يَد` (hand) and `رِجْل` (leg/foot) are all feminine (so → `هَذِهِ`), while `أَنْف` (nose) and `فَم` (mouth) are masculine (→ `هَذَا`).
4. **The feminine in iḍāfa and "whose?" questions** — no new grammar here, just Chapter 4–5 machinery applied to feminine nouns: `بِنْتُ يَاسِرٍ` (Yasir's daughter), `أُخْتُ الْمُهَنْدِسِ` (the engineer's sister), `سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ` (the director's car), `سَيَّارَةُ مَنْ هَذِهِ ؟` (whose car is this?).

Plus a generous batch of new vocabulary — feminine family words (`بِنْت`, `أُخْت`, `أُمّ`), household objects (`مِكْوَاة`, `دَرَّاجَة`, `سَاعَة`, `مِلْعَقَة`, `قِدْر`, `ثَلَّاجَة`, `قَهْوَة`, `شَاي`, `نَافِذَة`), farm/everyday words (`بَقَرَة`, `فَلَّاح`), and body parts (`أَنْف`, `فَم`, `عَيْن`, `أُذُن`, `يَد`, `رِجْل`).

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter 6 sirf ek cheez sikhaata hai — par poori tarah: **mu'annath (feminine)**. Asli switch hai `ة` (taa marbuuta) — yeh lafz ke aakhir mein lag jaaye to lafz feminine ban jaata hai (`مُهَنْدِس → مُهَنْدِسَة`). Phir feminine subject ke saath profession aur adjective bhi `ة` leta hai (`هِيَ طَبِيبَةٌ`, `هِيَ جَمِيلَةٌ`). Do-teen lafz `ة` ke baghair bhi feminine hote hain (`عَيْن`, `أُذُن`). `هَذِهِ` (Ch 3 se), iḍāfa aur "kis ka?" sab feminine lafzon par wahi tarah chalte hain. Yeh chapter ka dil = `ة` ka rule.

Work through the four concepts at your own pace. For each, ask yourself: *can I explain this using only Ch 1–5 grammar plus the idea of "the `ة` switch"?* (*Kya main yeh pichhle paanch chapters aur "`ة` wala switch" se samjha sakti hoon?*)

## 1 · The feminine marker `ة` — التَّأْنِيث

This is the heart of the chapter. Everything else flows from it.

Arabic divides **every** noun into two genders: **masculine (مُذَكَّر · *mudhakkar*)** and **feminine (مُؤَنَّث · *mu'annath*)**. There is no "neuter", no "it". A pen is masculine; a car is feminine. A teacher (male) is masculine; a teacher (female) is feminine.

The most common way Arabic *shows* that a word is feminine is by adding the letter **`ة` (taa marbuuta — *baandhi hui taa*)** to the end:

| Masculine (مُذَكَّر) | → | Feminine (مُؤَنَّث) | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| `مُسْلِمٌ` | → | `مُسْلِمَةٌ` | a Muslim (man → woman) |
| `مُهَنْدِسٌ` | → | `مُهَنْدِسَةٌ` | engineer (m → f) |
| `طَبِيبٌ` | → | `طَبِيبَةٌ` | doctor (m → f) |
| `مُدَرِّسٌ` | → | `مُدَرِّسَةٌ` | teacher (m → f) |
| `كَبِيرٌ` | → | `كَبِيرَةٌ` | big |
| `جَدِيدٌ` | → | `جَدِيدَةٌ` | new |

The textbook states the rule plainly: *the taa marbuuta enters at the end of the masculine noun to make it feminine.* That's the whole mechanism. Take the masculine form, stick a `ة` on the end, and you have the feminine.

### What the `ة` looks and sounds like

- **Written:** `ة` — a `هـ` shape with the two dots of `ت` on top. At the end of a word it sits as `ـة` (joined) or `ة` (standalone).
- **Pronounced:** at a *pause* (end of speech) it sounds like a soft **"-ah"** (`مُهَنْدِسَة` → *muhandisah*). In *connected* speech, with a case-ending after it, it sounds like a full **"t"** (`مُهَنْدِسَةٌ` → *muhandisatun*; `سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ` → *sayyāratu l-mudīr*). Your classmates already met this in Ch 3 — `ة` is "h" at pause, "t" when connected.

**Yaad rakho:** `ة` (taa marbuuta) = feminine banaane wala switch. Mardaana lafz le lo, aakhir mein `ة` laga do — feminine taiyaar (`طَبِيب → طَبِيبَة`). Likhne mein `هـ` ke upar `ت` ke do nuqte. Bolne mein: rukne par "-ah", aage kuch ho to poora "t" (`سَيَّارَةُ المُدير` = *sayyāratu l-mudīr*). Yeh "t/h" wala farq Ch 3 mein dekha tha.

### Why this is the chapter's big idea

Up to now, gender has been something your classmates *noticed* (a `ة` here, a `هَذِهِ` there). Now it becomes something they can *operate*: given any masculine word, produce its feminine. That's an active skill, and it unlocks the rest of the lesson — because once you can make a noun feminine, you have to make everything that agrees with it feminine too (next concept).

## 2 · Feminine agreement — `هِيَ طَبِيبَةٌ`, `دَرَّاجَةٌ جَدِيدَةٌ`

Once a subject is feminine, the words that *describe* it have to be feminine as well. This is **agreement** (*muṭābaqah* — *milaan*), and it's where the `ة` rule earns its keep.

Look at the lesson's own dialogue:

> أَهِيَ أَيْضًا مُهَنْدِسَةٌ ؟ — لَا، هِيَ طَبِيبَةٌ.
> *"Is she also an engineer? — No, she is a doctor."*

The subject is `هِيَ` (she — feminine). So the profession words take the `ة`: `مُهَنْدِسَة`, `طَبِيبَة`. You could **not** say *`هِيَ طَبِيبٌ`* (with the masculine form) — the gender has to match.

And with adjectives:

> هَذِهِ سَاعَةُ عَلِيٍّ. هِيَ جَمِيلَةٌ جِدًّا.
> *"This is Ali's watch. It is very beautiful."*

`سَاعَة` (watch) is feminine (it has a `ة`), so the adjective `جَمِيل` becomes `جَمِيلَة`.

> هَذِهِ دَرَّاجَةٌ جَدِيدَةٌ، وَدَرَّاجَةُ أَنَسٍ قَدِيمَةٌ.
> *"This is a new bicycle, and Anas's bicycle is old."*

`دَرَّاجَة` (bicycle) is feminine → its adjectives are `جَدِيدَة` (new) and `قَدِيمَة` (old), both with `ة`.

### The front end of agreement: `هَذِهِ` (review from Ch 3)

The demonstrative `هَذِهِ` is the *same* agreement rule operating at the front of the sentence. Feminine noun → `هَذِهِ` (not `هَذَا`). Your classmates already have this from Chapter 3; Chapter 6 simply surrounds it with the rest of the feminine system. Notice how the whole sentence becomes feminine-flavoured once the subject is feminine:

- `هَذِهِ دَرَّاجَةٌ جَدِيدَةٌ` — *"this (f) is a new (f) bicycle (f)"* — three feminine markers in a row.

### One thing to be careful about (for you, not class)

The attributive pairing `دَرَّاجَةٌ جَدِيدَةٌ` ("a new bicycle" — noun + matching adjective) is technically the construction called **النَّعْت (the adjective / naʿt)**. The textbook does **not** name it or give its full rules until **Lesson 9**. So in Chapter 6 you should teach agreement *descriptively* — *"the adjective copies the `ة` of the noun"* — without introducing the formal terms `نَعْت` / `مَنْعُوت` or the full four-way agreement rule (gender, number, definiteness, case). Show the pattern; save the theory for Lesson 9.

**Yaad rakho:** Feminine subject → uske saath aane wale profession aur adjective bhi feminine (`ة` ke saath). `هِيَ مُهَنْدِسَةٌ`, `هِيَ طَبِيبَةٌ`, `هِيَ جَمِيلَةٌ`, `دَرَّاجَةٌ جَدِيدَةٌ`. `هَذِهِ` (Ch 3 se) bhi isi milaan ka hissa hai. **Sahi:** `هِيَ طَبِيبَةٌ`. **Galat:** `هِيَ طَبِيبٌ`. Magar — adjective ka poora "naʿt" rule abhi mat kholo; woh Lesson 9 ki cheez hai. Aaj sirf itna: *"adjective bhi `ة` copy karta hai."*

## 3 · Feminine *without* `ة` — the body-part wrinkle

Here is the one place Chapter 6 surprises a beginner. **Not every feminine noun has a `ة`.** A handful of common nouns are feminine "by nature" (*samāʿī* — heard/memorised, not signalled by a marker). The lesson introduces this idea gently, through **body parts**.

Look at how the textbook points at body parts:

- `هَذَا أَنْفٌ وَهَذَا فَمٌ` — *"this is a nose and this is a mouth"* — `أَنْف` and `فَم` are **masculine** → `هَذَا`.
- `هَذِهِ أُذُنٌ وَهَذِهِ عَيْنٌ` — *"this is an ear and this is an eye"* — `أُذُن` and `عَيْن` are **feminine** → `هَذِهِ` (even though there's no `ة`!).
- `هَذِهِ يَدٌ وَهَذِهِ رِجْلٌ` — *"this is a hand and this is a leg/foot"* — `يَد` and `رِجْل` are also **feminine** → `هَذِهِ` (again, no `ة`).

So `عَيْن`, `أُذُن`, `يَد` and `رِجْل` are feminine *despite having no `ة`*, and you can only tell because the textbook uses `هَذِهِ` with them. Notice the pattern: all four are **organs that come in a pair** (two eyes, two ears, two hands, two feet) — that's a genuinely useful memory hook.

### How to handle this without overwhelming the class

Don't open the full "categories of feminine" rule (place names, `شَمْس`/`أَرْض`/`نَار`, etc.) — that's far beyond Lesson 6. But the **paired-organs** hook *is* worth giving the class, because the lesson hands you four clean examples:

> *"Zyaada-tar feminine lafzon mein `ة` hota hai. Magar kuch lafz `ة` ke baghair bhi feminine hote hain. Aaj jism ke woh ange milenge jo jodi mein aate hain — `عَيْن` (aankh), `أُذُن` (kaan), `يَد` (haath), `رِجْل` (taang/paaon) — sab feminine, isliye `هَذِهِ`. Aur `أَنْف` (naak), `فَم` (mooh) masculine, isliye `هَذَا`."*

The memory hook for you and the class alike: **body parts that come in a pair tend to be feminine** in Arabic — and this lesson's four (`عَيْن`, `أُذُن`, `يَد`, `رِجْل`) are exactly those. Don't dress it up as a formal rule; just let them memorise these few.

**Yaad rakho:** Sab feminine lafzon mein `ة` zaroori nahin. Jodi-wale ange — `عَيْن` (aankh), `أُذُن` (kaan), `يَد` (haath), `رِجْل` (taang) — `ة` ke baghair bhi feminine → `هَذِهِ`. `أَنْف` (naak), `فَم` (mooh) — masculine → `هَذَا`. Class ko yeh memory-hook do: *"jism ke jo ange do-do hote hain woh aksar feminine."* Poora feminine-categories rule abhi mat kholo.

## 4 · The feminine in iḍāfa and "whose?" — consolidation

There is **no new grammar** in this concept — and that's the point. Chapter 6 takes the iḍāfa (Ch 4), the `لِمَنْ ؟` question (Ch 4), and the `سَيَّارَةُ مَنْ ؟` question (Ch 5), and runs them all with *feminine* nouns, so your classmates see that the machinery doesn't change with gender.

From the lesson:

- **Feminine iḍāfa** (feminine word as the *first* noun, the muḍāf):
  - `بِنْتُ يَاسِرٍ` — *"Yasir's daughter"*
  - `أُخْتُ الْمُهَنْدِسِ` — *"the engineer's sister"*
  - `سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ` — *"the director's car"*
  - `بَقَرَةُ الْفَلَّاحِ` — *"the farmer's cow"*
  - `دَرَّاجَةُ عَمَّارٍ` — *"Ammar's bicycle"*
  - `سَاعَةُ عَلِيٍّ` — *"Ali's watch"*

  Notice: the first noun is feminine and ends in `ـةُ` (a single ḍamma, no tanween — exactly the Ch 4 rule: the muḍāf takes no `الـ` and no tanween). The `ة` is pronounced as a full "t" here because the word is connected to what follows: *bintu Yāsir*, *sayyāratu l-mudīr*.

- **"Whose?" with a feminine thing:**
  - `سَيَّارَةُ مَنْ هَذِهِ ؟ — هَذِهِ سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ` — *"Whose car is this? — This is the director's car."* (Ch 5's `قَلَمُ مَنْ` pattern, now with a feminine thing and `هَذِهِ`.)
  - `لِمَنْ هَذِهِ ؟ — هَذِهِ لِخَالِدٍ` — *"Whose is this? — This is Khalid's."* (Ch 4's `لِمَنْ`, now with `هَذِهِ`.)

The lesson also recaps `مَنْ هَذِهِ ؟` (who is this? — for a female person) and `مَا هَذِهِ ؟` (what is this? — for a feminine thing), both from the Ch 2 question set, now with `هَذِهِ`.

**Yaad rakho:** Iḍāfa aur "kis ka?" wale sawaal gender se nahin badalte. Feminine lafz muḍāf (pehla lafz) ban jaaye to wahi teen rule: `الـ` nahin, tanween nahin, `ـةُ` (ek pesh). `بِنْتُ يَاسِرٍ`, `سَيَّارَةُ المُدِيرِ`, `بَقَرَةُ الفَلَّاحِ`. Sawaal: `سَيَّارَةُ مَنْ هَذِهِ ؟`, `لِمَنْ هَذِهِ ؟`, `مَنْ هَذِهِ ؟`, `مَا هَذِهِ ؟`. Saari purani machinery — bas feminine lafzon ke saath.

## Vocabulary spotlight — the feminine world

Chapter 6's vocabulary is unusually concrete and easy to picture — it's mostly household objects, family members, and body parts. Three groups worth flagging:

**Feminine family words (new):**
- `بِنْت` (*bint*) — daughter / girl
- `أُخْت` (*ukht*) — sister
- `أُمّ` (*umm*) — mother

These join the family vocabulary from Ch 4 (`أَب`, `أَخ`, `ابْن`, `عَمّ`, `خَال`). Note all three (`بِنْت`, `أُخْت`, `أُمّ`) are feminine *without* a `ة` — but classmates won't trip on this because the words obviously refer to females. Use them as the gentle, "obvious" cases of `ة`-less feminines.

**Household / everyday objects:**
- `مِكْوَاة` (*mikwāh*) — iron (for pressing clothes) — fem
- `دَرَّاجَة` (*darrājah*) — bicycle — fem
- `سَاعَة` (*sāʿah*) — watch / clock — fem
- `مِلْعَقَة` (*milʿaqah*) — spoon — fem
- `ثَلَّاجَة` (*thallājah*) — refrigerator — fem
- `قَهْوَة` (*qahwah*) — coffee — fem
- `نَافِذَة` (*nāfidhah*) — window — fem
- `بَقَرَة` (*baqarah*) — cow — fem
- `شَاي` (*shāy*) — tea — *masculine* (nice contrast: `الشَّايُ` vs feminine `الْقَهْوَةُ`)
- `قِدْر` (*qidr*) — cooking pot — *masculine* (see note below)

**Body parts:**
- `أَنْف` (*anf*) — nose (masculine)
- `فَم` (*fam*) — mouth (masculine)
- `عَيْن` (*ʿayn*) — eye (feminine, no `ة`)
- `أُذُن` (*udhun*) — ear (feminine, no `ة`)
- `يَد` (*yad*) — hand (feminine, no `ة`)
- `رِجْل` (*rijl*) — leg / foot (feminine, no `ة`)

**Other new words:** `فَلَّاح` (farmer — masculine), `الْمُدِير` (the director / manager — masculine), `سَرِيع`/`سَرِيعَة` (fast), `جِدًّا` (very), and the agreement adjectives `جَدِيد`/`جَدِيدَة` (new), `قَدِيم`/`قَدِيمَة` (old), `جَمِيل`/`جَمِيلَة` (beautiful), plus `جَالِس` (sitting, m) / `وَاقِفَة` (standing, f). New name: `أَنَس` (*Anas*).

> **⚠️ Watch-out for you:** `قِدْر` (cooking pot) is **masculine** even though it lives among the feminine kitchen objects — the lesson sentence is `هَذِهِ مِلْعَقَةٌ وَهَذِهِ قِدْرٌ` (the textbook does use `هَذِهِ` for `قِدْر` in the dialogue, but `قِدْر` is generally treated as masculine/either-gender in the dictionaries — *don't make this a teaching point*; just teach the words and follow the book's `هَذِهِ` for the pot if a classmate reads that exact sentence). Don't build a sub-rule around it.

**Yaad rakho:** Vocabulary aasaan hai — ghar ki cheezein, jism ke ange, rishtedaar. `بِنْت` (beti/ladki), `أُخْت` (behen), `أُمّ` (maa) — feminine bina `ة` ke (par obvious). Ghar ki cheezein (`مِكْوَاة`, `دَرَّاجَة`, `سَاعَة`, `مِلْعَقَة`, `ثَلَّاجَة`, `قَهْوَة`, `نَافِذَة`) `ة` ke saath feminine. Jism (jodi-wale): `عَيْن`/`أُذُن`/`يَد`/`رِجْل` feminine; `أَنْف`/`فَم` masculine.

## The big picture — what Chapter 6 silently does

Step back. Chapters 1–5 built the *nominal sentence* and the *iḍāfa*. Chapter 6 adds the dimension that runs through **all** of Arabic grammar from here on: **gender**.

1. **Gender is now an active skill, not just a recognition.** Before, classmates *noticed* feminine words. Now they can *produce* the feminine of any word by adding `ة`.
2. **Agreement begins.** This is the first time classmates make *two* words match (subject + profession, subject + adjective). Agreement will reappear constantly — in the formal adjective rule (Lesson 9), in the dual, in plurals, in verbs. Lesson 6 plants the seed.
3. **The demonstrative grid is almost complete.** `هَذَا` (Ch 1), `ذَلِكَ` (Ch 2), `هَذِهِ` (Ch 3, consolidated here). The only missing corner is `تِلْكَ` (that — feminine far) — which is **Lesson 7. Do not pre-teach it.**
4. **The pronoun `هِيَ`** (Ch 5) gets a real workout — almost every feminine sentence in the lesson uses it.

The chapter feels light because there's only one rule — but that one rule (`ة` → feminine → make everything agree) is one of the load-bearing pillars of the whole language.

**Yaad rakho:** Ch 6 ek hi rule sikhaata hai par woh poori zaban ka stamboon hai — **gender aur agreement**. Pehle feminine sirf pehchaante the, ab banaate bhi hain (`ة` laga ke). Pehli baar do lafz aapas mein match karte hain (subject + adjective). Demonstrative grid ab `تِلْكَ` ke ilaawa poora — par `تِلْكَ` Lesson 7 ki cheez hai, abhi mat sikhaao.

## The landmines for Urdu-speaking adult learners

### Landmine 1 · The "default to masculine" reflex (the big one)

This is Chapter 6's version of Chapter 5's gender-mismatch landmine — and it's the chapter's central hammer. Urdu *does* have gender, but it doesn't map onto Arabic. *kitaab* is feminine in Urdu (*kitaab achhi hai*) but **masculine** in Arabic (`كِتَابٌ` → `هُوَ`, `هَذَا`). *makaan* is masculine in Urdu and Arabic both. The Urdu instinct will actively mislead. And for *producing* the feminine, classmates will simply forget the `ة` and say `هِيَ طَبِيبٌ` instead of `هِيَ طَبِيبَةٌ`.

**Counter-strategy:** Make the `ة` switch a physical, visible action. On the board, write a masculine word, then *physically add* the `ة` in a different colour while the class says "**-ah!**". Do it ten times:

> `مُسْلِم` → `مُسْلِم` **`ة`** &nbsp;·&nbsp; `طَبِيب` → `طَبِيب` **`ة`** &nbsp;·&nbsp; `مُهَنْدِس` → `مُهَنْدِس` **`ة`** &nbsp;·&nbsp; `كَبِير` → `كَبِير` **`ة`**

Then drill agreement: you say a feminine subject, class completes with a feminine predicate. *"hiya... ?"* → *"...muhandisatun!"*

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Urdu ki gender Arabic mein kaam nahin aati — 'kitaab' Urdu mein feminine hai magar Arabic mein `كِتَاب` masculine hai (`هُوَ`, `هَذَا`). To Urdu wali aadat bhool jao. Arabic mein feminine banaane ka tareeqa ek hi hai: lafz ke aakhir mein `ة` lagao. `طَبِيب` (mard doctor) → `طَبِيبَة` (aurat doctor). Aur jab subject feminine ho to saath wala lafz bhi `ة` le — `هِيَ طَبِيبَةٌ`, na ki `هِيَ طَبِيبٌ`. `ة` mat bhoolo — yahi aaj ka sabse bada rule hai."*

### Landmine 2 · Forgetting the `ة` on the adjective/profession (agreement)

Even classmates who pick `هِيَ` and `هَذِهِ` correctly will get the subject right but leave the *predicate* masculine: `هَذِهِ سَيَّارَةٌ جَدِيدٌ` (feminine noun, masculine adjective). The agreement has to flow all the way through.

**Counter-strategy:** Teach the "**chain reaction**" image. Once the subject is feminine, the `ة` has to ripple to every describing word. Drill three-word feminine sentences where classmates must put `ة` on *both* the noun and the adjective: `هَذِهِ دَرَّاجَةٌ جَدِيدَةٌ`, `هَذِهِ سَاعَةٌ جَمِيلَةٌ`, `هَذِهِ بِنْتٌ صَغِيرَةٌ`. Underline both `ة`s in red.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Agar subject feminine hai to sirf shuruaat nahin — poora jumla feminine. `هَذِهِ` feminine, noun feminine (`سَيَّارَة`), aur adjective bhi feminine (`جَدِيدَة`). Ek `ة` chhoot gaya to galti. Jaise ek switch dab gaya to poori line roshan — `ة` ki line poori honi chahiye: `هَذِهِ سَيَّارَةٌ جَدِيدَةٌ`."*

### Landmine 3 · `عَيْن` / `أُذُن` — feminine without a `ة`

Classmates will reasonably assume "no `ة` = masculine" and say `هَذَا عَيْنٌ`. The body parts break that assumption.

**Counter-strategy:** Don't fight it with a rule — fight it with memorisation and the picture. Point at the eye in the diagram, say `هَذِهِ عَيْنٌ`, have them repeat. Point at the nose: `هَذَا أَنْفٌ`. Make a tiny two-column list on the board — `هَذَا` (nose, mouth) vs `هَذِهِ` (eye, ear, hand, leg) — and give them the hook: *"do-do wale ange feminine."* Acknowledge openly: *"yeh yaad karna padega."*

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Aam tor par `ة` na ho to lafz masculine hota hai. Magar kuch lafz `ة` ke baghair bhi feminine hain — koi rule nahin, yaad karo. Aaj jism ke jodi-wale ange: `عَيْن` (aankh), `أُذُن` (kaan), `يَد` (haath), `رِجْل` (taang) — sab feminine, isliye `هَذِهِ`. Aur `أَنْف` (naak), `فَم` (mooh) — masculine, `هَذَا`. Trick: do-do hone wale ange feminine."*

### Landmine 4 · Dropping the iḍāfa rules when the first noun is feminine

After a whole chapter focused on `ة`, classmates may "over-apply" tanween or `الـ` to feminine first-nouns of an iḍāfa: `السَّيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ` or `سَيَّارَةٌ الْمُدِيرِ`. The Ch 4 rule still holds: the muḍāf (first noun) takes **no `الـ`, no tanween** — just `ـةُ`.

**Counter-strategy:** Re-use Ch 4's "find the mistake" format with feminine examples. Put up `سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ` (correct) next to `السَّيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ` (wrong) and `سَيَّارَةٌ الْمُدِيرِ` (wrong) and ask which is right and why.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Iḍāfa ka rule gender se nahin badalta. Pehla lafz (muḍāf) — chahe feminine ho — par `الـ` nahin, tanween nahin. Sirf ek pesh: `سَيَّارَةُ المُدِيرِ` (director ki gaadi). `السَّيَّارَةُ المُدِيرِ` galat, `سَيَّارَةٌ المُدِيرِ` bhi galat. Wahi Ch 4 wale teen rule."*

## Suggested 60-minute class plan

*Class shuru karne se pehle:* board par chaar cheezein likho — chapter ka Arabic title (`الدَّرْسُ السَّادِسُ`), the `ة` switch demo (`طَبِيب → طَبِيبَة`), a two-column gender chart (`هَذَا` / `هَذِهِ` with 4 nouns each, including `عَيْن`/`أُذُن` on the `هَذِهِ` side), and one feminine iḍāfa (`سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ`).

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–5 min | Greetings · 2-min recap: `هُوَ`/`هِيَ` from Ch 5 (point at objects, get the pronoun); 1 quick iḍāfa drill. |
| 5–18 min | **The `ة` switch** (the hammer). Demo masculine → feminine by adding `ة`, in colour, 10 times. Class chants the "-ah". Drill: you give masculine, they give feminine. |
| 18–30 min | **Agreement.** Feminine subject → feminine predicate. Professions (`هِيَ طَبِيبَةٌ`, `هِيَ مُهَنْدِسَةٌ`) then adjectives (`دَرَّاجَةٌ جَدِيدَةٌ`, `سَاعَةٌ جَمِيلَةٌ`). Underline both `ة`s. 8 sentences. |
| 30–40 min | **`هَذِهِ` + the feminine vocabulary.** Hold up / point at pictures: `مِكْوَاة`, `دَرَّاجَة`, `سَاعَة`, `مِلْعَقَة`, `بَقَرَة`. Each: *"mā hādhihi? — hādhihi …"*. |
| 40–48 min | **Body parts + the `ة`-less feminines.** Face/limb diagrams. `هَذَا أَنْفٌ / فَمٌ` vs `هَذِهِ عَيْنٌ / أُذُنٌ / يَدٌ / رِجْلٌ`. Give the "paired organs are feminine" hook. Two-column board list to memorise. |
| 48–56 min | **Feminine iḍāfa & "whose?".** `بِنْتُ يَاسِرٍ`, `أُخْتُ الْمُهَنْدِسِ`, `سَيَّارَةُ مَنْ هَذِهِ ؟`. "Find the mistake" with `السَّيَّارَةُ المُدِيرِ`. |
| 56–60 min | Wrap up · new vocabulary recap · the Qur'an payoff (`لِلَّهِ الْمَشْرِقُ وَالْمَغْرِبُ`) · assign exercises. |

**Yaad rakho:** Ch 6 ka pacing aaraam wala hai — sirf ek bada rule (`ة` switch + agreement) aur teen chhote (body-part feminines, vocabulary, iḍāfa recap). Sabse zyaada waqt `ة` switch aur agreement par lagao (concept 1 + 2) — yahi chapter ka dil hai. Body parts aur vocabulary picture-based hain, jaldi chal jaate hain.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 6

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum Chapter X mein dekhenge"*:

- ❌ `تِلْكَ` (that — feminine far) — **Lesson 7.** This is genuinely the very next lesson; do not pre-teach the feminine far-pointer.
- ❌ The formal adjective rule — `النَّعْت` / `مَنْعُوت` and the full four-way agreement (gender, number, definiteness, case) — **Lesson 9.** In Ch 6, show agreement *descriptively* ("the adjective copies the `ة`") only.
- ❌ Other pronouns — `أَنَا` (I), `أَنْتَ` (you), `نَحْنُ` (we), `هُمْ` (they). Stick to `هُوَ`/`هِيَ` from Ch 5.
- ❌ The full *categories* of `ة`-less feminine nouns (place names, `أَرْض`/`شَمْس`/`نَار`, the "feminine of hearing" rule). Just give the paired-organs hook and memorise `عَيْن`/`أُذُن`/`يَد`/`رِجْل` (and the obvious `بِنْت`/`أُخْت`/`أُمّ`) as they come.
- ❌ The *ghayr munṣarif* (diptote) rule — even though `أَنَس` and the feminine names sit nearby. Same hands-off policy as Ch 5.
- ❌ Feminine plural agreement, or the feminine of verbs (`خَرَجَتْ`, `ذَهَبَتْ`) — verbs aren't taught yet; if they appear in an exercise, treat them as recognition vocabulary only.
- ❌ Sound/broken plurals of feminine nouns — **Lesson 13.**
- ❌ Don't build a rule around `قِدْر` (pot). Just teach the word; follow the book's wording if the exact sentence comes up.

**Yaad rakho:** Sabse common sawaal: *"`تِلْكَ` kab seekhenge?"* Jawab: *"Bilkul agle hafte (Lesson 7) — pehle `هَذِهِ` aur feminine pakka kar lo."* Doosra: *"adjective ka poora rule?"* — *"Lesson 9 mein."* Sequencing par bharosa karo — ek waqt mein ek hi cheez.

## Self-check before teaching

Before the class, make sure:

- [ ] You can produce the feminine of any masculine word instantly by adding `ة` (`مُسْلِم → مُسْلِمَة`, `كَبِير → كَبِيرَة`). *(The `ة` switch — reflex.)*
- [ ] You can build 5 feminine sentences with full agreement — subject + profession/adjective both feminine (`هِيَ طَبِيبَةٌ`, `هَذِهِ دَرَّاجَةٌ جَدِيدَةٌ`). *(Agreement — `ة` flows through.)*
- [ ] You can list, without hesitation, which body parts are `هَذَا` and which are `هَذِهِ` (`أَنْف`/`فَم` vs `عَيْن`/`أُذُن`/`يَد`/`رِجْل`). *(The `ة`-less feminines — the paired-organs hook.)*
- [ ] You can build 5 feminine iḍāfa phrases correctly (no `الـ`, no tanween on the first noun): `بِنْتُ يَاسِرٍ`, `سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ`. *(Ch 4 rule + feminine.)*
- [ ] You can ask "whose car is this?" in both forms (`سَيَّارَةُ مَنْ هَذِهِ ؟` and `لِمَنْ هَذِهِ ؟`). *(Ch 4–5 questions + `هَذِهِ`.)*
- [ ] You can explain in one Urdu sentence why Urdu gender doesn't help (*"'kitaab' Urdu mein feminine, Arabic mein masculine — Urdu wali aadat bhool jao"*).
- [ ] You can answer "what about `تِلْكَ`?" with calm confidence: *"Agle hafte."*

If yes to all seven — *tum tayar ho.*

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Are they adding the `ة` to make feminines, and **not** forgetting it on the predicate? *(The core skill of Ch 6 — agreement all the way through.)*
2. Are they picking `هَذِهِ` for feminine and `هَذَا` for masculine consistently — including the `ة`-less `عَيْن`/`أُذُن`/`يَد`/`رِجْل`? *(Gender recognition, including exceptions.)*
3. Do they keep the iḍāfa rules when the first noun is feminine (no `الـ`, no tanween)? *(Old rule, new gender.)*
4. Can they ask "whose?" about a feminine thing in both forms? *(Question patterns + `هَذِهِ`.)*
5. Are they resisting the Urdu-gender instinct (not calling `كِتَاب` feminine)? *(The cross-language interference.)*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh paanch points batayenge ke Lesson 7 (`تِلْكَ`) par jaana hai ya `ة`/agreement dohraana hai. **Sabse important: agreement** — agar 3+ classmates predicate par `ة` bhool rahe hain (`هِيَ طَبِيبٌ`), to Lesson 9 ka naʿt rule mushkil ho jaayega. Ch 6 ka core wahi hai — `ة` ka switch poore jumle mein chale.
