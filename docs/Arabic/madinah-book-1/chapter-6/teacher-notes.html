How Ch 6 connects back

Chapter 6 grows directly out of earlier chapters:

  • The demonstrative grid nears completion. Ch 1's هَذَا, Ch 2's ذَلِكَ, Ch 3's هَذِهِ — and Ch 6 turns هَذِهِ into a full feminine system. The only missing corner is تِلْكَ (Lesson 7 — don't pre-teach).
  • The Ch 3 gender rule becomes a skill. Ch 3 taught "ة-ending → feminine → هَذِهِ" as a recognition rule. Ch 6 makes it productive: add the ة to build the feminine, and make everything agree.
  • Iḍāfa and "whose?" carry over unchanged. Ch 4's iḍāfa rules and لِمَنْ, plus Ch 5's قَلَمُ مَنْ, all work identically with feminine nouns.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Make a masculine noun or adjective feminine by adding ة (مُهَنْدِس → مُهَنْدِسَة).
  2. Match a profession or adjective to a feminine subject: هِيَ طَبِيبَةٌ, دَرَّاجَةٌ جَدِيدَةٌ.
  3. Use هَذِهِ (review) with feminine nouns, and recognise the feminine all the way through a sentence.
  4. Recognise that a few nouns are feminine without a ة — the paired body parts عَيْن (eye), أُذُن (ear), يَد (hand), رِجْل (leg/foot).
  5. Build feminine iḍāfa phrases keeping the Ch 4 rules (بِنْتُ يَاسِرٍ, سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ).
  6. Ask "whose?" about a feminine thing in both forms (سَيَّارَةُ مَنْ هَذِهِ ؟ / لِمَنْ هَذِهِ ؟).
  7. Use the new vocabulary: بِنْت، أُخْت، أُمّ، مِكْوَاة، دَرَّاجَة، سَاعَة، مِلْعَقَة، ثَلَّاجَة، قَهْوَة، نَافِذَة، بَقَرَة، أَنْف، فَم، عَيْن، أُذُن، يَد، رِجْل.

1 · التَّأْنِيث — the ة feminine marker

The heart of the chapter. Arabic divides every noun into masculine (مُذَكَّر) and feminine (مُؤَنَّث). The usual way to make a word feminine is to add the letter ة (taa marbuuta) to its end.

Where this fits: Ch 3 taught you to recognise feminine nouns by their ة. Ch 6 teaches you to make them: take any masculine word, add ة, and it becomes feminine.

ة · taa marbuuta · the feminine marker

Meaning
The letter added to the end of a masculine noun/adjective to make it feminine. The textbook's rule: the taa marbuuta enters at the end of the masculine noun to make it feminine.
Roman Urdu
Feminine banaane wala "switch". Mardaana lafz le lo, aakhir mein ة laga do — feminine taiyaar.
Rule
Masculine (مُذَكَّر) + ة = feminine (مُؤَنَّث). Pronounced "-ah" at pause, full "t" when a word follows.
Example
مُهَنْدِسٌ ← مُهَنْدِسَةٌ · طَبِيبٌ ← طَبِيبَةٌ muhandisun → muhandisatun · ṭabībun → ṭabībatun · "engineer / doctor (m → f)" · "(mard → aurat)"

مُذَكَّر & مُؤَنَّث — the two genders

Meaning
Arabic sorts every noun into exactly two genders — masculine and feminine. There is no neuter "it".
مُذَكَّر
mudhakkar — masculine. Takes هَذَا / هُوَ.
مُؤَنَّث
mu'annath — feminine. Takes هَذِهِ / هِيَ.
Roman Urdu
Arabic mein har lafz ya mardaana (مُذَكَّر) ya zanaana (مُؤَنَّث) — "it" / neuter hota hi nahin. Mardaana → هَذَا/هُوَ; zanaana → هَذِهِ/هِيَ.
Example
قَلَمٌ (مذكر) · سَاعَةٌ (مؤنث) qalamun (m) · sāʿatun (f) · "a pen / a watch"

💡 Teaching tip — make the ة a physical action

Write a masculine word on the board, then physically add the ة in a second colour while the class says "-ah!" out loud. Do it ten times rapid-fire: مُسْلِم → مُسْلِمَة, كَبِير → كَبِيرَة, طَبِيب → طَبِيبَة. The colour + the chant burns the switch into memory.

⚠️ Urdu gender will mislead

Urdu has gender, but it does not match Arabic. kitaab is feminine in Urdu but كِتَابٌ is masculine in Arabic (هَذَا, هُوَ). Tell the class: "Urdu wali gender bhool jao — Arabic ki gender alag hai. ة dekho, ya yaad karo."

2 · Feminine agreement — هِيَ طَبِيبَةٌ, دَرَّاجَةٌ جَدِيدَةٌ

Once the subject is feminine, the words that describe it — a profession or an adjective — also take the ة. The ة "flows" through the sentence.

Where this fits: This is the first time classmates make two words match. هَذِهِ (Ch 3) is the front of the agreement; the predicate (profession/adjective) is the back. The formal adjective rule (النَّعْت) waits for Lesson 9 — teach the pattern only, not the theory.

Profession agreement — هِيَ طَبِيبَةٌ

Meaning
When the subject is feminine, the job-title describing it must be feminine too — formed by adding ة to the masculine profession word.
Roman Urdu
Feminine subject ke saath profession bhi ة wala: هِيَ طَبِيبَةٌ (woh aurat doctor hai), na ki هِيَ طَبِيبٌ.
Rule
A feminine subject takes a feminine profession word (with ة). هِيَ طَبِيبٌ (masculine form) is wrong.
Example (from the lesson)
أَهِيَ أَيْضًا مُهَنْدِسَةٌ ؟ — لَا، هِيَ طَبِيبَةٌ a-hiya ayḍan muhandisatun? — lā, hiya ṭabībatun · "Is she also an engineer? — No, she is a doctor" · "Kya woh bhi engineer hai? — Nahin, woh doctor hai"

Adjective agreement — دَرَّاجَةٌ جَدِيدَةٌ

Meaning
An adjective describing a feminine noun must itself be feminine — it "copies" the noun's ة.
Roman Urdu
Adjective noun ki ة "copy" karta hai. سَاعَة (feminine) → جَمِيلَة, جَدِيدَة.
Rule
The adjective copies the ة of a feminine noun. Feminine noun → feminine adjective.
Example 1
هَذِهِ سَاعَةُ عَلِيٍّ. هِيَ جَمِيلَةٌ جِدًّا hādhihi sāʿatu ʿaliyyin. hiya jamīlatun jiddan · "This is Ali's watch. It is very beautiful"
Example 2
هَذِهِ دَرَّاجَةٌ جَدِيدَةٌ، وَدَرَّاجَةُ أَنَسٍ قَدِيمَةٌ hādhihi darrājatun jadīdatun, wa-darrājatu Anasin qadīmatun · "This is a new bicycle, and Anas's bicycle is old"

💡 Teaching tip — the "chain reaction" image

Once the subject is feminine, the ة has to ripple to every describing word — like one switch lighting a whole line. Drill three-word feminine sentences and underline both ةs in red: هَذِهِ دَرَّاجَةٌ جَدِيدَةٌ, هَذِهِ سَاعَةٌ جَمِيلَةٌ.

⚠️ Don't teach the formal adjective rule (النَّعْت) yet

The pairing "noun + matching adjective" is technically النَّعْت (naʿt), but the textbook only names it and gives its full four-way agreement rule in Lesson 9. In Ch 6, teach agreement descriptively — "the adjective copies the ة" — and stop there.

3 · Feminine without ة — the body-part wrinkle

Not every feminine noun has a ة. A few common nouns are feminine "by nature" — in this lesson, the paired body parts عَيْن (eye), أُذُن (ear), يَد (hand), رِجْل (leg/foot).

Where this fits: This breaks the simple assumption "no ة = masculine". You can only tell عَيْن/أُذُن/يَد/رِجْل are feminine because the textbook uses هَذِهِ with them. Treat it as memorisation (hook: paired organs are feminine), not a rule.

The ة-less feminines in this lesson

Meaning
A few nouns are feminine even though they carry no ة — in this lesson, the body parts that come in a pair.
Feminine
عَيْنٌ · أُذُنٌ · يَدٌ · رِجْلٌ → هَذِهِ ʿaynun · udhunun · yadun · rijlun · "eye · ear · hand · leg/foot" — feminine despite no ة
Masculine
أَنْفٌ · فَمٌ → هَذَا anfun · famun · "nose · mouth"
Roman Urdu
Aam tor par ة na ho to masculine — magar jodi-wale ange عَيْن (aankh), أُذُن (kaan), يَد (haath), رِجْل (taang) ة ke baghair bhi feminine. أَنْف (naak), فَم (mooh) masculine.
Example
هَذَا أَنْفٌ وَهَذَا فَمٌ · هَذِهِ عَيْنٌ وَهَذِهِ أُذُنٌ · هَذِهِ يَدٌ وَهَذِهِ رِجْلٌ hādhā anfun wa-hādhā famun · hādhihi ʿaynun wa-hādhihi udhunun · hādhihi yadun wa-hādhihi rijlun · "This is a nose and this is a mouth · this is an eye and this is an ear · this is a hand and this is a leg"

The "obvious" ة-less feminines

Meaning
The female-person words are also feminine without a ة — the "easy" cases, since they obviously name females.
Family words
بِنْتٌ · أُخْتٌ · أُمٌّ bintun · ukhtun · ummun · "daughter/girl · sister · mother"
Why no trouble
بِنْت, أُخْت and أُمّ are also feminine without a ة, but classmates won't trip — the words obviously refer to females. Use them as the "easy" examples of ة-less feminines, then extend to the paired organs.
Roman Urdu
بِنْت (beti/ladki), أُخْت (behen), أُمّ (maa) — yeh bhi ة ke baghair feminine, par obvious hain. Inse عَيْن/أُذُن/يَد/رِجْل wali baat samjhao.

💡 Teaching tip — memorise with the picture + the paired-organs hook

Use the face/limb diagrams. Point at each part and say the demonstrative: "hādhā anfun" (nose), "hādhihi ʿaynun" (eye), "hādhihi yadun" (hand). Make a tiny two-column board list — هَذَا (nose, mouth) vs هَذِهِ (eye, ear, hand, leg) — and give the hook: "jism ke jo ange do-do hote hain woh feminine."

⚠️ Don't open the full feminine-categories rule

There is a bigger system (certain place names, شَمْس/أَرْض/نَار, etc.) — but it is far beyond Lesson 6. Give the "paired organs are feminine" hook for this lesson's four (عَيْن/أُذُن/يَد/رِجْل) plus the obvious بِنْت/أُخْت/أُمّ — and stop there.

4 · The feminine in iḍāfa & "whose?"

No new grammar — Chapter 4's iḍāfa, Ch 4's لِمَنْ, and Ch 5's قَلَمُ مَنْ, all run with feminine nouns. The machinery doesn't change with gender.

Where this fits: A consolidation block. When a feminine word is the first noun (muḍāf) of an iḍāfa, the Ch 4 rules still hold: no الـ, no tanween — just ـةُ.

Feminine iḍāfa — the muḍāf is feminine

Meaning
A feminine noun can be the first noun (muḍāf) of a possessive iḍāfa — "the X's Y" — with no change to the Ch 4 rules.
Roman Urdu
Pehla lafz feminine ho to bhi: الـ nahin, tanween nahin, sirf ek pesh (ـةُ). ة yahaan poora "t" boli jaati hai.
Rule
Same 3 Ch 4 rules: the first noun takes no الـ, no tanween — here it ends in a single ـةُ. The ة sounds like a full "t" (connected): bintu Yāsir, sayyāratu l-mudīr.
Examples
بِنْتُ يَاسِرٍ · أُخْتُ الْمُهَنْدِسِ · سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ · بَقَرَةُ الْفَلَّاحِ bintu Yāsir · ukhtu l-muhandis · sayyāratu l-mudīr · baqaratu l-fallāḥ · "Yasir's daughter · the engineer's sister · the director's car · the farmer's cow"

"Whose?" about a feminine thing

Ch 5 way (iḍāfa)
سَيَّارَةُ مَنْ هَذِهِ ؟ — هَذِهِ سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ sayyāratu man hādhihi? — hādhihi sayyāratu l-mudīr · "Whose car is this? — This is the director's car"
Ch 4 way (لِـ)
لِمَنْ هَذِهِ ؟ — هَذِهِ لِخَالِدٍ li-man hādhihi? — hādhihi li-Khālidin · "Whose is this? — This is Khalid's"
Who / what (review)
مَنْ هَذِهِ ؟ · مَا هَذِهِ ؟ man hādhihi? · mā hādhihi? · "Who is this (f)? · What is this (f)?"
Roman Urdu
Sare sawaal gender se nahin badalte — bas هَذِهِ lag jaata hai feminine cheez ke liye.

⚠️ Keep the iḍāfa rules with feminine first-nouns

After a whole chapter on ة, classmates may over-add الـ or tanween: السَّيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ or سَيَّارَةٌ الْمُدِيرِ. Both wrong. The muḍāf stays bare: سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ. Re-run Ch 4's "find the mistake" with feminine examples.

⚠️ The ة-less feminines in this lesson

Several of this chapter's words are feminine despite having no ة: the paired organs عَيْن (eye), أُذُن (ear), يَد (hand), رِجْل (leg/foot), and the female-person words بِنْت (girl), أُخْت (sister), أُمّ (mother). All take هَذِهِ / هِيَ. Don't open the full feminine-categories rule — give the paired-organs hook and memorise these.

⚠️ Note on قِدْر (pot)

قِدْر is generally masculine (or either-gender) in the dictionaries, even though it sits among the feminine kitchen objects. If the exact lesson sentence هَذِهِ مِلْعَقَةٌ وَهَذِهِ قِدْرٌ comes up, follow the book — but don't build a sub-rule around it. Just teach the word.

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Forgetting the ة on the predicate (broken agreement)

Classmates pick هِيَ/هَذِهِ correctly but leave the profession/adjective masculine: هِيَ طَبِيبٌ, هَذِهِ سَيَّارَةٌ جَدِيدٌ. The ة must reach every describing word. Drill three-word feminine sentences; underline both ةs.

2. The Urdu-gender reflex

Urdu gender doesn't match Arabic. Classmates may call كِتَاب feminine (it's feminine in Urdu) — but it's masculine in Arabic. Tell them: "Urdu wali gender bhool jao." In Arabic, look for the ة or memorise the exceptions.

3. Using هَذَا for عَيْن / أُذُن / يَد / رِجْل

Since there's no ة, classmates assume masculine: هَذَا عَيْنٌ. Wrong — the paired organs are feminine: هَذِهِ عَيْنٌ, هَذِهِ أُذُنٌ, هَذِهِ يَدٌ, هَذِهِ رِجْلٌ. Memorise with the picture + the paired-organs hook.

4. Putting الـ or tanween on a feminine muḍāf

After a chapter full of ة, classmates over-mark the first noun of an iḍāfa: السَّيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ or سَيَّارَةٌ الْمُدِيرِ. The Ch 4 rule still holds — the muḍāf is bare: سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ.

5. Reaching for تِلْكَ (not yet!)

Once classmates have هَذِهِ (feminine "this"), they'll ask for the feminine "that". That's تِلْكَ — Lesson 7. Don't pre-teach it. Say: "Agle hafte."

📿 Connect to the Qur'an

The lesson prints its own Qur'anic phrase (Exercise 5), and the feminine ة the class learned today is everywhere in the Qur'an. Use 1–2 of these in class.

Surah Al-Baqarah · 2:115 — the lesson's own verse

لِلَّهِ الْمَشْرِقُ وَالْمَغْرِبُ

li-llāhi l-mashriqu wa-l-maghrib — "To Allah belong the East and the West."

This phrase is printed in the lesson itself (Exercise 5), so it's an authentic in-lesson verse. Students recognise:

  • لِلَّهِ — the لِـ + اللَّه structure from Ch 4 (لِـ drops the alif of الـ → لِلَّـ), with اللَّه in kasra.
  • الْمَشْرِقُ (the East) and الْمَغْرِبُ (the West) — two الـ-nouns the class can now read.

الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ — also printed in Exercise 5

الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ

al-ḥamdu li-llāh — "All praise is for Allah."

Students recognise: the everyday phrase, now fully decodable — الْحَمْدُ (the praise) + لِلَّهِ (لِـ "for" + اللَّه in kasra). A satisfying payoff: a phrase they say daily, now grammar they understand.

The ة you learned today is everywhere

The feminine marker ة is one of the most common letters in the Qur'an. Point out words classmates already know that carry it:

الْكَعْبَة · الْفَاتِحَة · الْبَقَرَة · سُورَة · آيَة · جَنَّة

al-Kaʿbah · al-Fātiḥah · al-Baqarah · sūrah · āyah · jannah — "the Ka'ba · the Opening · the Cow (surah) · a chapter · a verse · paradise".

Students recognise: the same ة they practised today marks all of these as feminine. This is recognition only — a connection, not a verse to recite.
