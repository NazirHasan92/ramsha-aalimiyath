⏱️ A grammar-heavy chapter — consider two sittings

Four new things in one lesson. A clean split: Class 1 = the dialogue + the feminine ـكِ + greetings + the vocative يَا (the "talking" half); Class 2 = the feminine verb ـتْ + the relative pronouns الَّذِي/الَّتِي (the "grammar" half). If you do it in one hour, protect the time for الَّذِي/الَّتِي.

★ Master Overview — نقشۂ الدرس

One bird's-eye map: Chapter 12 is a conversation lesson — a dialogue between two girls that introduces four new tools. Three of them are just the class's old gender-matching reflex (هُوَ/هِيَ) applied in new places: on "you" (ـكَ/ـكِ), on the verb (خَرَجَ/خَرَجَتْ), and on the relative (الَّذِي/الَّتِي) — plus the little calling-word يَا.

How Ch 12 connects back

This lesson turns the class's noun-world into speech:

  • The tails (Ch 10) gain their feminine partner: ـكَ → ـكِ, so greetings (السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكِ, كَيْفَ حَالُكِ) become real speech.
  • The gender reflex (هُوَ/هِيَ, Ch 5; هَذَا/هَذِهِ, Ch 6/7) now also marks the verb (خَرَجَ/خَرَجَتْ) and the relative (الَّذِي/الَّتِي).
  • The adjective of Ch 9 (الْفَتَاةُ الْجَمِيلَةُ) grows into a whole clause: الْفَتَاةُ الَّتِي مَعَكِ — describing with a sentence, not one word.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Greet correctly with السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكِ (to a female) / عَلَيْكَ (to a male), and ask/answer كَيْفَ حَالُكِ ؟ — أَنَا بِخَيْرٍ وَالْحَمْدُ لِلهِ.
  2. Use the feminine "you" ـكِ (اسْمُكِ, أَبُوكِ, مَعَكِ) and flip a sentence between ـكَ and ـكِ.
  3. Call/address someone with the vocative يَا (يَا مُحَمَّدُ, يَا أُخْتِي, يَا خَالَتِي).
  4. Add the feminine ـتْ to a past verb for a female doer (خَرَجَتْ, ذَهَبَتْ, جَلَسَتْ), and read the ـتِ-before-الـ smoothing (جَلَسَتِ الطَّالِبَةُ).
  5. Use the relative pronouns الَّذِي (after a masculine noun) / الَّتِي (after a feminine noun) to describe with a clause.

1 · The feminine "you" ـكِ — and the greetings

The lesson opens here. Chapter 10 taught ـكَ ("your", to a male); Lesson 12 adds its twin ـكِ ("your", to a female). The only difference is the vowel on the ك: fatḥa for a man, kasra for a woman. This is its first proper introduction.

Where this fits: Ch 10 gave the four tails (ـِي/ـكَ/ـهُ/ـها). Ch 12 fills the gap with ـكِ — the same gender-matching the class has done since هُوَ/هِيَ, now on the person being addressed.

ـكِ · the feminine "your"

Meaning
"your", spoken to a female. The twin of Ch 10's ـكَ ("your", to a male). اسْمُكِ = "your name", أَبُوكِ = "your father", مَعَكِ = "with you".
Roman Urdu
ـكِ = "tera/tumhara" aurat ko (zer). ـكَ mard ko (zabar). Sirf ك par harakah ka farq.
Rule
ـكَ has a fatḥa (male); ـكِ has a kasra (female). Match the tail to the gender of the person you are talking to. The plural ـكُمْ / عَلَيْكُمْ is Lesson 14–15 — not now.
Example
مَا اسْمُكِ ؟ — اسْمِي آمِنَةُ . أَيْنَ أَبُوكِ ؟ mā smuki? — ismī āminah. ayna abūki? · "What is your name? — My name is Āminah. Where is your father?"

The greetings (a fixed, beautiful opener)

Meaning
السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكِ وَرَحْمَةُ اللهِ وَبَرَكَاتُهُ = "Peace be upon you, and the mercy of Allah, and His blessings" (to a female). كَيْفَ حَالُكِ ؟ = "How are you?" (lit. "how is your condition?").
Roman Urdu
السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكِ = salaam (aurat ko). كَيْفَ حَالُكِ ؟ = "tum kaisi ho?" Jawab: أَنَا بِخَيْرٍ وَالْحَمْدُ لِلهِ = "main theek hoon, alhamdulillah".
Rule
كَيْفَ ("how?") is a new question word. بِخَيْرٍ = بِـ (Ch 4) + خَيْر ("goodness"). To a male, the greeting is عَلَيْكَ / حَالُكَ.
Example
السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكِ . — وَعَلَيْكِ السَّلَامُ . كَيْفَ حَالُكِ ؟ as-salāmu ʿalayki. — wa-ʿalayki s-salām. kayfa ḥāluki? · "Peace be upon you. — And upon you peace. How are you?"

⚠️ ـكِ for a woman, ـكَ for a man

Urdu's "tumhara" is gender-neutral, so classmates will say اسْمُكَ to a woman. Make it physical: point at a male (اسْمُكَ, zabar) vs a female (اسْمُكِ, zer). It is the same هُوَ/هِيَ gender reflex — now on the person you address.

2 · The vocative يَا — calling / addressing someone

يَا is the little word you place before a name to call to or address someone — exactly like English "O …" or Urdu "ai … / o …". Tiny, but it is the start of every direct address.

Where this fits: The dialogue is full of direct address (يَا بِنْتُ), and the exercises drill يَا + family words carrying the Ch 10 "my" tail (يَا أَبِي, يَا أُخْتِي).

يَا · the vocative ("O …")

Meaning
The word that turns a name into a call: مُحَمَّدٌ ("Muhammad") → يَا مُحَمَّدُ ("O Muhammad"). Used whenever you speak to someone by name or relation.
Roman Urdu
يَا = pukaarne ka lafz, jaise "ai / o". يَا مُحَمَّدُ (O Muhammad), يَا بِنْتُ (O larki).
Rule
With family words, attach the "my" tail (ـِي, Ch 10): يَا أَبِي, يَا أُخْتِي, يَا خَالَتِي. A called name takes a single ḍamma (read يَا مُحَمَّدُ as printed — no tanween). Don't teach the special يَا أَبَتِ form.
Example
أَيْنَ قَلَمُكَ يَا أَبِي ؟ — أَيْنَ قَلَمُكِ يَا أُمِّي ؟ ayna qalamuka yā abī? — ayna qalamuki yā ummī? · "Where is your pen, O my father? — Where is your pen, O my mother?"

💡 Exercise 2 drills two things at once

The book's Exercise 2 has the student re-address the sentence to a female: أَيْنَ قَلَمُكَ يَا أَبِي ؟ → أَيْنَ قَلَمُكِ يَا أُمِّي ؟. So every swap drills the vocative يَا and the ـكَ→ـكِ flip together. Masc→fem pairs: يَا أَخِي→يَا أُخْتِي, يَا خَالِي→يَا خَالَتِي, يَا خَالِدُ→يَا خَدِيجَةُ.

3 · Feminine past-verb agreement — the ـتْ ending

The class's first productive change to a verb. The past verbs خَرَجَ/ذَهَبَ/جَلَسَ were recognition-only; now there is one rule: when the doer is female, the verb grows a quiet ـتْ.

Where this fits: The same gender-matching as هُوَ/هِيَ — now on the verb. Teach only "female doer → ـتْ"; the full conjugation system stays deferred.

ـتْ · the feminine ending on a past verb

Meaning
When the doer (الْفَاعِل) is female, a past verb adds a quiet ـتْ (a ت with sukoon): خَرَجَ → خَرَجَتْ ("she went out"), ذَهَبَ → ذَهَبَتْ, جَلَسَ → جَلَسَتْ.
Roman Urdu
Karne wali aurat ho to verb par ـتْ: ذَهَبَتْ = "woh (f.) gayi". Wahi هُوَ/هِيَ wala gender — ab verb par.
Rule
Only this: male doer → bare verb; female doer → ـتْ. Don't conjugate further ("you went / they went / I went" all later). When ـتْ meets the next word's الـ, the sukoon becomes a kasra: جَلَسَتِ الطَّالِبَةُ (same smoothing as مِنَ الـ, Ch 4/5).
Example
خَرَجَ مُحَمَّدٌ مِنَ الْبَيْتِ . — خَرَجَتْ آمِنَةُ مِنَ الْبَيْتِ . kharaja muḥammadun mina l-bayt. — kharajat āminatu mina l-bayt. · "Muhammad went out of the house. — Āminah went out of the house."

⚠️ The ـتْ follows the doer's gender — both ways

Two opposite slips: forgetting ـتْ for a female doer (ذَهَبَ أُمِّي ✗ → ذَهَبَتْ أُمِّي ✓), and over-adding it to a male doer (خَرَجَتْ مُحَمَّدٌ ✗). Use a two-column board: male → bare, female → ـتْ. This is exactly what Exercise 3 (أَنِّثْ الْفَاعِل) drills.

4 · Relative pronouns الَّذِي / الَّتِي — "the one who / which"

The headline grammar. In Ch 9 the class only recognised الَّذِي; now they use it. الَّذِي (masc) / الَّتِي (fem) glue a whole describing sentence onto a noun — where a single adjective isn't enough.

Where this fits: The adjective of Ch 9 (الْفَتَاةُ الْجَمِيلَةُ, "the beautiful girl") grows into a clause (الْفَتَاةُ الَّتِي مَعَكِ, "the girl who is with you"). The choice الَّذِي/الَّتِي is pure gender-matching.

الَّذِي / الَّتِي · the relative pronouns

Meaning
Both mean "who / which / that". الَّذِي (alladhī) after a masculine noun; الَّتِي (allatī) after a feminine noun. They attach a describing sentence: الْفَتَاةُ الَّتِي مَعَكِ = "the girl who is with you".
Roman Urdu
الَّذِي/الَّتِي = "jo / jis". Poora jumla ism par chipka dete hain. Mard ism → الَّذِي; aurat ism → الَّتِي (هَذَا/هَذِهِ ki tarah).
Rule
Used only after a definite (الـ) noun: الطِّفْلُ الَّذِي…, الْفَتَاةُ الَّتِي…. Never after an indefinite noun. Teach only the two singulars — duals/plurals (الَّذِينَ…) are L13–15.
Example
مَنِ الْفَتَى الَّذِي خَرَجَ مِنْ بَيْتِكَ ؟ — مَنِ الْفَتَاةُ الَّتِي خَرَجَتْ ؟ mani l-fatā lladhī kharaja min baytika? — mani l-fatātu llatī kharajat? · "Who is the young man who went out of your house? — Who is the girl who went out?"

⚠️ Gender match + the definite-noun ticket

Two errors: a gender mismatch (الْفَتَاةُ الَّذِي ✗ → الَّتِي ✓), and — the deeper Urdu trap — using a relative after an indefinite noun. Urdu "jo" works after any noun; Arabic needs the الـ first. Box the antecedent's الـ on the board: it is the "ticket" to use الَّذِي/الَّتِي. Note الَّتِي often pulls a feminine verb: السَّيَّارَةُ الَّتِي خَرَجَتْ (three gender-marks in a row).

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Wrong "you" gender — ـكَ to a woman

Urdu "tumhara" is gender-neutral. To a female it is ـكِ (kasra): اسْمُكِ ✓. To a male, ـكَ (fatḥa). Drill by pointing at the actual person.

2. Dropping يَا (or adding tanween to the name)

To call someone, put يَا first: يَا مُحَمَّدُ, يَا أُخْتِي. The called name takes a single ḍamma — يَا مُحَمَّدُ, not يَا مُحَمَّدٌ ✗.

3. Wrong ـتْ on the verb

Female doer → add ـتْ (ذَهَبَتْ آمِنَةُ). Male doer → bare verb (ذَهَبَ مُحَمَّدٌ). Don't add ـتْ to a male doer, and don't forget it for a female one.

4. جَلَسَتْ الطَّالِبَةُ read with a sukoon clash

When ـتْ meets الـ, the sukoon becomes a kasra: جَلَسَتِ الطَّالِبَةُ ✓. Same smoothing as مِنَ الـ — teach the sound, not the rule.

5. Relative pronoun mistakes

Gender mismatch (الْفَتَاةُ الَّذِي ✗ → الَّتِي ✓) and using a relative after an indefinite noun (Urdu "jo" habit). Rule: الـ-noun first, then الَّذِي/الَّتِي.

📿 Connect to the Qur'an

This lesson's grammar — the relative pronouns الَّذِي/الَّتِي, the vocative يَا, and the feminine ـكِ — is everywhere in the Qur'an. Three verses your class can connect to today's words.

Surah Al-ʿAlaq · 96:1 — الَّذِي (relative, masc) + ـكَ

اقْرَأْ بِاسْمِ رَبِّكَ الَّذِي خَلَقَ

iqraʾ bi-smi rabbika lladhī khalaq — "Read in the name of your Lord who created."

Students recognise: الَّذِي = "who" — exactly today's relative pronoun (after the masculine رَبّ) — plus رَبِّكَ with the masculine "your" tail ـكَ. The very first words ever revealed.

Surah Al-Baqarah · 2:24 — الَّتِي (relative, fem)

فَاتَّقُوا النَّارَ الَّتِي وَقُودُهَا النَّاسُ وَالْحِجَارَةُ

fa-ttaqu n-nāra llatī waqūduhā n-nāsu wa-l-ḥijārah — "So fear the Fire whose fuel is men and stones."

Students recognise: الَّتِي = "which" — the feminine relative, here describing النَّار ("the Fire", a feminine noun). A whole clause ("whose fuel is men and stones") glued onto one noun — exactly Concept 4.

Surah Al-Fajr · 89:27–28 — the vocative يَا + the feminine ـكِ

يَا أَيَّتُهَا النَّفْسُ الْمُطْمَئِنَّةُ ارْجِعِي إِلَىٰ رَبِّكِ

yā ayyatuha n-nafsu l-muṭmaʾinnah, irjiʿī ilā rabbiki — "O reassured soul, return to your Lord."

Students recognise: the calling-word يَا (Concept 2) and the feminine "your" ـكِ in رَبِّكِ ("your Lord", addressed to the soul — Concept 1). Recognition only: ارْجِعِي ("return!") is a feminine command — point at it, don't teach the verb form (verbs come later).
