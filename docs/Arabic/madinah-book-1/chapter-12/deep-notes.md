> **📖 A note before you start — this is a grammar-heavier conversation lesson.** Content read directly from the textbook pages (PDF pp. 63–67). After the gentle reading lesson of Chapter 11, Lesson 12 is busier — it is built around a **dialogue between two girls (Suʿād and a girl, Āminah)** and packs four new things into one natural conversation:
> 1. **The feminine "you" — `ـكِ`** — the partner of Ch 10's masculine `ـكَ`. When you address a **female**, "your" becomes `ـكِ`: `اسْمُكِ` ("your name"), `حَالُكِ` ("your condition"), `أَبُوكِ` ("your father"). The lesson opens with it: `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكِ` ("peace be upon you", to a woman). This is its **first proper introduction**.
> 2. **The vocative `يَا`** — the little word you put before a name to **call/address** someone: `يَا بِنْتُ` ("O girl"), `يَا مُحَمَّدُ` ("O Muhammad"), `يَا أَبِي` ("O my father").
> 3. **Feminine past-verb agreement `ـتْ`** — when the **doer is female**, the past verb grows a quiet `ـتْ`: `خَرَجَ` → `خَرَجَتْ` ("she went out"), `ذَهَبَ` → `ذَهَبَتْ` ("she went"), `جَلَسَ` → `جَلَسَتْ` ("she sat").
> 4. **The relative pronouns `الَّذِي` / `الَّتِي`** — "the one who / which / that". `الَّذِي` for a masculine noun, `الَّتِي` for a feminine noun. They glue a whole describing sentence onto a noun: `الْفَتَاةُ الَّتِي مَعَكِ` ("the girl **who is** with you").
>
> That's a full plate, so plan a **tight 60 minutes** — and seriously consider **splitting it into two sittings** (Class 1 = greetings + `ـكِ` + the vocative `يَا`; Class 2 = the feminine verb + `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي`). The good news: the new vocabulary is tiny (just the family-aunt words `الْعَمَّة`/`الْخَالَة`, plus `الْفَلَّاح` and `مُسْتَشْفَى الْوِلَادَة`), so the whole hour goes into the grammar.

# Part A · Understanding the chapter

For eleven chapters your class has built a complete little world of **nouns** — naming things, owning them, pointing at them, putting them in places, describing them. Lesson 12 is where Arabic starts to feel like a **conversation**: two people greet each other, ask after each other's families, and describe people with whole clauses ("the girl who is with you", "the child who is with you"). To do that, the lesson hands you four new tools at once.

In one sentence: **Chapter 12 teaches the feminine "you" (`ـكِ`), the calling-word (`يَا`), the feminine ending on a past verb (`ـتْ`), and the relative pronouns (`الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي`) — all woven into a greeting-and-introduction dialogue between two girls.**

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter 12 ek **baat-cheet** wala sabaq hai — do larkiyon (Suʿād aur Āminah) ka dialogue. Char nayi cheezein: (1) `ـكِ` — larki ko "tera/tumhara" (`اسْمُكِ`); (2) `يَا` — kisi ko **bulane/pukaarne** ka lafz (`يَا مُحَمَّدُ`); (3) `ـتْ` — past verb par jab karne wali **aurat** ho (`ذَهَبَتْ` = woh (f.) gayi); (4) `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي` — "jo" (mard ke liye `الَّذِي`, aurat ke liye `الَّتِي`). Char cheezein ek ghante mein bhaari hai — **do baithak** behtar.

## The dialogue (read it first — everything grows out of it)

Here is the conversation the lesson is built on (PDF pp. 63–64). Read it slowly; every new tool appears inside it.

> سُعَادُ : السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكِ وَرَحْمَةُ اللهِ وَبَرَكَاتُهُ .
> الْبِنْتُ : وَعَلَيْكِ السَّلَامُ وَرَحْمَةُ اللهِ وَبَرَكَاتُهُ .
> — كَيْفَ حَالُكِ يَا بِنْتُ ؟
> — أَنَا بِخَيْرٍ ، وَالْحَمْدُ لِلهِ .
> — مِنْ أَيْنَ أَنْتِ ؟
> — أَنَا مِنَ الرِّيَاضِ .
> — مَا اسْمُكِ ؟
> — اسْمِي آمِنَةُ .
> — أَيْنَ أَبُوكِ ؟
> — أَبِي هُنَا فِي الْمَدِينَةِ الْمُنَوَّرَةِ . هُوَ مُوَجِّهٌ فِي الْمَدْرَسَةِ الثَّانَوِيَّةِ .
> — وَأَيْنَ أُمُّكِ ؟
> — هِيَ أَيْضًا هُنَا . هِيَ طَبِيبَةٌ فِي مُسْتَشْفَى الْوِلَادَةِ .
> — وَمَنْ هَذِهِ الْفَتَاةُ الَّتِي مَعَكِ ؟ أَهِيَ أُخْتُكِ ؟
> — لَا . هِيَ بِنْتُ عَمِّي .
> — مَا اسْمُهَا ؟
> — اسْمُهَا فَاطِمَةُ .
> — أَهِيَ زَمِيلَتُكِ ؟
> — لَا . أَنَا فِي الْمَدْرَسَةِ الْمُتَوَسِّطَةِ ، وَهِيَ فِي الْمَدْرَسَةِ الثَّانَوِيَّةِ .
> — أَلَكِ أُخْتٌ ؟
> — لَا . مَا لِي أُخْتٌ .
> — أَلَكِ أَخٌ ؟
> — نَعَمْ . لِي أَخٌ كَبِيرٌ ، وَهُوَ طَالِبٌ بِالْجَامِعَةِ .
> — وَمَنْ هَذَا الطِّفْلُ الَّذِي مَعَكِ ؟
> — هُوَ ابْنُ أَخِي .
> — مَا اسْمُهُ ؟
> — اسْمُهُ سَعْدٌ .
> — أَأُمُّكِ فِي الْبَيْتِ الْآنَ ؟
> — لَا . ذَهَبَتْ إِلَى الْمُسْتَشْفَى .

Notice how natural it is: it is mostly **review** (the tails `اسْمِي`/`اسْمُهَا`, `لِي أَخٌ`, `بِنْتُ عَمِّي`, `هُوَ`/`هِيَ`, prepositions + kasra) stitched together with **four new threads**. Those four threads are the chapter.

**Yaad rakho:** Pehle poora dialogue parho. Zyaada tar **purana** hai (dumein, `لِي أَخٌ`, `هُوَ`/`هِيَ`); sirf char nayi cheezein ismein piroyi gayi hain. Woh char = yeh sabaq.

## 1 · The feminine "you" — `ـكِ`

In Chapter 10 your class learned the four tails, including `ـكَ` ("your", spoken **to a male**): `اسْمُكَ` ("your name"), `بَيْتُكَ` ("your house"). Lesson 12 introduces its twin — `ـكِ`, the same "your", but spoken **to a female**:

> `اسْمُكَ` (to a man) ↔ `اسْمُكِ` (to a woman) — "your name"
> `حَالُكَ` ↔ `حَالُكِ` — "your condition"
> `أَبُوكَ` ↔ `أَبُوكِ` — "your father"
> `عَلَيْكَ` ↔ `عَلَيْكِ` — "upon you"

The only difference is the vowel on the `ك`: **`ـكَ` has a fatḥa (zabar)** for a male, **`ـكِ` has a kasra (zer)** for a female. Everything else stays the same. This is exactly the `هُوَ`/`هِيَ` and `هَذَا`/`هَذِهِ` gender logic the class already owns — Arabic simply marks the gender of the **person you are talking to**, not just the person you are talking about.

The dialogue is full of it because Suʿād is talking to a girl: `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكِ`, `كَيْفَ حَالُكِ`, `مَا اسْمُكِ`, `أَيْنَ أَبُوكِ`, `أُمُّكِ`, `أُخْتُكِ`, `زَمِيلَتُكِ`, `مَعَكِ`, `أَلَكِ أُخْتٌ`.

### The greetings (the lesson's opening)

The lesson opens on the full Islamic greeting — worth teaching as a fixed, beautiful phrase:

> `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكِ وَرَحْمَةُ اللهِ وَبَرَكَاتُهُ` — "Peace be upon you, and the mercy of Allah, and His blessings." (to a female)
> reply: `وَعَلَيْكِ السَّلَامُ وَرَحْمَةُ اللهِ وَبَرَكَاتُهُ`

And the "how are you" exchange:

> `كَيْفَ حَالُكِ ؟` — "How are you?" (lit. "how is your condition?", to a female)
> `أَنَا بِخَيْرٍ ، وَالْحَمْدُ لِلهِ` — "I am well, and praise be to Allah."

> **For you, not the class:** the everyday greeting you all use, `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ`, has the **plural** `ـكُمْ` ("you all"). The lesson here uses the **singular feminine** `عَلَيْكِ` because one girl is greeting one girl. The plural `ـكُمْ` (and `عَلَيْكُمْ`) is **Lesson 14–15** — don't teach it now, even though everyone knows the phrase. Stay on `عَلَيْكِ` (one female) / `عَلَيْكَ` (one male) this lesson. `كَيْفَ` ("how") is a new question word; `بِخَيْرٍ` is `بِـ` ("with/in", Ch 4) + `خَيْر` ("goodness").

**Yaad rakho:** `ـكَ` = "tera/tumhara" **mard** ko (zabar). `ـكِ` = "tera/tumhara" **aurat** ko (zer). Sirf `ك` par harakah ka farq: `اسْمُكَ` (mard) / `اسْمُكِ` (aurat). Dialogue mein har jagah `ـكِ` kyunke Suʿād ek larki se baat kar rahi hai. Greeting: `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكِ` (aurat ko), `كَيْفَ حَالُكِ` = "tum kaisi ho?", jawab `أَنَا بِخَيْرٍ وَالْحَمْدُ لِلهِ`. **Note:** rozmarra wala `عَلَيْكُمْ` (`ـكُمْ` = sab) Lesson 14–15 hai — abhi sirf `عَلَيْكِ` (ek aurat) / `عَلَيْكَ` (ek mard).

## 2 · The vocative `يَا` — calling / addressing someone

`يَا` (yā) is the little word Arabic puts **before a name** when you call to or address that person — exactly like English "O …" or the Urdu **"ai …" / "o …"**:

> `يَا بِنْتُ` — "O girl!"
> `يَا مُحَمَّدُ` — "O Muhammad"
> `يَا فَاطِمَةُ` — "O Fatima"

Its grammatical name is `حَرْفُ النِّدَاء` (the calling-particle) and the called noun is the `مُنَادَى` ("the one called") — but your class does **not** need those names; they need the habit of dropping `يَا` in front of a name when they speak to someone.

### `يَا` + "my …" (the family words)

The most common pattern in the lesson's exercises is `يَا` + a family word carrying the `ـِي` "my" tail (Ch 10):

> `يَا أَبِي` — "O my father" · `يَا أُمِّي` — "O my mother"
> `يَا أَخِي` — "O my brother" · `يَا أُخْتِي` — "O my sister"
> `يَا عَمِّي` / `يَا عَمَّتِي` — "O my (paternal) uncle / aunt"
> `يَا خَالِي` / `يَا خَالَتِي` — "O my (maternal) uncle / aunt"
> `يَا أُسْتَاذِي` / `يَا أُسْتَاذَتِي` — "O my teacher (m / f)"

Notice `أَبِي` keeps the "my"-form you learned in Ch 10 (the `و` of `أَبُو` drops for "my"): `يَا أَبِي`, not `يَا أَبُوي`.

### The drill the lesson actually does (Exercise 2)

The book's Exercise 2 asks the student to **re-address the sentence to a female** by swapping the `مُنَادَى`. So you see masculine→feminine pairs:

> `أَيْنَ قَلَمُكَ يَا أَبِي ؟` → `أَيْنَ قَلَمُكِ يَا أُمِّي ؟`
> `يَا أَخِي` → `يَا أُخْتِي` · `يَا خَالِي` → `يَا خَالَتِي` · `يَا عَمِّي` → `يَا عَمَّتِي`
> `يَا خَالِدُ` → `يَا خَدِيجَةُ` · `يَا حَامِدُ` → `يَا سُعَادُ`

So this exercise quietly drills **both** new threads at once: the vocative `يَا` **and** the `ـكَ`→`ـكِ` switch (because addressing a female flips every "your" to `ـكِ`).

> **For you, not the class:** there is a special vocative form of "O my father" — `يَا أَبَتِ` — which the Qur'an uses (e.g. Yūsuf's speech). This lesson does **not** use it; it uses the plain `يَا أَبِي`. Don't introduce `يَا أَبَتِ` — it will just confuse. Also, a called name normally takes a single ḍamma (`يَا مُحَمَّدُ`, not `يَا مُحَمَّدٌ` — no tanween) — you can let the class read it as printed without naming the rule.

**Yaad rakho:** `يَا` = kisi ko **pukaarne** ka lafz, jaise Urdu "ai / o". `يَا مُحَمَّدُ` (O Muhammad), `يَا بِنْتُ` (O larki). Family lafzon ke saath `ـِي` "mera" ke saath: `يَا أَبِي`, `يَا أُمِّي`, `يَا أُخْتِي`, `يَا خَالَتِي`. Exercise 2 mein larki ko address karte waqt har "your" `ـكِ` ban jaata hai (`قَلَمُكَ يَا أَبِي` → `قَلَمُكِ يَا أُمِّي`) — yani `يَا` aur `ـكِ` ek saath drill hote hain. **Mat parhao:** `يَا أَبَتِ` (special form, Qur'an wali) — yahan sirf `يَا أَبِي`.

## 3 · Feminine past-verb agreement — the `ـتْ` ending

Until now, the past-tense verbs `خَرَجَ` ("went out"), `ذَهَبَ` ("went"), `جَلَسَ` ("sat") were **recognition-only** — the class just read them as fixed words (Ch 5/8). Lesson 12 makes **one** thing about them productive: when the **doer (`الْفَاعِل`) is female**, the verb grows a quiet `ـتْ` (a `ت` with a sukoon) on the end:

> `خَرَجَ مُحَمَّدٌ` ("Muhammad went out") → `خَرَجَتْ آمِنَةُ` ("Āminah went out")
> `ذَهَبَ أَبِي` ("my father went") → `ذَهَبَتْ أُمِّي` ("my mother went")
> `جَلَسَ الطَّالِبُ` ("the student (m) sat") → `جَلَسَتِ الطَّالِبَةُ` ("the student (f) sat")

In the dialogue: `أُمُّكِ … ذَهَبَتْ إِلَى الْمُسْتَشْفَى` ("your mother … went to the hospital"). This is the same gender-matching instinct as `هُوَ`/`هِيَ` and `ـكَ`/`ـكِ` — Arabic just marks the doer's gender on the verb too. The book drills it in **Exercise 3** ("`أَنِّثْ الْفَاعِل`" = "make the doer feminine"): the student takes a sentence with a male doer and rewrites it with a female doer + `ـتْ`.

> **Important scope line:** you are teaching **only** "add `ـتْ` for a female doer" on these three already-known past verbs. You are **not** teaching the past-tense system, the doer's case-ending, or any other person ("you went", "they went", "I went"). The verb conjugation tables are a much later phase — keep the focus razor-thin: *male doer → bare verb; female doer → `ـتْ`*.

### The one small sound-rule (Exercise 4): `ـتْ` → `ـتِ` before `الـ`

The book has a tiny observation exercise (`تَأَمَّلْ` = "observe"):

> `جَلَسَتْ الطَّالِبَةُ` → `جَلَسَتِ الطَّالِبَةُ`  (pattern: `ـْ + ال` → `ـِ + ال`)

When that final `ـتْ` (sukoon) bumps straight into the `الـ` of the next word, the sukoon turns into a **kasra** to smooth the join: `جَلَسَتِ الطَّالِبَةُ` ("the girl sat"), `ذَهَبَتِ الْمُدَرِّسَةُ` ("the (female) teacher went"). This is the **same** smoothing your class already met in `مِنَ الـ` (Ch 4) and `هَمْزَة الْوَصْل` (Ch 5) — Arabic dislikes a sukoon crashing into the `الـ`. Teach it as a **sound**, not a rule: "`خَرَجَتْ`" on its own, but "`خَرَجَتِ الْبِنْتُ`" when `الـ` follows.

**Yaad rakho:** Past verb par jab **karne wali aurat** ho → `ـتْ` lagao: `خَرَجَ` (mard) / `خَرَجَتْ` (aurat), `ذَهَبَ` / `ذَهَبَتْ`, `جَلَسَ` / `جَلَسَتْ`. Wahi `هُوَ`/`هِيَ` wali gender-matching. Dialogue: `ذَهَبَتْ إِلَى الْمُسْتَشْفَى` (woh (f.) hospital gayi). **Sirf itna:** mard → bare verb, aurat → `ـتْ`. Poori verb conjugation **mat** parhao. Chhoti si awaaz-rule: jab `ـتْ` ke baad `الـ` aaye to sukoon **zer** ban jaata hai — `جَلَسَتِ الطَّالِبَةُ` (wahi `مِنَ الـ` wali smoothing).

## 4 · The relative pronouns `الَّذِي` / `الَّتِي` — "the one who / which"

This is the headline grammar of the lesson. In Chapter 9 the class **met** `الَّذِي` but only to recognise it ("a super-adjective"); the full system was deferred to "Lesson 12". This is that lesson — now they **use** it.

`الَّذِي` and `الَّتِي` both mean **"who / which / that"** — they glue a whole describing **sentence** onto a noun, where a single adjective isn't enough:

> single adjective: `الْفَتَاةُ الْجَمِيلَةُ` — "the **beautiful** girl"
> relative clause: `الْفَتَاةُ **الَّتِي مَعَكِ**` — "the girl **who is with you**"

The choice between them is pure **gender-matching** (the class's favourite move):

> **`الَّذِي`** (alladhī) → after a **masculine** noun: `الطِّفْلُ الَّذِي مَعَكِ` ("the child who is with you"), `الْفَتَى الَّذِي خَرَجَ` ("the young man who went out"), `الْمِفْتَاحُ الَّذِي عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ` ("the key which is on the desk").
> **`الَّتِي`** (allatī) → after a **feminine** noun: `الْفَتَاةُ الَّتِي مَعَكِ` ("the girl who is with you"), `السَّيَّارَةُ الَّتِي خَرَجَتْ` ("the car which went out"), `السَّاعَةُ الَّتِي عَلَى السَّرِيرِ` ("the watch which is on the bed").

### The rule that trips Urdu speakers: definite noun only

In Arabic, `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي` are used **only after a definite noun** (one with `الـ`, or a name, etc.). Every example in the lesson proves it: `الْفَتَاةُ` (with `الـ`) + `الَّتِي`; `الطِّفْلُ` (with `الـ`) + `الَّذِي`. You will **not** see `فَتَاةٌ الَّتِي` — for an *indefinite* "a girl who…", Arabic drops the relative pronoun entirely (that's a later subtlety; don't open it). For this lesson the safe, simple shape is:

> **`الـ`-noun + `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي` + describing sentence.**

The describing sentence after it can be a `مَعَكِ` ("with you"), a preposition phrase (`عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ`), or a little verb sentence (`خَرَجَ مِنَ الْبَيْتِ`, `خَرَجَتْ مِنَ الْمَدْرَسَةِ`). Note the feminine-verb agreement from §3 re-fires here: `السَّيَّارَةُ الَّتِي خَرَجَتْ` (feminine noun → `الَّتِي` → feminine verb `خَرَجَتْ`) — three gender-marks lined up.

> **For you, not the class:** `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي` are the *singular* relatives. The duals (`اللَّذَانِ`/`اللَّتَانِ`) and plurals (`الَّذِينَ`/`اللَّاتِي`) are later (the plural arc, L13–15). Don't list the whole grid; teach only the two singulars. Also resist the formal terms (`اسْم مَوْصُول` / `صِلَة`) beyond a passing mention — the book uses `اسْم مَوْصُول` in Exercise 6's instruction, so you may name it once, but don't build a terminology lecture.

**Yaad rakho:** `الَّذِي` / `الَّتِي` = "jo / jis ne / jo cheez". Poora **jumla** ek ism par chipka dete hain (jab ek sifat kaafi na ho): `الْفَتَاةُ الَّتِي مَعَكِ` = "woh larki **jo** tere saath hai". Gender match: **mard** ism → `الَّذِي`; **aurat** ism → `الَّتِي`. **Bara qaida (Urdu wale yahan phisalte hain):** `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي` sirf **khaas (`الـ` wale)** ism ke baad — `الطِّفْلُ الَّذِي…`, `الْفَتَاةُ الَّتِي…`. "Aam" ism ke saath nahin. Aur `الَّتِي` ke baad aksar feminine verb `خَرَجَتْ` aata hai (gender phir match). **Sirf do** (mufrad) parhao — `الَّذِينَ`/dual/plural baad mein (L13–15).

## The big picture — what Chapter 12 silently does

Step back. Chapter 12 is the chapter where the noun-world learns to **talk**:

1. **It completes the "you" axis.** The class had `ـكَ` (to a male); now `ـكِ` (to a female) fills the gap, so they can address either person correctly — and the greetings (`السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكِ`, `كَيْفَ حَالُكِ`) become real, usable speech.
2. **It gives them a way to call out.** `يَا` is tiny but transformative — it turns "Muhammad" into "**O** Muhammad", the start of every direct address.
3. **It cracks open the verb — just a sliver.** `ـتْ` for a female doer is the class's *first* productive change to a verb. It is deliberately the only verb-change taught; everything else about verbs waits.
4. **It upgrades description from a word to a clause.** `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي` let them say not just "the beautiful girl" but "the girl **who is sitting in front of the teacher**" — a huge expressive jump, and the natural next step after the adjective agreement of Ch 9.

Every one of the four reuses the gender-matching reflex the class has drilled since `هُوَ`/`هِيَ`: male vs female on "you" (`ـكَ`/`ـكِ`), on the verb (`خَرَجَ`/`خَرَجَتْ`), and on the relative (`الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي`). Frame the whole chapter that way and the four "new" things become **one** familiar instinct applied in three new places, plus the little calling-word `يَا`.

**Yaad rakho:** Ch 12 woh sabaq hai jahan ism-ki-duniya **bolna** seekhti hai: greeting (`عَلَيْكِ`), pukaarna (`يَا`), verb par gender (`ذَهَبَتْ`), aur poore jumle se sifat (`الَّتِي مَعَكِ`). Char cheezein lekin ek hi purani aadat — **gender-matching** (`هُوَ`/`هِيَ` wali) — teen nayi jagahon par + chhota sa `يَا`. Isi tarah samjhao to bojh aadha ho jaata hai.

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

Now you understand the chapter. This section is how to *deliver* it. This is a **grammar-heavy** lesson (four new threads), so the single most important decision is **pacing**: do not try to cram all four into one rushed hour.

> **⏱️ Pacing — strongly consider two sittings.** Lesson 12 has four new things. A clean split:
> - **Class 1 (≈55 min):** the dialogue read-through + the feminine `ـكِ` + the greetings + the vocative `يَا`. (The "talking" half.)
> - **Class 2 (≈55 min):** feminine past-verb `ـتْ` (+ the `ـتِ`-before-`الـ` sound) + the relative pronouns `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي`. (The "grammar" half.)
>
> If you must do it in one hour, the one-sitting plan below is tight — protect the time for `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي`, which is the deepest idea.

## The landmines for Urdu-speaking adult learners

### Landmine 1 · Using `ـكَ` for a woman (or `ـكِ` for a man)

Urdu's "tera/tumhara" is **gender-neutral** — there's no spoken difference for addressing a man vs a woman. So classmates will say `اسْمُكَ` to a woman or `اسْمُكِ` to a man without noticing.

**Counter-strategy:** Make it physical. Point at a male classmate: "`اسْمُكَ`?" (zabar). Point at a female: "`اسْمُكِ`?" (zer). Run a quick round-the-room where the answer's tail must match the person you point at. Tie it to the gender reflex they already have (`هُوَ`/`هِيَ`).

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Urdu mein 'tumhara' har kisi ke liye ek hi hai — lekin Arabic mein farq hai. **Mard** ko: `ـكَ` (zabar) — `اسْمُكَ`. **Aurat** ko: `ـكِ` (zer) — `اسْمُكِ`. Wahi `هُوَ`/`هِيَ` wala gender — ab us shakhs par jis se baat kar rahe ho. Mard ko `ـكِ` ya aurat ko `ـكَ` ghalat."*

### Landmine 2 · Forgetting `يَا` before a name (or mispronouncing the called name with tanween)

In Urdu you can call someone just by their name. Classmates may drop `يَا`, or add a tanween to the called name (`يَا مُحَمَّدٌ` ✗).

**Counter-strategy:** Drill `يَا` + name as a fixed habit: "to call someone, put `يَا` first." Read the Exercise-2 list aloud as call-outs. Let them read the single ḍamma on the name (`يَا مُحَمَّدُ`) as printed — don't lecture the no-tanween rule, just model it.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Kisi ko bulana ho to naam se pehle `يَا` lagao: `يَا مُحَمَّدُ` (O Muhammad), `يَا فَاطِمَةُ`. Family ke liye `ـِي` 'mera' ke saath: `يَا أَبِي`, `يَا أُخْتِي`, `يَا خَالَتِي`. Bulaye gaye naam par tanween nahin — sirf ek pesh: `يَا مُحَمَّدُ`, `يَا مُحَمَّدٌ` nahin."*

### Landmine 3 · Forgetting the `ـتْ` for a female doer (or over-using it)

Two opposite errors: (a) saying `ذَهَبَ أُمِّي` ✗ (no `ـتْ`) for a female doer, or (b) the keen student adding `ـتْ` everywhere, even to a male doer (`خَرَجَتْ مُحَمَّدٌ` ✗).

**Counter-strategy:** Two-column board: **male doer → bare verb** (`خَرَجَ مُحَمَّدٌ`) | **female doer → `ـتْ`** (`خَرَجَتْ آمِنَةُ`). Do Exercise 3 together: read the male-doer sentence, then "make the doer a woman" and add `ـتْ`. Keep repeating: *the `ـتْ` follows the **doer's** gender.*

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Verb par `ـتْ` sirf jab **karne wali aurat** ho. `خَرَجَ مُحَمَّدٌ` (mard, bare) — `خَرَجَتْ آمِنَةُ` (aurat, `ـتْ`). Mard ke saath `ـتْ` mat lagao (`خَرَجَتْ مُحَمَّدٌ` ghalat), aur aurat ke saath `ـتْ` mat bhoolo. Yeh `ـتْ` **fāʿil** (karne wale) ke gender par hai."*

### Landmine 4 · Using `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي` with the wrong gender — or after an indefinite noun

(a) Gender mismatch: `الْفَتَاةُ الَّذِي` ✗ (should be `الَّتِي`). (b) The deeper Urdu trap: Urdu "jo" works after *any* noun, so classmates will try `فَتَاةٌ الَّتِي` ✗ (relative after an indefinite noun).

**Counter-strategy:** For (a), reuse the gender reflex: "feminine noun → `الَّتِي`, like `هَذِهِ`." For (b), give one firm rule with a visual: **`الـ`-noun first, *then* `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي`.** Box the antecedent's `الـ` on the board so they see the definite article is the "ticket" to use a relative. Drill only definite examples (the lesson only gives definite ones).

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي` ka gender ism se match: mard ism → `الَّذِي`, aurat ism → `الَّتِي` (`هَذَا`/`هَذِهِ` ki tarah). Aur **bara qaida:** `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي` sirf **khaas (`الـ` wale)** ism ke baad — `الطِّفْلُ الَّذِي`, `الْفَتَاةُ الَّتِي`. Urdu 'jo' har jagah chalta hai, Arabic mein nahin: 'aam' ism ke saath relative nahin lagta (woh baad ki baat hai)."*

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 12

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum baad ke sabaq mein dekhenge"*:

- ❌ **The plural "you" `ـكُمْ` / `عَلَيْكُمْ`** — even though everyone says `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ` daily. This lesson is **singular** (`عَلَيْكِ` / `عَلَيْكَ`). Plural tails are **Lesson 14–15**.
- ❌ **The full verb-conjugation system.** You teach **only** "add `ـتْ` for a female doer" on the three known past verbs (`خَرَجَ`/`ذَهَبَ`/`جَلَسَ`). No "you went / they went / I went", no present tense (`مُضَارِع`), no doer's case-name (`فَاعِل` rafʿ rule).
- ❌ **The special vocative `يَا أَبَتِ`** (and `يَا أَخَتِ` etc.). The lesson uses plain `يَا أَبِي`. Don't introduce the `ـَتِ` vocative form.
- ❌ **The dual & plural relatives** (`اللَّذَانِ`/`اللَّتَانِ`, `الَّذِينَ`/`اللَّاتِي`). Only the two singulars `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي`.
- ❌ **Relative pronoun after an indefinite noun** (the "dropped relative" subtlety) — keep every example a definite `الـ`-noun.
- ❌ **The diptote (`غَيْر مُنْصَرِف`) rule** — names like `آمِنَةُ`, `فَاطِمَةُ`, `خَدِيجَةُ`, `سُعَادُ` take no tanween; say *"khaas naam hain"* and don't open the rule (Lesson 14+).
- ❌ **The formal `نَصْب`/case terminology** and the grammatical names `حَرْف النِّدَاء`/`مُنَادَى`/`صِلَة` beyond a single passing mention of `اسْم مَوْصُول` (which the book itself uses in Exercise 6).

**Yaad rakho:** `عَلَيْكُمْ` (plural) — Lesson 14–15, abhi sirf `عَلَيْكِ`/`عَلَيْكَ`. Poori verb conjugation **mat** — sirf "aurat → `ـتْ`". `يَا أَبَتِ` (special form) mat. Dual/plural relatives (`الَّذِينَ`…) mat — sirf `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي`. "Aam" ism ke saath relative mat. Diptote naam — bata do, rule mat kholo. Grammar ke formal naam (`مُنَادَى`/`صِلَة`) mat ginwaao.

## Self-check before teaching

Before the class, make sure:

- [ ] You can run the full greeting both ways: `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكِ` (to a woman) / `عَلَيْكَ` (to a man), and `كَيْفَ حَالُكِ ؟ — أَنَا بِخَيْرٍ وَالْحَمْدُ لِلهِ`.

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Do they match the "you" tail to the person — `ـكَ` to a man, `ـكِ` to a woman? *(Today's `ـكِ`.)*
2. Do they put `يَا` before a name when calling, with a single ḍamma (no tanween)? *(The vocative.)*
3. Do they add `ـتْ` for a female doer — and *not* for a male doer? *(Feminine verb agreement.)*
4. Do they read `جَلَسَتِ الطَّالِبَةُ` with a kasra (not `جَلَسَتْ الطَّالِبَةُ` with a clash)? *(The `الـ` smoothing.)*
5. Do they pick `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي` by the noun's gender? *(Gender match.)*
6. Do they use `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي` only after a definite `الـ`-noun? *(The definite rule.)*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh sabaq class ko **bolna** sikhata hai — isliye diagnosis: kya "you" ka gender sahi (`ـكَ`/`ـكِ`)? Kya `يَا` lag raha hai? Kya aurat doer par `ـتْ` aa raha hai (aur mard par nahin)? Kya `جَلَسَتِ الطَّالِبَةُ` zer se parh rahe hain? Kya `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي` ka gender match hai aur sirf `الـ` wale ism ke baad? Agla sabaq (Lesson 13): **jama** (plurals) — `هَؤُلَاءِ`/`أُولَئِكَ`, plural pronouns. Aaj uska zikr nahin.
