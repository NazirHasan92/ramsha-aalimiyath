🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Point to a distant object and say "That is a ___" in Arabic, using ذَلِكَ.
  2. Contrast near vs far masculine demonstratives — هَذَا vs ذَلِكَ.
  3. Ask "What is this?" using مَا هَذَا ؟ — for non-humans.
  4. Ask "Who is this?" using مَنْ هَذَا ؟ — for humans.
  5. Use أَمْ to offer a choice in a question: "Is this X or Y?".
  6. Pronounce ذ correctly — like English th in "this", not z.
  7. Recognize, write, and use ~16 new common nouns (river, sea, mountain, milk, honey, friend, imam, etc.).

1 · ذَلِكَ — "That" (far masculine)

The partner of last week's هَذَا — same job, different distance.

ذَلِكَ

Meaning
"That" — used when pointing to something far (not within reach).
Roman Urdu
"Woh" / "woh hai" (jab kisi door wali cheez ki taraf ishaara kar rahe hon).
Important
Three things to remember:
  1. For singular masculine far objects only. (Feminine "that" — تِلْكَ — comes much later.)
  2. Pronounce the ذ like English "th" in "this". NOT "z". So: dhaa-li-ka, not zaa-li-ka.
  3. The middle alif is silent — like هَذَا last week. Say "dhaa-li-ka", not "dhaa-AA-li-ka".
Example
ذَلِكَ بَيْتٌ dhaalika baytun · "That is a house" · "Woh ek ghar hai"

The near/far pair — قَرِيب وَ بَعِيد

Near
هَذَا قَلَمٌ haadhaa qalamun · "This is a pen" · "Yeh ek qalam hai"
Far
ذَلِكَ قَلَمٌ dhaalika qalamun · "That is a pen" · "Woh ek qalam hai"
Rule
Same noun — different demonstrative depending on whether the object is near (in reach) or far (across the room, outside, etc.).
Roman
Wahi lafz "qalam" — par jab qareeb ho to "haadhaa", jab door ho to "dhaalika". Hath se ishaara karke hamesha sikhao — bina point kiye yeh dono ek jaise lagte hain.

💡 Teaching tip — make it physical

Put two identical objects in the room — one on the table, one on the windowsill. Walk to the table, point: "haadhaa qalamun". Walk to the window, point: "dhaalika qalamun". Have classmates do the same. The body's distance feel anchors the rule — it's not a vocabulary problem, it's a spatial-pointing problem.

⚠️ Pronunciation watch — the most common Ch 2 mistake

Urdu speakers will say zaalika. The Arabic ذ is NOT z — it's th (as in English this, that, the). Drill this from minute one: have classmates say English "this" slowly, feel the tongue between the teeth, then flip to "dhaa-li-ka". Without this fix, every ذ word for the rest of the book will be mispronounced.

2 · مَا هَذَا ؟ — "What is this?"

The question word for things, animals, and non-humans.

Where this fits: Ch 1 gave students one question form — yes/no (أَ). This is their second question form — "what?" — used to ask about non-humans.

The "maa" question pattern

English
مَا means "what?" — used at the start of a sentence to ask about a thing/animal.
Roman Urdu
"Maa" = "kya?" — sirf cheezon, jaanwaron aur ghair-insaan ke baare mein sawal poochne ke liye.
Pattern
Q: مَا هَذَا ؟ (maa haadhaa?)
A: هَذَا ___ (haadhaa ___)
Example
مَا هَذَا ؟ — هَذَا كِتَابٌ maa haadhaa? — haadhaa kitaabun. · "What is this? — This is a book." · "Yeh kya hai? — Yeh ek kitaab hai."
مَا ذَلِكَ ؟ — ذَلِكَ نَهْرٌ maa dhaalika? — dhaalika nahrun. · "What is that? — That is a river." · "Woh kya hai? — Woh ek nahar hai."

💡 Teaching tip

The answer to maa haadhaa? is a Chapter 1 sentence. You're not teaching new sentence structure — you're teaching a new way to trigger last week's pattern. Use this to your advantage: every maa drill secretly re-drills Ch 1.

⚠️ The Arabic question mark

Use ؟ (mirrored question mark) at the end of Arabic questions, not ?. Right-to-left writing — everything reverses, including punctuation.

3 · مَنْ هَذَا ؟ — "Who is this?"

The question word for humans only.

Where this fits: partner to مَا. Together they cover every "what/who is this?" situation. The split is grammatically active in Arabic — humans get their own question word.

The "man" question pattern

English
مَنْ means "who?" — used at the start of a sentence to ask about a person.
Roman Urdu
"Man" = "kaun?" — sirf logon (insaanon) ke baare mein sawal poochne ke liye.
Rule
Arabic forces the split: man for humans, maa for non-humans. This is grammatical, not a politeness choice. The line is hard.
Roman
Yeh strict rule hai — Urdu mein "kya" har cheez par chal jaata hai, par Arabic mein insaan-cheez ka split pakka hai. Insaan → man, cheez → maa.
Example
مَنْ هَذَا ؟ — هَذَا وَلَدٌ man haadhaa? — haadhaa waladun. · "Who is this? — This is a boy." · "Yeh kaun hai? — Yeh ek ladka hai."
مَنْ ذَلِكَ ؟ — ذَلِكَ خَالِي man dhaalika? — dhaalika khaalee. · "Who is that? — That is my maternal uncle." · "Woh kaun hai? — Woh mera maamoon hai."

💡 Teaching tip — drill the pair together

Don't teach مَا and مَنْ in separate slots. Drill them alternating: pen → maa, classmate's face → man, book → maa, photo of teacher → man, key → maa. After 10 alternations the classmates' brains will form the insaan/cheez reflex automatically.

⚠️ Common mistake

Classmates will reach for maa when pointing at a person — Urdu's kya defaults dominate. The fix: deliberately model the wrong version, let them hear how it sounds, and correct in the same breath. "Maa haadhaa?" — point at a classmate — pause — "galat. Insaan ke liye man."

4 · أَمْ — "or" in questions

The word that lets a question offer a choice between two options.

Where this fits: Ch 1's question particle أَ only gave yes/no. أَمْ upgrades that — now the question can offer two options, and the answer becomes a full sentence (not yes/no).

The "am" pattern

English
أَمْ = "or" — used inside a question to offer two choices. The asker expects the listener to pick one.
Roman Urdu
"Am" = "ya" — sawal ke andar do options dene ke liye. Jaisa Urdu mein: "kya yeh A hai ya B?"
Structure
أَ + [option 1] + أَمْ + [option 2] + ؟
Both options stay in their indefinite form (with tanween).
Example
أَهَذَا قَلَمٌ أَمْ مِفْتَاحٌ ؟ — هَذَا قَلَمٌ a-haadhaa qalamun am miftaaḥun? — haadhaa qalamun. · "Is this a pen or a key? — This is a pen." · "Kya yeh qalam hai ya chaabi? — Yeh qalam hai."
أَذَلِكَ جَبَلٌ أَمْ حَجَرٌ ؟ — ذَلِكَ جَبَلٌ a-dhaalika jabalun am ḥajarun? — dhaalika jabalun. · "Is that a mountain or a stone? — That is a mountain." · "Woh pahaad hai ya patthar? — Woh pahaad hai."

The "full-sentence answer" rule

Rule
Do not answer with naʿam (yes) or laa (no). Do not answer with just the noun. The answer must be a full nominal sentence.
Roman
"Am" wale sawal ka jawab "haan/nahin" nahin chalega. Aur sirf naam ("qalamun") bhi kaafi nahin. Poora Ch 1 wala jumla chahiye: "haadhaa qalamun".
Why?
An am question isn't yes/no — it's "pick which one". So the answer has to *show* the choice, not just acknowledge it.

💡 Teaching tip — model the wrong answer first

Ask "a-haadhaa qalamun am miftaaḥun?". Pause. Say in a confused tone: "naʿam? — yeh kaafi nahin. Qalamun? — yeh bhi adhoora. Poora jumla: haadhaa qalamun." Demonstrating the wrong half-answers locks the full-answer rule in place.

⚠️ Don't pre-teach أَوْ

Arabic has a second word for "or" — أَوْ — used for general "or" outside questions. It's not in this chapter. If classmates ask, say "yeh aage chapters mein milega — abhi sirf أَمْ, sawal ke andar wala".

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Saying "zaalika" instead of "dhaalika"

The single most common Ch 2 error. Urdu treats ذ as a z sound. The Arabic ذ is the English th in "this". Drill: have classmates say English "this" slowly, feel the tongue between the teeth, then flip to "dhaa-li-ka" — same tongue position. If you let this slide on day one, every ذ word for the next 20 chapters will be wrong.

2. Lengthening the silent alif in ذَلِكَ

Classmates may say "dhaa-AA-li-ka" — over-stressing the middle. Correct: it's "dhaa-li-ka" — the alif represents the "aa" sound in "dhaa", not a separate syllable. Same trick as هَذَا last week.

3. Using مَا for humans

Classmates point at a person and ask "maa haadhaa?" — Urdu's kya defaults bleed through. Correct: humans need مَنْ. The split is grammatical, not optional. Drill alternating items so they form the reflex.

4. Half-answering an "am" question

"a-haadhaa qalamun am miftaaḥun?" — classmates answer with "naʿam", or just "qalamun". Neither is correct. The answer must be a full Ch 1 sentence: "haadhaa qalamun". Model the wrong versions out loud so they hear them as incomplete.

5. Not pointing physically

If classmates just say هَذَا and ذَلِكَ without gesturing, the distinction collapses in their heads — both words feel like "this". Insist on physical pointing: arm raised for far, hand close for near. Body anchors the rule.

📿 Connect to the Qur'an

Adult learners stay motivated when they see Chapter 2 grammar in the actual Qur'an. Use 1–2 of these in class.

Surah Al-Baqarah · 2:2

ذَٰلِكَ الْكِتَابُ لَا رَيْبَ فِيهِ

dhaalika al-kitaabu laa rayba fīh — "That is the Book — there is no doubt in it."

Students recognise: ذَلِكَ — the far masculine demonstrative, just learned today! Also الْكِتَابُ = the Book, with الـ from Ch 1.

Surah Al-Baqarah · 2:6

إِنَّ الَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا سَوَاءٌ عَلَيْهِمْ أَأَنذَرْتَهُمْ أَمْ لَمْ تُنذِرْهُمْ لَا يُؤْمِنُونَ

...sawaa'un ʿalayhim a-andhartahum am lam tundhirhum... — "It is the same for them whether you warn them or do not warn them..."

Students recognise: أَمْ — the "or" in a choice question, just learned today!

Surah Al-Baqarah · 2:31

قَالَ أَنبِئُونِي بِأَسْمَاءِ هَٰؤُلَاءِ

qaala anbi'ūnī bi-asmaa'i haa'ulaa'i — "He said: 'Inform me of the names of these.'"

Students recognise: the هَـٰ at the start of هَؤُلَاءِ — same family as هَذَا. (The plural هَؤُلَاءِ "these" is from the same demonstrative family — but tell them we'll meet it formally much later.)
