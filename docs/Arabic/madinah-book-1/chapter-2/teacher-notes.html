🪶 A light, confidence-building lesson

Only one item is genuinely new in Lesson 2: ذَلِكَ ("that", far). The question words مَا / مَنْ and the question-answer pattern أَ … نَعَمْ / لَا are all review from Lesson 1, here re-drilled with the new far demonstrative. Teach it as "one new word + comfortable practice", not five new things.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Point to a far masculine thing and name it with ذَلِكَ ("that") — and contrast it with هَذَا ("this").
  2. Pronounce ذَلِكَ correctly: soft ذ (dh, not z), a hidden long ā, and a crisp final -ka.
  3. Ask مَا ذَلِكَ ؟ ("what is that?") for things and مَنْ ذَلِكَ ؟ ("who is that?") for people.
  4. Ask a yes/no question with أَ and answer it with نَعَمْ or with لَا + a full re-statement.
  5. Recognise and use the 4 new words — إِمَام، حَجَر، سُكَّر، لَبَن — plus the Lesson-1 nouns the lesson re-uses.

ذَلِكَ — the far demonstrative

Meaning
ذَلِكَ = "that" — pointing to a masculine thing that is far. It is the distant counterpart of هَذَا ("this", near).
Roman Urdu
ذَلِكَ = "woh" (jo door hai). هَذَا = "yeh" (jo paas hai). Yeh mapping bilkul theek baithta hai — yeh/woh.
Important / Rule
Written ذلك (no alif), but said with a hidden long ā — exactly like هَذَا. The ذ is the soft "th" of that (not "z"); the final ـكَ is "-ka" with a fatḥa.
Example
هَذَا مَسْجِدٌ وَذَلِكَ بَيْتٌ hādhā masjidun wa-dhālika baytun · "This is a mosque and that is a house" · "Yeh masjid hai aur woh ghar hai"

💡 Teaching tip — point with your body

Touch a near object → "hādhā …"; point across the room → "dhālika …". Make the class copy the gesture as they say the word. The Urdu yeh/woh split makes this click instantly — anchor every ذَلِكَ to "woh".

⚠️ The ذ sound returns from Lesson 1

Drill هَذَا and ذَلِكَ together so the class hears the same soft ذ twice: hā-dhā, dhā-li-ka — never "zālika". Tongue lightly on the teeth.

2 · Asking — مَا ذَلِكَ ؟ / مَنْ ذَلِكَ ؟

The same two question words from Lesson 1, now aimed at the far thing. Arabic keeps a strict human / non-human split.

Where this fits: in Lesson 1 the class asked مَا هَذَا ؟ / مَنْ هَذَا ؟. Lesson 2 simply swaps in ذَلِكَ — no new question word.

مَنْ for people · مَا for things

Meaning
مَنْ ذَلِكَ ؟ = "Who is that?" (for a person). مَا ذَلِكَ ؟ = "What is that?" (for a thing or animal).
Roman Urdu
مَنْ = "kaun" (sirf insaan). مَا = "kya" (cheez ya jaanwar). Ghoda/gadha/kutta/billi bhi مَا — kyunke insaan nahin.
Important / Rule
Animals are non-human → always مَا. Only people take مَنْ. This human/non-human line is grammatically real in Arabic — but for now teach only the surface rule.
Example
مَنْ هَذَا وَمَنْ ذَلِكَ ؟ — هَذَا مُدَرِّسٌ وَذَلِكَ إِمَامٌ man hādhā wa-man dhālika? — hādhā mudarrisun wa-dhālika imāmun · "Who is this and who is that? — This is a teacher and that is an imam"

💡 Teaching tip — sort the room

Point at people → force مَنْ; point at the star, stone, bed, animals → force مَا. Rapid alternation drills the split better than any explanation.

3 · The Question أَ and the Answer لَا

Pure Lesson-1 review, spotlighted: ask with أَ, and if the guess is wrong, answer لَا + the correct identification.

Where this fits: Lesson 1 introduced أَ ("is…?") with the answers نَعَمْ / لَا. Lesson 2 reuses them with ذَلِكَ and drills the courteous full "no" answer.

Ask with أَ, answer with نَعَمْ / لَا

Meaning
Glue أَ to the front to make a yes/no question. Answer "yes" with نَعَمْ, or "no" with لَا — then re-state what it really is.
Roman Urdu
أَ saamne lagao = sawaal ("kya yeh…?"). Jawaab: نَعَمْ (haan) / لَا (nahin). Phir poora jumla bhi do — "لَا، ذَلِكَ قِطٌّ".
Important / Rule
لَا here is just the spoken "no" that opens a correction — not a sentence-negation rule. Don't extend it. The full answer (no + re-statement) is the polite, complete form.
Example
أَذَلِكَ كَلْبٌ ؟ — لَا، ذَلِكَ قِطٌّ a-dhālika kalbun? — lā, dhālika qiṭṭun · "Is that a dog? — No, that is a cat" · "Kya woh kutta hai? — Nahin, woh billi hai"

⚠️ Answer in a full sentence

Discourage a bare "lā". The complete answer corrects and re-states: لَا، ذَلِكَ قِطٌّ. This is good manners and doubles as more ذَلِكَ practice.

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Saying "zālika" instead of "dhālika"

Urdu speakers read ذ as "z". It is the soft "th" of English that. Drill it together with هَذَا so they hear the same ذ twice.

2. Dropping the hidden long ā ("dha-li-ka")

No alif is written, so the long vowel gets lost. Stretch it when modelling: dhā-li-ka, just like the long ā in هَذَا.

3. Using هَذَا for far things

Classmates default to the word they know best. Anchor the Urdu split: هَذَا = yeh (near), ذَلِكَ = woh (far). Gesture as you say it.

4. Using مَا for people

"mā hādhā?" pointed at the imam. Fix on sight: a person always takes مَنْ. Things and animals take مَا.

5. A bare "lā" with no re-statement

Push for the full answer: لَا، ذَلِكَ قِطٌّ — "No, that is a cat." Correct and re-state.

📿 Connect to the Qur'an — ذَٰلِكَ in the very first āyah of al-Baqarah

Lesson 2 teaches no Qur'anic vocabulary directly — but its one new word, ذَلِكَ, opens the most famous early āyah of the Qur'an. Show the class they can already spot it.

Surah Al-Baqarah · 2:2

ذَٰلِكَ الْكِتَابُ لَا رَيْبَ ۛ فِيهِ

dhālika l-kitābu lā rayba fīh — "That is the Book; there is no doubt in it."

Spot it: the āyah opens with ذَٰلِكَ — the very "that" they learned today (here with the small dagger-alif written out). And the same لَا "no/not" they used in the dog/cat answer appears as لَا رَيْبَ — "no doubt". Two Lesson-2 words, live in the Qur'an's opening page.

💡 How to use this in class

Write only the first two words on the board: ذَٰلِكَ … and ask "what does ذَٰلِكَ mean?" They will answer "that / woh". That recognition moment — finding today's word in the Qur'an — is the emotional pay-off of the lesson. Don't dwell on the other words yet.
