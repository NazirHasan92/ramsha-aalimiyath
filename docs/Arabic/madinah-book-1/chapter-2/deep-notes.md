# Part A · Understanding the chapter

Lesson 2 is a small, gentle lesson — and that is by design. Lesson 1 gave your classmates *one* pointing word: **هَذَا** ("this", for something near). Lesson 2 hands them its twin: **ذَلِكَ** ("that", for something far). That is the **only genuinely new building block in the whole lesson.**

Everything else on these two pages — **مَا** ("what?"), **مَنْ** ("who?"), the question hamza **أَ** ("is…?"), and the answers **نَعَمْ** ("yes") / **لَا** ("no") — your classmates already met in Lesson 1. Lesson 2 simply takes that whole Lesson-1 toolkit and re-runs it on the *far* pointing word. So the lesson really does two things:

1. **Introduces `ذَلِكَ`** — "that" — the far counterpart of `هَذَا`.
2. **Re-drills the Lesson-1 question/answer machinery** (`مَا` / `مَنْ` / `أَ` / `نَعَمْ` / `لَا`) — now pointing into the distance.

> **Be honest with yourself about what's "new" here.** If you teach this as "five new things", you will overwhelm the class with material they already know and dilute the one thing that matters. The lesson is *one new word* (`ذَلِكَ`) plus a lot of comfortable review. Treat it that way — it's a confidence-building lesson.

**Yaad rakho:** Lesson 2 mein sirf **ek** nayi cheez hai — `ذَلِكَ` ("woh", door wali cheez ke liye). Baaqi sab — `ما`، `من`، `أ`، `نعم`، `لا` — Lesson 1 ka maal hai, bas ab door wali cheez par dobara istemaal ho raha hai.

## 1 · `ذَلِكَ` — the far demonstrative

Arabic, like Urdu, distinguishes *near* from *far* when pointing:

| | Arabic | reads as | meaning | Urdu |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **near** | `هَذَا` | *hādhā* | this (near, masculine) | yeh |
| **far** | `ذَلِكَ` | *dhālika* | that (far, masculine) | woh |

This maps onto Urdu almost perfectly: **`هَذَا` = yeh, `ذَلِكَ` = woh.** That single mapping is the most powerful teaching tool in the whole lesson — lean on it.

Both words are **masculine** demonstratives. (The feminine pair — `هَذِهِ` "this" and `تِلْكَ` "that" — belong to later lessons. Don't open them; every noun in this lesson is masculine.)

### The silent "long alif" inside `ذَلِكَ`

Look carefully at how the book spells it: **ذلك** — just three letters (dhāl, lām, kāf), no alif written between the dhāl and the lām. Yet you *pronounce* a long **ā**: "dhā‑li‑ka". The long vowel is there in the sound but hidden in the spelling.

This is the **exact same trick** your classmates already swallowed in Lesson 1 with `هَذَا` (written hā‑dhā, pronounced with a long ā that isn't fully written). So you don't need to explain it as a new rule — you can say "same hidden long-alif as in `هَذَا`; just copy how I say it." (The technical name for this dagger-alif convention comes much later in the book — don't name it now.)

> Note on the title page: the book stretches the title decoratively as **ذَالِــكَ** (with a visible alif). In the actual lesson sentences it is written **ذَلِكَ**. Teach the body spelling.

### The `ذ` sound — carry the Lesson-1 warning forward

`ذَلِكَ` opens with **ذ** — the soft "th" of English *this/that* (IPA /ð/), **not** a "z". So it is *dhā*‑li‑ka, never *zā*‑li‑ka. This is the very same `ذ`/`z` landmine your classmates fought in Lesson 1's `هَذَا` — and it will come straight back, because Urdu speakers habitually read `ذ` as "z".

**Yaad rakho:** `ذَلِكَ` = *dhā‑li‑ka* (woh). `ذ` woh naram "th" hai (jaise English "this"), "z" nahin. Aur beech mein ek chhupa hua lamba "ā" hai — bilkul `هَذَا` jaisa. End mein `ـكَ` = "ka" (zabar ke saath), "ki/ke" nahin.

## 2 · Asking about "that" — `مَا ذَلِكَ ؟` and `مَنْ ذَلِكَ ؟`

Once your classmates can point far, they can ask about far things. The two question words are the **same two from Lesson 1** — Arabic just keeps the human / non-human split alive:

- **`مَا ذَلِكَ ؟`** — *mā dhālika?* — "What is that?" → for **things and animals** (non-human).
- **`مَنْ ذَلِكَ ؟`** — *man dhālika?* — "Who is that?" → for **people** (human).

From the lesson's own sentences:

> `مَا ذَلِكَ ؟ — ذَلِكَ نَجْمٌ` · *mā dhālika? — dhālika najmun* · "What is that? — That is a star."
> `مَنْ هَذَا وَمَنْ ذَلِكَ ؟ — هَذَا مُدَرِّسٌ وَذَلِكَ إِمَامٌ` · *man hādhā wa-man dhālika? — hādhā mudarrisun wa-dhālika imāmun* · "Who is this and who is that? — This is a teacher and that is an imam."

### Why Arabic splits "what" from "who"

In English one word can blur the line, and Urdu's "kaun/kya" splits loosely. Arabic is strict: a **person** is asked about with `مَنْ`, a **thing or animal** with `مَا`. Animals count as `مَا` (non-human) — that's why the lesson's horse, donkey, dog, and cat are all introduced by pointing / `مَا`, never `مَنْ`.

This human/non-human line is not a quirk — it runs deep through Arabic grammar (it resurfaces later in agreement and plurals). For now your classmates only need the surface rule: **person → `مَنْ`, everything else → `مَا`.**

**Yaad rakho:** `مَنْ` = "kaun" (sirf insaan ke liye). `مَا` = "kya" (cheez ya jaanwar ke liye). Ghoda, gadha, kutta, billi — sab `مَا` (kyunke insaan nahin). Yeh dono lafz naye nahin — Lesson 1 se aaye hain, bas ab `ذَلِكَ` ke saath.

## 3 · The yes/no question `أَ` and its answers `نَعَمْ` / `لَا`

The third strand is, again, pure Lesson-1 review — now featured prominently because the lesson wants to drill the **"no" answer**:

> `أَذَلِكَ كَلْبٌ ؟ — لَا، ذَلِكَ قِطٌّ` · *a‑dhālika kalbun? — lā, dhālika qiṭṭun* · "Is that a dog? — No, that is a cat."

Three pieces, all from Lesson 1:

- **`أَ`** — the question hamza, glued to the front: `أَذَلِكَ` = "Is that…?" (Lesson 1.)
- **`نَعَمْ`** — "yes" (Lesson 1).
- **`لَا`** — "no" (Lesson 1) — the piece Lesson 2 puts in the spotlight.

The pattern is: **ask with `أَ` → if the guess is wrong, answer `لَا` + the correct identification.** Notice the answer is a *full* sentence: not just "لَا", but "لَا، ذَلِكَ قِطٌّ" ("No, that is a cat"). That full-answer habit — correct *and* re-state — is the courteous, complete way to answer, and it doubles as more `ذَلِكَ` practice.

> **`لَا` here is simply the answer "no" — not yet a grammatical negation tool.** Arabic later uses `لَا` and other particles to *negate sentences* ("this is **not** a…"), but Lesson 2 does **not** go there. Here `لَا` only means the spoken "no" that opens a correction. Don't extend it into sentence-negation rules — that's for a later lesson.

**Yaad rakho:** `أَ` saamne lagao = sawaal ("kya yeh…?"). Jawaab: `نَعَمْ` (haan) ya `لَا` (nahin). Aur "نَعَمْ/لَا" ke baad poora jumla bhi do — `لَا، ذَلِكَ قِطٌّ` ("nahin, woh billi hai"). `لَا` yahan sirf "nahin" hai — abhi koi grammar-negation rule nahin.

---

## The big picture — what this chapter is silently doing

Step back. Lesson 2 looks like several topics but is really **one new word riding on familiar tools**:

1. **`ذَلِكَ`** doubles your classmates' pointing range — they can now name *near* (`هَذَا`) **and** *far* (`ذَلِكَ`) masculine things.
2. The **`مَا` / `مَنْ`** split and the **`أَ` … `نَعَمْ` / `لَا`** question-answer pattern are simply re-exercised — consolidation, not new load.
3. The four new vocabulary words (`إِمَام`, `حَجَر`, `سُكَّر`, `لَبَن`) plus a handful of Lesson-1 nouns give plenty to point at.

By the end your classmates can run a complete little pointing dialogue: *point near → "hādhā …", point far → "dhālika …", ask "mā/man dhālika?", answer with "naʿam" or "lā + correction".* That is a real, usable conversational skeleton built almost entirely from material they already own — which is exactly why this lesson feels easy and earns confidence.

**Yaad rakho:** Lesson 2 ke baad classmates paas (`هَذَا`) aur door (`ذَلِكَ`) dono ki cheezein bata sakte hain, sawaal kar sakte hain (`ما`/`من`), aur haan/nahin mein poora jawaab de sakte hain. Sab kuch taqreeban pehle se aata tha — sirf `ذَلِكَ` naya hai.

Now you understand what the chapter is doing. This section is how to *deliver* it.

---

## The landmines for Urdu-speaking adult learners

### Landmine 1 · Reading `ذ` as "z" → "zālika"

Urdu speakers habitually pronounce `ذ` as "z", so `ذَلِكَ` comes out as *zālika*. Correct sound: *dhālika* — the soft "th" of English *that*.

**Counter-strategy:** This is the same fight as Lesson 1's `هَذَا`. Put `هَذَا` and `ذَلِكَ` side by side on the board and drill the `ذ` in both together — *hā‑**dhā**, **dhā**‑li‑ka* — so they hear it's the *same* sound twice. Tongue lightly on the teeth, not "z".

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`ذ` ko "z" mat parho. Yeh naram "th" hai — jaise English mein 'that', 'this'. `هَذَا` ka `ذ` aur `ذَلِكَ` ka `ذ` — bilkul ek hi awaaz. Zabaan halke se daaton ko chhuye, 'z' nahin."*

### Landmine 2 · Missing the hidden long "ā" → "dhalika" (short)

Because no alif is written, classmates read `ذَلِكَ` with a short a — "dha‑li‑ka" — instead of a long "dhā‑li‑ka".

**Counter-strategy:** Tie it to `هَذَا`, which they already say with a long ā. "Same hidden long alif." Stretch it audibly when you model it: *dhaaa‑li‑ka.*

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`ذَلِكَ` likha to bina alif ke jaata hai, par bolte waqt ek lamba 'ā' hai — 'dhā‑li‑ka', na ke 'dha‑li‑ka'. Bilkul `هَذَا` jaisa chhupa hua lamba alif. Mere peeche kheench ke bolo."*

### Landmine 3 · Near / far mix-up (`هَذَا` vs `ذَلِكَ`)

Some classmates will use `هَذَا` for everything (it's the word they know best) and forget to switch to `ذَلِكَ` for far things.

**Counter-strategy:** Use the Urdu mapping — it's exact. `هَذَا` = **yeh** (paas), `ذَلِكَ` = **woh** (door). Physically point: touch a near object → "hādhā", point across the room → "dhālika". Make them do the gesture as they say the word.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Aasaan tareeqa: `هَذَا` = 'yeh' (jo paas hai), `ذَلِكَ` = 'woh' (jo door hai). Paas ki cheez par haath rakho — 'hādhā'; door ki taraf ishaara karo — 'dhālika'. Ishaare ke saath bolo."*

### Landmine 4 · Using `مَا` for people

Classmates reach for `مَا` ("what") for everything, including people — "mā hādhā?" while pointing at the imam.

**Counter-strategy:** One firm rule, repeated: **person → `مَنْ`.** The lesson's own teacher/imam sentence (`مَنْ هَذَا وَمَنْ ذَلِكَ ؟`) is the perfect drill — point at people, force `مَنْ`; point at the stone/star/bed, force `مَا`.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Insaan ke liye hamesha `مَنْ` (kaun) — `مَا` nahin. Imam, ustaad, ladka → `مَنْ`. Pathar, sitaara, palang, jaanwar → `مَا`. Galti ho to foran theek karo: 'insaan hai? to مَنْ.'"*

### Landmine 5 · The final `ـكَ` ending

`ذَلِكَ` ends in `ـكَ` with a **fatḥa** — "‑ka", not "‑ki" or "‑ke". Urdu speakers may soften it.

**Counter-strategy:** Crisp fatḥa: *dhāli‑**ka**.* (Don't explain *why* the kāf is there — it's just part of the word for now.)

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Aakhir mein `ـكَ` par zabar — 'ka', saaf. 'dhāli‑ka'. 'ki' ya 'ke' nahin."*

## Suggested 45–60 minute class plan

*Class shuru karne se pehle:* board par chaar cheezein likho — chapter ka Arabic title (`الدَّرْسُ الثَّانِي`), `هَذَا` ↔ `ذَلِكَ` ka yeh/woh map, char naye lafz (`إِمَام، حَجَر، سُكَّر، لَبَن`), aur 6–8 objects (kuch paas, kuch door) jinhe point karoge.

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–5 min | Greetings · recap Lesson 1's `هَذَا` (point near, "hādhā …") |
| 5–18 min | Introduce `ذَلِكَ` — yeh/woh mapping; point near vs far; drill the `ذ`/long-ā sound with the class |
| 18–30 min | `مَا ذَلِكَ ؟` / `مَنْ ذَلِكَ ؟` — point + ask + answer, using the 4 new words + known nouns; enforce person→`مَنْ` |
| 30–40 min | The `أَذَلِكَ …؟ — لَا، ذَلِكَ …` correction pattern (dog/cat); full-sentence answers |
| 40–50 min | Mixed pair drill: one points (near/far) & asks, partner answers; read the lesson's تمرين sentences aloud |
| 50–60 min | Qur'an payoff (`ذَٰلِكَ الْكِتَابُ`) · quick Q&A · assign handout exercises |

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh halka lesson hai — jaldi nahin, balki *confidence* banane par focus karo. `هَذَا`/`ذَلِكَ` ka yeh/woh map baar baar dohraao. `ذ` ki awaaz par sakht raho.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 2

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum aage dekhenge"*:

- ❌ **`أَمْ`** ("or", for choice questions) — **this lesson does not contain it at all.** Do not introduce it; do not mention it. (An earlier version of these materials wrongly added it — it is not Lesson 2 content.)
- ❌ **`أَوْ`** (the other "or") — also not here. Skip.
- ❌ **`الـ`** ("the", the definite article) — **Lesson 3.** Every noun in Lesson 2 is still bare/indefinite (tanween).
- ❌ **`هَذِهِ`** ("this", feminine) — **Lesson 6.** Keep all demonstratives masculine.
- ❌ **`تِلْكَ`** ("that", feminine) — **Lesson 7.** Don't pair it with `ذَلِكَ` yet.
- ❌ **`لَا` as a sentence-negation rule** — Lesson 2 uses `لَا` only as the spoken answer "no". Don't build negation grammar on it.
- ❌ **Any case-ending names** (the role of the ḍamma) — much later. Just say the words.

**Yaad rakho:** Pre-loading madad nahin karti — overwhelm karti hai. Sab se zaroori: **`أَمْ` is lesson mein hai hi nahin** — use bilkul mat lao. Book ki tarteeb par bharosa karo.

## Self-check before teaching

Before the class, make sure:

- [ ] You can say `ذَلِكَ` perfectly — soft `ذ`, hidden long ā, crisp `ـكَ` — every time. *(Har baar sahi: dhā‑li‑ka.)*
- [ ] You can run the near/far contrast instantly: point near → `هَذَا`, point far → `ذَلِكَ`. *(yeh/woh, bina sochay.)*
- [ ] You can ask and answer both `مَا ذَلِكَ ؟` and `مَنْ ذَلِكَ ؟` correctly, choosing the right question word for person vs thing. *(Insaan → مَنْ, cheez → مَا.)*
- [ ] You can say the 4 new words with correct harakaat: `إِمَامٌ، حَجَرٌ، سُكَّرٌ، لَبَنٌ`. *(Char lafz sahi pesh/zabar ke saath.)*
- [ ] You can point to `ذَٰلِكَ` in al-Baqarah 2:2 and explain it's the same "that". *(Qur'an mein `ذٰلك` dikhaa sako.)*

If yes to all five — *tum tayar ho.*

---

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Are they saying `ذَلِكَ` with a soft `ذ` (dhā), not "z"? *(zālika to nahin keh rahe?)*
2. Are they stretching the hidden long ā? *(dhā‑li‑ka, na ke dha‑li‑ka?)*
3. Are they switching `هَذَا` ↔ `ذَلِكَ` correctly for near vs far? *(yeh/woh theek?)*
4. Are they using `مَنْ` for people and `مَا` for things/animals? *(insaan par مَنْ?)*
5. Are they giving full-sentence answers (`لَا، ذَلِكَ …`), not bare "lā"? *(poora jawaab de rahe hain?)*

**Yaad rakho:** Agar 3+ classmates `ذ`/`z` par ya near/far par phans rahe hain — Lesson 3 (`الـ`) par jaane se pehle ek chhoti revision karwao. Yeh neev (`هَذَا`/`ذَلِكَ` aur unka sahi uchaaran) aage har lesson mein kaam aayegi.
