# Part A · Understanding the chapter

Chapter 1 gave classmates *one* word for "this" (`هَذَا`) and *one* way to ask a question (`أَ`). Chapter 2 doesn't introduce new architecture — it **opens out** Chapter 1's architecture in two directions:

1. **Distance** — they could only point to things *near*. Now they can also point to things *far*.
2. **Specificity of questions** — they could only ask yes/no (*"is this a house?"*). Now they can ask *what* and *who* (*"what is this? who is this?"*) and offer a *choice* (*"is this X or Y?"*).

That's the chapter in one breath. Surface-level: 4 new little words. Underneath: the demonstrative grid expands, and the interrogative system gets its first real shape.

**Yaad rakho:** Ch 2 koi nayi grammar machine nahin laata — Ch 1 wali machine ke aur "buttons" khol raha hai. Pointing ka daayra badh raha hai (near → far), aur sawal ka daayra badh raha hai (haan/nahin → kya/kaun/ya).

Work through the four concepts below at your own pace. For each, ask yourself: *can I explain this to a classmate using only Chapter 1 vocabulary and one new word?* (*Kya main yeh sirf Ch 1 ke lafz aur ek naye lafz se samjha sakti hoon?*) If yes — move on. If not — re-read.

## 1 · ذَلِكَ — completing the near/far axis

`ذَلِكَ` (*dhaalika* · *"woh"*) is the **far masculine demonstrative** (*ism al-ishaarah lil-baʿīd* — *door wale ke liye ishaarah wala lafz*). It points to something *distant* and *masculine*.

### Where this sits in the bigger grid

Remember the 4-cell grid from Ch 1 Part A? Last time we only had one cell filled. After today's class, you fill in the second:

|   | Near (*qareeb* · قریب) | Far (*door* · دور) |
|---|---|---|
| **Masculine** (*mudhakkar*) | `هَذَا` haadhaa (Ch 1) | **`ذَلِكَ` dhaalika (← today)** |
| **Feminine** (*mu'annath*) | `هَذِهِ` haadhihi — **Ch 3** | `تِلْكَ` tilka — later |

Two cells down, two to go. The whole grid will be visible by mid-Ch 3.

**Yaad rakho:** Tum log ko poora grid maaloom hai — par class ko abhi sirf upar wali row dikhao (masculine row). Feminine row Ch 3 ka kaam hai. Aaj sirf "qareeb mardaana" aur "door mardaana" ka contrast banta hai.

### The "silent alif" pattern repeats

You write `ذَلِكَ` but say *"dhaalika"*. Where does the long *aa* come from? Same place as in `هَذَا` — from a hidden dagger alif sitting *between* `ذ` and `ل`. In fully vocalised editions you sometimes see it as `ذَٰلِكَ` with a small **dagger alif** (ـٰ) above the ذ. The big-looking ا in the middle isn't there at all — what looks like alif in some spellings is actually that dagger.

So the rule from Ch 1 transfers exactly: *the visible spelling lies; the long aa is in the middle*. If your classmates internalised `هَذَا = haa-dhaa` last week, `ذَلِكَ = dhaa-li-ka` should land with one sentence: *"yeh wahi silent alif wala pattern hai — naya nahin."*

**Yaad rakho:** Pichhle hafte wala dagger-alif trick yahaan bhi chalti hai — *"dhaa"* ki *aa* andar baithi hai, *"ka"* ke pehle wali ل se pehle. Class ko bolo: *"Yaad hai pichhle hafte hum ne kaha tha — likhne mein alif beech mein, sunaayi 'aa' beech mein? Wahi cheez yahaan bhi hai."*

### Pronunciation: dhaalika, not "zaalika"

The letter `ذ` is the English **th** in "this", "that", "the" — a *voiced* interdental. Urdu speakers consistently flatten it to *z* (so *dhaalika* becomes *zaalika*, *haadhaa* becomes *haaza*). This is the single biggest pronunciation landmine in Ch 2.

The Urdu sound system has no native `ذ` — Urdu's ذ is borrowed from Arabic but pronounced as ز. To produce real `ذ`, place the tongue *between* the teeth (not behind), and let the air pass through. It vibrates (it's voiced — *jharnaadar*) unlike its cousin `ث` (*th* in *think*, unvoiced).

**Yaad rakho:** Class mein zaalika nahin — **dhaalika**. Zubaan daaton ke beech mein laao, "th" jaisa English mein "this" hota hai. Yeh aawaz Urdu mein nahin hai, isi liye log apne aap "z" bol dete hain. Pehle din se hi pakdo — warna habit pakkad ho jaayegi. Khud bhi practice karo aaina ke saamne — zubaan beech mein.

### The case ending — same as Chapter 1

`هَذَا` and `ذَلِكَ` are both **mabnī** (مَبْنِي — *jamke baithne wala*) — their endings *never change* across the case system. That fatḥa at the end of `ذَلِكَ` is fixed; it won't shift to a ḍamma or kasra later. Same with `هَذَا`.

Most nouns later in the book *will* shift endings based on case (you saw a hint of this in Ch 1 with `baytun → al-baytu`). But the demonstratives are quiet exceptions — they sit fixed in any sentence position.

**Yaad rakho (apne liye — class ko nahin batana):** Demonstratives "mabnī" hain — inka aakhir nahin badalta. Yeh ek discount hai jo Arabic deti hai beginners ko — orna har lafz ke aakhir mein dhyaan dena padta. Class ko abhi sirf bolo: *"haadhaa aur dhaalika ka aakhir hamesha aisa hi rehta hai."* "Mabnī" lafz baad ke chapters mein samjha denge.

## 2 · مَا — asking about *things*

`مَا` (*maa* · *"kya"*) is the **interrogative pronoun for non-humans** — things, animals, abstract concepts. *"What is this?"* = `مَا هَذَا ؟` (*maa haadhaa?* · *"yeh kya hai?"*).

### The pattern is the same as أَ

Chapter 1's question particle `أَ` came at the start of a full sentence. `مَا` works the same way — it sits at the start, and the rest of the sentence (`هَذَا`, the noun) follows naturally:

- Statement: `هَذَا قَلَمٌ` (*haadhaa qalamun* — *"yeh ek qalam hai"*)
- Yes/no question (Ch 1): `أَهَذَا قَلَمٌ ؟` (*a-haadhaa qalamun?* — *"kya yeh ek qalam hai?"*)
- *What*-question (today): `مَا هَذَا ؟` (*maa haadhaa?* — *"yeh kya hai?"*)
- Answer: `هَذَا قَلَمٌ` (*haadhaa qalamun* — *"yeh ek qalam hai"*)

Notice: the **answer** to `مَا هَذَا ؟` is just a regular Ch 1 nominal sentence. You're not teaching a new sentence pattern — you're teaching a new way to *ask* for the Ch 1 pattern.

**Yaad rakho:** *Maa* ka sawal pooch lo — jawab vahi Ch 1 wala "haadhaa + ism" hai. Yani classmates ne sawal banana seekha, par jawab dene mein woh Ch 1 wali skill use kar rahe hain. Yeh confidence build karta hai — *"main jo seekh chuki hoon woh phir kaam aa raha hai."*

### Why "maa" and not "kya-jaisa-kuch"

Arabic's `مَا` is famously polyfunctional — it can mean *"what"*, *"that which"*, *"not"* (a negation particle), or be relative pronoun. In Ch 2, you only meet **one meaning**: the question word for things.

The other meanings come later (`مَا` as negation in Ch 11+, relative `مَا` even later). Don't pre-load. Right now: *"maa = kya, sawal mein."*

**Yaad rakho (apne liye):** *Maa* ke kayi roop hain Arabic mein, par Ch 2 mein sirf "kya?" wala. Agar smart classmate poochhe — *"yeh wahi maa hai jo Surah Fatihah mein 'maa malik' wagairah mein aata hai?"* — jawab: *"Haan, lafz wahi hai, par uska doosra istemaal aage chapters mein milega. Abhi sirf sawal ke liye."*

### The Arabic question mark

Arabic uses `؟` (a *mirrored* question mark) not `?`. The hook curves the other way because Arabic reads right-to-left. When you write on the board, write the mirrored version — most fonts handle this automatically, but if you're handwriting, draw the curve facing right.

**Yaad rakho:** Likhte waqt sawal ke aakhir mein `؟` (uljha hua wala) lagao, English wala `?` nahin. Yeh bhi RTL ka hissa hai — sab kuch ulta direction. Chhoti baat hai par naye classmates bhool jaate hain.

## 3 · مَنْ — asking about *people*

`مَنْ` (*man* · *"kaun"*) is the **interrogative pronoun for humans**. *"Who is this?"* = `مَنْ هَذَا ؟` (*man haadhaa?* — *"yeh kaun hai?"*).

### Arabic forces the human/non-human split

In English: *"What is that?"* works for both a person and an object, though *"who is that?"* is preferred for people. The line is soft.

In Arabic, the line is **hard**. You **must** use:
- `مَنْ` for humans (boy, man, teacher, friend, imam, son, doctor)
- `مَا` for non-humans (book, pen, stone, animal, abstract concept)

This human/non-human distinction is not just a politeness preference — it's grammatically active throughout Arabic. Later in the book you'll see that **plural non-human nouns trigger *feminine singular* verb agreement** (a "broken" agreement system) — and that strange-looking rule traces back to the same human/non-human split that Ch 2 is quietly planting today.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh sirf "tehzeeb" ka maamla nahin — Arabic mein insaan aur ghair-insaan grammar ke level pe alag categories hain. Ch 2 mein yeh batao ke "logon ke liye *man*, cheezon ke liye *maa*". Bahut aage chal kar yeh aur cheezein bhi explain karega — par abhi sirf yeh do-tukde wali line.

### Two surprises about مَنْ

**Surprise 1: the sukoon at the end.** `مَنْ` ends in *sukoon* (نْ, no vowel) — silent ن. In Urdu/English, beginners may try to vocalise it as *"mana"* or *"mani"*. Pronounce it crisp: **man** (rhymes with English "fun" but with the *a* of *cat*) — the ن is silent at the very end, like the *n* in *"sun"*.

**Surprise 2: same spelling as "مَنْ" the relative pronoun.** Later in the book, you'll see `مَنْ` used as a relative pronoun (*"the one who..."*). Same spelling. Same pronunciation. Different role. Don't pre-teach.

**Yaad rakho:** *Man* ko sirf ek lafz ki tarah bolo — *"man"* — koi extra vowel nahin. ن silent hai aakhir mein. Aur lafz ke doosre roles aage chapters mein khulenge.

### Pairing مَنْ and مَا in drill

When you teach this in class, **don't teach `مَنْ` and `مَا` in separate slots**. They have to come together. Pull out a pen and a classmate's photo. Hold up the pen → "*maa haadhaa?*" Point at the photo → "*man haadhaa?*" Alternate 10 times. Their brains will form the human/object reflex within minutes.

**Yaad rakho (apne liye):** Pair-drill karwaao — alag-alag mat sikhao. Yeh do alag jhaadiyan nahin, ek hi jhaadi ki do shaakhein hain. Kano ko sath-sath sunwao.

## 4 · أَمْ — choice in questions

`أَمْ` (*am* · *"ya"*) is the word **"or"** when offering a choice *inside a yes/no question*. Example: `أَهَذَا قَلَمٌ أَمْ مِفْتَاحٌ ؟` (*a-haadhaa qalamun am miftaaḥun?* — *"kya yeh qalam hai ya chaabi?"*).

### Two "ors" in Arabic — but classmates only need one

Arabic has **two** words for "or":
- `أَمْ` — **exclusive choice in a question** (*"is it X or Y?"* — expecting one of the two)
- `أَوْ` — **general inclusive "or"** (*"bring a pen or a pencil"* — either works, mostly in statements)

Ch 2 only introduces `أَمْ`. Don't pre-teach `أَوْ`. If a classmate asks: *"Yeh Ch 2 ke baad aayega — abhi sirf sawal ke andar wali 'or' (أَمْ)."*

Why does the distinction exist? In classical Arabic, `أَمْ` triggers a specific intonation — the speaker is *choosing between* two options, and expects the listener to pick one. `أَوْ` is flatter — *"any of these is fine"*. The Qur'an makes heavy use of `أَمْ` in rhetorical questions (*"is X or Y? (the answer is obvious...)"*) — which is partly why this distinction matters for you as a Qur'an-reader, not just for grammar.

**Yaad rakho:** Class ko sirf *am* batao — *aw* baad mein. Sawal ke andar choice → *am*. Bas. Tum khud yaad rakho ke do "or" hain Arabic mein, par class ko ek waqt mein ek hi cheez dena hai.

### The structure of an "am" question

The question particle `أَ` still does the work of making it a question. `أَمْ` just sits between the two options:

```
أَ      [option 1]     أَمْ     [option 2]     ؟
question particle       "or"
```

So: `أَهَذَا قَلَمٌ أَمْ مِفْتَاحٌ ؟`

Both option-nouns stay in their indefinite form (both end in tanween). There's no case-change happening — this isn't a place where Arabic gets exotic. It's literally *"a-this pen or key?"*.

**Yaad rakho:** Structure simple hai: شروع mein "أَ", beech mein "أَمْ", aur dono lafz indefinite (tanween wale). Likhne mein bhi simple — bas dono ke beech "am" daal do. Koi case-vase nahin badalta abhi.

### Answering an "am" question

The answer to *"X or Y?"* is **a full statement with the correct option**, not just one word:

- Q: `أَهَذَا قَلَمٌ أَمْ مِفْتَاحٌ ؟` (*"a-haadhaa qalamun am miftaaḥun?"* — *"kya yeh qalam hai ya chaabi?"*)
- A: `هَذَا قَلَمٌ` (*"haadhaa qalamun."* — *"yeh qalam hai."*)

Not: *"naʿam"* (yes). Not: *"qalamun"* (just "pen"). The full nominal sentence — because the *am* question isn't really yes/no; it's *"pick which one"*.

**Yaad rakho:** *Am* wale sawal ka jawab *na'am* ya *laa* nahin — poora jumla. Yeh classmates pakka galat karenge pehli baar. *"Yeh kya hai ya woh hai?"* — *"yeh fulaan hai."* Half-answer mat dene do.

## The big picture — what this chapter is silently teaching

Step back and look at all four concepts together. Chapter 2 is doing three things at once:

1. **Filling out grids.** Last week the demonstrative grid had 1/4 cells. This week 2/4. Next chapter it'll jump to 3/4 (with `هَذِهِ`). The chapter is teaching students that Arabic grammar lives in *systems*, not loose facts. (*Arabic ki cheezein "grids" mein milti hain — ek cheez seekho, uska doosra bhai/behen aage milta hai.*)

2. **Introducing the question-word family.** Last week one question particle (`أَ`). This week three more (`مَا`, `مَنْ`, `أَمْ`). Next week comes `أَيْنَ` (where). The "question family" is being seeded across chapters — by Ch 5 they'll have the full set. (*Sawalaat ka khandaan — har chapter mein ek-do naye sawal ke lafz.*)

3. **Re-using Ch 1 patterns without saying so.** The answer to every Ch 2 question is a Ch 1 nominal sentence. Without naming it, the chapter is *reinforcing* Ch 1 architecture by giving classmates new ways to *trigger* the same response pattern. (*Ch 2 ke sawal, Ch 1 ke jawab — yeh repeat-by-stealth hai.*)

**Yaad rakho:** Dr. Abdur Rahim har chapter mein pichhle chapter ko upar uthata hai. Tumhe yeh dekhna hai class mein — agar koi Ch 1 ka point bhool gaya hai, Ch 2 mein woh apne aap dohraega *kyunki* sawal poochne ka matlab hai jawab dena, aur jawab hi Ch 1 ki cheez hai. Tumhe alag se "revision" karwaane ki zaroorat nahin — drills khud revise kar denge.

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

Now you understand what the chapter is doing. This section is how to *deliver* it to your classmates.

---

## The five landmines for Urdu-speaking adult learners

### Landmine 1 · "Zaalika" instead of "dhaalika"

Almost every Urdu-speaker, on first attempt, will say *zaalika* (with a *z*). Urdu treats `ذ` as a *z* sound natively. The English **th** in *"this"* / *"that"* / *"the"* — which is what `ذ` actually is — isn't part of either Urdu or English-by-Urdu-speakers' default phonology.

**Counter-strategy:** Don't just say "pronounce it dhaalika". Do this exact 3-step drill:

1. Have them say English *"this"* slowly. Notice the tongue position — *between the teeth*, vibrating.
2. Now have them say *"thaa-thaa"* (just the `th` of *"this"*, drawn out).
3. Now flip to *"dhaa-li-ka"* — same starting tongue position. Confirm by feel, not by sound.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Dekho — English mein 'this' bolte ho, na? Apni zubaan ki position dekho — daaton ke beech mein hai, baahar nikli hui hai. Bilkul wahi position 'dhaalika' ke shuru mein chahiye. Urdu mein ذ ko 'z' bolte hain, par Arabic ki asli ذ 'th' (jaise this mein) hai. Aaj se zubaan beech mein laao — daaton ke peechhe nahin."*

### Landmine 2 · Pointing the wrong direction (or not pointing at all)

`هَذَا` requires you to be pointing at something **near**. `ذَلِكَ` requires you to be pointing at something **far**. If classmates are sitting at desks and just *saying* the words without pointing, the distinction collapses for them — both words feel the same.

**Counter-strategy:** Make every drill physical. Put two identical pens — one on the table in front of the classmate, one on the windowsill across the room. The near one is `هَذَا`. The far one is `ذَلِكَ`. Have them physically point with their hand and walk through "*haadhaa qalamun · dhaalika qalamun*" — same word for "pen", different demonstrative because of distance.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Bina hath uthaaye yeh dono lafz ek jaise lagte hain. Hath se ishaara karo — qareeb wali cheez 'haadhaa', door wali 'dhaalika'. Hath ki distance se feel aati hai. Kursi pe baith ke yeh nahin seekh sakte — utho, hath uthao, point karo. Yeh jismani drill hai."*

### Landmine 3 · "Maa" for humans

Classmates default to one question word for everything — in English they'd say *"what's that?"* about a person ("what's that thing you mentioned?"). In Urdu, *"yeh kya hai?"* and *"yeh kaun hai?"* coexist, but *kya* tends to dominate in informal speech. So when they meet `مَا` and `مَنْ`, they'll forget the split and reach for `مَا` even when pointing at a person.

**Counter-strategy:** Drill the **wrong** version explicitly so the class *hears* how wrong it sounds, then correct. Point at a classmate and ask *"maa haadhaa?"* — pause — *"galat. Maa cheezon ke liye hai. Insaan ke liye man. Man haadhaa?"* — they'll laugh and the rule will stick.

Then rapid-fire 10 alternations: pen, photo, book, classmate, key, teacher's nameplate, water bottle, friend's face, paper, neighbour. Each one — they have one second to call out *maa* or *man*.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Urdu mein 'kya' aur 'kaun' dono chalti hain, magar 'kya' zyaada bolte hain rozaana zindagi mein. Arabic mein yeh strict hai — insaan ke liye sirf 'man', cheez ke liye sirf 'maa'. Yeh galti pehli class mein hi rok do — warna pakkad ho jaayegi. Hum is rule ko 'insaan ya cheez?' kehte hain — har sawal se pehle yeh poochho apne aap se."*

### Landmine 4 · Half-answering an "am" question

When you ask *"a-haadhaa qalamun am miftaaḥun?"* — classmates will instinctively answer one of:
- *"naʿam"* (yes — but yes to *what*?)
- *"qalamun"* (just the noun, without `هَذَا`)
- A confused silence

The correct answer is a full Ch 1 sentence: *"haadhaa qalamun."* They've forgotten the lesson from last week — that sentences need both Mubtada' and Khabar.

**Counter-strategy:** Before introducing `أَمْ`, do a quick 2-minute revision: hold a pen, point, say *"haadhaa qalamun"*. Then immediately introduce *am*: *"jab main poochhoon — qalam ya chaabi? — to tum poora jawab dete ho. Sirf 'qalamun' nahin — 'haadhaa qalamun'."* Drill this with 8–10 examples before moving on.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Jab 'ya' wala sawal aaye — 'kya yeh qalam hai ya chaabi?' — to jawab mein 'haan' ya 'nahin' kaam nahin karta. Aur sirf naam bata dena bhi kaafi nahin. Poora jumla: 'yeh qalam hai' (haadhaa qalamun). Sawal mein do options diye gaye the — tumhe ek pe khada hona hai, aur Ch 1 wali poori jumla banaani hai."*

### Landmine 5 · Mixing up the silent and visible alif in ذَلِكَ

`ذَلِكَ` is written with **one visible alif** (in `ـٰ` or as ا between ذ and ل) and **classmates will not see it as the same pattern as هَذَا**. They'll think it's a fresh, new alif to lengthen. The result: *"dhaa-AA-li-ka"* with an over-stressed middle.

**Counter-strategy:** When you write `ذَلِكَ` on the board, write it as `ذَٰلِكَ` with the explicit dagger alif (same as you did with `هَـٰذَا` last week). Draw a curved arrow on the board from the `ا` of `هَـٰذَا` to the `ـٰ` of `ذَٰلِكَ` and say: *"Pichhle hafte wala silent alif — yahaan bhi hai. Naya kuch nahin."*

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Pichhle hafte tumne 'haa-dhaa' seekha — beech mein chhoti alif chhupi hui thi. Aaj 'dhaa-li-ka' mein bhi wahi cheez hai — 'dhaa' ki 'aa' beech mein hai, alif lambi nahin. Naya pattern nahin — wahi puraana. Sirf shuruaat ki ذ aur aakhir mein 'li-ka' badle hain."*

## Suggested 60-minute class plan

*Class shuru karne se pehle:* board par teen cheezein likho — chapter ka Arabic title (`الدَّرْسُ الثَّانِي`), aaj ke 4 rules ki ek-line tagline har, aur do columns banao — *qareeb* (near) ke neeche `هَذَا`, *door* (far) ke neeche `ذَلِكَ`.

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–5 min | Greetings · 2-minute Ch 1 revision: drill 5 nouns with `haadhaa` + `أَ` |
| 5–15 min | Introduce `ذَلِكَ` — physical pointing drill: 8 objects in room, near vs far, *haadhaa qalamun · dhaalika qalamun* |
| 15–25 min | `مَنْ` and `مَا` together — 10 rapid-fire items: pen, photo, book, classmate, key, etc. Class calls out *maa* or *man* |
| 25–40 min | Combine — *man dhaalika? · maa haadhaa?* — pair work, alternating partners |
| 40–50 min | `أَمْ` — choice questions; pair work *a-haadhaa X am Y?* with full-sentence answers |
| 50–58 min | New vocab drill — 15 new words with `هَذَا` and `ذَلِكَ` |
| 58–60 min | Wrap up · assign exercises from handout |

**Yaad rakho:** Ch 2 ka pacing tight rakhna padega — char concepts hain, har ek ko apna time chahiye. Agar `أَمْ` ka time kam pade to next class mein dohrao — yeh sab se nazuk concept hai (full-sentence answer wala). `ذَلِكَ` ka physical drill mat short karo — yeh chapter ki neev hai.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 2

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum Chapter X mein dekhenge"*:

- ❌ Feminine demonstratives (`هَذِهِ` near, `تِلْكَ` far) — **Chapter 3**
- ❌ Plural demonstratives (`هَؤُلَاءِ` "these", `أُولَئِكَ` "those") — **much later**
- ❌ `أَوْ` (general "or") — **later**
- ❌ `مَا` as negation ("not") — **much later**
- ❌ `أَيْنَ ؟` (where?) — **Chapter 3**
- ❌ Case-endings of nouns after prepositions (jarr) — **Chapter 3**
- ❌ Iʿrāb terminology (rafʿ, naṣb, jarr) — **Chapter 5**

**Yaad rakho:** Ek aam savaalat jo classmates poochenge: *"feminine kaise bolte hain?"* — jawab: *"Chapter 3."* Doosra: *"'where' kaise poochhte hain?"* — jawab: *"Chapter 3."* Teesra: *"'or' ke aur tareeqe?"* — jawab: *"Aage chapters mein."* Yeh teen jawab bilkul ratta lagao — clear awaaz mein, koi guilt nahin.

## Self-check before teaching

Before the class, make sure:

- [ ] You can pronounce **dhaalika** (not *zaalika*) confidently — say it 10 times into a mirror. *(Aaina ke saamne 10 baar bolo — zubaan beech mein.)*

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Are they saying *zaalika* instead of *dhaalika*? *(Sab se common galti — pehli class ke baad bhi pakkadne mein time lagega.)*
2. Are they pointing physically when using `هَذَا` / `ذَلِكَ`? *(Bina hath ke yeh do lafz blend ho jaate hain.)*
3. Are they using `مَا` for people? *(Insaan-cheez wala rule miss kar rahe hain?)*
4. Are they answering `أَمْ` questions with just "yes/no" or just a single word? *(Half-answer de rahe hain?)*
5. Are they still inserting "is/hai" into nominal sentences? *(Ch 1 ka pichhla landmine — Ch 2 mein bhi pakdo.)*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh paanch points tumhe batayenge ke Ch 3 par jaana hai ya Ch 2 ke kisi hisse ko dohraana hai. Agar 3+ classmates `dhaalika`/`zaalika` ki galti kar rahe hain — pronunciation drill ek aur 10-min session karwaao Ch 3 par jaane se pehle. Foundation mazboot honi chahiye, warna har naya chapter pichhli galti par build karega.
