# Part A · Understanding the chapter

This is the lesson everything has been building toward. Across the book the class collected the everyday detached pronouns piece by piece: `هُوَ`/`هِيَ` (Ch 5), `أَنَا`/`أَنْتَ` (Ch 9), `أَنْتِ` (Ch 12), the plurals `هُمْ`/`هُنَّ` (Ch 13), and `نَحْنُ`/`أَنْتُمْ` (Ch 14). After Chapter 14 there was a single gap — the women's "you all". Lesson 15 fills that one box, adds its attached tail and its verb ending, and then **prints the entire table** so the class can see the finished system.

In one sentence: **Chapter 15 teaches `أَنْتُنَّ` ("you all", women), its tail `ـكُنَّ` ("your"), and the matching verb `ذَهَبْتُنَّ` ("you women went") — and crowns the book with the complete detached-pronoun table (p.91).**

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh sabaq woh hai jiske liye poora arc bana tha. Chapter 14 ke baad sirf **ek** khaana khaali tha — aurton ka "tum sab". Lesson 15 us khaana ko bharta hai: **`أَنْتُنَّ`** ("tum sab", auratein) + tail **`ـكُنَّ`** ("tumhara") + verb **`ذَهَبْتُنَّ`** ("tum auratein gayin") — aur phir (p.91) **poora detached-pronoun table** bana deta hai. Saara nizaam ek frame mein, koi khaana khaali nahin. (Dual — `أَنْتُمَا`/`هُمَا`, "do log" — **Lesson 16+**, abhi bilkul nahin.)

## The dialogue (read it first — everything grows out of it)

**Zaynab** meets a group of young women (`الفَتَيَات`, "the girls") — the daughters of Shaykh ʿAbbās — and chats with them. Because she is talking **to a group of women**, every "you" becomes the feminine "you all" (`ـكُنَّ`), and the verb of address takes the feminine-plural ending (`ـتُنَّ`). Read it slowly and watch the doubled-nūn (`نّ`) light up — that `نّ` sound is the signature of "a group of women".

> — **الفَتَيَاتُ :** السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ وَرَحْمَةُ اللهِ وَبَرَكَاتُهُ .
> — **زَيْنَبُ :** وَعَلَيْكُمُ السَّلَامُ وَرَحْمَةُ اللهِ وَبَرَكَاتُهُ … مَنْ **أَنْتُنَّ** يَا أَخَوَاتُ ؟
> — **إِحْدَاهُنَّ :** **نَحْنُ** بَنَاتُ الشَّيْخِ عَبَّاسٍ .
> — **زَيْنَبُ :** أَهْلًا وَسَهْلًا وَمَرْحَبًا . أُمُّ**كُنَّ** أُسْتَاذَتِي . كَيْفَ حَالُهَا ؟
> — **إِحْدَاهُنَّ :** هِيَ بِخَيْرٍ وَالْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ .
> — **زَيْنَبُ :** أَيْنَ هِيَ الْآنَ ؟ — **إِحْدَاهُنَّ :** هِيَ الْآنَ فِي الرِّيَاضِ .
> — **زَيْنَبُ :** مَتَى ذَهَبَتْ ؟ — **إِحْدَاهُنَّ :** ذَهَبَتْ قَبْلَ أُسْبُوعٍ .
> — **زَيْنَبُ :** مَنْ ذَهَبَ مَعَهَا ؟ — **إِحْدَاهُنَّ :** ذَهَبَ مَعَهَا أَخُونَا إِبْرَاهِيمُ .
> — **زَيْنَبُ :** كَيْفَ حَالُ**كُنَّ** ؟ — **إِحْدَاهُنَّ :** **نَحْنُ** بِخَيْرٍ وَالْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ .
> — **زَيْنَبُ :** فِي أَيِّ مَدْرَسَةٍ **أَنْتُنَّ** ؟
> — **إِحْدَاهُنَّ :** **نَحْنُ** فِي الْمَدْرَسَةِ الْمُتَوَسِّطَةِ .
> — **زَيْنَبُ :** مَتَى اخْتِبَارُ**كُنَّ** ؟ — **إِحْدَاهُنَّ :** اخْتِبَارُ**نَا** بَعْدَ شَهْرٍ .
> — **زَيْنَبُ :** أَ**ذَهَبْتُنَّ** إِلَى الْمَدْرَسَةِ الْيَوْمَ ؟
> — **إِحْدَاهُنَّ :** نَعَمْ . **ذَهَبْنَا** وَرَجَعْنَا .

Notice how little is genuinely new. The whole question-machine is old (`مَنْ`، `أَيْنَ`، `كَيْفَ حَالُ…`، `أَيِّ مَدْرَسَةٍ`، prepositions + kasra، `أَخُونَا` from the five nouns، `نَحْنُ`/`ـنَا` from last lesson). The only new gears are the feminine-plural set: the pronoun `أَنْتُنَّ`, the tail `ـكُنَّ`, and the verb ending `ـتُنَّ` (`ذَهَبْتُنَّ`). One small word — `إِحْدَاهُنَّ` ("one of them", the women) — answers throughout; treat it as a ready-made phrase, not a new grammar point.

**Yaad rakho:** Pehle dialogue parho. Zaynab ek group (`الفَتَيَات`, naujawan larkiyan) se baat kar rahi hai — isliye har "tum" → aurton wala "**tum sab**" (`ـكُنَّ`: `أُمُّكُنَّ`, `حَالُكُنَّ`, `اخْتِبَارُكُنَّ`), aur verb `أَذَهَبْتُنَّ` (`ـتُنَّ`). Group jawab deta hai "**hum / hamara**" (`نَحْنُ`/`ـنَا` — Ch 14 se, gender-free). Naya sirf: aurton ki teen cheezein — `أَنْتُنَّ`, `ـكُنَّ`, `ـتُنَّ`. Pehchaan: doubled `نّ` ki awaaz = "aurton ka group".

## 1 · `أَنْتُنَّ` ("you all", women) and the tail `ـكُنَّ` ("your")

In Ch 14 the class met `أَنْتُمْ` ("you all", men/mixed) with its tail `ـكُمْ`. When you address **a group of women only**, both take the feminine-plural shape:

> **`أَنْتُنَّ`** (antunna) = "you all" — addressing a group of **women**.
> **`ـكُنَّ`** (-kunna) = "your" / "you all's" — `بَيْتُكُنَّ` (your house), `أُمُّكُنَّ` (your mother), `مَدْرَسَتُكُنَّ` (your school), `حَالُكُنَّ` (your state), `أَخُوكُنَّ` (your brother — the five-nouns shape, as always), `اخْتِبَارُكُنَّ` (your exam).

The signature of the feminine plural is the **doubled nūn** (`نّ`, a shadda). The class already met it last lesson in `هُنَّ` ("they", women) and the tail `ـهُنَّ` ("their", women). Lesson 15 is simply the **2nd-person** version of that same `نّ` sound: `أَنْتُنَّ` / `ـكُنَّ`. Point at the rhyme and it lands at once — *`هُنَّ`/`ـهُنَّ` was "they-women / their"; `أَنْتُنَّ`/`ـكُنَّ` is "you-women / your".*

> **For you, not the class:** the masculine/feminine plural pairs are perfectly parallel — `أَنْتُمْ`/`أَنْتُنَّ` and `ـكُمْ`/`ـكُنَّ`. The masculine ends in a plain `…مْ` (sukūn-mīm); the feminine ends in the doubled `…نَّ` (shadda-nūn). Same slot, two genders. The five nouns keep their long-vowel shape under `ـكُنَّ` exactly as under every other tail (`أَبُوكُنَّ`, `أَخُوكُنَّ`) — recall, don't re-teach.

**Yaad rakho:** `أَنْتُمْ` (mardon ka "tum sab", Ch 14) ka aurton wala version = **`أَنْتُنَّ`**. Tail `ـكُمْ` ka aurton wala version = **`ـكُنَّ`** ("tumhara", auraton ka): `بَيْتُكُنَّ`, `أُمُّكُنَّ`, `حَالُكُنَّ`. **Pehchaan:** doubled `نّ` (shadda) = aurton ka group — wahi `نّ` jo pichhle sabaq ke `هُنَّ`/`ـهُنَّ` mein tha. Mard = `…م`, aurat = `…نّ`. (Five nouns: `أَخُوكُنَّ` — purana shape, naya nahin.)

## 2 · The feminine-plural verb of address — `ذَهَبْتُنَّ` ("you women went")

When you ask a question of, or report about, a group of women, the past verb takes the feminine-plural ending `ـتُنَّ`:

> **"you all (women)" → add `ـتُنَّ`:** `ذَهَبَ` → **`ذَهَبْتُنَّ`** ("you women went"), `خَرَجَ` → `خَرَجْتُنَّ`, `رَجَعَ` → `رَجَعْتُنَّ`.

The dialogue shows it in the closing exchange: `أَذَهَبْتُنَّ إِلَى الْمَدْرَسَةِ الْيَوْمَ ؟ — نَعَمْ ، ذَهَبْنَا وَرَجَعْنَا` ("Did you (women) go to school today? — Yes, we went and came back").

This is the last piece of the **verb-of-address grid**, which the book finally draws in full on p.91:

| addressing | one person | a group |
|---|---|---|
| **a man / men** | `أَيْنَ ذَهَبْتَ ؟` (you, m.sg) | `أَيْنَ ذَهَبْتُمْ ؟` (you all, m) |
| **a woman / women** | `أَيْنَ ذَهَبْتِ ؟` (you, f.sg) | `أَيْنَ ذَهَبْتُنَّ ؟` (you all, f) |
| **the speaker(s)** | `أَنَا ذَهَبْتُ` (I went) | `نَحْنُ ذَهَبْنَا` (we went) |

Only the bottom-right corner of the feminine row — `ذَهَبْتُنَّ` — is new; the rest the class has already met (`ذَهَبْتَ` Ch 12, `ذَهَبْتِ` Ch 12, `ذَهَبْتُمْ`/`ذَهَبْنَا` Ch 14, `ذَهَبْتُ` the speaker form). So the grid simply **completes**.

> **A landmine you must see yourself first.** Three feminine-flavoured verb forms now look and sound dangerously alike:
> - `ذَهَبْنَ` = "**they** (women) went" (3rd person, Lesson 13) — ending `ـْنَ`, single nūn.
> - `ذَهَبْتُنَّ` = "**you** (women) went" (2nd person, this lesson) — ending `ـتُنَّ`, with a `ت` and a doubled nūn.
> - `ذَهَبْنَا` = "**we** went" (1st person, Ch 14, gender-free) — ending `ـنَا`.
>
> The difference is the little `ت` ("you") and the doubled `نّ`. Drill the contrast directly.

> **Scope line:** teach **only** the `ـتُنَّ` ("you women") ending on the known verbs (`ذَهَبَ`/`خَرَجَ`/`رَجَعَ`/`جَلَسَ`). This is **not** the present tense or the full conjugation table — just the spoken address form from the dialogue.

**Yaad rakho:** Aurton ke group se baat karte waqt verb: **`ـتُنَّ`** (`ذَهَبْتُنَّ` — "tum auratein gayin"). Dialogue: `أَذَهَبْتُنَّ ؟ — نَعَمْ ، ذَهَبْنَا`. Ab verb-of-address grid **mukammal** ho gaya (p.91) — sirf `ذَهَبْتُنَّ` naya hai. **Khatra (teen milte-julte form):** `ذَهَبْنَ` (woh auratein gayin, L13, `ـْنَ`) · `ذَهَبْتُنَّ` (tum auratein gayin, ab, `ـتُنَّ`) · `ذَهَبْنَا` (hum gaye, Ch 14, `ـنَا`). Farq: chhota `ت` ("tum") aur doubled `نّ`. (Sirf `ـتُنَّ` ending — poori conjugation nahin.)

## 3 · The new time words — `قَبْلَ` / `بَعْدَ` / `مَتَى` (+ `أُسْبُوع`، `شَهْر`)

Alongside the grammar, Lesson 15 hands the class a small, immediately useful vocabulary set for **talking about time** (new-words box, p.91):

> - **`قَبْلَ`** (qabla) = "before" — `قَبْلَ أُسْبُوعٍ` (a week ago / before a week), `قَبْلَ الصَّلَاةِ` (before the prayer), `قَبْلَ الْأَذَانِ` (before the adhān).
> - **`بَعْدَ`** (baʿda) = "after" — `بَعْدَ شَهْرٍ` (after a month / in a month), `بَعْدَ الدَّرْسِ` (after the lesson), `بَعْدَ الصَّلَاةِ` (after the prayer).
> - **`مَتَى`** (matā) = "when?" — `مَتَى ذَهَبَتْ ؟` (when did she go?), `مَتَى اخْتِبَارُكُنَّ ؟` (when is your exam?).
> - plus `الْأُسْبُوع` (week), `الشَّهْر` (month), `الْأَذَان` (the call to prayer), `الصَّلَاة` (the prayer), `الِاخْتِبَار` (the exam), and the verb `رَجَعَ` (he returned / came back).

`قَبْلَ` and `بَعْدَ` behave like the location words `تَحْتَ`/`أَمَامَ`/`خَلْفَ` the class already knows: the word ends in a fatḥa and the **next noun takes kasra** (`قَبْلَ أُسْبُوعٍ`، `بَعْدَ شَهْرٍ`، `بَعْدَ الدَّرْسِ`). So nothing structurally new — they are simply **time** versions of the same "muḍāf" behaviour. (Keep the ẓarf/muḍāf-ilayhi theory to yourself, as with `تَحْتَ`.)

> **For you, not the class:** `مَتَى` and `كَيْفَ` appear in this box too, though the class has met them in passing before — the book gathers them here as part of the question-word toolkit. No need to present them as brand new; just confirm them.

**Yaad rakho:** Naye time-lafz (p.91): **`قَبْلَ`** ("pehle/se pehle" — `قَبْلَ أُسْبُوعٍ`), **`بَعْدَ`** ("baad" — `بَعْدَ شَهْرٍ`), **`مَتَى`** ("kab?"). Saath: `أُسْبُوع` (hafta), `شَهْر` (maheena), `الأَذَان` (azaan), `الصَّلَاة` (namaz), `اخْتِبَار` (imtihaan), `رَجَعَ` (wapas aaya). **`قَبْلَ`/`بَعْدَ` `تَحْتَ` jaise hain** — agla noun **kasra** leta hai (`قَبْلَ أُسْبُوعٍ`). Naya structure nahin, sirf time wala version.

## 4 · ★ The payoff — the complete detached-pronoun table (p.91)

This is the moment the whole arc has been building toward. On the last page the book lays out **every detached pronoun** in two columns — singular (`لِلْمُفْرَد`) and plural (`لِلْجَمْع`):

| person | singular (`لِلْمُفْرَد`) | plural (`لِلْجَمْع`) |
|---|---|---|
| **he / they (m)** | `هُوَ طَالِبٌ` | `هُمْ طُلَّابٌ` |
| **she / they (f)** | `هِيَ طَالِبَةٌ` | `هُنَّ طَالِبَاتٌ` |
| **you (m)** | `أَنْتَ طَالِبٌ` | `أَنْتُمْ طُلَّابٌ` |
| **you (f)** | `أَنْتِ طَالِبَةٌ` | `أَنْتُنَّ طَالِبَاتٌ` |
| **I / we (m)** | `أَنَا طَالِبٌ` | `نَحْنُ طُلَّابٌ` |
| **I / we (f)** | `أَنَا طَالِبَةٌ` | `نَحْنُ طَالِبَاتٌ` |

Read down the columns and the **patterns** jump out — this is the real teaching gift of the finished table:

1. **The `هـ` family points away from us** (3rd person, "he/she/they"): `هُوَ`/`هِيَ`/`هُمْ`/`هُنَّ`.
2. **The `أنت` family points at the listener** (2nd person, "you…"): `أَنْتَ`/`أَنْتِ`/`أَنْتُمْ`/`أَنْتُنَّ` — all four built on the same `أَنْتـ` stem, with the ending telling gender + number.
3. **The `أنا`/`نحن` family is the speaker** (1st person, "I/we").
4. **`أَنَا` and `نَحْنُ` don't change for gender** — one `أَنَا` for a man or a woman, one `نَحْنُ` for any group. (That's why the table shows `أَنَا` twice — same word, both rows.) Everywhere else, **`…م`/`…َ` is masculine** and **`…نّ`/`…ِ` is feminine.**

> **For you, not the class:** this is the natural close of Book 1's foundational pronoun system, and a clean stopping point. The next number up — the **dual** (`أَنْتُمَا`/`هُمَا`، `طَالِبَانِ`) — is **Lesson 16+**, a whole new phase. Don't add a "dual" column to this table, even to be complete; the book deliberately closes the singular/plural set here. Let the table be *finished* as the book finishes it.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh sabaq ka inaam (p.91): **poora detached-pronoun table** — singular + plural, dono columns. Padhne ke fayde (patterns): (1) `هـ` khandaan = doosre log (`هُوَ`/`هِيَ`/`هُمْ`/`هُنَّ`); (2) `أنت` khandaan = sunne wala (`أَنْتَ`/`أَنْتِ`/`أَنْتُمْ`/`أَنْتُنَّ` — sab ek `أَنْتـ` par); (3) `أنا`/`نحن` = bolne wala; (4) `أَنَا`/`نَحْنُ` gender se nahin badalte, baaki sab mein mard = `…م`, aurat = `…نّ`. **Chart mukammal ho gaya — koi khaana khaali nahin.** (Dual — agla number, L16+; iss table mein column mat barhao.)

## 5 · ★ The big picture — fifteen lessons, one finished system

Step all the way back. This is the **last** lesson of the foundational arc, so it is worth seeing what the class now owns:

- **Two-word nominal sentences** with no "is" (`هَذَا بَيْتٌ`) — Ch 1.
- **The full demonstrative grid** — `هَذَا`/`ذَلِكَ`/`هَذِهِ`/`تِلْكَ`/`هَؤُلَاءِ`/`أُولَئِكَ` — Ch 1–13.
- **The definite article, adjectives, sun/moon letters** — Ch 3.
- **Prepositions + the kasra they trigger; iḍāfa** — Ch 4–5, 8.
- **Singular & plural nouns, sound & broken** — Ch 13.
- **The naʿt (adjective agreement) rule** — Ch 9.
- **The attached possessive tails** — `ـِي`/`ـكَ`/`ـكِ`/`ـهُ`/`ـهَا`/`ـهُمْ`/`ـهُنَّ`/`ـكُمْ`/`ـنَا` and now **`ـكُنَّ`** — Ch 10–15.
- **The past verb of address & speaker** — `ذَهَبْتُ`/`ذَهَبْتَ`/`ذَهَبْتِ`/`ذَهَبْتُمْ`/`ذَهَبْتُنَّ`/`ذَهَبْنَا` and the 3rd-person `ذَهَبَ`/`ذَهَبَتْ`/`ذَهَبُوا`/`ذَهَبْنَ` — Ch 12–15.
- **And, completed today, the entire detached-pronoun table.**

That is a genuinely complete *foundation*: a beginner who owns Lessons 1–15 can read and build simple Arabic nominal sentences, ask and answer about people and places, and recognise huge stretches of Qur'anic and everyday Arabic. The road continues (the dual, numbers, colours, and the present-tense verb await in Lesson 16 onward), but Lesson 15 is a real summit — the place where the scaffolding becomes a building.

> **For you, not the class:** resist the urge to "round things off" by previewing Lesson 16. The class has just earned a finished chart — let that be the note the course ends on. A clean, complete Book-1 foundation is worth far more than a half-opened door to duals and verbs they aren't ready for.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh aakhri foundational sabaq hai — dekho class ke paas ab kya hai: do-lafz wale jumle (`هَذَا بَيْتٌ`), poora demonstrative grid, `الـ` + adjectives + sun/moon, prepositions + kasra + iḍāfa, singular/plural nouns, naʿt rule, saare attached tails (`ـكُنَّ` tak), past verb ka poora address/speaker set, aur **aaj — mukammal pronoun table**. Yeh ek sachchi mukammal **buniyaad** hai. (Aage dual, numbers, colours, present-tense verb — Lesson 16+ — magar abhi rukna sahi hai. Class ko mukammal chart ka ehsaas do; agla darwaza mat kholo.)

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

## The landmines for Urdu-speaking adult learners

### Landmine 1 · Confusing the three "…n" verb forms (`ذَهَبْنَ` / `ذَهَبْتُنَّ` / `ذَهَبْنَا`)

This is the big one. Three feminine-flavoured forms sit close together: `ذَهَبْنَ` ("they-women went", L13), `ذَهَبْتُنَّ` ("you-women went", today), `ذَهَبْنَا` ("we went", Ch 14). A beginner will reach for the wrong one in fast Q-and-A.

**Counter-strategy:** Put all three on the board and circle the distinguishing piece: `ذَهَبْنَ` (just `ـْنَ` — *they*), `ذَهَبْتُنَّ` (a `ت` for "you" + doubled `نّ` — *you women*), `ذَهَبْنَا` (`ـنَا` — *we*). Drill a three-way switch: "they-women went / you-women went / we went."

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Teen milte-julte verb: `ذَهَبْنَ` ('woh auratein gayin' — sirf `ـْنَ`), `ذَهَبْتُنَّ` ('tum auratein gayin' — `ت` + doubled `نّ`), `ذَهَبْنَا` ('hum gaye' — `ـنَا`). Pehchaan: chhota `ت` = 'tum', `نّ` = aurton ka group, `ـنَا` = 'hum'. Board par teeno likho aur baari-baari bolo."*

### Landmine 2 · Using the masculine `ـكُمْ`/`أَنْتُمْ` for a group of women

Because `ـكُمْ`/`أَنْتُمْ` are so fresh (Ch 14) and `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ` is a daily reflex, classmates will default to the masculine form even when addressing women.

**Counter-strategy:** Teach `أَنْتُنَّ`/`ـكُنَّ` as the **`نّ` twin** of last week's forms. Write the pair side by side — `أَنْتُمْ`/`أَنْتُنَّ`, `ـكُمْ`/`ـكُنَّ` — and say: *"men end in `…م`, women end in `…نّ`."* Tie the `نّ` to last lesson's `هُنَّ` so it feels familiar, not new.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Aurton ke group ke liye `أَنْتُمْ`/`ـكُمْ` nahin — `أَنْتُنَّ`/`ـكُنَّ`. Asaan qaida: mard = `…م`, aurat = `…نّ`. Yeh wahi `نّ` hai jo pichhle sabaq ke `هُنَّ` ('woh auratein') mein tha. `بَيْتُكُمْ` (mardon ko) ↔ `بَيْتُكُنَّ` (aurton ko)."*

### Landmine 3 · Dropping the kasra after `قَبْلَ` / `بَعْدَ`

`قَبْلَ`/`بَعْدَ` behave like `تَحْتَ`/`أَمَامَ` — the next noun takes kasra (`قَبْلَ أُسْبُوعٍ`، `بَعْدَ شَهْرٍ`). Beginners forget and say `قَبْلَ أُسْبُوعٌ` ✗.

**Counter-strategy:** Slot them into the existing "kasra family" the class already knows (`فِي`/`عَلَى`/`تَحْتَ`/`أَمَامَ`…). *"A new pair joins the kasra family — `قَبْلَ`/`بَعْدَ`. Same rule: the next word ends in `ـٍ`/`ـِ`."* Drill `قَبْلَ أُسْبُوعٍ`، `بَعْدَ الصَّلَاةِ`.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`قَبْلَ`/`بَعْدَ` bhi `تَحْتَ`/`أَمَامَ` ke khandaan se — agla noun **kasra** leta hai: `قَبْلَ أُسْبُوعٍ` (na ke `أُسْبُوعٌ`), `بَعْدَ الصَّلَاةِ`. Wahi purana kasra-qaida, naye lafz par."*

### Landmine 4 · Adding a "dual" or asking about "you two"

Because the table feels so complete, a sharp classmate may ask "what about *two* women / *two* men?" — reaching for the dual.

**Counter-strategy:** Welcome the question, then park it warmly: *"Beautiful question — Arabic does have a special 'two' form, but that's the **next** lesson's adventure. Today we finish the one-and-many table; the 'two' is a whole new number we'll meet in Book… next."* Do **not** write `أَنْتُمَا`/`هُمَا`.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Agar koi poochhe 'do log/do auratein?' — shabaash, achha sawaal! Magar Arabic ka 'do' wala form (dual) **agle sabaq** ki baat hai, ek naya number. Aaj sirf 'ek' aur 'bahut' wala table mukammal karenge. `أَنْتُمَا`/`هُمَا` abhi mat likho."*

### Landmine 5 · Reciting the greeting incompletely

The lesson opens with the **full** greeting `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ وَرَحْمَةُ اللهِ وَبَرَكَاتُهُ`. Classmates habitually stop at `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ`.

**Counter-strategy:** Use the opening as a memorise-and-recite moment, exactly like Ch 14's creedal sentence. Build it in three pieces — `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ` / `وَرَحْمَةُ اللهِ` / `وَبَرَكَاتُهُ` — and have the class greet each other with the full form. It's a spiritual win and a perfect `ـكُمْ` review.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Sabaq poore salaam se shuru hota hai: `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ وَرَحْمَةُ اللهِ وَبَرَكَاتُهُ`. Aksar log sirf `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ` par ruk jaate hain — aaj poora seekho aur ek doosre ko poora salaam karo. (Yeh `ـكُمْ` ka revision bhi hai.)"*

## Suggested class plan (one sitting, ≈60 min)

*Board before class:* the title (`الدَّرْسُ الْخَامِسَ عَشَرَ`); the `…م` vs `…نّ` pair (`أَنْتُمْ`/`أَنْتُنَّ`, `ـكُمْ`/`ـكُنَّ`); and an **empty** two-column pronoun grid to fill in live during the last third.

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–6 min | The **full greeting** `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ وَرَحْمَةُ اللهِ وَبَرَكَاتُهُ` — recite together. Recap last lesson's `أَنْتُمْ`/`ـكُمْ`. |
| 6–20 min | **`أَنْتُنَّ` + `ـكُنَّ`** — the `نّ` twin of `أَنْتُمْ`/`ـكُمْ`. Tie to `هُنَّ`. Drill `بَيْتُكُنَّ`، `أُمُّكُنَّ`، `حَالُكُنَّ`. Read the first half of the dialogue. |
| 20–30 min | **The verb `ذَهَبْتُنَّ`** — and the three-way contrast `ذَهَبْنَ`/`ذَهَبْتُنَّ`/`ذَهَبْنَا` (Landmine 1). The dialogue's closing pair `أَذَهَبْتُنَّ ؟ — ذَهَبْنَا`. |
| 30–40 min | **Time words** `قَبْلَ`/`بَعْدَ`/`مَتَى` (+ `أُسْبُوع`/`شَهْر`). Slot `قَبْلَ`/`بَعْدَ` into the kasra family. Quick drill: `قَبْلَ الصَّلَاةِ`، `بَعْدَ الْأَذَانِ`. |
| 40–54 min | **★ The complete pronoun table** — fill the empty grid on the board, column by column. Read the four patterns aloud (the `هـ`, `أنت`, `أنا/نحن` families; `…م` vs `…نّ`). |
| 54–60 min | **Celebrate the finished foundation** (Concept 5). Homework. End on the full greeting again. |

**Yaad rakho:** Ek baithak kaafi hai (~60 min). Yeh halka, **khushi wala** sabaq hai — zyada-tar pichhle hafte ka aurton-wala aks. Shuru poore salaam se, phir `أَنْتُنَّ`/`ـكُنَّ` (= `أَنْتُمْ`/`ـكُمْ` ka `نّ` twin), phir verb `ذَهَبْتُنَّ`, phir time-lafz. **Aakhri tihaai: poora pronoun table** board par bharo, patterns parho. Aakhir mein mukammal buniyaad ki mubaarakbaad.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 15

Because this is the FINAL lesson, the temptation is the **opposite** of pre-teaching — it's *over-reaching* to "round things off." Resist these:

- ❌ **The dual (`الْمُثَنَّى`) — "you two / they two"** — `أَنْتُمَا`/`هُمَا`, `طَالِبَانِ`. A **different number**, **Lesson 16+**. Do not add a dual column to the table, even to look complete. This is the single biggest temptation of the final lesson.
- ❌ **The present-tense verb / full conjugation** — teach only the past `ـتُنَّ` ending on the known verbs. No `يَذْهَبُ`/`تَذْهَبُ`.
- ❌ **The diptote (`غَيْر مُنْصَرِف`) rule as a named system** — `إِبْرَاهِيمُ` appears in the dialogue with no tanween, as always; read as printed, say nothing about the rule. (The book still hasn't named it — this is the 5th read-only encounter.)
- ❌ **The ẓarf / muḍāf-ilayhi theory behind `قَبْلَ`/`بَعْدَ`** — just teach "next word takes kasra," like `تَحْتَ`.
- ❌ **A "complete verb table"** beyond the address/speaker grid the book draws — don't bolt on persons or tenses the book doesn't show on p.91.

**Yaad rakho:** Aakhri sabaq ka khatra = pre-teaching nahin, balki **zyada karna** ("course mukammal karne" ke chakkar mein). Mat karo: **Dual** (`أَنْتُمَا`/`هُمَا`, "do log") — agla number, L16+, table mein column mat barhao (yeh sabse bada lalach hai). Present-tense / poori conjugation — sirf past `ـتُنَّ`. Diptote rule — bata mat, `إِبْرَاهِيمُ` jaise likha hai waise parho. `قَبْلَ`/`بَعْدَ` ki theory — sirf "agla noun kasra". Book ke p.91 grid se zyada verb-table mat banao.

## Self-check before teaching

Before the class, make sure:

- [ ] You can use `أَنْتُنَّ` ("you all", women) and the tail `ـكُنَّ` on any noun (`بَيْتُكُنَّ`, `أُمُّكُنَّ`), and you connect the `نّ` to last lesson's `هُنَّ`.
- [ ] You can form and contrast the three look-alikes `ذَهَبْنَ` / `ذَهَبْتُنَّ` / `ذَهَبْنَا` and say which is "they / you / we".
- [ ] You can use `قَبْلَ`/`بَعْدَ`/`مَتَى` correctly, with kasra after `قَبْلَ`/`بَعْدَ` (`قَبْلَ أُسْبُوعٍ`).
- [ ] You can draw the **complete** detached-pronoun table from memory and name the four reading-patterns (`هـ`, `أنت`, `أنا/نحن` families; `…م`/`…نّ`).
- [ ] You can recite the **full** greeting `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ وَرَحْمَةُ اللهِ وَبَرَكَاتُهُ`.
- [ ] You know to *defer the dual* — and to let the table be finished as the book finishes it, with no extra column.

If yes to all six — *tum tayar ho*. This is the lesson where the whole system clicks shut. Teach it like the arrival it is.

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Do they use `أَنْتُنَّ`/`ـكُنَّ` (not `أَنْتُمْ`/`ـكُمْ`) for a group of women? *(Concept 1, Landmine 2.)*
2. Do they keep `ذَهَبْنَ` / `ذَهَبْتُنَّ` / `ذَهَبْنَا` apart? *(Concept 2, Landmine 1.)*
3. Do they put kasra after `قَبْلَ`/`بَعْدَ` (`بَعْدَ شَهْرٍ`)? *(Concept 3, Landmine 3.)*
4. Can they read the **complete** pronoun table down both columns and explain a pattern? *(Concept 4.)*
5. Do they greet with the **full** form `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ وَرَحْمَةُ اللهِ وَبَرَكَاتُهُ`? *(Landmine 5.)*
6. Do they *resist* inventing a dual — and treat the table as finished? *(The deferral line.)*
