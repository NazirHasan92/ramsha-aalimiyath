How Ch 15 closes the system

Every new piece is the feminine twin of a Ch 14 form — same slot, new gender:

  • The pronoun (Ch 14): أَنْتُمْ ("you all", men) → أَنْتُنَّ ("you all", women) — the last empty box of the grid.
  • The tail (Ch 14): ـكُمْ → ـكُنَّ ("your", to women) — same five-nouns shape (أَخُوكُنَّ).
  • The verb (Ch 14): ذَهَبْتُمْ → ذَهَبْتُنَّ ("you women went").

Men end in …م, women end in the doubled …نّ — the same نّ as last lesson's هُنَّ. After today, the table has no empty boxes. (The dual is a different number — Lesson 16+.)

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Use أَنْتُنَّ ("you all", women) and the attached tail ـكُنَّ ("your") — بَيْتُكُنَّ, أُمُّكُنَّ.
  2. Form and tell apart the feminine-plural verb ذَهَبْتُنَّ ("you women went") from ذَهَبْنَ ("they-women") and ذَهَبْنَا ("we").
  3. Use the time words قَبْلَ (before), بَعْدَ (after), مَتَى (when), with kasra after قَبْلَ/بَعْدَ.
  4. Read and explain the complete detached-pronoun table (p.91).
  5. Recite the full greeting السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ وَرَحْمَةُ اللهِ وَبَرَكَاتُهُ.

1 · أَنْتُنَّ ("you all", women) & the tail ـكُنَّ ("your")

Addressing a group of women, أَنْتُمْ (Ch 14) becomes أَنْتُنَّ, and the tail ـكُمْ becomes ـكُنَّ. The signature is the doubled نّ — the same sound as last lesson's هُنَّ ("they", women).

Where this fits: أَنْتُمْ/ـكُمْ (Ch 14) and هُنَّ/ـهُنَّ (Ch 13) scaled to the 2nd-person feminine. The dialogue is full of it: مَنْ أَنْتُنَّ ؟, كَيْفَ حَالُكُنَّ ؟, أُمُّكُنَّ أُسْتَاذَتِي.

أَنْتُنَّ / ـكُنَّ · "you all" / "your" (women)

Meaning
أَنْتُنَّ = "you all" (a group of women). The tail ـكُنَّ = "your" (of you women): بَيْتُكُنَّ (your house), أُمُّكُنَّ (your mother), مَدْرَسَتُكُنَّ (your school), حَالُكُنَّ (your state), اخْتِبَارُكُنَّ (your exam).
Roman Urdu
أَنْتُنَّ = "tum sab" (auratein). Tail ـكُنَّ = "tumhara" (aurton ka): بَيْتُكُنَّ (tumhara ghar). Pehchaan: doubled نّ = aurton ka group — wahi نّ jo هُنَّ mein hai.
Rule
Perfect parallel with the masculine: أَنْتُمْ/أَنْتُنَّ, ـكُمْ/ـكُنَّ. Men end in a plain …مْ; women end in the doubled …نَّ. On the five nouns the long-vowel shape stays: أَخُوكُنَّ, أَبُوكُنَّ (recall from Ch 10, don't re-teach).
Example
مَنْ أَنْتُنَّ يَا أَخَوَاتُ ؟ — أُمُّكُنَّ أُسْتَاذَتِي . man antunna yā akhawāt? — ummukunna ustādhatī. · "Who are you (all, women)? — Your mother is my teacher."

💡 Teach it as the نّ twin of last lesson

Write the pairs side by side: أَنْتُمْ / أَنْتُنَّ and ـكُمْ / ـكُنَّ. One rule does it: "men …م, women …نّ." Tie the نّ to هُنَّ ("they", women) from last lesson so it feels familiar, not new.

2 · The verb ذَهَبْتُنَّ ("you women went") & the address grid

The feminine-plural past verb of address adds ـتُنَّ. This is the last corner of the verb-of-address grid, which the book draws in full on p.91. Watch the three look-alikes: ذَهَبْنَ (they-women) / ذَهَبْتُنَّ (you-women) / ذَهَبْنَا (we).

Where this fits: Extends the past-verb agreement of Ch 12–14. Teach only the ـتُنَّ ending on the known verbs (ذَهَبَ/خَرَجَ/رَجَعَ) — no present tense, no full table. The dialogue: أَذَهَبْتُنَّ إِلَى الْمَدْرَسَةِ ؟ — نَعَمْ ، ذَهَبْنَا وَرَجَعْنَا.

ذَهَبْتُنَّ · "you (women) went"

Meaning
Add ـتُنَّ for "you all (women)": ذَهَبْتُنَّ, خَرَجْتُنَّ, رَجَعْتُنَّ. The ت says "you", the doubled نّ says "women".
Roman Urdu
"Tum sab" (auratein) → ـتُنَّ (ذَهَبْتُنَّ — "tum auratein gayin"). Chhota ت = "tum", نّ = aurton ka group.
Rule
Keep three apart: ذَهَبْنَ = "they-women" (ـْنَ, L13); ذَهَبْتُنَّ = "you-women" (ـتُنَّ, now); ذَهَبْنَا = "we" (ـنَا, Ch 14, gender-free). Only the ـتُنَّ ending is new; teach it on the known verbs only.
Example
أَذَهَبْتُنَّ إِلَى الْمَدْرَسَةِ الْيَوْمَ ؟ — نَعَمْ ، ذَهَبْنَا وَرَجَعْنَا . a-dhahabtunna ilā l-madrasati l-yawm? — naʿam, dhahabnā wa-rajaʿnā. · "Did you (women) go to school today? — Yes, we went and came back."

3 · Time words — قَبْلَ (before) / بَعْدَ (after) / مَتَى (when)

The new-words box (p.91) hands the class a small, useful time vocabulary. قَبْلَ/بَعْدَ behave exactly like تَحْتَ/أَمَامَ — the next noun takes kasra. Nothing structurally new.

Where this fits: قَبْلَ/بَعْدَ join the "kasra family" the class already owns (فِي/عَلَى/تَحْتَ/أَمَامَ). مَتَى ("when?") joins the question words. The dialogue: مَتَى ذَهَبَتْ ؟ — قَبْلَ أُسْبُوعٍ; اخْتِبَارُنَا بَعْدَ شَهْرٍ.

قَبْلَ / بَعْدَ / مَتَى · before / after / when

Meaning
قَبْلَ = "before" (قَبْلَ الصَّلَاةِ = before the prayer); بَعْدَ = "after" (بَعْدَ الْأَذَانِ = after the adhān); مَتَى = "when?".
Roman Urdu
قَبْلَ = "pehle / se pehle"; بَعْدَ = "baad"; مَتَى = "kab?". قَبْلَ أُسْبُوعٍ = "ek hafta pehle".
Rule
قَبْلَ/بَعْدَ work like تَحْتَ/أَمَامَ: the word ends in fatḥa and the next noun takes kasra — قَبْلَ أُسْبُوعٍ, بَعْدَ شَهْرٍ, بَعْدَ الدَّرْسِ. (Keep the ẓarf theory to yourself.)
Example
مَتَى ذَهَبَتْ ؟ — ذَهَبَتْ قَبْلَ أُسْبُوعٍ . — اخْتِبَارُنَا بَعْدَ شَهْرٍ . matā dhahabat? — dhahabat qabla usbūʿ. — ikhtibārunā baʿda shahr. · "When did she go? — She went a week ago. — Our exam is after a month."

💡 Slot them into the kasra family

The class already knows the next-noun-takes-kasra reflex from فِي/عَلَى/تَحْتَ/أَمَامَ. Just say "two new members join — قَبْلَ/بَعْدَ — same rule." No new mechanism to learn. Wahi purana kasra-qaida, naye lafz par.

💡 Read the patterns aloud — that's the teaching gift

  • The هـ family points away from us (3rd person): هُوَ/هِيَ/هُمْ/هُنَّ.
  • The أنت family points at the listener (2nd person) — all four built on أَنْتـ: أَنْتَ/أَنْتِ/أَنْتُمْ/أَنْتُنَّ.
  • The أنا/نحن family is the speaker (1st person).
  • أَنَا and نَحْنُ don't change for gender (shown twice — same word). Everywhere else, …م/…َ = masculine, …نّ/…ِ = feminine.

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Confusing the three "…n" verbs

ذَهَبْنَ ("they-women", L13) / ذَهَبْتُنَّ ("you-women", now) / ذَهَبْنَا ("we", Ch 14). Circle the distinguishing piece: a ت for "you" + doubled نّ = ذَهَبْتُنَّ. Drill the three-way switch.

2. Using masculine أَنْتُمْ/ـكُمْ for women

The fresh Ch 14 forms (and the عَلَيْكُمْ reflex) make them default to …م. Teach أَنْتُنَّ/ـكُنَّ as the نّ twin: "men …م, women …نّ."

3. Dropping the kasra after قَبْلَ/بَعْدَ

They say قَبْلَ أُسْبُوعٌ ✗. Slot قَبْلَ/بَعْدَ into the kasra family (تَحْتَ/أَمَامَ): the next noun ends in ـٍ — قَبْلَ أُسْبُوعٍ.

4. Reaching for the dual ("you two / they two")

Because the table feels complete, a sharp classmate asks about "two". Welcome it, then park it: "that's a special 'two' form — next lesson's adventure." Don't write أَنْتُمَا/هُمَا.

5. Reciting the greeting incompletely

The lesson opens with the full السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ وَرَحْمَةُ اللهِ وَبَرَكَاتُهُ. Classmates stop at السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ — build the full form in three pieces and have them greet each other with all of it.

6. Adding a tanween to إِبْرَاهِيمُ (and other names)

إِبْرَاهِيمُ appears in the dialogue with a plain ـُ, no tanween — read it as printed. The "why" (غَيْر مُنْصَرِف) is still deferred; the book hasn't named the rule.

📿 Connect to the Qur'an

Today's feminine-plural forms — أَنْتُنَّ, ـكُنَّ, the verb ـتُنَّ — and the wider plural set appear in the Qur'an. Three verses your class can connect to the lesson's own words.

Surah Yūsuf · 12:51 — ـكُنَّ ("your") + the verb ـتُنَّ

قَالَ مَا خَطْبُكُنَّ إِذْ رَاوَدتُّنَّ يُوسُفَ عَن نَّفْسِهِ

qāla mā khaṭbukunna idh rāwadttunna yūsufa ʿan nafsih — "He said: What was your affair when you sought to seduce Yūsuf?"

Students recognise: خَطْبُكُنَّ = "your (women's) affair" — the tail ـكُنَّ (Concept 1); and رَاوَدتُّنَّ carries the verb ending ـتُنَّ ("you women did") — exactly Concept 2. Both of today's new forms in one verse.

Surah Al-Aḥzāb · 33:33 — ـكُنَّ ("your", to the women)

وَقَرْنَ فِي بُيُوتِكُنَّ

wa-qarna fī buyūtikunna — "And stay in your houses." (part of 33:33, addressed to the wives of the Prophet ﷺ)

Students recognise: بُيُوتِكُنَّ = "your houses" — the tail ـكُنَّ on a plural noun, addressed to a group of women. The same form as the lesson's بَيْتُكُنَّ / مَدْرَسَتُكُنَّ.

Surah Al-Baqarah · 2:187 — the whole plural set together

هُنَّ لِبَاسٌ لَّكُمْ وَأَنْتُمْ لِبَاسٌ لَّهُنَّ

hunna libāsun lakum wa-antum libāsun lahunna — "They are a garment for you, and you are a garment for them."

Students recognise: four plural pronouns/tails in one phrase — هُنَّ ("they", f), ـكُمْ ("you all"), أَنْتُمْ ("you all", m), ـهُنَّ ("them", f). A perfect "the chart clicks shut" verse — the finished plural system working all at once.
