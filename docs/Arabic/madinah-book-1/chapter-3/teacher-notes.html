How Ch 3 connects back

Two threads from earlier chapters land here:

  • Ch 1's case system (the 6-cell ending grid) — Ch 1 only showed the top row (rafʿ: ـٌ / ـُ). Ch 3 unlocks the bottom row (jarr: ـٍ / ـِ). Don't name it "jarr" in class — just say "the ending becomes kasra after a preposition".
  • Ch 2's demonstrative grid — Ch 2 closed the masculine row (هَذَا + ذَلِكَ). Ch 3 opens the feminine row with هَذِهِ. One cell left (تِلْكَ, far feminine, much later).

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Use the 4 basic prepositions: فِي (in), عَلَى (on), إِلَى (to), مِنْ (from).
  2. Apply the kasra ending on the noun that follows a preposition: al-baytu → fil-bayti; baytun → fee baytin.
  3. Recognise that عَلَى and إِلَى end in alif-maqsoorah (looks like ى, sounds like long -aa).
  4. Apply the مِنَ الْ rule (ن takes a fatḥa when meeting الـ).
  5. Ask "Where is...?" using أَيْنَ, and answer with a preposition phrase.
  6. Distinguish the twins: مَنْ (who) vs مِنْ (from) — same letters, different vowel.
  7. Use the feminine demonstrative هَذِهِ with feminine nouns (those ending in ة).
  8. Recognize and use ~10 new nouns: room, kitchen, market, school, garden, bag, pocket, etc.

1 · The four prepositions — حُرُوف الجَرّ

A small family of "anchor words" that locate, direct, or originate a noun. Today's set is the foundation set for the rest of Book 1.

Where this fits: Ch 1 and Ch 2 sentences were Mubtada' + Khabar (two nouns). Now the Khabar can also be a preposition + noun — "the book is on the desk". Sentence stretches without breaking.

فِي · fee · in / inside

Meaning
"in / inside" — placing a noun inside a container, building, or space.
Roman Urdu
"Mein" — jaise "ghar mein", "kitaab bag mein".
Rule
The noun after فِي takes kasra on its ending (see Concept 2).
Example
الْكِتَابُ فِي الْحَقِيبَةِ al-kitaabu fil-ḥaqeebati · "The book is in the bag" · "Kitaab bag mein hai"

عَلَى · ʿalā · on / upon

Meaning
"on / upon" — placing a noun on a surface (table, desk, floor, etc.).
Roman Urdu
"Par" — jaise "mez par", "kitaab kursi par".
Important
The final ى is alif-maqsoorah — pronounced as long "aa", NOT "y". Say ʿa-laa, not ʿa-lay. In vocalised editions you'll see عَلَىٰ with a dagger-alif on top — that's the reading aid.
Example
الْقَلَمُ عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ al-qalamu ʿalal-maktabi · "The pen is on the desk" · "Qalam mez par hai"

إِلَى · ilā · to / towards

Meaning
"to / towards" — direction of motion or destination.
Roman Urdu
"Tak / ko / ki taraf" — jaise "school ki taraf", "masjid tak".
Important
Same alif-maqsoorah rule as عَلَى. Say i-laa, not i-lay.
Example
الطَّرِيقُ إِلَى الْمَدْرَسَةِ aṭ-ṭareequ ilal-madrasati · "The road to the school" · "Raasta school ki taraf"

مِنْ · min · from / out of

Meaning
"from / out of" — origin, source.
Roman Urdu
"Se / mein se" — jaise "market se", "ghar se".
Important — two things
  1. The final ن is silent (sukoon). Pronounce: min, crisp and short. No phantom vowel.
  2. When مِنْ meets الـ, the ن takes a fatḥa to bridge the gap: مِنْ + الْبَيْتِ → مِنَ الْبَيْتِ ("min-al → minal").
Example
هَذَا مِنَ السُّوقِ haadhaa minas-sūqi · "This is from the market" · "Yeh bazaar se hai"

💡 Teaching tip — drill them as a foursome

Don't teach all four prepositions in 30 seconds and move on. Take one (فِي), drill 8 nouns with it, then add the next. Each new preposition is also a chance to drill the kasra rule again (Concept 2). Roman Urdu equivalents anchor the meaning: fee = mein, ʿalā = par, ilā = tak / ki taraf, min = se. Use these natural Urdu equivalents — your classmates' inner translation is already running in Urdu.

⚠️ Don't pre-teach other prepositions

Arabic has many more prepositions: بِـ (with/by), لِـ (for), عَنْ (about), مَعَ (with). Don't introduce them today. لِـ is the focus of Chapter 4; the others come later. If a classmate asks: "yeh aage chapters mein milengi — abhi sirf yeh chaar."

2 · The noun ending shifts to kasra

The single most important rule of this chapter. If classmates miss this, the rest of Book 1 gets shaky.

Where this fits: Ch 1 quietly showed one ending shift (baytun → al-baytu) — that was about definiteness. This is a different kind of shift — caused by the preposition. The noun's ending now flips below the letter — pesh becomes zer.

The rule — one line

English
After one of the four prepositions, the noun's ending changes to kasra. Pesh below the letter. The "doubling" stays if the noun was indefinite.
Roman Urdu
Preposition ke baad wale ism ke aakhir mein zer (ـِ) aa jaati hai. Agar lafz mein "ال" hai — sirf ek zer. Agar nahin hai (indefinite) — do-zer (`ـٍ`), yani "-in" wali aawaz.
Compare
Default form (rafʿ): الْبَيْتُ al-baytu
After preposition: فِي الْبَيْتِ fil-bayti

Default form (rafʿ, indefinite): بَيْتٌ baytun
After preposition: فِي بَيْتٍ fee baytin

Full drill set — read aloud across

Definite
الْبَيْتُ → فِي الْبَيْتِ → عَلَى الْبَيْتِ → إِلَى الْبَيْتِ → مِنَ الْبَيْتِ al-baytu → fil-bayti → ʿalal-bayti → ilal-bayti → minal-bayti
Indefinite
بَيْتٌ → فِي بَيْتٍ → عَلَى بَيْتٍ → إِلَى بَيْتٍ → مِنْ بَيْتٍ baytun → fee baytin → ʿalā baytin → ilā baytin → min baytin
Roman
Aakhir ki aawaz pakdo — har baar -i ya -in. Yeh "kasra wala family" hai. Pesh hat gayi, zer aa gayi.

The مِنَ الْ bridge

Rule
When مِنْ (silent ن) is followed by الـ, the ن takes a fatḥa to glide into the next word.
Pattern
مِنْ + الْبَيْتِ → مِنَ الْبَيْتِ min + al-bayti → minal-bayti
Why?
Same phonological smoothing rule as sun letters (Ch 1) — Arabic doesn't tolerate two silent consonants colliding. Just say "`مِنْ` + `الْ` = `مِنَ الْ`" as a memorised pair.
Roman
"Min" + "al-" = "minal-" — ن par zabar aa jaati hai. Yeh pair yaad rakho — har baar isi tarah chalega.

💡 Teaching tip — colour-code the ending

On the board, write the ending vowel in a different colour. الْبَيْتُ with a red ـُ, then below it write فِي الْبَيْتِ with a red ـِ. The eye picks up what the ear has trouble with. Repeat with 5 nouns. After this drill, the rule is in their muscle memory.

⚠️ The biggest Ch 3 mistake

Classmates will say "fee baytun", "ʿalal-maktabu", "minal-baytu" — keeping the old rafʿ ending. Two chapters of training will make this stubborn. Catch this on day one — if you let it slide, every preposition phrase for the next 20 chapters will be wrong.

⚠️ Don't use the words "jarr", "case", or "iʿrāb"

You know the underlying system (see Part A of deep-notes.md). Your classmates don't need it yet. Stick to: "preposition ke baad zer aa jaati hai." The technical terms arrive in Chapter 5.

3 · أَيْنَ ؟ — "Where?"

The natural question to use Chapter 3's prepositions — it asks for a location, and the answer uses today's preposition + kasra rule.

Where this fits: 5th member of the question family (after أَ, مَا, مَنْ, أَمْ). The answer to "where?" is always a preposition phrase — so this concept silently re-drills Concept 1 and Concept 2.

The "ayna" question pattern

English
أَيْنَ means "where?" — used at the start of a question to ask for a location.
Roman Urdu
"Ayna" = "kahaan?" — sawal mein, kisi ki jagah poochne ke liye.
Pattern
Q: أَيْنَ ___ ؟ (ayna ___?)
A: ___ [preposition] ___ (subject + preposition + noun-with-kasra)
Example
أَيْنَ الْقَلَمُ ؟ — الْقَلَمُ عَلَى الْكِتَابِ ayna l-qalamu? — al-qalamu ʿalal-kitaabi · "Where is the pen? — The pen is on the book" · "Qalam kahaan hai? — Qalam kitaab par hai"
أَيْنَ الْإِمَامُ ؟ — الْإِمَامُ فِي الْمَسْجِدِ ayna l-imaamu? — al-imaamu fil-masjidi · "Where is the imam? — The imam is in the mosque" · "Imam kahaan hai? — Imam masjid mein hai"

⚠️ The "twin words" — مَنْ / مِنْ

Same letters
مَنْ / مِنْ Both: م + ن
مَنْ
man — "who?" (Ch 2). م has fatḥa.
مِنْ
min — "from" (Ch 3). م has kasra.
Roman
Bilkul ek jaisi likhayi — sirf م par zabar ya zer ka farq. Likhne aur padhne mein dono mein vowels lagaao — bina vowels ke confuse honge.

💡 Teaching tip — let "ayna" do double-duty

Every "ayna ___?" question forces the answerer to use a preposition + kasra ending. So this is the cleanest drill in the chapter — one question word that recycles all four prepositions and the case rule in every answer. Use it heavily in the second half of class.

⚠️ Three-way contrast — put on the board from minute one

Write all three side by side, with vowels:

  • مَنْ = man = "who?" (Ch 2) — for asking about a person
  • مِنْ = min = "from" (Ch 3) — a preposition
  • أَيْنَ = ayna = "where?" (Ch 3) — for asking about location

Drill alternating: point at a classmate ("man haadhaa?"), point at a doorway ("min ayna?"), point at a missing object ("ayna l-qalamu?"). Hearing all three back to back builds the contrast.

هَذِهِ

Meaning
"This" — pointing to something nearby AND feminine.
Roman Urdu
"Yeh" — par sirf zanaana (feminine) lafz ke saath. Mardaana ke saath هَذَا hi rahega.
Important
Three things to remember:
  1. For singular feminine nouns near you. Far feminine (تِلْكَ) comes much later.
  2. Same silent-alif pattern: spelled هَذِهِ, said haa-dhi-hi. The "aa" is between ه and ذ.
  3. The first ه takes a fatḥa, the middle ذ takes kasra, the final ه takes kasra too — haa-dhi-hi.
Example
هَذِهِ غُرْفَةٌ haadhihi ghurfatun · "This is a room" · "Yeh ek kamra hai"

How to spot a feminine noun

Rule
The most common marker: the noun ends in ة (taa marbuuta — "the tied ta", a ه with two dots above).
Roman
"Taa marbuuta" — gol-mol wali "h" jaisi letter (ة). Yeh feminine ki sab se aam pehchaan hai. Aakhir mein ة dekho → feminine → هَذِهِ.
Today's feminine nouns
غُرْفَةٌ · مَدْرَسَةٌ · حَدِيقَةٌ · حَقِيبَةٌ ghurfatun (room), madrasatun (school), ḥadeeqatun (garden), ḥaqeebatun (bag)
Exceptions (not for today)
Some feminine nouns don't end in ة — body parts that come in pairs (يَدٌ hand, عَيْنٌ eye) and a few intrinsic ones (أُمٌّ mother, شَمْسٌ sun). Don't teach these exceptions yet — just teach the ة rule.

Near demonstrative pair — هَذَا vs هَذِهِ

Masculine
هَذَا بَيْتٌ haadhaa baytun · "This is a house"
Feminine
هَذِهِ غُرْفَةٌ haadhihi ghurfatun · "This is a room"
Rule
Match the demonstrative to the noun's gender. Look at the noun's ending: ة? → use هَذِهِ. No ة? → use هَذَا.
Roman
Lafz ki "jodi" yaad karo: هَذَا mardaana ke saath, هَذِهِ zanaana ke saath. Aakhir mein ة dekha? هَذِهِ lagao.

💡 Teaching tip — glue feminine nouns to هَذِهِ

Don't introduce "ghurfatun" on its own first. Introduce it as "haadhihi ghurfatun" from the start. Same for the other feminine nouns. The demonstrative becomes inseparable from the gender — and the reflex is built in. Then alternate with masculine: "haadhaa qalamun · haadhihi ghurfatun · haadhaa kitaabun · haadhihi madrasatun" — 10 alternations and the brain has the pattern.

⚠️ Don't drill the t/h pause-form yet

The ة has two pronunciations — t in connected speech, h at pause. The book vocalises with tanween (ghurfatun) — stick with the t-form. The pause-form ("ghurfah") is a Qur'an recitation skill — Chapter 5+ territory. Don't introduce it today.

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Forgetting the kasra ending after a preposition

The #1 Ch 3 mistake. Classmates say "fee baytun" or "ʿalal-maktabu" — keeping the old ḍamma ending. Two chapters of rafʿ-drill makes this habit stubborn. Catch this on day one. Write the ending in red on the board. Drill "al-baytu → fil-bayti" 8 times before adding the next preposition.

2. Skipping the doubled kasra (ـٍ = "-in") on indefinite nouns

Classmates say "fee baytin" as "fee bayti" or "fee bayt" — they don't hear the doubling. Compare to the Urdu word "taqreeban" (do-zabar) — same idea, opposite vowel direction. Drill: "baytun → fee baytin", ear-train the "-un → -in" flip.

3. Confusing مَنْ (who?) with مِنْ (from)

Same letters, different vowel. Both just learned in successive chapters. Always vocalise both on the board for the first two weeks. Drill them alternating to build the contrast.

4. Using هَذَا for feminine nouns

After two chapters of هَذَا and ذَلِكَ, classmates default to masculine. They'll say "haadhaa ghurfah". Glue every feminine noun to هَذِهِ from the moment it's introduced — never let a feminine noun appear alone in a drill.

5. Reading عَلَى and إِلَى as ending in "-y"

The ى (alif-maqsoorah) at the end looks like ي but sounds like long "aa". Classmates will say "ʿa-lay" and "i-lay". Fix: write them with the explicit dagger-alif first (عَلَىٰ, إِلَىٰ), then drill the "-aa" ending 5 times each.

6. Forgetting the مِنَ الْ bridge

Classmates say "min al-bayti" with a hard stop, instead of "minal-bayti". The ن needs a fatḥa when meeting الـ. Teach this as a memorised pair: "`مِنْ` + `الْ` = `مِنَ الْ`".

📿 Connect to the Qur'an

Chapter 3 grammar is everywhere in the Qur'an — prepositions are some of the most common words in the Book. Use 1–2 of these in class.

Surah Al-Falaq · 113:1–2

قُلْ أَعُوذُ بِرَبِّ الْفَلَقِ ۝ مِن شَرِّ مَا خَلَقَ

qul aʿūdhu bi-rabbi l-falaqi · min sharri maa khalaq — "Say: I seek refuge with the Lord of the daybreak · from the evil of what He created."

Students recognise: مِنْ — the "from" preposition, just learned! And notice شَرِّ — the noun after مِنْ takes kasra. Pattern: preposition → kasra ending.

Surah An-Nas · 114:6

مِنَ الْجِنَّةِ وَالنَّاسِ

min-al-jinnati wa-n-naas — "From [among] the jinn and mankind."

Students recognise: the مِنَ الْ bridge — ن takes a fatḥa when meeting الـ. And notice the kasra endings on الْجِنَّةِ and النَّاسِ — both after the preposition.

Surah Ash-Shura · 42:52 (closing phrase)

وَإِنَّكَ لَتَهْدِي إِلَىٰ صِرَاطٍ مُّسْتَقِيمٍ

wa-innaka la-tahdī ilā ṣirāṭin mustaqīm — "And indeed, you guide to a straight path."

Students recognise: إِلَى — the "to" preposition (with the dagger-alif above ى showing the "-aa" sound). And صِرَاطٍ — indefinite kasra-tanween ("-in") on the noun after the preposition.

Surah At-Takwir · 81:26

فَأَيْنَ تَذْهَبُونَ

fa-ayna tadhhabūn — "Where then are you going?"

Students recognise: أَيْنَ — the "where?" question word, just learned today!
