🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Add الـ to any noun to make it "the ___", dropping the tanween doubling (baytun → al-baytu).
  2. Build a describing sentence: a definite noun + a bare (indefinite) adjective — al-qalamu maksūrun, "the pen is broken".
  3. Keep the adjective bare — الـ on the noun, nothing on the describing word.
  4. Tell sun letters from moon letters, and pronounce الـ correctly before each (al-qamar vs ash-shams).
  5. Recognise and use ~24 new words — mostly adjectives (new, old, hot, cold, big, small, open, broken …).

1 · الـ — The Definite Article ("the")

The single prefix the whole lesson hangs on. In one stroke it turns "a thing" into "the (specific) thing".

Where this fits: in Lesson 1 the student named bare nouns (baytun). Now they make them definite — al-baytu — the subject of every describing sentence to come.

The rule of الـ

Meaning
الـ = English "the". Attach it to the front of any indefinite noun to make it definite (specific).
Roman Urdu
"Al-" = "the" / "woh khaas". Kisi bhi nakira (aam) ism ke shuru mein lagaa do — woh maʿrifa (khaas) ban jaata hai.
Important / Rule
Indefinite nouns end in tanween (a doubled vowel: ـٌ -un). When الـ is added, the doubling drops — you are left with a single ḍamma (ـُ -u). The vowel stays; only the doubling goes.
Example
بَيْتٌ → الْبَيْتُ baytun → al-baytu · "a house" → "the house" · ek ghar → woh ghar

💡 Teaching tip

Draw two columns: aam (a ___) and khaas (the ___). Take 6–7 Lesson-1 nouns the class already knows and write each in both forms: baytun / al-baytu, qalamun / al-qalamu, kitaabun / al-kitaabu. After a few, they spot the dropping doubling themselves — no grammar lecture needed.

2 · The Describing Sentence — "the X is Y"

The pay-off of الـ: a definite noun plus a bare adjective makes a complete statement, still with no word for "is".

Where this fits: Lesson 1 = "this is a house" (naming). Lesson 3 = "the house is big" (describing). Same no-verb nominal sentence — now with an adjective doing the describing.

The pattern: definite noun + bare adjective

Meaning
Put an adjective after a definite noun and you have a full sentence: "the [noun] is [adjective]". The describing word carries no الـ.
Roman Urdu
Khaas ism (الـ wala) + ek sifat (bare) = poora jumla. "Woh cheez aisi HAI." Sifat par `الـ` nahin lagti.
Important / Rule
الـ on the noun, nothing on the adjective. The noun ends in a single ḍamma (definite); the adjective ends in tanween (indefinite). That definite + indefinite split is what makes it a sentence, not just a phrase. Still no word for "is".
Example
الْبَابُ مَفْتُوحٌ al-bābu maftūḥun · "The door is open" · "Darwaaza khula hai"

⚠️ Keep the adjective bare

The most common slip once الـ is fresh: putting it on the describing word too. The thing gets الـ; the describing word stays bare (tanween). Correct it on sight — don't lecture on why yet.

3 · Sun Letters & Moon Letters — الْحُرُوفُ الشَّمْسِيَّةُ وَالْقَمَرِيَّةُ

The pronunciation rule that decides whether the ل of الـ is heard or swallowed. Spelling never changes — only the sound.

Where this fits: now that the student adds الـ to everything, they must know how to say it — and that depends entirely on the next letter.

The names — the test word IS the rule

Meaning
Moon letters take their name from الْقَمَر (al-qamar — L heard). Sun letters from الشَّمْس (ash-shams — L swallowed, ش doubles).
Roman Urdu
"Qamar" (chand) ke ق par "al-" saaf — to ق jaisi 14 letters = qamariyya (moon). "Shams" (sooraj) ke ش par "al-" ki L gum, ش double — to ش jaisi 14 letters = shamsiyya (sun).
Important / Rule
This is a pronunciation rule only. You always write الـ with ل. The visual cue that the ل is silent: a shadda (ـّ) on the next letter.
Example
الْقَمَرُ · الشَّمْسُ al-qamaru (moon, L heard) · ash-shamsu (sun, L swallowed)

Lesson examples
التَّاجِرُ · الثَّوْبُ · الدِّيكُ · الرَّجُلُ · الشَّمْسُ · النَّجْمُ at-tājiru, ath-thawbu, ad-dīku, ar-rajulu, ash-shamsu, an-najmu — NOT "al-…", the next letter doubles.

💡 Memory hook for adult learners

Tell them: "The Sun is loud — it eats the L." Point at the sun (الشمس) → ash-shams, the L is swallowed. The Moon is gentle (القمر) → it lets the L through. After this image, students rarely forget.

⚠️ Ears before eyes

Drill the pairs aloud first — al-bayt / ash-shams, al-qamar / an-najm — before writing anything. In plain text the shadda is often missing, so a learner reading "by eye" will say "al-shams". The rule lives in the ear.

💡 The sentences reuse known nouns

The describing sentences are built on nouns the class already met in Lessons 1–2 (الْبَيْت، الْكِتَاب، الْقَلَم، الْبَاب، الْوَلَد، الْمَسْجِد، الْحَجَر، اللَّبَن، الْمَاء…). Today's new learning is the adjectives plus the three nouns above — so spend drill time on the adjectives.

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Keeping the doubling after الـ ("al-baytun")

Student says al-baytun instead of al-baytu. Correct: when الـ is added, the doubling drops — single ḍamma only. Write baytun → al-baytu side by side and circle the change.

2. Saying "al-shams" instead of "ash-shams"

Sun-letter assimilation is unfamiliar to Urdu/English speakers. Drill it aloud: ash-shams, an-najm, ar-rajul, ad-deek, at-tuffaaḥ — comparing with the moon letters al-bayt, al-qamar, al-kitaab.

3. Putting الـ on the adjective

Student writes al-qalamu al-maksūru. The thing gets الـ; the describing word stays bare. Keep every adjective in tanween. (Don't show the definite-adjective form — that's a later lesson.)

4. Inserting a word for "is"

"The pen is broken" tempts a learner to add a word for "is". Remind them: the two words sit side by side — al-qalamu maksūrun — the silence is the "is".

5. Mixing up the size/length adjectives

طَوِيل (tall/long) vs قَصِير (short); كَبِير (big) vs صَغِير (small). Anchor طويل/قصير to height/length and كبير/صغير to size with a quick board sketch.

📿 Connect to the Qur'an — sun & moon letters in recitation

Lesson 3 teaches no Qur'anic vocabulary directly — but the sun/moon rule is a recitation (tajwīd) rule. Show the class that what they learned today is exactly why we recite ar-Raḥmān, not al-Raḥmān. This is the emotional pay-off of the lesson.

Surah Al-Fātiḥah · 1:2 — a MOON letter (ل heard)

الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ رَبِّ الْعَالَمِينَ

al-ḥamdu lillāhi rabbi l-ʿālamīn — "All praise is for Allah, Lord of the worlds."

Hear it: ح is a moon letter, so the L is clear — al-ḥamd. Just learned today!

Surah Al-Fātiḥah · 1:3 — a SUN letter (ل swallowed)

الرَّحْمَٰنِ الرَّحِيمِ

ar-raḥmāni r-raḥīm — "The Most Compassionate, the Most Merciful."

Hear it: ر is a sun letter, so the L is swallowed and ر doubles — ar-raḥmān, NOT "al-raḥmān". The shadda on ر is the cue.

The two refuge-sūrahs — one of each

قُلْ أَعُوذُ بِرَبِّ الْفَلَقِ  ·  قُلْ أَعُوذُ بِرَبِّ النَّاسِ

…bi-rabbi l-falaq (113:1) — ف moon letter, L heard: al-falaq.
…bi-rabbi n-nās (114:1) — ن sun letter, L swallowed: an-nās.

Same الـ, two sounds — exactly today's rule, in two sūrahs they recite every day.
