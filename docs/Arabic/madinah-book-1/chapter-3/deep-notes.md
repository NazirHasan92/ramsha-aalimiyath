# Part A · Understanding the chapter

Lesson 3 is where Arabic stops being a list of labels ("this is a pen") and starts becoming a language you can *say things with*. It hands you three tools that work together:

1. **الـ** — the prefix that turns "a pen" into "the pen".
2. **The descriptive sentence** — putting an adjective after a definite noun: *"the pen is broken"*, *"the door is open"*.
3. **Sun & moon letters** — the pronunciation rule that decides whether the **ل** of `الـ` is heard or swallowed.

Notice the design: the book teaches `الـ` first, then immediately uses it as the *subject* of dozens of describing sentences, then explains the one pronunciation rule that `الـ` triggers. Everything in this lesson hangs off that little prefix.

**Yaad rakho:** Poora Lesson 3 ek chhote prefix `الـ` ke ird-gird ghoomta hai — pehle woh seekho, phir us se sentences banao, phir us ka bolne ka rule (sun/moon) samjho. Teen cheezein, ek hi jad (root).

> **A note on this edition.** Many other printings of Madinah Book 1 squeeze `الـ` and sun/moon letters into Lesson 1. **This edition does not** — it deliberately holds them back to Lesson 3, after the learner is comfortable with bare indefinite nouns (Lesson 1) and `ذَلِكَ`/`مَا`/`مَنْ` (Lesson 2). So when you teach, treat `الـ` as genuinely **new** — your classmates have *not* met it before.

## 1 · الـ — what "definiteness" really is

`الـ` translates as "the", but the grammatical idea underneath is bigger than the English word.

### Definiteness is a binary

Every Arabic noun is in one of two states:

- **نَكِرَة** (*nakira*) — indefinite — *aam ism* (general / any one) — `بَيْتٌ` "a house"
- **مَعْرِفَة** (*maʿrifa*) — definite — *khaas ism* (a specific one) — `الْبَيْتُ` "the house"

`الـ` is *one* way to make a noun definite. (Names like `مُحَمَّد`, and the noun-noun construction, are other ways — those come in later lessons. Don't open them now.)

**Yaad rakho:** Arabic mein har ism ya to *aam* (nakira) hai ya *khaas* (maʿrifa). `الـ` lagaane se aam → khaas. Yeh sirf "the" nahin — yeh ism ki ek haalat (state) hai.

### Why the tanween drops

`بَيْتٌ` ends in `ـٌ` — a doubled ḍamma, called **tanween**. That doubling is a marker that lives *only* on indefinite nouns. The moment the noun becomes definite (you add `الـ`), the doubling has no reason to be there — so it drops. What's left is a single ḍamma `ـُ`:

> `بَيْتٌ → الْبَيْتُ` · *baytun → al-baytu* · "a house → the house"
> `قَلَمٌ → الْقَلَمُ` · *qalamun → al-qalamu* · "a pen → the pen"
> `كِتَابٌ → الْكِتَابُ` · *kitābun → al-kitābu* · "a book → the book"

The vowel itself (ḍamma) never changed — only the *doubling* went away. (Why ḍamma and not something else? Because these nouns are sitting in the subject role, which always carries ḍamma. That role has a formal name — but the book doesn't name it for a while yet, so neither will you.)

**Yaad rakho:** `الـ` lagaane se sirf *doubling* jaati hai, harakat wahi rehti hai. *Baytun* (do-pesh) → *al-baytu* (ek pesh). Classmates ko bas itna: "al- lagao, doubling hat jaati hai."

### Spelling the alif of الـ

You always write `ال` with an alif. But that alif is a **connecting alif** — its vowel is only pronounced when nothing comes before it. Say `الْبَيْتُ` on its own → "al-baytu". Say it after another word and the "a" of "al" slips away in speech. Your classmates don't need the *name* for this yet (it gets named in a later lesson) — for now they just copy how you say it.

**Yaad rakho (apne liye):** Us connecting-alif ka rasmi naam baad ke lesson mein aayega. Abhi naam mat lo — class ko sirf sunaao, woh naqal kar lenge.

## 2 · The descriptive sentence — putting an adjective to work

In Lesson 1 the learner said *"this is a house"* — pointing and naming. Lesson 3 lets them **describe**:

> `الْقَلَمُ مَكْسُورٌ` · *al-qalamu maksūrun* · "The pen is broken."
> `الْبَابُ مَفْتُوحٌ` · *al-bābu maftūḥun* · "The door is open."
> `الْوَلَدُ جَالِسٌ، وَالْمُدَرِّسُ وَاقِفٌ` · *al-waladu jālisun, wa-l-mudarrisu wāqifun* · "The boy is sitting, and the teacher is standing."

### The shape of it

Two parts, no verb (the same "no word for *is*" rule from Lesson 1):

- **A definite noun** (with `الـ`) — this is what we're talking about. *al-qalamu.*
- **An indefinite adjective** (with tanween) — this is what we say about it. *maksūrun.*

The technical names are **مُبْتَدَأ** (*mubtada'* — the thing we start with) and **خَبَر** (*khabar* — the news about it) — the two pillars of a nominal sentence. You should know these names. **Your classmates do not need the Arabic terms in Lesson 3** — the book itself teaches these sentences purely by example, with no terminology on the page. Teach the *pattern*, not the labels: *"the definite thing + a describing word = a full sentence."*

**Yaad rakho:** Andar se yeh wahi *jumla ismiyya* hai jo Lesson 1 se chal raha hai — pehla hissa khaas ism (mubtada'), doosra hissa describing word (khabar). Par class ko `مبتدأ`/`خبر` ke naam mat ratao — sirf pattern: "khaas cheez + ek sifat = poora jumla."

### The one thing that quietly matters: the noun is definite, the adjective is NOT

Look at the endings:

| | word | ending | state |
|---|---|---|---|
| noun (mubtada') | `الْقَلَمُ` | single ḍamma `ـُ` | definite (`الـ`) |
| adjective (khabar) | `مَكْسُورٌ` | tanween ḍamma `ـٌ` | indefinite |

The noun gets `الـ`; the adjective stays bare (tanween). This split — **definite + indefinite** — is exactly what makes it a *sentence* ("the pen **is** broken") rather than a *phrase*.

> **For your understanding only — do NOT teach this, and do NOT show classmates the form.** If you instead made *both* words definite (`الْقَلَمُ الْمَكْسُورُ`), the meaning collapses from a sentence into a phrase: "the broken pen". That definite-adjective construction is a *named grammatical relationship* introduced **later in the book** — it is not Lesson 3's job. In class, keep every adjective bare (tanween). If a sharp classmate writes `الـ` on the adjective, just say: *"abhi sifat par `الـ` nahin lagti — woh aage aayega."* Don't name it, don't show the example.

**Yaad rakho:** Lesson 3 ka raaz: ism par `الـ`, sifat par tanween (bare). Yahi farq sentence ko phrase banne se rokta hai. *"Al-qalamu maksūrun"* = "qalam toota hua HAI" (jumla). Dono par `الـ` lagaane wali shakl baad ke lesson ka kaam hai — abhi na naam lo, na dikhaao.

### All the endings here are the same kind

Every noun and adjective in this lesson's describing sentences ends in a ḍamma — single on the definite noun, doubled (tanween) on the indefinite adjective. There is no new ending to learn — no kasra, no fatḥa. That keeps the lesson clean: the *only* new ending-behaviour is "tanween drops when `الـ` arrives", which you already covered in Concept 1.

**Yaad rakho:** Is lesson mein koi nayi harakat nahin — sab pesh (ḍamma) par hai. Kasra/zer wagairah Lesson 4+ ka maamla hai. Abhi sirf pesh.

## 3 · Sun & moon letters — the phonology behind `الـ`

This is the "magical-seeming" rule of the chapter — but it has a real, physical reason.

### Why the 14/14 split is not arbitrary

The 14 **sun letters** are articulated at the *front/middle* of the mouth — tongue against the teeth or the ridge just behind them:

> ت · ث · د · ذ · ر · ز · س · ش · ص · ض · ط · ظ · ل · ن

The 14 **moon letters** are articulated deeper (throat, soft palate) or with the lips:

> ء · ب · ج · ح · خ · ع · غ · ف · ق · ك · م · هـ · و · ي

The **ل** of `الـ` is itself a front-of-mouth (alveolar) consonant. When the *next* letter is also front-of-mouth (a sun letter), the tongue doesn't bother moving — it just merges the two, and the next letter doubles (you see a **shadda ـّ**). When the next letter is articulated far away (a moon letter), the ل stays clear because there's nothing nearby to merge into.

> **Moon (ل heard):** `الْقَمَرُ` *al-qamaru* · `الْبَابُ` *al-bābu* · `الْفَمُ` *al-famu*
> **Sun (ل silent, next letter doubles):** `الشَّمْسُ` *ash-shamsu* · `النَّجْمُ` *an-najmu* · `الرَّجُلُ` *ar-rajulu* · `التُّفَّاحُ` *at-tuffāḥu*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh science hai, magic nahin. `ل` aur agle harf ki dosti: dono "paas" (moonh ke saamne) → merge ho jaate hain, `ل` gaayab, agla harf double (shadda). Dono "door" → `ل` saaf sunaayi deti hai. Zubaan ki natural sustii.

### The names

- **Sun letters** (*ḥurūf shamsiyyah*, الْحُرُوفُ الشَّمْسِيَّةُ) — *sooraj wali letters*. Named after `الشَّمْس` "the sun" (starts with ش, a sun letter — the L is swallowed).
- **Moon letters** (*ḥurūf qamariyyah*, الْحُرُوفُ الْقَمَرِيَّةُ) — *chand wali letters*. Named after `الْقَمَر` "the moon" (starts with ق, a moon letter — the L is clear).

The test word *is* the rule: `الشَّمْس` (ash-shams) hides its L; `الْقَمَر` (al-qamar) shows it.

### Spelling never changes

`الـ` is *always* written with ل, even when the ل is silent. Writing stays; only pronunciation changes. The visual cue you look for is the **shadda** on the next letter — that doubling is what replaces the swallowed ل.

**Yaad rakho:** Likhne mein `ل` hamesha rahegi — bolne mein gaayab ho sakti hai. Shadda (ـّ) hi nishaani hai ke `ل` is baar silent hai. "Likhna alag, bolna alag."

## The big picture — what this chapter is silently teaching

Step back. Three small tools, but together they unlock real Arabic:

1. **Definiteness is a grammatical state** — `الـ` flips a noun from *aam* to *khaas*, and the tanween doubling drops as proof.
2. **You can now make statements, not just labels** — definite noun + bare adjective = "the X is Y". Arabic still uses no word for "is".
3. **Pronunciation runs on its own rules** — the ل of `الـ` obeys the sun/moon split regardless of spelling.

After this lesson your classmates can read most simple sentences and signboards, and — crucially — they can *hear* why the Qur'an says *ar-Raḥmān* and not *al-Raḥmān*. That payoff (Part B) is the emotional high point of the lesson.

**Yaad rakho:** Lesson 3 ke baad classmates asaan jumle padh sakte hain AUR Qur'an ki tilāwat ka ek raaz (sun/moon) samajh jaate hain. Yahi is sabaq ka inaam hai.

## The five landmines for Urdu-speaking adult learners

### Landmine 1 · Keeping the doubling after الـ ("al-baytun")

Because the doubling is a *sound* they're used to (`taqreeban`), classmates keep it even after adding `الـ` — they say *al-baytun* instead of *al-baytu*.

**Counter-strategy:** Write the pair side by side and circle the change: `بَيْتٌ` (two pesh) → `الْبَيْتُ` (one pesh). Make them say both, slowly, hearing the "-un" disappear.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Dekho — `baytun` mein do-pesh hai (un ki awaaz). Jaise hi `al-` lagao, doubling gir jaati hai: `al-baytu`. Aakhir mein sirf ek pesh — 'u'. 'Al-baytUN' bolna ghalat hai."*

### Landmine 2 · Sun-letter assimilation ("al-shams" instead of "ash-shams")

Urdu and English don't assimilate the L like this, and the shadda is often missing in plain text — so classmates read `الشمس` literally as "al-shams".

**Counter-strategy:** Drill 8–10 pairs **aloud, ears first, before any writing**:

- al-bayt / **ash**-shams
- al-qamar / **an**-najm
- al-kitaab / **ar**-rajul
- al-baab / **ad**-deek
- al-famu / **at**-tuffaaḥ

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Pehle kano se sun lo. Kuch mein 'L' saaf sunaayi degi (al-qamar), kuch mein gaayab ho jaayegi aur agla harf double ho jaayega (ash-shams). Pehle moonh se feel karo — fir spelling. Yeh rule kaano ka hai, aankhon ka nahin."*

### Landmine 3 · Putting الـ on the adjective

Some classmates, having just learned `الـ`, sprinkle it everywhere — including on the describing word: they write `الْقَلَمُ الْمَكْسُورُ`. That silently changes "the pen is broken" (a sentence) into "the broken pen" (a phrase) — and the sentence loses its meaning.

**Counter-strategy:** State one firm rule and repeat it: **the thing gets `الـ`, the describing word stays bare.** Don't explain *why* yet, and don't show the `الـ`-adjective form — just correct it on sight.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yaad rakho — `الـ` sirf us cheez par jiske baare mein baat ho rahi hai. Sifat (describing word) bare rehti hai, tanween ke saath. `Al-qalamu maksūrun` — qalam par `al-`, 'maksūr' par kuch nahin. Sifat par `الـ` mat lagao — woh baat aage aayegi."*

### Landmine 4 · The "is" gap returns

"The pen is broken" has an "is". Classmates' inner ear wants a word for it and they try to insert one.

**Counter-strategy:** Reuse the Lesson 1 move — count words on the board:

- Urdu: *qalam / toota hua / hai* (3)
- English: *the pen / is / broken* (3)
- Arabic: *al-qalamu / maksūrun* (**2**)

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Wahi purana usool — Arabic mein 'hai' ka koi lafz nahin. Dono lafz jod do, beech ki khaali jagah hi 'hai' ka kaam karti hai. `Al-qalamu maksūrun` — bas do lafz."*

### Landmine 5 · "Long vs tall vs small vs short" — adjective confusion

This lesson has lookalike pairs: `طَوِيل` (tall/long) vs `قَصِير` (short), and `كَبِير` (big) vs `صَغِير` (small) vs `قَصِير` (short). Urdu speakers blur "chhota" (small) with "pasta" (short).

**Counter-strategy:** Pair them on the board with a quick sketch — a *tall* man (`طَوِيل`) next to a *short* one (`قَصِير`); a *big* stone (`كَبِير`) next to a *small* one (`صَغِير`). Anchor `طَوِيل/قَصِير` to height/length and `كَبِير/صَغِير` to size.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`طويل` = lamba/ooncha. `قصير` = pasta/chhota (lambai mein). `كبير` = bada (size mein). `صغير` = chhota (size mein). Aadmi `طويل` ya `قصير` hota hai; pathar `كبير` ya `صغير`."*

## Suggested 60-minute class plan

*Class shuru karne se pehle:* board par teen cheezein likho — chapter ka Arabic title (`الدَّرْسُ الثَّالِثُ`), aaj ke 3 rules ki ek-line tagline, aur 6–8 objects jinhe tum point kar ke describe karogi.

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–5 min | Greetings · today's 3 tools in three sentences |
| 5–15 min | `الـ` drill: take known Lesson-1 nouns, add `الـ`, hear the doubling drop (*baytun → al-baytu*) |
| 15–30 min | Describing sentences: point + describe — *al-qalamu maksūrun, al-bābu maftūḥun* — whole class, then individuals. Keep adjectives bare. |
| 30–42 min | Sun vs Moon letters — **ear training first** (8–10 pairs), then show the two 14-letter lists and the shadda cue |
| 42–52 min | Mixed drill: read the lesson's numbered sentences aloud, applying sun/moon correctly |
| 52–60 min | Qur'an payoff (*al-ḥamd* vs *ar-Raḥmān*) · Q&A · assign handout exercises |

**Yaad rakho:** Sun/moon drill skip mat karna chahe time tang ho — yeh is chapter ka sab se important hissa hai (aur Qur'an se direct juda hai). Describing sentences mein har baar yaad dilao: ism par `الـ`, sifat bare.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 3

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum aage dekhenge"*:

- ❌ The definite-adjective **phrase** (`الْقَلَمُ الْمَكْسُورُ` = "the broken pen") and its agreement rule — **a later lesson**. Don't even show the form.
- ❌ Feminine nouns/adjectives and the `ة` marker — **Lesson 6**. Keep all of today's vocab masculine.
- ❌ The feminine "this" (`هَذِهِ`) — **Lesson 6**.
- ❌ Prepositions (`فِي`, `عَلَى`, `مِنْ`, `إِلَى`) and the kasra they trigger — **Lesson 4**.
- ❌ A formal name for the connecting alif — **a later lesson** (use it, don't name it).
- ❌ Formal case names (the ḍamma's role-name) — **much later**.

**Yaad rakho:** Pre-loading madad nahin karti — overwhelm karti hai. Book ki tarteeb par bharosa karo. Khaaskar `الـ`-wali-sifat ki shakl bilkul mat dikhana — woh Lesson 3 ka kaam nahin.

## Self-check before teaching

Before the class, make sure:

- [ ] You can say all 24 new words confidently, with correct tanween / `الـ`. *(Har lafz sahi harakat ke saath.)*
- [ ] You can take any masculine noun and build a describing sentence: *al-X-u Y-un.* *(Koi bhi ism le ke "al-X-u Y-un" bana sako.)*
- [ ] You can read a mixed list of `الـ`-words applying sun/moon correctly, every time. *(Sun/moon rule har lafz par bina sochay.)*
- [ ] You can explain — in one sentence each — why the tanween drops, why the adjective stays bare, and why some L's go silent. *(Teen rules ek-ek line Urdu mein.)*
- [ ] You can point to `الْحَمْد` (moon) and `الرَّحْمٰن` (sun) in al-Fātiḥah and explain the L. *(Fātiḥah mein sun/moon dikhaa sako.)*

If yes to all five — *tum tayar ho.*

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Are they dropping the tanween doubling after `الـ`? *(al-baytu, not al-baytun?)*
2. Are they assimilating sun letters? *(ash-shams, not al-shams?)*
3. Are they keeping the adjective bare (no `الـ` on it)? *(Sifat par `الـ` to nahin laga rahe?)*
4. Are they resisting the urge to insert a word for "is"? *(Beech mein koi extra lafz to nahin?)*
5. Can they tell a sun letter from a moon letter on sight (shadda cue)? *(Shadda dekh ke pehchaan rahe hain?)*

**Yaad rakho:** Agar 3+ classmates kisi ek point par phans rahe hain — Lesson 4 par jaane se pehle ek choti revision karwao. Sun/moon ki neev mazboot honi chahiye, kyunke Qur'an ki tilāwat usi par khadi hai.
