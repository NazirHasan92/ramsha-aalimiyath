# Part A В· Understanding the chapter

Chapters 1 and 2 stayed inside a very safe box: every noun the classmates spoke ended in `ЩЂЩЊ` (-un). Sentences were just *"this/that + noun"* or *"what/who is this?"*. Nothing about the noun ever *changed*.

Chapter 3 is where that box cracks open.

It does three things at once:

1. **Prepositions** (*бёҐurЕ«f al-jarr* В· Ш­ЩЏШ±ЩЏЩ€ЩЃ Ш§Щ„Ш¬ЩЋШ±Щ‘ вЂ” *jarr wale harf*) arrive: `ЩЃЩђЩЉ`, `Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰`, `ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰`, `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’`. Classmates can now say *"in the house"*, *"on the desk"*, *"to the school"*, *"from the market"*.
2. **The noun changes its ending** after a preposition. `ЩЂЩЊ` becomes `ЩЂЩЌ`; `ЩЂЩЏ` becomes `ЩЂЩђ`. This is the first time the classmates see a noun *shift* вЂ” the first taste of Arabic's case system (the *iКїrДЃb* system you previewed in Ch 1 Part A).
3. **The feminine demonstrative** `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ` (*haadhihi* вЂ” "this", feminine) arrives. Combined with `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§` (Ch 1) and `Ш°ЩЋЩ„ЩђЩѓЩЋ` (Ch 2), three of the four cells of the demonstrative grid are now filled. Gender stops being invisible.

Plus a small fourth concept вЂ” `ШЈЩЋЩЉЩ’Щ†ЩЋ Шџ` (*"where?"*) вЂ” which is the natural question word to ask about location, so it slots in here neatly.

**Yaad rakho:** Ch 3 mein teen badi cheezein hain вЂ” prepositions, noun ka aakhri harkat badalna (zer/kasra), aur feminine "yeh" (`Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ`). Aur ek chhoti cheez вЂ” "kahaan?" (`ШЈЩЋЩЉЩ’Щ†ЩЋ`). Yeh chapter chhoti par bahut bhaari hai вЂ” kyunki yahaan se Arabic ki *case-system* khulti hai.

Work through the four concepts below at your own pace. For each, ask yourself: *can I explain this to a classmate without using the word "case" or "iКїrДЃb"?* (*Kya main yeh classmate ko bina "case" ya "iКїrДЃb" kahe samjha sakti hoon?*) If yes вЂ” move on. If not вЂ” re-read.

## 1 В· Prepositions вЂ” the four little anchors

A **preposition** (*бёҐarf jarr* В· Ш­ЩЋШ±Щ’ЩЃ Ш¬ЩЋШ±Щ‘ вЂ” *jarr wala harf*) is a tiny word that connects a noun to the rest of a sentence by giving it a location, direction, or origin. In English: *in, on, to, from, at, by, with*. In Urdu: *mein, par, tak, se, ke saath*. Arabic has a whole family of them; Chapter 3 introduces four.

### The four prepositions of Ch 3

| Arabic | Roman | English | Urdu equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|
| `ЩЃЩђЩЉ` | *fД«* | in / inside | *mein* (jaise *"ghar mein"*) |
| `Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰` | *КїalДЃ* | on / upon | *par* (jaise *"mez par"*) |
| `ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰` | *ilДЃ* | to / towards | *tak / ko / ki taraf* |
| `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’` | *min* | from / out of | *se / mein se* |

That's the whole set for this chapter. Memorise them as a foursome вЂ” they will appear *together* throughout the rest of Book 1 (and the rest of your Arabic life).

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh chaar lafz ek "team" hain вЂ” `ЩЃЩђЩЉ`, `Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰`, `ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰`, `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’`. Inhe ek saath yaad karo, alag-alag nahin. Aur Urdu equivalents bhi yaad karo: *mein, par, tak, se*. Class ko in Urdu equivalents se hi pakdaao вЂ” English se nahin, kyunki classmates ka apna inner translation Urdu hi mein chalta hai.

### Two writing peculiarities

**`Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰` and `ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰` both end in `Щ‰` (alif-maqsoorah)** вЂ” a letter that *looks* like a dotless ЩЉ but *sounds* like a long *aa*. Don't be fooled by the visual вЂ” read both as ending in *"-aa"*:

- `Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰` в†’ *"Кїa-laa"* (NOT *"Кїa-lay"*)
- `ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰` в†’ *"i-laa"* (NOT *"i-lay"*)

This is the same letter you'll see at the end of `ЩЉЩЏШµЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‘Щ‰` and many other words. Some unvocalised Qur'an editions write it with a small dagger-alif above (`Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰Щ°`, `ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰Щ°`) as a reading aid вЂ” same trick you saw with `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§` and `Ш°ЩЋЩ„ЩђЩѓЩЋ`.

**`Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’` ends in sukoon** вЂ” the Щ† is silent at the very end. Pronounce it crisp: *min* (with the *i* of *"sit"*, not *"see"*). Don't add a phantom vowel ("mina", "mini") вЂ” that comes later only when Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’ runs into a vowel-starting word (which we'll see in concept 2).

**Yaad rakho:** `Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰` aur `ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰` ke aakhir mein ya jaisa harf (Щ‰) hai, par sunaayi "aa" deti hai вЂ” yeh alif-maqsoorah (*chhoti alif*) hai, "y" ki aawaz nahin. Aur `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’` ka Щ† silent hai вЂ” sirf "min", "mina" nahin.

### What prepositions *do* вЂ” they "govern"

In Arabic, prepositions don't just sit there as decoration. They actively *change* the noun that comes after them. The technical term for this relationship is *jarr* (literally "pulling / dragging" вЂ” the preposition "pulls" the noun into a particular ending). But you should **not say "jarr" to your classmates in Ch 3**. They will meet that word in Ch 5. For now, the rule is simple:

> **After one of these four prepositions, the noun's ending changes to *kasra*.**

That's the entire mechanism вЂ” and it's the focus of Concept 2 below.

**Yaad rakho (apne liye):** "Jarr" ka asli matlab hai *"khainchna"* вЂ” preposition apne baad wale ism ko ek khaas ending par kheench leti hai. Yeh poetic image hi *case* system ka core hai. Class ko abhi yeh nahin batana вЂ” Ch 5 ka kaam hai. Abhi sirf: *"yeh chaar lafz aate hain to noun ke aakhir mein zer aa jaata hai"*. Bas.

### Examples вЂ” feel the four together

Read these out loud. Don't worry about the kasra ending yet вЂ” that's Concept 2. Just hear the prepositions doing their job:

- `Ш§Щ„Щ’ЩѓЩђШЄЩЋШ§ШЁЩЏ ЩЃЩђЩЉ Ш§Щ„Щ’Ш­ЩЋЩ‚ЩђЩЉШЁЩЋШ©Щђ` вЂ” *al-kitaabu fil-бёҐaqeebati* вЂ” "The book is in the bag" В· *"Kitaab bag mein hai."*
- `Ш§Щ„Щ’Щ‚ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ…ЩЏ Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰ Ш§Щ„Щ’Щ…ЩЋЩѓЩ’ШЄЩЋШЁЩђ` вЂ” *al-qalamu Кїalal-maktabi* вЂ” "The pen is on the desk" В· *"Qalam mez par hai."*
- `ЩЉЩЋШ°Щ’Щ‡ЩЋШЁЩЏ ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰ Ш§Щ„Щ’Щ…ЩЋШЇЩ’Ш±ЩЋШіЩЋШ©Щђ` вЂ” wait, "yadhhabu" is a verb вЂ” **don't teach this**. Use noun-only: `Ш§Щ„Ш°ЩЋЩ‘Щ‡ЩЋШ§ШЁЩЏ ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰ Ш§Щ„Щ’Щ…ЩЋШЇЩ’Ш±ЩЋШіЩЋШ©Щђ` вЂ” hmm, also too advanced. The book sticks to: `ШЈЩЋЩЉЩ’Щ†ЩЋ ШЄЩЋШ°Щ’Щ‡ЩЋШЁЩЏШџ вЂ” ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰ Ш§Щ„Щ’Щ…ЩЋШЇЩ’Ш±ЩЋШіЩЋШ©Щђ` ("where are you going? вЂ” to the school") in later chapters. For Ch 3 just use the preposition + noun fragment.
  - Cleaner Ch 3 sentence: `Ш§Щ„Щ’Щ€ЩЋЩ„ЩЋШЇЩЏ Ш®ЩЋШ±ЩЋШ¬ЩЋ Щ…ЩђЩ†ЩЋ Ш§Щ„Щ’ШЁЩЋЩЉЩ’ШЄЩђ` вЂ” also a verb. Skip.
  - **The book's actual Ch 3 sentences are nominal**: `Ш§Щ„Щ’ЩѓЩђШЄЩЋШ§ШЁЩЏ Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰ Ш§Щ„Щ’Щ…ЩЋЩѓЩ’ШЄЩЋШЁЩђ` style вЂ” using prepositions to *locate* the subject in a Mubtada' + Khabar frame, where the Khabar is a preposition-phrase. That's what you'll teach.
- `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§ Ш§Щ„Щ’ЩѓЩђШЄЩЋШ§ШЁЩЏ Щ…ЩђЩ†ЩЋ Ш§Щ„Щ’Щ…ЩЋЩѓЩ’ШЄЩЋШЁЩЋШ©Щђ` вЂ” *haadhal-kitaabu minal-maktabati* вЂ” "This book is from the library" В· *"Yeh kitaab library se hai."*

**Yaad rakho:** Ch 3 mein prepositions sirf *location/origin* ke liye use ho rahi hain вЂ” verbs ke saath nahin. Mubtada' + (preposition + ism) = poora jumla. *"Kitaab mez par hai"* вЂ” verb ki zaroorat nahin (jaisa Ch 1 mein "hai" silent thi, wahi rule abhi bhi chal raha hai).

## 2 В· The case-change вЂ” the noun's ending shifts to *kasra*

This is the first time in the book that a *noun* (not a particle or pronoun) changes form. Pay attention.

### The pattern

Take any indefinite noun ending in `ЩЂЩЊ` (-un, the citing form). Put a preposition before it. The ending shifts:

| Before preposition | After preposition |
|---|---|
| `ШЁЩЋЩЉЩ’ШЄЩЊ` *baytun* | `ЩЃЩђЩЉ ШЁЩЋЩЉЩ’ШЄЩЌ` *fee baytin* |
| `Щ…ЩЋЩѓЩ’ШЄЩЋШЁЩЊ` *maktabun* | `Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰ Щ…ЩЋЩѓЩ’ШЄЩЋШЁЩЌ* КїalДЃ maktabin* |
| `ШіЩЏЩ€Щ‚ЩЊ` *sЕ«qun* | `ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰ ШіЩЏЩ€Щ‚ЩЌ* ilДЃ sЕ«qin* |
| `ШЁЩЋЩЉЩ’ШЄЩЊ` *baytun* | `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’ ШЁЩЋЩЉЩ’ШЄЩЌ* min baytin* |

The doubling stays (it's still indefinite), but the *direction* of the doubling flips from бёЌamma-on-top to kasra-below: `ЩЂЩЊ` в†’ `ЩЂЩЌ`. From *"un"* to *"in"*.

For definite nouns (with `Ш§Щ„ЩЂ`), the same shift happens but without the doubling вЂ” because Ch 1 already taught that definite nouns don't carry tanween:

| Before preposition | After preposition |
|---|---|
| `Ш§Щ„Щ’ШЁЩЋЩЉЩ’ШЄЩЏ` *al-baytu* | `ЩЃЩђЩЉ Ш§Щ„Щ’ШЁЩЋЩЉЩ’ШЄЩђ` *fil-bayti* |
| `Ш§Щ„Щ’Щ…ЩЋЩѓЩ’ШЄЩЋШЁЩЏ` *al-maktabu* | `Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰ Ш§Щ„Щ’Щ…ЩЋЩѓЩ’ШЄЩЋШЁЩђ* Кїalal-maktabi* |
| `Ш§Щ„Щ’Щ…ЩЋШЇЩ’Ш±ЩЋШіЩЋШ©ЩЏ` *al-madrasatu* | `ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰ Ш§Щ„Щ’Щ…ЩЋШЇЩ’Ш±ЩЋШіЩЋШ©Щђ* ilal-madrasati* |
| `Ш§Щ„ШіЩЏЩ‘Щ€Щ‚ЩЏ` *as-sЕ«qu* | `Щ…ЩђЩ†ЩЋ Ш§Щ„ШіЩЏЩ‘Щ€Щ‚Щђ* minas-sЕ«qi* |

From *"u"* (бёЌamma) to *"i"* (kasra). The single ending flips below the letter.

**Yaad rakho:** Ek line ka rule: *"preposition ke baad noun ke aakhir mein **zer** (kasra) aa jaati hai."* Doubled (indefinite) bhi `ЩЂЩЌ` ban jaata hai (do-zer), single (definite) bhi `ЩЂЩђ` ban jaata hai (ek zer). Bas. Class ko isi roop mein dena вЂ” "kasra" lafz use kar sakti ho, par "case" / "iКїrДЃb" / "jarr" nahin.

### Why does this happen?

You owe yourself the *why*, even if your classmates don't get it. The answer goes back to ancient Semitic grammar вЂ” and once you see it, the rest of Arabic case-marking stops feeling random.

Every Arabic noun has **three** "states" or *cases* (you saw the full grid in Ch 1 Part A):

- **RafКї** (nominative В· *uthaane wala* вЂ” the "subject" case) вЂ” ending `ЩЂЩЏ` / `ЩЂЩЊ`. This is the citing form, the "default", the form a noun takes when it's the subject (the Mubtada' or anything else playing a leading role).
- **Naб№Јb** (accusative В· *daalne wala* вЂ” the "object" case) вЂ” ending `ЩЂЩЋ` / `ЩЂЩ‹`. Comes later (Ch 5+).
- **Jarr** (genitive В· *khainchne wala* вЂ” the "after-a-preposition / possessive" case) вЂ” ending `ЩЂЩђ` / `ЩЂЩЌ`. This is what arrives today.

Historically, in Semitic languages, prepositions were felt to "possess" or "pull" their nouns вЂ” a kind of grammatical possession ("the X belonging to the preposition's space"). So they triggered the same ending as possessive constructions. Modern grammarians just memorise the rule. The poetic image is still helpful: the preposition is *pulling* (*kheench* / *jarr*) the noun toward itself, and the kasra-ending is the visible mark of being pulled.

**Yaad rakho (apne liye вЂ” class ko nahin batana):** *"Jarr"* ka literal matlab *"khainchna"* hai вЂ” preposition apne baad wali ism ko *kasra* ki taraf kheech leti hai. Ch 1 Part A wala 6-cell grid ab apni neechi row dikha raha hai (indefinite-jarr `ЩЂЩЌ`, definite-jarr `ЩЂЩђ`). Class ko Ch 5 mein yeh poora system khulega вЂ” abhi sirf pattern dikhao, theory nahin.

### The `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’` exception вЂ” the silent Щ† wakes up

Look at the last row of the table: `Щ…ЩђЩ†ЩЋ Ш§Щ„ШіЩЏЩ‘Щ€Щ‚Щђ` вЂ” *minas-sЕ«qi*. Not *"minas-sЕ«qi"* preceded by *"min"* with sukoon. The Щ† has acquired a fatбёҐa.

What happened? When `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’` (which normally ends in sukoon вЂ” silent Щ†) meets a definite noun starting with `Ш§Щ„ЩЂ`, the alif of `Ш§Щ„ЩЂ` is an *alif al-waб№Јl* (a "connecting alif") and gets swallowed in connected speech. The Щ† of `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’`, no longer able to sit on a sukoon next to another consonant (the Щ„ of `Ш§Щ„ЩЂ`), takes on a small linking vowel вЂ” a fatбёҐa вЂ” to bridge the gap.

So:
- `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’ ШЁЩЋЩЉЩ’ШЄЩЌ` в†’ *min baytin* (Щ† with sukoon вЂ” works, because *baytin* starts with a consonant *b*).
- `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’ Ш§Щ„Щ’ШЁЩЋЩЉЩ’ШЄЩђ` в†’ *minal-bayti* (the Щ† needs a vowel because the Ш§ of `Ш§Щ„Щ’` drops in pronunciation, leaving `lal-bayti`; the Щ† gets a fatбёҐa so the speaker can glide into it).

This is the same phonological smoothing rule that gave you sun letters in Ch 1 вЂ” Arabic doesn't like two unhelpful sukoons sitting next to each other. It always finds a way to bridge them.

**Yaad rakho:** `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’` + `Ш§Щ„Щ’___` = `Щ…ЩђЩ†ЩЋ Ш§Щ„Щ’___`. Щ† par fatбёҐa aa jaati hai *kyunki* `Ш§Щ„Щ’` ki alif gum ho jaati hai вЂ” aur do silent harf side-by-side Arabic ko pasand nahin. Sun-letter rule wala hi pattern hai вЂ” Arabic apne aap ko smooth banaati hai. Class ko yeh tafseelan nahin batana вЂ” sirf bolo: *"`Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’` + `Ш§Щ„Щ’` = `Щ…ЩђЩ†ЩЋ Ш§Щ„Щ’` вЂ” Щ† par zabar lag jaata hai."* Pattern recognition, theory baad mein.

### What's NOT changing

A lot is staying the same вЂ” note this for your own peace of mind:

- The **demonstratives** (`Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§`, `Ш°ЩЋЩ„ЩђЩѓЩЋ`, `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ`) **never change their endings**, ever. They are *mabnД«* (you noted this in Ch 2 Part A). So in `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§ Ш§Щ„Щ’ЩѓЩђШЄЩЋШ§ШЁЩЏ Щ…ЩђЩ†ЩЋ Ш§Щ„Щ’Щ…ЩЋЩѓЩ’ШЄЩЋШЁЩЋШ©Щђ`, only `Ш§Щ„Щ’Щ…ЩЋЩѓЩ’ШЄЩЋШЁЩЋШ©Щђ` shifted to kasra; `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§` stayed exactly as it was.
- The **Mubtada'** (subject) of the sentence stays in rafКї. In `Ш§Щ„Щ’ЩѓЩђШЄЩЋШ§ШЁЩЏ Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰ Ш§Щ„Щ’Щ…ЩЋЩѓЩ’ШЄЩЋШЁЩђ`, only `Ш§Щ„Щ’Щ…ЩЋЩѓЩ’ШЄЩЋШЁЩђ` is in kasra (because of the preposition); `Ш§Щ„Щ’ЩѓЩђШЄЩЋШ§ШЁЩЏ` stays in its rafКї form. The case-change happens *only to the noun directly after the preposition*.
- The `Ш§Щ„ЩЂ` itself, sun/moon letter rules, the absence of "is/hai" вЂ” all of Ch 1 and Ch 2 still applies.

**Yaad rakho:** Sirf ek noun badal raha hai вЂ” preposition ke baad wala. Pichhli saari rules pakki rahti hain. Mubtada' apne rafКї-form mein hai, demonstrative apne hi roop mein, Ш§Щ„ЩЂ apna kaam kar raha hai вЂ” sirf preposition ke baad wali ism par kasra aati hai.

## 3 В· `ШЈЩЋЩЉЩ’Щ†ЩЋ Шџ` вЂ” asking "where?"

`ШЈЩЋЩЉЩ’Щ†ЩЋ` (*ayna* В· *"kahaan?"*) is the question word for **location**. It sits at the start of the question, exactly like `Щ…ЩЋШ§` and `Щ…ЩЋЩ†Щ’` from Ch 2.

### The pattern

- Question: `ШЈЩЋЩЉЩ’Щ†ЩЋ Ш§Щ„Щ’ЩѓЩђШЄЩЋШ§ШЁЩЏ Шџ` вЂ” *ayna l-kitaabu?* вЂ” "Where is the book?" В· *"Kitaab kahaan hai?"*
- Answer: `Ш§Щ„Щ’ЩѓЩђШЄЩЋШ§ШЁЩЏ Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰ Ш§Щ„Щ’Щ…ЩЋЩѓЩ’ШЄЩЋШЁЩђ` вЂ” *al-kitaabu Кїalal-maktabi* вЂ” "The book is on the desk" В· *"Kitaab mez par hai."*

Notice: the *answer* uses today's prepositions. `ШЈЩЋЩЉЩ’Щ†ЩЋ` is the question word that triggers a preposition-phrase answer. This is the same teaching trick the book pulled in Ch 2 вЂ” give classmates a new way to ask for what they're learning to say.

**Yaad rakho:** `ШЈЩЋЩЉЩ’Щ†ЩЋ` ka jawab hamesha preposition + ism hota hai (location batata hai). Yani sawal banane se classmates apne aap aaj wali preposition + kasra wali skill use karte hain вЂ” *do skills ek hi drill mein dohrayi jaati hain*. Smart sequencing hai.

### One spelling note

`ШЈЩЋЩЉЩ’Щ†ЩЋ` ends in fatбёҐa (`ЩЂЩЋ`), so the Щ† is pronounced *"na"*, not silent. Don't confuse it with `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’` (whose Щ† *is* silent). And **definitely** don't confuse it with `Щ…ЩЋЩ†Щ’` from Ch 2 (*man* вЂ” "who?"). Three words, three different jobs:

| Word | Sound | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| `Щ…ЩЋЩ†Щ’` | *man* | who? (Ch 2) |
| `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’` | *min* | from (Ch 3) |
| `ШЈЩЋЩЉЩ’Щ†ЩЋ` | *ayna* | where? (Ch 3) |

The first two are written with the same two letters (Щ… + Щ†) вЂ” the *only* difference is the vowel on the Щ… (fatбёҐa vs kasra). This is going to be a real source of confusion in class (see Landmine 4 in Part B).

**Yaad rakho:** `Щ…ЩЋЩ†Щ’` (kaun) aur `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’` (se) вЂ” *same letters, alag harkat*. Aur `ШЈЩЋЩЉЩ’Щ†ЩЋ` (kahaan) bilkul alag вЂ” alif + ya + noon. Class ko teen lafz ek saath board par likho aur compare karwaao вЂ” warna pakkad ho jaayegi galat.

## 4 В· `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ` вЂ” feminine "this", and gender wakes up

`Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ` (*haadhihi* В· *"yeh"* вЂ” feminine) is the **near feminine demonstrative**. It points to something nearby and feminine. Same job as `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§`, just for the other gender.

### The grid is now 3/4 full

|   | Near (*qareeb*) | Far (*door*) |
|---|---|---|
| **Masculine** (*mudhakkar*) | `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§` haadhaa (Ch 1) | `Ш°ЩЋЩ„ЩђЩѓЩЋ` dhaalika (Ch 2) |
| **Feminine** (*mu'annath*) | **`Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ` haadhihi (в†ђ today)** | `ШЄЩђЩ„Щ’ЩѓЩЋ` tilka вЂ” later |

One more cell to go (`ШЄЩђЩ„Щ’ЩѓЩЋ`, far feminine, much later). The masculine row is now closed; today you open the feminine row.

**Yaad rakho:** Grid ki upar wali row Ch 2 ke baad mukammal thi. Aaj neechi row khulti hai вЂ” `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ`. `ШЄЩђЩ„Щ’ЩѓЩЋ` baad ke chapters mein. Classmates ko yeh grid dikhao вЂ” visual seedha samajh aata hai.

### The same silent-alif pattern, again

`Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ` is written with the same hidden dagger-alif as `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§` вЂ” written *hДЃ-dha-hi*, said *haa-dhi-hi*. The "aa" is between `Щ‡` and `Ш°`, not at the end. In vocalised editions you'll see `Щ‡ЩЋЩЂЩ°Ш°ЩђЩ‡Щђ` with the dagger alif above the first Щ‡.

So the pattern is now:

- `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§` в†’ *haa-dhaa* (Ch 1)
- `Ш°ЩЋЩ„ЩђЩѓЩЋ` в†’ *dhaa-li-ka* (Ch 2)
- `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ` в†’ *haa-dhi-hi* (Ch 3)

Same silent-aa trick all three times. If you've drilled the trick in Ch 1 and Ch 2, `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ` should land in one sentence: *"Wahi pattern вЂ” dagger alif beech mein. Naya nahin."*

**Yaad rakho:** Teesri baar wahi rule вЂ” beech mein chhupi hui alif. Class ko bolo: *"Pichhli do baar yeh dekh chuke ho вЂ” `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§` mein, `Ш°ЩЋЩ„ЩђЩѓЩЋ` mein. Ab teesri baar `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ` mein. Naya nahin вЂ” wahi puraana pattern."*

### How do you spot a feminine noun?

Most Arabic feminine nouns end in `ЩЂШ©` вЂ” a special letter called **taa marbuuta** (*"bandhi hui ta"* вЂ” the "tied ta"). Visually it's a `Щ‡` with two dots above it: Ш©. Examples from this chapter's vocab:

- `ШєЩЏШ±Щ’ЩЃЩЋШ©ЩЊ` *ghurfatun* вЂ” a room
- `Щ…ЩЋШЇЩ’Ш±ЩЋШіЩЋШ©ЩЊ* madrasatun* вЂ” a school
- `Ш­ЩЋШЇЩђЩЉЩ‚ЩЋШ©ЩЊ* бёҐadeeqatun* вЂ” a garden
- `Ш­ЩЋЩ‚ЩђЩЉШЁЩЋШ©ЩЊ* бёҐaqeebatun* вЂ” a bag
- `ШіЩЋЩЉЩЋЩ‘Ш§Ш±ЩЋШ©ЩЊ* sayyДЃratun* вЂ” a car (if introduced)

These all need `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ` (not `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§`).

There are also feminine nouns that *don't* end in Ш© вЂ” body parts that come in pairs (like `ЩЉЩЋШЇЩЊ` "hand", `Ш№ЩЋЩЉЩ’Щ†ЩЊ* "eye"), and a handful of "intrinsically feminine" words (`ШЈЩЏЩ…ЩЊЩ‘` "mother", `ШЈЩЏШ®Щ’ШЄЩЊ` "sister", `ШґЩЋЩ…Щ’ШіЩЊ` "sun"). The book introduces these gradually. For Ch 3, just teach the rule: *"if the noun ends in Ш©, it's feminine в†’ use `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ`."* Don't open the exceptions yet.

**Yaad rakho:** Aam rule: aakhir mein Ш© в†’ feminine ism в†’ `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ`. Exceptions (jaise `ЩЉЩЋШЇ`, `ШЈЩЏЩ…Щ‘`, `ШґЩЋЩ…Щ’Ші`) baad ke chapters mein khulenge вЂ” abhi sirf Ш© wala rule sikhao. Class ko bolo: *"Aaj ke 5-6 feminine lafz sab Ш© par khatam hote hain вЂ” yeh sab se aam pehchaan hai."*

### A note on the *sound* of taa marbuuta

Taa marbuuta is the most subtle letter in Arabic. It is pronounced:

- as *"h"* (silent t) when you stop at the word вЂ” *"haadhihi ghurfah"* (at pause)
- as *"t"* when the word is connected to what comes next вЂ” *"haadhihi ghurfatun jameelatun"* (in connected speech)

This dual pronunciation is well-loved by classical grammarians and confusing for beginners. **Do NOT drill the t/h split in Ch 3.** Just introduce the *t*-form for now (since the book always vocalises it with tanween: `ШєЩЏШ±Щ’ЩЃЩЋШ©ЩЊ` = *ghurfatun*). The pause-form (*"ghurfah"*) is a Qur'an-recitation skill вЂ” Ch 5+ territory.

**Yaad rakho:** Taa marbuuta ka *t* vs *h* wala maamla matlabi rakh do вЂ” abhi sirf *t*-form (tanween wala) sikhao. *Ghurfatun*, *madrasatun*, *бёҐaqeebatun*. *H*-form Qur'an padhne mein chalti hai вЂ” woh aage ka kaam hai. Yahaan complicate mat karo.

### Examples with `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ`

Read aloud:

- `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ ШєЩЏШ±Щ’ЩЃЩЋШ©ЩЊ` вЂ” *haadhihi ghurfatun* вЂ” "This is a room" В· *"Yeh ek kamra hai."*
- `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ Щ…ЩЋШЇЩ’Ш±ЩЋШіЩЋШ©ЩЊ* вЂ” *haadhihi madrasatun* вЂ” "This is a school" В· *"Yeh ek school hai."*
- `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ Ш­ЩЋЩ‚ЩђЩЉШЁЩЋШ©ЩЊ* вЂ” *haadhihi бёҐaqeebatun* вЂ” "This is a bag" В· *"Yeh ek bag hai."*
- `Щ…ЩЋШ§ Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ Шџ вЂ” Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ Ш­ЩЋШЇЩђЩЉЩ‚ЩЋШ©ЩЊ* вЂ” *maa haadhihi? вЂ” haadhihi бёҐadeeqatun* вЂ” "What is this? вЂ” This is a garden" В· *"Yeh kya hai? вЂ” Yeh ek baagh hai."*

Notice the last one: the Ch 2 question word `Щ…ЩЋШ§` works with `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ` exactly as it did with `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§`. Nothing new вЂ” gender just matches.

**Yaad rakho:** `Щ…ЩЋШ§ Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§ Шџ` aur `Щ…ЩЋШ§ Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ Шџ` вЂ” sirf gender ka farq. Ch 2 ki saari skills `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ` ke saath bhi chalti hain. Class ko bolo: *"Sawal banane ka tarika wahi вЂ” sirf gender match karna hai."*

## The big picture вЂ” what this chapter is silently teaching

Step back and look at all four concepts together. Chapter 3 is doing four things at once вЂ” and each is foundational:

1. **The case system has begun.** Ch 1 hinted at it (`baytun в†’ al-baytu`). Ch 3 makes it *visible*: the same noun now shows up in *two* different endings depending on what's before it (`al-baytu` vs `fil-bayti`). Classmates won't have words for this yet, but their ears will start hearing it. (*Case-system khul gaya вЂ” naam abhi nahin diya, par aawaz mein farq sunaayi de raha hai.*)

2. **Gender has become active.** Ch 1 and Ch 2 quietly used only masculine nouns. Ch 3 introduces feminine nouns and a feminine demonstrative вЂ” gender is now a thing classmates have to *track*. (*Gender ab "active" ho gaya вЂ” har naye noun par sochna hai mardaana hai ya zanaana.*)

3. **The question family has expanded.** Ch 1 had `ШЈЩЋ` (yes/no). Ch 2 added `Щ…ЩЋШ§`, `Щ…ЩЋЩ†Щ’`, `ШЈЩЋЩ…Щ’`. Ch 3 adds `ШЈЩЋЩЉЩ’Щ†ЩЋ`. Five question types in three weeks. (*Sawal ka khandaan barhta jaa raha hai вЂ” har chapter mein nayi sawal-wali lafz.*)

4. **Sentence structure has grown.** Ch 1's sentences were two words (Mubtada' + Khabar, both nouns). Ch 3's sentences can now have *three* parts (Mubtada' + preposition + ism вЂ” the Khabar is a *prepositional phrase*). The sentence has stretched without breaking. (*Jumla badi ho gayi вЂ” par toot-foot nahin, sirf khabar ki jagah preposition-phrase aa gaya.*)

**Yaad rakho:** Ch 3 sab chhoti lagti hai par bahut zyaada plant kar raha hai вЂ” case, gender, question expansion, sentence growth. Dr. Abdur Rahim ki khaasiyat yahi hai: surface par chaar chhoti cheezein, neeche poori grammar shift. Tumhe yeh dimagh mein rakhna hai jab teach karo вЂ” agar 60 minute mein sab nahin ho paaya, *kasra rule* aur *hadhihi* sab se important hain. Baaki next session mein bhi reinforce ho jaayega.

## The five landmines for Urdu-speaking adult learners

### Landmine 1 В· Forgetting to switch the noun ending to kasra

This is the **biggest** Ch 3 landmine, and it will haunt the rest of the book if not caught now. Classmates will say:

- *"fee baytun"* (wrong вЂ” should be *fee baytin*)
- *"Кїalal-maktabu"* (wrong вЂ” should be *Кїalal-maktabi*)
- *"minal-baytu"* (wrong вЂ” should be *minal-bayti*)

They've spent two chapters drilling `ЩЂЩЊ` / `ЩЂЩЏ` endings as the "default". The brain is now habituated. The case-change feels invisible to them вЂ” they don't even notice the ending has shifted.

**Counter-strategy:** Don't introduce all four prepositions and *then* the kasra rule. Introduce one preposition (`ЩЃЩђЩЉ`) immediately followed by the rule. Drill 8вЂ“10 nouns with `ЩЃЩђЩЉ` *only* until the ending shift is loud in their ears. Then add `Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰`, then `ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰`, then `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’`. Each new preposition is a chance to re-drill the rule, not to introduce more vocabulary.

Also: write the ending in a *different colour* on the board. `Ш§Щ„Щ’ШЁЩЋЩЉЩ’ШЄЩЏ` with a red `ЩЂЩЏ`, then `ЩЃЩђЩЉ Ш§Щ„Щ’ШЁЩЋЩЉЩ’ШЄЩђ` with a red `ЩЂЩђ`. Visual contrast does what words can't.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Dekho вЂ” ab tak hum ne har lafz ke aakhir mein pesh (ЩЂЩЏ) ya do-pesh (ЩЂЩЊ) lagayi hai. Aaj ek nayi cheez: jab `ЩЃЩђЩЉ`, `Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰`, `ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰`, `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’` mein se koi bhi lafz aage aata hai, to is ke baad wale ism ke aakhir mein **zer** (ЩЂЩђ) aa jaati hai. `Ш§Щ„Щ’ШЁЩЋЩЉЩ’ШЄЩЏ` tha вЂ” `ЩЃЩђЩЉ Ш§Щ„Щ’ШЁЩЋЩЉЩ’ШЄЩђ` ho gaya. Pesh hat gaya, zer aa gaya. Yeh sab se zaroori rule hai aaj ka вЂ” agar yeh chhoot gaya, baaki sab adhoora reh jaayega."*

### Landmine 2 В· Skipping the doubled-kasra (`ЩЂЩЌ`) on indefinite nouns

When the noun is indefinite (no `Ш§Щ„ЩЂ`), the kasra after a preposition is *doubled*: `ЩЂЩЌ` (-in). Most Urdu-speakers will pronounce it as a single kasra (-i) or skip it entirely. They've never had to *hear* a kasra-tanween in everyday Urdu/English вЂ” even `taqreeban` (a familiar Urdu word) is fatбёҐa-tanween, not kasra-tanween.

Result: *"fee baytin"* becomes *"fee bayti"* or *"fee bayt"*. The grammar is right; the sound is wrong.

**Counter-strategy:** Ear-train before drill. Compare three pairs aloud, slowly:

- `ШЁЩЋЩЉЩ’ШЄЩЊ` вЂ” *baytun* (do-pesh) в†’ `ЩЃЩђЩЉ ШЁЩЋЩЉЩ’ШЄЩЌ` вЂ” *fee baytin* (do-zer)
- `Щ‚ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ…ЩЊ* вЂ” *qalamun* в†’ `Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰ Щ‚ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ…ЩЌ` вЂ” *КїalДЃ qalamin*
- `ЩѓЩђШЄЩЋШ§ШЁЩЊ* вЂ” *kitaabun* в†’ `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’ ЩѓЩђШЄЩЋШ§ШЁЩЌ* вЂ” *min kitaabin*

The pattern is *"-un becomes -in"* вЂ” same structure, opposite vowel direction.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Aap log 'taqreeban' (ШЄЩЋЩ‚Щ’Ш±ЩђЩЉШЁЩ‹Ш§) bolte ho вЂ” woh do-zabar hai (`ЩЂЩ‹`). Ab uska bhai dekho вЂ” do-zer (`ЩЂЩЌ`). 'Fee baytin' вЂ” sun rahe ho? Aakhir mein 'in' wala sound. Pesh ki jagah zer вЂ” par doubling baqi hai. Yeh aapke kaano mein zaroor aana chahiye. Agar 'fee bayt' bola вЂ” adhoora hai. 'Fee bayti' bhi adhoora вЂ” abhi kuch kam hai. Poora: 'fee baytin'."*

### Landmine 3 В· Confusing `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’` ("from") with `Щ…ЩЋЩ†Щ’` ("who?")

Same two letters (Щ… + Щ†). Only the vowel on the Щ… differs (kasra в†’ "min", fatбёҐa в†’ "man"). Classmates will:

- Write `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’` when they mean `Щ…ЩЋЩ†Щ’` and vice versa
- Read `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’` as *"man"* and `Щ…ЩЋЩ†Щ’` as *"min"* вЂ” the vowel signs blur for beginners

This confusion compounds because `Щ…ЩЋЩ†Щ’` was *just* learned last week.

**Counter-strategy:** Put both words on the board side by side from minute one. Make a "twin words" callout:

> **`Щ…ЩЋЩ†Щ’` = *man* = "who?" (Ch 2)** вЂ” fatбёҐa on Щ…
> **`Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’` = *min* = "from" (Ch 3)** вЂ” kasra on Щ…

Drill alternation: point at a person вЂ” *"man haadhaa?"* вЂ” then point at a doorway вЂ” *"min ayna?"* (where from?). Repeat 5 times. The brain forms the contrast by hearing them back-to-back.

Also: when you write either word on the board, **vocalise it** with the harakat (especially in the first two classes). Don't leave them bare.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Dekho вЂ” `Щ…ЩЋЩ†Щ’` aur `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’` likhne mein bilkul ek jaisi hain, par yeh do alag lafz hain. `Щ…ЩЋЩ†Щ’` mein Щ… par zabar hai вЂ” yeh *kaun?* hai (jo Ch 2 mein seekha tha). `Щ…ЩђЩ†Щ’` mein Щ… par zer hai вЂ” yeh *se* hai (aaj ka naya lafz). Pehchaan: zabar/zer ka farq, par poora matlab badal jaata hai. Likhne mein hamesha harakat lagao pehle hafte вЂ” bina harakat ke confuse honge."*

### Landmine 4 В· Using `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§` for feminine nouns

Classmates have spent two chapters using `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§` for *everything*. Now suddenly some nouns demand `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ`. The instinct will be to keep saying `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§` вЂ” especially for the new feminine vocab (room, school, garden, bag), because the words themselves are unfamiliar and the gender-rule is new.

You'll hear: *"haadhaa ghurfah"* (wrong вЂ” should be *haadhihi ghurfatun*).

**Counter-strategy:** Don't introduce feminine nouns in their masculine form first. Introduce each feminine noun *paired with `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ`*, never alone. Every drill: *"haadhihi ghurfatun, haadhihi madrasatun, haadhihi бёҐadeeqatun"* вЂ” the demonstrative is *glued* to the noun.

Also: write the Ш©-ending in a distinctive colour every time it appears. Train the eye to spot Ш© в†’ feminine в†’ `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ`.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Aaj se ek pakka rule: agar lafz ke aakhir mein Ш© (taa marbuuta вЂ” gol wali 'h' jaisi) hai, to woh lafz **mu'annath** (feminine) hai. Aur feminine lafz ke saath sirf `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ` chalega вЂ” `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§` nahin. `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§` aur `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ` dono ek-doosre ke 'jodi-daar' hain вЂ” har lafz ki apni jodi hai, badli jaa nahin sakti. Pehchaan: aakhir mein Ш© dekho вЂ” fauran `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩђЩ‡Щђ` ka istemaal karo. Ш© nahin hai? `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§` chalegi. Bahut seedha rule hai вЂ” bas yaad rakhna hai."*

### Landmine 5 В· The alif-maqsoorah at end of `Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰` and `ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰`

Both `Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰` and `ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰` end in `Щ‰` вЂ” a letter that looks like dotless ЩЉ but is pronounced as long *aa*. Beginners who have learned the alphabet will read `Щ‰` as a *y* (because that's what ЩЉ is), and pronounce:

- `Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰` as *"Кїalay"* (wrong вЂ” should be *"Кїalaa"*)
- `ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰` as *"ilay"* (wrong вЂ” should be *"ilaa"*)

This is one of the trickier reading rules in early Arabic. The same letter form codes two different sounds depending on context.

**Counter-strategy:** When you first write these on the board, write them with the dagger-alif on top: `Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰Щ°`, `ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰Щ°`. The dagger explicitly says *"this 'y'-looking thing is actually an 'aa'"*. Then say each one slowly, exaggerating the *"-aa"* sound. Drill 5 examples each before moving on.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh aakhir wali Щ‰ вЂ” yeh ya nahin hai, yeh **alif-maqsoorah** (chhoti alif) hai. Likhne mein ya jaisi dikhti hai, par sunaayi 'aa' deti hai. `Ш№ЩЋЩ„ЩЋЩ‰` ko 'Кїa-laa' bolo, 'Кїa-lay' nahin. `ШҐЩђЩ„ЩЋЩ‰` ko 'i-laa' bolo, 'i-lay' nahin. Yeh ek puraana writing convention hai вЂ” jaisa `Щ‡ЩЋШ°ЩЋШ§` mein dagger alif tha. Yahaan bhi waisa hi. Pehli baar likhte waqt main board par dagger lagaa ke dikhaaongi вЂ” phir aap khud yaad kar lo."*
