★ Master Overview — نقشۂ الدرس

One bird's-eye map: Chapter 11 is a reading lesson — the passage بَيْتِي ("My House") consolidates everything from Chapters 4–10 in connected prose, and adds just two small new things: فِيهِ/فِيهَا ("in it") and the devotional phrase أُحِبُّ … ("I love …").

How Ch 11 connects back

Chapter 11 collects ten chapters into one paragraph — it is the moment the class reads a whole piece of real Arabic:

  • The possessive tails (Ch 10) are everywhere — بَيْتِي, غُرْفَتِي, أَبِي, اسْمُهُ/اسْمُهَا — and now extend onto فِي (فِيهِ/فِيهَا).
  • iḍāfa (Ch 4) runs live: غُرْفَةُ أَخِي, نَافِذَةُ غُرْفَتِي — bare first noun, owner second.
  • Demonstratives (Ch 1/7), أَمَامَ (Ch 8), عَلَى/تَحْتَ (Ch 3/5), naʿt (Ch 9), لِي أَخٌ (Ch 10) all appear — so the passage is a built-in diagnostic of what's still shaky.

🌿 A light, joyful chapter — read aloud

After the heavy Ch 9 and Ch 10, this lesson is a breather. There is almost no new grammar; the work is reading the passage aloud together, pausing at each tail and iḍāfa for review. A comfortable 60 minutes. If your class's Ch 10 tails are shaky, this lesson is the remedial round — slow down on the possessive chains.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Read and use فِيهِ ("in it", m) and فِيهَا ("in it", f) to answer مَنْ فِي …؟ / مَاذَا فِي …؟.
  2. Read the connected passage بَيْتِي fluently, recognising the possessive tails, iḍāfa chains, demonstratives, and prepositions in it.
  3. Keep a tailed noun bare — no الـ, no tanween (بَيْتِي, not الْبَيْتِي).
  4. Read the أُحِبُّ … phrases (أُحِبُّ اللَّهَ, أُحِبُّ النَّبِيَّ ﷺ) as fixed expressions — without conjugating the verb.
  5. Recognise أُسَامَةُ/سُعَادُ as names that take no tanween (read-only).

1 · فِيهِ / فِيهَا — "in it"

The one genuinely new structure — and a tiny step from Ch 10. The tail ـهُ/ـها now sits on the preposition فِي to mean "in it", so you can answer a question without repeating the noun. The lesson underlines فِيهِ/فِيهَا in its exercises — this is the focus.

Where this fits: Ch 10 put the four tails on nouns and on عِنْدَ/لِـ/مَعَ. Ch 11 shows the same ـهُ/ـها tail on فِي — quietly teaching that any preposition can carry it.

فِيهِ / فِيهَا · "in it"

Meaning
فِيهِ = "in it / in him" (for a masculine word), فِيهَا = "in it / in her" (for a feminine word). It is فِي ("in") + the Ch 10 tail ـهُ/ـها.
Roman Urdu
فِيهِ = "us mein" (mard/cheez), فِيهَا = "us mein" (aurat/cheez). Yeh فِي + Ch 10 ki ـهُ/ـها dum hai.
Rule
After فِي, the "his" tail reads with a kasra: فِيهِ (fee-hi), not فِيهُ. Teach the sound, not the reason. Negate "there is no one" with مَا فِيهِ أَحَدٌ.
Example
مَاذَا فِي الْحَقِيبَةِ ؟ — فِيهَا كِتَابِي وَقَلَمِي وَدَفْتَرِي mādhā fi l-ḥaqībah? — fīhā kitābī wa-qalamī wa-daftarī · "What is in the bag? — In it are my book, my pen, and my notebook."

⚠️ فِيهِ, not فِيهُ

Classmates have just drilled ـهُ with a ḍamma (اسْمُهُ, عِنْدَهُ) in Ch 10, so they'll say فِيهُ ✗. After فِي it is فِيهِ (kasra) ✓. Drill it as one fixed sound: fee-hi · fee-hā.

2 · Reading the passage بَيْتِي — possessive chains & iḍāfa

The heart of the lesson. A first-person description of a house, built almost entirely from review grammar: the "my" tail everywhere, iḍāfa chains, demonstratives, and prepositions + kasra. This is where you read aloud and diagnose.

Possessive chains — the "my" tail in prose

Meaning
The passage describes the speaker's own things, so the ـِي ("my") tail is on almost every noun: بَيْتِي (my house), غُرْفَتِي (my room), سَرِيرِي (my bed), سَاعَتِي/قَلَمِي/كِتَابِي/حَقِيبَتِي.
Roman Urdu
"Mera" wali dum har lafz par: بَيْتِي (mera ghar), غُرْفَتِي (mera kamra), سَرِيرِي (mera palang). Sab Ch 10 ka dohraav.
Rule
A tailed noun is already definite → no الـ, no tanween (Ch 10). Read the chain fluently; correct any الْبَيْتِي ✗ back to بَيْتِي ✓.
Example
هَذَا بَيْتِي . بَيْتِي أَمَامَ الْمَسْجِدِ . فِيهِ حَدِيقَةٌ صَغِيرَةٌ hādhā baytī. baytī amāma l-masjid(i). fīhi ḥadīqatun ṣaghīrah · "This is my house. My house is in front of the mosque. In it is a small garden."

iḍāfa chains — غُرْفَةُ أَخِي ("my brother's room")

Meaning
A noun owned by another: غُرْفَةُ أَخِي = "the room of my brother", نَافِذَةُ غُرْفَتِي = "the window of my room". The second noun is already definite (by its own tail).
Roman Urdu
غُرْفَةُ أَخِي = "mere bhai ka kamra". Doosra lafz (أَخِي) maalik — pehle se khaas.
Rule
First noun of an iḍāfa is bare: no الـ, no tanween, just a ḍamma (غُرْفَةُ) — Ch 4. الْغُرْفَةُ أَخِي ✗.
Example
هَذِهِ غُرْفَةُ أَخِي . وَتِلْكَ غُرْفَةُ أُخْتِي hādhihi ghurfatu akhī. wa-tilka ghurfatu ukhtī · "This is my brother's room. And that is my sister's room."

💡 Teaching tip — read aloud, pause, ask "what / why"

You read a sentence; the class echoes. At each tail, iḍāfa, demonstrative, or preposition, pause and ask "what is this, and why?" — this turns the reading into live revision of Chapters 4–10. Cover all five paragraphs. Every clean read of بَيْتِي (not الْبَيْتِي) cements the Ch 10 rule.

⚠️ أُسَامَةُ / سُعَادُ — read-only (no tanween)

Both names take a single ـُ (no tanween) — they are diptote (غَيْر مُنْصَرِف), like زَيْنَبُ/خَدِيجَةُ. The book is only having the student read them. If asked, say "khaas naam hain, tanween nahin lete" and don't open the diptote rule (Lesson 14+).

3 · أُحِبُّ … — "I love …"

The warm close of the lesson. أُحِبُّ ("I love") is a new word — but it is a verb, and the class is not learning verbs yet. Handle it exactly like ذَهَبَ/خَرَجَ: a fixed phrase to read, not to conjugate.

Where this fits: Recognition-only vocabulary, the same policy as the past-tense verbs in Ch 5/8/10. The reward is the class's first complete sentences of love and faith, built from words they own.

أُحِبُّ · uḥibbu · "I love"

Meaning
"I love / I like". A first-person verb — but taught here only as a fixed phrase to read, so the class can say أُحِبُّ اللَّهَ, أُحِبُّ أَبِي وَأُمِّي, أُحِبُّ اللُّغَةَ الْعَرَبِيَّةَ.
Roman Urdu
أُحِبُّ = "main muhabbat karta/karti hoon". Verb hai — lekin abhi ذَهَبَ/خَرَجَ ki tarah sirf parho, banao mat.
Rule
Recognition-only — do not conjugate ("he loves / you love" is far later). The word after أُحِبُّ wears a fatḥa (it's the thing loved): read it as printed; don't name the case.
Example
أُحِبُّ اللَّهَ . أُحِبُّ النَّبِيَّ ﷺ . أُحِبُّ اللُّغَةَ الْعَرَبِيَّةَ uḥibbu llāh(a) · uḥibbu n-nabiyy(a) ﷺ · uḥibbu l-lughata l-ʿarabiyyah · "I love Allah · I love the Prophet ﷺ · I love the Arabic language."

⚠️ Don't conjugate أُحِبُّ

When a classmate asks "how do I say he loves / you love?", warmly defer: "Arabic verbs are a big system we'll start later — for now, أُحِبُّ = 'I love'; just read it." Keep the class reading the love-phrases, not building them. (Same policy as ذَهَبَ/خَرَجَ.)

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Reading فِيهِ as "fee-hu"

After Ch 10's ـهُ (ḍamma), classmates say فِيهُ ✗. After فِي it is فِيهِ (kasra) ✓. Drill the sound: fee-hi · fee-hā.

2. Conjugating أُحِبُّ

أُحِبُّ = "I love" — recognition-only, like ذَهَبَ/خَرَجَ. Don't teach "he loves / you love"; verbs are a later phase. Just read the phrase.

3. Adding الـ to a tailed noun

الْبَيْتِي ✗ → بَيْتِي ✓. The tail already makes it definite (Ch 10). One owner-mark is enough — re-read the sentence cleanly.

4. Mis-reading the iḍāfa غُرْفَةُ أَخِي

The first noun is bare (no الـ, no tanween, just a ḍamma): غُرْفَةُ أَخِي ✓, not الْغُرْفَةُ أَخِي ✗. The owner (أَخِي) comes second (Ch 4).

5. Putting tanween on أُسَامَةُ / سُعَادُ

These names take a single ـُ (no tanween) — diptote, read-only. Say "khaas naam hain" and don't open the rule (Lesson 14+).

📿 Connect to the Qur'an

This lesson's two new pieces — فِيهِ/فِيهَا ("in it") and the root of أُحِبُّ ("love") — are both everywhere in the Qur'an. Three verses your class can connect to today's words.

Surah Al-Baqarah · 2:2 — فِيهِ ("in it")

ذَٰلِكَ الْكِتَابُ لَا رَيْبَ ۛ فِيهِ

dhālika l-kitābu lā rayba fīh — "That is the Book about which there is no doubt."

Students recognise: فِيهِ = "in it" — exactly today's word — and ذَٰلِكَ (that, Ch 2) and لَا (no, Ch 1). The opening of the Qur'an's longest surah, built from words they own.

Surah Al-Qadr · 97:4 — فِيهَا ("in it / therein")

تَنَزَّلُ الْمَلَائِكَةُ وَالرُّوحُ فِيهَا بِإِذْنِ رَبِّهِم مِّن كُلِّ أَمْرٍ

tanazzalu l-malāʾikatu wa-r-rūḥu fīhā bi-idhni rabbihim min kulli amr — "The angels and the Spirit descend therein by the permission of their Lord for every affair."

Students recognise: فِيهَا = "in it" (referring to Laylat al-Qadr — the Night) — the feminine partner of فِيهِ. From a short surah many already know by heart.

Surah Āl ʿImrān · 3:31 — the root of أُحِبُّ (love, ح‑ب‑ب)

قُلْ إِن كُنتُمْ تُحِبُّونَ اللَّهَ فَاتَّبِعُونِي يُحْبِبْكُمُ اللَّهُ

qul in kuntum tuḥibbūna llāha fa-ttabiʿūnī yuḥbibkumu llāh — "Say: if you love Allah, then follow me; Allah will love you."

Students recognise: the root ح‑ب‑ب ("love") of today's أُحِبُّ — here in the forms تُحِبُّونَ ("you love") and يُحْبِبْكُمُ ("He will love you"). Recognition only: those are advanced verb forms — point at the shared root, don't teach the conjugation (verbs come much later).
