# Part A · Understanding the chapter

For ten chapters you built the bricks: a noun, `الـ`, iḍāfa, demonstratives, prepositions + kasra, gender agreement, adjectives, detached pronouns, and finally (Ch 10) the attached pronoun tails. Lesson 11 is the chapter where the bricks become a **wall you can read**. The textbook gives a single first-person passage — a boy describing his house, his room, his furniture, his brother's and sister's rooms, his parents, and finally what he loves — and almost every word in it is something your class already knows, now flowing together.

In one sentence: **Chapter 11 is a reading lesson — it consolidates the possessive tails (`بَيْتِي`, `غُرْفَتِي`), iḍāfa (`غُرْفَةُ أَخِي`), `لِي أَخٌ`, and the demonstratives in connected prose; and it adds just two small new things — `فِيهِ`/`فِيهَا` ("in it") and the devotional phrase `أُحِبُّ …` ("I love …").**

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter 11 **halka** hai — ek **parhne** wala sabaq. Naam: `بَيْتِي` ("mera ghar"). Pichhle saare sabaq (dumein, iḍāfa, `لِي أَخٌ`, `هَذِهِ`/`تِلْكَ`, `أَمَامَ`) ab ek **paragraph** mein ek saath aate hain. Sirf do nayi cheezein: `فِيهِ`/`فِيهَا` ("us mein") aur `أُحِبُّ …` ("main … se muhabbat karta hoon"). Baaki sab dohraav. Class ka maqsad: **bol-bol kar parhna** aur fluency.

## 1 · `فِيهِ` / `فِيهَا` — "in it" (the tail on a preposition)

This is the one genuinely new piece of grammar, and it is a tiny, natural step from Chapter 10. There, the four tails (`ـِي`/`ـكَ`/`ـهُ`/`ـها`) glued onto **nouns** (`اسْمُهُ`) and onto `عِنْدَ`/`لِـ`/`مَعَ` (`عِنْدَهُ`, `لَهُ`, `مَعَهُ`). Lesson 11 simply shows the same `ـهُ`/`ـها` tail on the preposition **`فِي`**:

> `فِي` + `ـهُ` → **`فِيهِ`** ("in it / in him")
> `فِي` + `ـها` → **`فِيهَا`** ("in it / in her")

It is used to answer "what / who is in …?" without repeating the noun:

> `مَنْ فِي هَذَا الْبَيْتِ ؟ — فِيهِ حَامِدٌ .` — "Who is in this house? — In it is Hamid."
> `مَاذَا فِي الْحَقِيبَةِ ؟ — فِيهَا كِتَابِي وَقَلَمِي وَدَفْتَرِي .` — "What is in the bag? — In it are my book, my pen, and my notebook."

And the passage opens with it: `بَيْتِي … فِيهِ حَدِيقَةٌ صَغِيرَةٌ` ("my house … in it is a small garden"); `هَذِهِ غُرْفَتِي . فِيهَا نَافِذَةٌ كَبِيرَةٌ` ("this is my room. In it is a big window").

### The one thing to notice: `ـهُ` → `ـهِ` after `فِي`

In Chapter 10 the "his" tail was `ـهُ` with a **ḍamma** (`اسْمُهُ`, `عِنْدَهُ`). But after `فِي` it becomes `ـهِ` with a **kasra**: `فِيهِ`, not `فِيهُ`. Why? The long `ي` of `فِي` is a kasra-sound, and Arabic likes the following vowel to harmonise with it — so `هُ` softens to `هِ`. You do **not** need to teach this as a rule; just teach the word `فِيهِ` as a fixed sound ("fee-hi") and let the ear do the work. (`فِيهَا` keeps its `ـها` unchanged — the `ا` is already an open sound.)

The book also uses it in a negative: `مَا فِيهِ أَحَدٌ` — "there is no one in it" (`مَا` = not, from Ch 1/2; `أَحَدٌ` = anyone/one).

**Yaad rakho:** `فِيهِ` = "us mein" (mard/cheez), `فِيهَا` = "us mein" (aurat/cheez). Yeh Ch 10 ki wahi `ـهُ`/`ـها` dum hai — ab `فِي` par. Ek baat: `فِي` ke baad `ـهُ` ka **pesh, zer** ban jaata hai → `فِيهِ` (fee-**hi**), `فِيهُ` nahin. Rule mat samjhao — bas "fee-hi" ki awaaz yaad karwao. `مَا فِيهِ أَحَدٌ` = "us mein koi nahin".

## 2 · The passage `بَيْتِي` — possessive chains & iḍāfa in connected prose

This is the heart of the lesson and where your class spends most of its time. The passage is built almost entirely from the possessive tail `ـِي` ("my"), strung together into a description. Read it slowly and you'll see every recent chapter at work:

> هَذَا بَيْتِي . بَيْتِي أَمَامَ الْمَسْجِدِ . بَيْتِي جَمِيلٌ . فِيهِ حَدِيقَةٌ صَغِيرَةٌ .
> هَذِهِ غُرْفَتِي . فِيهَا نَافِذَةٌ كَبِيرَةٌ وَمِرْوَحَةٌ جَمِيلَةٌ . هَذَا سَرِيرِي وَهَذَا كُرْسِيٌّ وَمَكْتَبِي . سَاعَتِي وَقَلَمِي وَكِتَابِي عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ . وَحَقِيبَتِي تَحْتَ الْمَكْتَبِ . نَافِذَةُ غُرْفَتِي مَفْتُوحَةٌ .
> هَذِهِ غُرْفَةُ أَخِي . وَتِلْكَ غُرْفَةُ أُخْتِي . غُرْفَةُ أَخِي كَبِيرَةٌ وَغُرْفَةُ أُخْتِي صَغِيرَةٌ . غُرْفَةُ أَخِي أَمَامَ غُرْفَتِي وَغُرْفَةُ أُخْتِي أَمَامَ الْمَطْبَخِ .
> لِي أَخٌ وَاحِدٌ اسْمُهُ أُسَامَةُ ، وَلِي أُخْتٌ وَاحِدَةٌ اسْمُهَا سُعَادُ .
> أَبِي وَأُمِّي فِي تِلْكَ الْغُرْفَةِ الْكَبِيرَةِ .

What's quietly happening (all review — name it to yourself, not necessarily to the class):

- **The "my" tail everywhere:** `بَيْتِي` (my house), `غُرْفَتِي` (my room), `سَرِيرِي` (my bed), `سَاعَتِي`/`قَلَمِي`/`كِتَابِي`/`حَقِيبَتِي`, `مَكْتَبِي`, `أَبِي`/`أُمِّي`/`أَخِي`/`أُخْتِي`. This is pure Chapter 10 reflex — a tailed noun takes **no `الـ`, no tanween**.
- **iḍāfa with a tailed second noun:** `غُرْفَةُ أَخِي` ("my brother's room"), `غُرْفَةُ أُخْتِي`, `نَافِذَةُ غُرْفَتِي` ("the window of my room"). First noun has no `الـ` and no tanween (Ch 4 rule); second noun is already definite by its own tail.
- **`فِيهِ`/`فِيهَا`** (today's new word) opening the descriptions.
- **Demonstratives:** `هَذَا بَيْتِي`, `هَذِهِ غُرْفَتِي`, `هَذِهِ غُرْفَةُ أَخِي`, and the far-feminine `تِلْكَ غُرْفَةُ أُخْتِي` (Ch 7) — near vs far, masc vs fem, all live in one paragraph.
- **`أَمَامَ` + kasra** (Ch 8): `بَيْتِي أَمَامَ الْمَسْجِدِ`, `غُرْفَةُ أُخْتِي أَمَامَ الْمَطْبَخِ`.
- **`عَلَى` / `تَحْتَ` + kasra** (Ch 3/5): `عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ`, `تَحْتَ الْمَكْتَبِ`.
- **`لِي أَخٌ` / `لِي أُخْتٌ`** ("I have a brother / a sister", Ch 10) + **`اسْمُهُ`/`اسْمُهَا`** ("his/her name is …", Ch 10): `لِي أَخٌ وَاحِدٌ اسْمُهُ أُسَامَةُ`.
- **Adjective agreement (naʿt, Ch 9):** `حَدِيقَةٌ صَغِيرَةٌ`, `نَافِذَةٌ كَبِيرَةٌ`, `الْغُرْفَةِ الْكَبِيرَةِ` (definite naʿt — both `الـ`, both kasra after `فِي`).

In other words, the passage is a **live exam of Chapters 4–10**. That's exactly why it matters: shaky rules surface here. If a classmate adds `الـ` to `بَيْتِي`, or says `هَذَا غُرْفَتِي` (wrong gender), or forgets the kasra after `أَمَامَ`, this is where you catch it.

> **Note for you on `أُسَامَةُ` and `سُعَادُ` (read-only):** both names take **no tanween** — they are diptote (`غَيْر مُنْصَرِف`), the same hands-off set as `حَمْزَةُ`/`زَيْنَبُ`/`خَدِيجَةُ` from earlier chapters. The book is just having the student read them. **Do not** open the diptote rule (that's Lesson 14+); if asked, say *"khaas naam hain, tanween nahin lete"* and move on.

**Yaad rakho:** Passage `بَيْتِي` poora **dohraav** hai — har lafz pichhle sabaq se. "Mera" wali dum (`بَيْتِي`, `غُرْفَتِي`…) har jagah; iḍāfa (`غُرْفَةُ أَخِي`); `هَذِهِ`/`تِلْكَ`; `أَمَامَ`/`عَلَى`/`تَحْتَ` + zer; `لِي أَخٌ` + `اسْمُهُ`; sifat (`حَدِيقَةٌ صَغِيرَةٌ`). Yeh ek **chalta-phirta imtihaan** hai Ch 4–10 ka. Yahan pakka karo: `بَيْتِي` par `الـ` na lage, gender sahi ho, zer na bhoolein. `أُسَامَةُ`/`سُعَادُ` — tanween nahin (diptote, sirf parhna, rule mat kholo).

## 3 · `أُحِبُّ …` — "I love …" (the devotional phrases)

The passage ends on a warm note, and the second exercise (`اقْرَأْ` = "read") is a little ladder of love:

> أَنَا أُحِبُّ أَبِي وَأُمِّي . وَأُحِبُّ أَخِي وَأُخْتِي .
> `(١)` أُحِبُّ أَبِي وَأُمِّي . `(٢)` أُحِبُّ أَخِي وَأُخْتِي .
> `(٣)` أُحِبُّ زَمِيلِي . `(٤)` أُحِبُّ أُسْتَاذِي .
> `(٥)` أُحِبُّ اللَّهَ . `(٦)` أُحِبُّ النَّبِيَّ ﷺ .
> `(٧)` أُحِبُّ اللُّغَةَ الْعَرَبِيَّةَ .

`أُحِبُّ` means "**I love**". It is, technically, a present-tense verb (1st person) — but **your class is not learning verbs yet**. Handle `أُحِبُّ` exactly the way you handled `ذَهَبَ`/`خَرَجَ`/`جَلَسَ` in earlier chapters: as a **fixed word to recognise and read**, not a form to conjugate. Teach it as: "`أُحِبُّ` = 'I love' — read the whole phrase; you'll learn how verbs are built much later."

### The one thing to point at (don't theorise): the ending after `أُحِبُّ`

Look at the last three: `أُحِبُّ اللَّهَ`, `أُحِبُّ النَّبِيَّ`, `أُحِبُّ اللُّغَةَ الْعَرَبِيَّةَ`. The word after `أُحِبُّ` ends in a **fatḥa** (`اللَّهَ`, `النَّبِيَّ`, `اللُّغَةَ`) — not the ḍamma you'd expect on a "naked" noun. That's because it's the **object** of "I love" (the thing being loved). You don't need to name this (the case is *naṣb* / accusative — a Lesson-far-ahead topic). Just let your class **read the fatḥa as printed** and notice "after `أُحِبُّ`, the next word wears a zabar." On the family words (`أَبِي`, `أَخِي`, `زَمِيلِي`, `أُسْتَاذِي`) the ending isn't visible anyway, because the `ـِي` tail covers it — which conveniently keeps the focus off the case system.

> **For you, not the class:** `أُحِبُّ` is `أَنَا` + the verb (root `ح-ب-ب`, "to love"). The object after it is *manṣūb* (accusative, fatḥa). The full present-tense verb system, the subject pronouns built into verbs, and the accusative as a named case all come much later in the book. For now this is purely a reading + devotion exercise: the class reads `أُحِبُّ اللَّهَ` and `أُحِبُّ النَّبِيَّ ﷺ` and feels the meaning — a beautiful, simple sentence of faith built from words they can almost fully parse.

**Yaad rakho:** `أُحِبُّ` = "main muhabbat karta/karti hoon". Yeh ek **verb** hai — lekin abhi verb ka nizaam nahin parhana. `ذَهَبَ`/`خَرَجَ` ki tarah: sirf **pehchaan kar parho**, banao mat. Ek cheez dikhao (samjhao mat): `أُحِبُّ` ke baad lafz par **zabar** (`اللَّهَ`, `النَّبِيَّ`, `اللُّغَةَ`) — kyunke woh "jise pyaar kiya" (object) hai. Naam (`نَصْب`) mat batao — bas zabar jaisa likha hai waisa parho. Family words (`أَبِي`, `أُسْتَاذِي`) par to dum ne ending chhupa di. Yeh sabaq ka dil hai: `أُحِبُّ اللَّهَ`, `أُحِبُّ النَّبِيَّ ﷺ`.

## Vocabulary spotlight — what's actually new

Lesson 11 has **no new-words box** — it is a reading lesson built on review vocabulary. Only a handful of items are genuinely new or worth flagging:

- **`أُحِبُّ`** — "I love" (read as a fixed phrase; don't conjugate).
- **`فِيهِ` / `فِيهَا`** — "in it" (the new little structure, §1).
- **`أُسْتَاذٌ`** (`أُسْتَاذِي` = my professor/teacher) — a teacher/professor (slightly more formal than `مُدَرِّس`).
- **`أَحَدٌ`** — "anyone / one" (used in `مَا فِيهِ أَحَدٌ` = "there is no one in it").
- **`مَطْبَخٌ`** — "kitchen" (`أَمَامَ الْمَطْبَخِ`).

**Everything else is review:** `بَيْت`, `غُرْفَة`, `سَرِير`, `كُرْسِيّ`, `مَكْتَب`, `سَاعَة`, `قَلَم`, `كِتَاب`, `حَقِيبَة`, `دَفْتَر`, `نَافِذَة`, `مِرْوَحَة`, `حَدِيقَة`, `مَسْجِد`, `سَيَّارَة`, `جَامِعَة`, `مُدِير`, `زَمِيل` (Ch 10), `أَب`/`أُم`/`أَخ`/`أُخْت`, the adjectives `جَمِيل`/`صَغِير`/`كَبِير`/`مَفْتُوح`, and the function words `هَذَا`/`هَذِهِ`/`تِلْكَ`/`فِي`/`عَلَى`/`تَحْتَ`/`أَمَامَ`/`لِـ`/`مَا`/`مَاذَا`/`مَنْ`/`الْآن`/`وَاحِد`.

**Yaad rakho:** Naye lafz box nahin hai — yeh **parhne** wala sabaq hai, review lafzon par. Sirf chand nayi cheezein: `أُحِبُّ` (main pyaar karta hoon), `فِيهِ`/`فِيهَا` (us mein), `أُسْتَاذ` (ustaad), `أَحَد` (koi), `مَطْبَخ` (kitchen/baawarchi-khana). Baaki sab purana.

## The big picture — what Chapter 11 silently does

Step back. Chapter 11 is the chapter where the class **stops collecting bricks and reads a wall**:

1. **It proves the system works.** Ten chapters of rules — tails, iḍāfa, demonstratives, prepositions, agreement — flow together in one natural paragraph that a beginner can read and understand. That is a huge confidence moment.
2. **It extends the tail one more step — onto `فِي`.** After Ch 10 put tails on nouns and on `عِنْدَ`/`لِـ`/`مَعَ`, `فِيهِ`/`فِيهَا` shows the same tail on `فِي`. The class quietly learns that *any* preposition can carry the tail.
3. **It makes Arabic devotional.** `أُحِبُّ اللَّهَ` / `أُحِبُّ النَّبِيَّ ﷺ` — the class's first complete sentences of love and faith, built almost entirely from words they own. This is the emotional payoff of the whole book so far.
4. **It is a diagnostic.** Because the passage uses everything, it shows you (and them) exactly which earlier rule is still shaky — before the heavier Lesson 12 (relative pronouns) and Lesson 13 (plurals) arrive.

The chapter is light because nothing big is new — but don't waste it. A reading lesson done well (read aloud, slowly, with everyone tracking each tail and iḍāfa) cements ten chapters at once.

**Yaad rakho:** Ch 11 woh sabaq hai jahan class **eenten jodna chhod kar deewar parhti hai** — das sabaq ke usool ek qudrati paragraph mein ek saath. Bara confidence ka lamha. Dum ab `فِي` par bhi (`فِيهِ`). Aur `أُحِبُّ اللَّهَ` — pehla poora jumla muhabbat aur imaan ka. Halka hai, lekin acchi tarah parhwao — ek reading-class das sabaq pakka kar deti hai.

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

Now you understand the chapter. This section is how to *deliver* it. Because this is a reading lesson, the shape of the class is different from a grammar chapter: **less explaining, more reading aloud together**. Your main tool is the passage itself.

> **⏱️ Pacing — comfortable 60 minutes.** Unlike Ch 9 and Ch 10, this lesson is light. Spend the bulk of the hour **reading the passage `بَيْتِي` aloud, line by line**, pausing at each tail and iḍāfa to let classmates say *what* it is and *why* (review). Reserve ~10 minutes for the one new structure (`فِيهِ`/`فِيهَا`) and ~8 minutes for the `أُحِبُّ …` devotional ladder at the end. If your class's Ch 10 tails are shaky, this lesson **is** your remedial round — slow down on the possessive chains.

---

## The landmines for Urdu-speaking adult learners

### Landmine 1 · Reading `فِيهِ` as "fee-hu" (the `ـهُ` → `ـهِ` shift)

Classmates have just drilled `ـهُ` with a ḍamma (`اسْمُهُ`, `عِنْدَهُ`) in Ch 10. So they will instinctively say `فِيهُ` ✗. After `فِي` it is `فِيهِ` (kasra) ✓.

**Counter-strategy:** Don't explain vowel harmony — just drill the **sound**. Say `فِيهِ` ("fee-hi") and `فِيهَا` ("fee-hā") several times and have them echo. Pair each with a question: `مَنْ فِي الْبَيْتِ ؟ — فِيهِ حَامِدٌ`. Treat `فِيهِ` as one word with a fixed sound, like a vocabulary item.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`فِي` ke baad 'his' wali dum **`ـهُ`** nahin, **`ـهِ`** ho jaati hai — `فِيهِ` (fee-**hi**), `فِيهُ` GHALAT. Kyun? `فِي` ki `ي` zer-wali awaaz hai, to agla bhi zer le leta hai. Rule yaad mat karo — bas awaaz yaad karo: fee-hi, fee-hā. `فِيهَا` waisa hi rehta hai."*

### Landmine 2 · Trying to conjugate `أُحِبُّ`

A sharp classmate will ask "how do I say *he* loves / *you* love / *they* love?" — and you'll be tempted to oblige. **Don't.** Verbs are a whole later phase of the book. `أُحِبُّ` is recognition-only here, exactly like `ذَهَبَ`/`خَرَجَ`.

**Counter-strategy:** Acknowledge the question warmly, then defer: "Great question — Arabic verbs are a big, beautiful system we'll start later. For now, `أُحِبُّ` = 'I love'; just read it." Keep the focus on *reading* the love-phrases, not building them.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`أُحِبُّ` = 'main pyaar karta/karti hoon'. Yeh ek verb hai — lekin verb ka poora nizaam **baad ke sabaq** mein. Abhi `ذَهَبَ`/`خَرَجَ` ki tarah: bas pehchaan kar parho, banao mat. 'Woh pyaar karta hai' / 'tum pyaar karte ho' — yeh sab baad mein. Aaj sirf `أُحِبُّ` parho aur mehsoos karo."*

### Landmine 3 · Adding `الـ` to a tailed noun in the passage (Ch 10 slip resurfacing)

Reading fast, classmates may "correct" `بَيْتِي` to `الْبَيْتِي` ✗ or stumble on `غُرْفَتِي`. The tail already makes it definite — no `الـ`.

**Counter-strategy:** When it happens, point back to the Ch 10 rule with one word: "tail = the." Re-read the sentence cleanly. This is the remedial value of the lesson — every clean read reinforces it.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Dum khud 'the' hai. `بَيْتِي` = 'mera ghar' — pehle se khaas. `الْبَيْتِي` GHALAT. (Ch 10 wala usool — yahan phir se pakka ho raha hai.) Jaldi mein `الـ` mat thoonso."*

### Landmine 4 · Mis-reading the iḍāfa chain `غُرْفَةُ أَخِي`

`غُرْفَةُ أَخِي` = "my brother's room" — first noun `غُرْفَةُ` (no `الـ`, no tanween, ḍamma), second noun `أَخِي` (already definite by its tail). Classmates may want `الْغُرْفَةُ` or `غُرْفَتِي أَخِي`.

**Counter-strategy:** Re-use the Ch 4 owner/owned diagram on the board: `غُرْفَةُ` (the thing) + `أَخِي` (the owner). First noun bare, second noun owns. Read several: `غُرْفَةُ أَخِي`, `غُرْفَةُ أُخْتِي`, `نَافِذَةُ غُرْفَتِي`.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`غُرْفَةُ أَخِي` = 'mere bhai ka kamra'. Iḍāfa: pehla lafz (`غُرْفَةُ`) — na `الـ`, na tanween, sirf pesh. Doosra lafz (`أَخِي`) — maalik, pehle se khaas (dum se). `الْغُرْفَةُ أَخِي` GHALAT. Ch 4 wala 'cheez phir maalik' yaad karo."*

## Suggested class plan (comfortable 60 minutes)

*Board before class:* the lesson title (`الدَّرْسُ الْحَادِيَ عَشَرَ`) and subtitle (`بَيْتِي`); the two new words `فِيهِ` / `فِيهَا` with "in it"; and a reminder strip of the four tails from Ch 10 (`ـِي · ـكَ · ـهُ · ـها`).

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–6 min | Greetings · quick review of Ch 10's tails (`اسْمِي`/`اسْمُكَ`/`اسْمُهُ`/`اسْمُها`) and `لِي أَخٌ`. |
| 6–16 min | **`فِيهِ` / `فِيهَا`** — "in it". Drill the sound; Q&A: `مَنْ فِي …؟ — فِيهِ …`, `مَاذَا فِي …؟ — فِيهَا …`. The `مَا فِيهِ أَحَدٌ` negative. |
| 16–42 min | **Read the passage `بَيْتِي` aloud, line by line.** You read a sentence, class echoes. At each tail / iḍāfa / demonstrative / preposition, pause and ask "what is this? why?" (review). Cover all five paragraphs. |
| 42–50 min | **The `أُحِبُّ …` ladder** (`اقْرَأْ`). Read all seven together, ending on `أُحِبُّ اللَّهَ` / `أُحِبُّ النَّبِيَّ ﷺ` / `أُحِبُّ اللُّغَةَ الْعَرَبِيَّةَ`. Point at the fatḥa after `أُحِبُّ` (don't theorise). |
| 50–60 min | **Reading exercise (`اقْرَأْ وَاكْتُبْ`)** — the five `فِيهِ`/`فِيهَا` Q&A sentences. Assign homework. |

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh **reading class** hai — kam samjhao, zyaada **bol-bol kar parho**. Aadha waqt passage `بَيْتِي` ko line-by-line parhne mein: aap parho, class dohraye, har dum/iḍāfa par ruk kar "yeh kya hai, kyun?" poochho (review). 10 min `فِيهِ`/`فِيهَا`, 8 min `أُحِبُّ …`. Ch 10 kamzor ho to yeh sabaq hi uska ilaaj — possessive chains par dheere chalo.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 11

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum baad ke sabaq mein dekhenge"*:

- ❌ **Conjugating verbs.** `أُحِبُّ` stays recognition-only (like `ذَهَبَ`/`خَرَجَ`). Don't teach "he loves / you love / they love", and don't teach the present-tense (`مُضَارِع`) system. That's a much later phase of the book.
- ❌ **The accusative case-name (`نَصْب`).** The fatḥa on `اللَّهَ`/`النَّبِيَّ`/`اللُّغَةَ` after `أُحِبُّ` is just "read it as printed". Don't name the case or explain the object rule.
- ❌ **`فِيهِ`/`فِيهَا` as a phonology rule.** Teach the *sound* "fee-hi / fee-hā", not the vowel-harmony reason behind the `ـهُ → ـهِ` shift.
- ❌ **The feminine `ـكِ` and plural tails** (`ـكُمْ`/`ـهُمْ`/`ـنَا`). Still singular here; the full pronoun set is **Lesson 15**.
- ❌ **The diptote (`غَيْر مُنْصَرِف`) rule** — even though `أُسَامَةُ`/`سُعَادُ` take no tanween. Same hands-off policy as `زَيْنَب`/`خَدِيجَة`. Full rule is **Lesson 14+**.
- ❌ **Building `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي` clauses** — relative pronouns are **Lesson 12** (next chapter). Don't pre-teach.
- ❌ **`بَعْدَ`/`قَبْلَ`, the vocative `يَا أَبَتِ`, feminine verb agreement** — all Lesson 12+ territory.

**Yaad rakho:** Sabse common sawaal: *"'woh pyaar karta hai' kaise?"* — jawab: *"verbs baad ke sabaq mein; aaj sirf `أُحِبُّ` parho."* `أُحِبُّ` ke baad zabar ka naam (`نَصْب`) mat batao. `فِيهِ` — awaaz sikhao, rule nahin. `ـكِ`/`ـكُمْ`/`ـنَا` — Lesson 13–15. Diptote naam (`أُسَامَةُ`/`سُعَادُ`) — bata do, rule mat kholo. `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي` — agla sabaq (Lesson 12), aaj nahin.

## Self-check before teaching

Before the class, make sure:

- [ ] You can read the whole `بَيْتِي` passage aloud fluently, with correct endings.
- [ ] You can use `فِيهِ`/`فِيهَا` to answer `مَنْ فِي …؟` / `مَاذَا فِي …؟`, and you read `فِيهِ` with a **kasra** (not `فِيهُ`).
- [ ] You can explain in one Urdu sentence why `بَيْتِي` takes no `الـ` (tail = definite — Ch 10).
- [ ] You can identify every iḍāfa in the passage (`غُرْفَةُ أَخِي`, `نَافِذَةُ غُرْفَتِي`) and say why the first noun is bare.
- [ ] You can read the `أُحِبُّ …` ladder and resist conjugating the verb or naming the accusative.
- [ ] You know `أُسَامَةُ`/`سُعَادُ` are diptote (no tanween) and you will **not** open the rule.

If yes to all six — *tum tayar ho*. This is a gentle, joyful lesson — let the class feel how far they've come.

## After class — diagnostic checklist

Because this lesson uses everything, it is your best chance to diagnose. For each classmate, watch for:

1. Do they read `فِيهِ` with a kasra and `فِيهَا` correctly, and use them to answer "who/what is in …?" *(Today's new bit.)*
2. Do they keep tailed nouns bare — `بَيْتِي`, not `الْبَيْتِي`? *(Ch 10 definiteness.)*
3. Do they read the iḍāfa chains (`غُرْفَةُ أَخِي`) with a bare first noun? *(Ch 4.)*
4. Do they keep the kasra after `أَمَامَ`/`عَلَى`/`تَحْتَ`? *(Ch 3/5/8.)*
5. Do they pick `هَذِهِ`/`تِلْكَ` by gender and distance, and `اسْمُهُ`/`اسْمُهَا` by the owner? *(Ch 6/7/10.)*
6. Do they keep `أُحِبُّ` and the names (`أُسَامَةُ`) at **recognition** level — without conjugating or adding tanween? *(Scope held.)*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh sabaq sab kuch use karta hai — isliye **diagnosis** ka behtareen mauqa. Agar `فِيهُ` nikal raha hai, ya `الْبَيْتِي` ban raha hai, ya `أَمَامَ` ke baad zer ghaayab — to woh purana sabaq dohrana hai. Ch 11 ka core: `فِيهِ`/`فِيهَا` + poora passage rawaani se parhna + `أُحِبُّ …` (sirf parhna). Agla sabaq (Lesson 12): `الَّذِي`/`التِي` — relative pronouns. Aaj uska zikr nahin.
