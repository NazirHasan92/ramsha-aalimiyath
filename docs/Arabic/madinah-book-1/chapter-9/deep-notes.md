# Part A · Understanding the chapter

For eight chapters the book built nouns, demonstratives, iḍāfa, prepositions, the feminine, and — in Chapter 8 — the demonstrative-with-a-definite-noun (`هَذَا الْبَيْتُ`). Chapter 9 takes the *next* logical step: now that a noun can be definite, the book teaches you how to **describe** it with an adjective. That's `النَّعْت`.

In one sentence: **Chapter 9 teaches the adjective (`النَّعْت`) — which comes *after* its noun (`المَنْعُوت`) and *copies* it in gender, definiteness, and ending — plus the speaker pronouns `أَنَا`/`أَنْتَ`, the location word `عِنْدَ`, and a first, recognition-only look at the relative pronoun `الَّذِي`.**

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter 9 **bhaari** hai — char cheezein ek saath. Sabse badi: `النَّعْت` (sifat) jo apne ism (`المَنْعُوت`) ke **peeche** aati hai aur uski **naqal** karti hai (gender, `الـ`, ending). Baqi teen halki: `أَنَا`/`أَنْتَ` (main/tum), `عِنْدَ` (ke paas), aur `الَّذِي` (jo/jis — sirf pehchaan, poora rule Lesson 12). Waqt zyada `النَّعْت` par lagao.

## 1 · `النَّعْت` / `المَنْعُوت` — the adjective and its noun (the big rule)

This is the heart of the chapter. An **adjective** in Arabic is called `النَّعْت` (*an-naʿt*) — also `الصِّفَة` (*aṣ-ṣifah*). The noun it describes is `المَنْعُوت` (*al-manʿūt*) — also `المَوْصُوف`. The textbook itself prints a little diagram on p. 53:

> `بَيْتٌ` ← (`مَنْعُوت`) &nbsp;&nbsp; `جَدِيدٌ` ← (`نَعْت`)
> "a house" (the described noun) · "new" (the adjective)

There are two things to learn, and the second is where everything happens.

### (a) The adjective comes *after* the noun

In English and Urdu, the adjective comes **before** the noun: "a **new** house", "**naya** ghar". In Arabic it comes **after**:

> `بَيْتٌ جَدِيدٌ` — *baytun jadīdun* — literally "a house new" = **"a new house"**.
> `طَالِبٌ مُجْتَهِدٌ` — "a hardworking student" (literally "a student hardworking").

This is a **word-order flip**, exactly like the iḍāfa flip in Chapter 4. The noun leads; the description trails.

### (b) The adjective *copies* the noun — three agreements

This is the rule that does all the work. The `نَعْت` must **match** its `مَنْعُوت` in three ways:

1. **Gender** — masculine noun → masculine adjective; feminine noun → feminine adjective:
   > `طَائِرٌ صَغِيرٌ` (a small bird, m) · `فَاكِهَةٌ لَذِيذَةٌ` (a delicious fruit, f — note the `ة` on *both*... well, on the adjective `لَذِيذَة`).
   > `لُغَةٌ سَهْلَةٌ` (an easy language, f) · `لُغَةٌ جَمِيلَةٌ` (a beautiful language, f).
   This is just Chapter 6's feminine-agreement rule, now with a name.

2. **Definiteness** — both indefinite, **or** both definite. This is the big one (see below).
   > `تَاجِرٌ غَنِيٌّ` (a rich merchant — both indefinite) · `الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ` (the new teacher — both definite, `الـ` on *both*).

3. **Ending (case)** — both carry the same ending. After a preposition both take kasra:
   > `فِي الْغُرْفَةِ الْكَبِيرَةِ` — "in the big room" — kasra on *both* `الْغُرْفَةِ` and `الْكَبِيرَةِ`.

### The crucial part: definiteness tells you "phrase" vs "sentence"

This is the deepest idea in the chapter, and it builds **directly** on Chapter 8's `هَذَا الْبَيْتُ`. Watch these two carefully:

> `الْمُدَرِّسُ جَدِيدٌ` — *al-mudarrisu jadīdun* — "**The teacher is new**." → a **SENTENCE** (mubtadaʾ + khabar).
> `الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ` — *al-mudarrisu l-jadīdu* — "**the new teacher**" → just a **PHRASE** (naʿt), waiting for a predicate.

What's the difference? **The `الـ` on the adjective.**
- Definite noun + **indefinite** adjective (`الْمُدَرِّسُ جَدِيدٌ`) = a *sentence* ("the teacher **is** new"). The mismatch in definiteness is the signal that one is the subject and the other is the predicate.
- Definite noun + **definite** adjective (`الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ`) = a *description*, a phrase ("the new teacher"). The match in definiteness glues them into one unit — and just like `هَذَا الْبَيْتُ` in Ch 8, it now needs a separate khabar to become a sentence:
  > `الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ فِي الْفَصْلِ` — "The new teacher is in the classroom." ✓

So the `الـ` on the adjective is the **same "open switch"** you met in Chapter 8. Add it, and the phrase opens up and needs finishing.

**Yaad rakho:** `النَّعْت` (sifat) do kaam karti hai. (a) Ism ke **peeche** aati hai (English/Urdu se ulta): `بَيْتٌ جَدِيدٌ` = "naya ghar". (b) Ism ki **naqal** karti hai teen cheezon mein: **gender** (`طَائِرٌ صَغِيرٌ` / `فَاكِهَةٌ لَذِيذَةٌ`), **`الـ`** (dono indefinite ya dono definite), aur **ending** (`فِي الْغُرْفَةِ الْكَبِيرَةِ` — dono par zer). Sabse aham: `الـ` ka khel — `الْمُدَرِّسُ جَدِيدٌ` = "ustad naya hai" (JUMLA), lekin `الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ` = "naya ustad" (PHRASE, abhi adhoora — Ch 8 wala "open switch" phir se). Phrase ko poora karo: `الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ فِي الْفَصْلِ`.

## 2 · `أَنَا` / `أَنْتَ` — the speaker pronouns

Chapter 5 gave the class their first pronouns: `هُوَ` (he/it) and `هِيَ` (she/it) — the *third* person ("the one we talk about"). Lesson 9 adds the *people in the conversation*:

- `أَنَا` (*anā*) — **"I"** (the speaker; same word for male or female).
- `أَنْتَ` (*anta*) — **"you"** (masculine; spoken *to* a male).

> `مَنْ أَنْتَ ؟ — أَنَا طَالِبٌ .` — "Who are you? — I am a student."
> `أَأَنْتَ طَالِبٌ جَدِيدٌ ؟ — نَعَمْ . أَنَا طَالِبٌ جَدِيدٌ .` — "Are you a new student? — Yes, I am a new student."

They work exactly like `هُوَ`/`هِيَ`: they sit at the front of a nominal sentence as the mubtadaʾ. `أَنَا طَالِبٌ` = "I am a student" (no word for "am" — same as every nominal sentence since Ch 1).

> **For you, not the class:** the feminine "you" is `أَنْتِ` (*anti*, with a kasra). The lesson uses only `أَنْتَ` (masculine). Teach `أَنَا` and `أَنْتَ`; mention `أَنْتِ` exists for a female but **don't drill it** — the full pronoun set is completed much later (Lesson 15). And the attached/possessive pronouns (`اسْمِي` "my name", `اسْمُكَ` "your name") are **Lesson 10** — not yet.

**Yaad rakho:** Ch 5 mein `هُوَ`/`هِيَ` (woh) mile the. Ab baat-cheet wale do: `أَنَا` (main — mard/aurat dono), `أَنْتَ` (tum — mard ke liye). Inhe bhi `هُوَ`/`هِيَ` ki tarah jumle ke shuru mein rakho: `أَنَا طَالِبٌ` (main taalib hoon — "hoon" ka koi lafz nahin, har nominal jumle ki tarah). `أَنْتِ` (aurat ke liye "tum") baad mein — abhi mat drill karo. `اسْمِي`/`اسْمُكَ` = Lesson 10.

## 3 · `عِنْدَ` — "at someone's place / with"

A small, useful word: `عِنْدَ` (*ʿinda*) means "at (the place of) / with / in the possession of". It works like the location-word family (`تَحْتَ`, `أَمَامَ`, `خَلْفَ`): it ends in a fatḥa, and the noun **after** it takes **kasra**:

> `أَيْنَ الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ ؟ — هُوَ عِنْدَ الْمُدِيرِ .` — "Where is the new teacher? — He is **at the director's** (with the director)."

So `عِنْدَ الْمُدِيرِ` = "at the director's place / with the director" — `الْمُدِيرِ` in kasra, same as everything after the location words.

> **For you, not the class:** `عِنْدَ` is a ẓarf, and the noun after it is its muḍāf ilayhi — which is *why* it takes kasra. You do not teach this; just add `عِنْدَ` to the board with `تَحْتَ`/`أَمَامَ`/`خَلْفَ` as "another word that puts the next noun in kasra". Later it will also carry attached pronouns (`عِنْدِي` "I have", `عِنْدَكَ` "you have") — that's Lesson 10, not now.

**Yaad rakho:** `عِنْدَ` = "ke paas / ke yahan". Jagah-batane wale khaandaan ka naya member (`تَحْتَ`, `أَمَامَ`, `خَلْفَ` ke saath) — agla lafz **zer** leta hai: `عِنْدَ الْمُدِيرِ` (director ke paas). Board par usi family mein likho. `عِنْدِي`/`عِنْدَكَ` (mere paas / tere paas) = Lesson 10, abhi nahin.

## 4 · `الَّذِي` — the relative pronoun (recognition only)

The lesson's reading passages (part ب) use a new little word a lot: `الَّذِي` (*alladhī*) — "who / which / that". It links a **describing clause** to a definite noun:

> `مَنْ ذَلِكَ الرَّجُلُ الطَّوِيلُ الَّذِي خَرَجَ الْآنَ مِنَ الْمَدْرَسَةِ ؟`
> "Who is that tall man **who** just left the school?"

> `الْكِتَابُ الَّذِي عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ لِلْمُدَرِّسِ .`
> "The book **which** is on the desk is the teacher's."

You can think of `الَّذِي` as a "super-adjective": where a one-word adjective (`الرَّجُلُ الطَّوِيلُ` — "the tall man") describes with a single word, `الَّذِي` describes with a whole *clause* (`الرَّجُلُ الَّذِي خَرَجَ` — "the man who left"). It only attaches to a **definite** masculine singular noun (hence the `الـ` built into it).

**Teach this as recognition only.** Tell the class: *"`الَّذِي` means 'who/which' — it starts a describing phrase. You'll learn how to build these fully in Lesson 12. For now, just recognise it when you read."* Don't make them produce relative clauses, and don't introduce the feminine `الَّتِي` (Lesson 12).

**Yaad rakho:** `الَّذِي` = "jo / jis" — ek poora describing-phrase jodta hai definite (mard) ism ke saath. Ek-lafz sifat (`الرَّجُلُ الطَّوِيلُ` — "lamba aadmi") vs `الَّذِي` jo poora jumla joRti hai (`الرَّجُلُ الَّذِي خَرَجَ` — "woh aadmi jo nikla"). **Sirf pehchaan** — banana mat sikhao, poora rule Lesson 12. `الَّتِي` (feminine) bhi abhi nahin.

## The big picture — what Chapter 9 silently does

Step back. Chapter 9 is a genuine milestone — it is where Arabic stops being a list of labelled objects and starts being **descriptive**:

1. **You can now describe.** Before Ch 9, you could *name* things (`هَذَا بَيْتٌ`), *point* at them (`تِلْكَ سَيَّارَةٌ`), *own* them (iḍāfa), and *locate* them (prepositions). Now you can give them **qualities** — new, old, big, small, rich, poor, beautiful, hardworking. That is a huge expressive jump.
2. **The definiteness signal becomes a master skill.** The `هَذَا بَيْتٌ` / `هَذَا الْبَيْتُ` lesson from Ch 8 now generalises: matching `الـ` = a description (phrase); mismatched `الـ` = a sentence. `الْمُدَرِّسُ جَدِيدٌ` ("the teacher is new") vs `الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ` ("the new teacher"). Reading Arabic correctly depends on catching this.
3. **The conversation widens.** With `أَنَا` and `أَنْتَ`, your classmates can finally talk *as themselves* and *to each other* — not just describe a third party.
4. **A door to clauses opens (`الَّذِي`).** The relative pronoun previews how Arabic will eventually build long, layered descriptions. The class only peeks through the door here; they walk through it in Lesson 12.

The chapter is heavy because it is foundational. `النَّعْت` is one of the pillars of Arabic syntax — take the time it needs.

**Yaad rakho:** Ch 9 ek milestone hai — yahan Arabic **describing** karna shuru karti hai. Pehle sirf naam (`هَذَا بَيْتٌ`), ishaarah (`تِلْكَ`), milkiyat (iḍāfa), jagah (preposition); ab **sifaat** (naya, purana, bada, chhota…). `الـ` ka signal ab master-skill: dono par `الـ` = description (phrase); ek par `الـ` = jumla. `أَنَا`/`أَنْتَ` se baat-cheet khulti hai. `الَّذِي` se lambe jumlon ka darwaza (poora Lesson 12 mein). Bhaari hai kyunke buniyaadi hai — `النَّعْت` ko waqt do.

## The landmines for Urdu-speaking adult learners

### Landmine 1 · The word-order flip — adjective *after* the noun (the first hurdle)

English and Urdu put the adjective **before** the noun ("a **new** house", "**naya** ghar"). Arabic puts it **after** (`بَيْتٌ جَدِيدٌ` — "a house new"). Classmates will instinctively say `جَدِيدٌ بَيْتٌ` ✗.

**Counter-strategy:** Reuse the iḍāfa word-order lesson from Ch 4 — Arabic likes the *main thing first*, the detail after. Drill it physically: hold up an object, name it, *then* describe it: `قَلَمٌ … جَدِيدٌ` ("a pen … new"). Build a rhythm: noun-first, always.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Urdu mein sifat pehle aati hai: 'naya ghar', 'bada darwaza'. Arabic ULTA hai — pehle ism, phir sifat: `بَيْتٌ جَدِيدٌ` (ghar naya), `بَابٌ كَبِيرٌ` (darwaza bada). Jaise iḍāfa mein ulta tha (Ch 4), waise yahan bhi. Pehle cheez ka naam, phir uski sifat."*

### Landmine 2 · `الْمُدَرِّسُ جَدِيدٌ` vs `الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ` (THE big one)

This is the trap that decides whether a classmate truly understands the chapter. With `الـ` on the adjective, it's a **phrase** ("the new teacher"); without, it's a **sentence** ("the teacher is new"). Classmates will read both as "the teacher is new", or both as "the new teacher", and the meaning collapses.

**Counter-strategy:** Use the Ch 8 "open switch" image again. `الْمُدَرِّسُ جَدِيدٌ` — different clothes (one has `الـ`, one doesn't) → they're a *sentence* (subject + predicate, "the teacher **is** new"). `الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ` — *same* clothes (both have `الـ`) → they're glued into a *phrase* ("the new teacher"), which is still open and needs a khabar (`… فِي الْفَصْلِ`). Drill the minimal pair on the board, side by side, over and over.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Dhyaan se! `الْمُدَرِّسُ جَدِيدٌ` (ek par `الـ`, doosre par nahin) = 'ustad naya hai' — poora JUMLA. `الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ` (dono par `الـ`) = 'naya ustad' — sirf PHRASE, abhi adhoora! (Ch 8 wala 'switch' phir se.) Poora karo: `الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ فِي الْفَصْلِ` (naya ustad class mein hai). Pehchaan: dono par `الـ` = sifat/description; sirf ek par `الـ` = jumla."*

### Landmine 3 · Forgetting the gender match on the adjective

The Ch 6 agreement rule re-fires inside `النَّعْت`. A feminine noun needs a feminine adjective: `لُغَةٌ سَهْلَةٌ` (an easy language) — `ة` on the adjective. Classmates forget: `لُغَةٌ سَهْلٌ` ✗.

**Counter-strategy:** Say openly: "the adjective wears the same clothes as the noun — if the noun is feminine, the adjective takes the `ة`." Drill feminine pairs: `فَاكِهَةٌ لَذِيذَةٌ`, `مَدِينَةٌ كَبِيرَةٌ`, `سَيَّارَةٌ جَمِيلَةٌ`. This is just Ch 6 with a name.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Sifat apne ism ke **kapde** pehnti hai. Ism feminine (`ة` wala) → sifat bhi `ة` legi: `لُغَةٌ سَهْلَةٌ` (aasaan zabaan), `مَدِينَةٌ كَبِيرَةٌ` (bada shahar). `لُغَةٌ سَهْلٌ` GHALAT. Yeh wahi Chapter 6 ka agreement hai — bas ab iska naam hai: `النَّعْت`."*

### Landmine 4 · Treating `أَنَا` / `أَنْتَ` as the verb "am/are"

The classic nominal-sentence slip, now with new pronouns. `أَنَا طَالِبٌ` = "I am a student" — but there is no word for "am". Classmates may hunt for one, or double up.

**Counter-strategy:** Reinforce the Ch 1/Ch 5 rule: a nominal sentence is two naked nouns; the "is/am/are" lives in the gap. `أَنَا` + `طَالِبٌ`, nothing between. Same as `هُوَ طَالِبٌ`.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`أَنَا طَالِبٌ` = 'main taalib hoon' — par 'hoon' ka koi alag lafz nahin! Bilkul `هُوَ طَالِبٌ` (woh taalib hai) jaisa. Do naam, beech mein kuch nahin — 'hai/hoon' khaali jagah mein chhupa hai. Yeh Chapter 1 ka nominal jumla hai."*

## Suggested class plan (split or extended — see pacing note)

*Board before class:* the lesson title (`الدَّرْسُ التَّاسِعُ`); the location family with `عِنْدَ` newly added (`فِي · عَلَى · إِلَى · مِنْ · لِـ · تَحْتَ · أَمَامَ · خَلْفَ · عِنْدَ`); and the minimal pair `الْمُدَرِّسُ جَدِيدٌ` / `الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ`.

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–8 min | Greetings · review Ch 8's `هَذَا الْبَيْتُ` (the "open switch") — `النَّعْت` rests on it. |
| 8–22 min | **`النَّعْت` — adjective after the noun.** The word-order flip (`بَيْتٌ جَدِيدٌ`). The three agreements: gender, definiteness, ending. Show the book's diagram (`بَيْتٌ` = `مَنْعُوت`, `جَدِيدٌ` = `نَعْت`, p. 53). |
| 22–38 min | **The big minimal pair:** `الْمُدَرِّسُ جَدِيدٌ` (sentence) vs `الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ` (phrase). Drill it relentlessly — this is the chapter. Finish phrases with a khabar. |
| 38–48 min | **`أَنَا` / `أَنْتَ`.** `مَنْ أَنْتَ ؟ — أَنَا طَالِبٌ`. No word for "am". Pair them with adjectives: `أَنَا طَالِبٌ مُجْتَهِدٌ`. |

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 9

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum Chapter X mein dekhenge"*:

- ❌ **Attached / possessive pronouns** (`اسْمِي` "my name", `اسْمُكَ` "your name", `عِنْدِي` "I have", `بَيْتُهُ` "his house"). That's **Lesson 10**, the very next lesson. Teach `عِنْدَ` + a visible noun only (`عِنْدَ الْمُدِيرِ`), not `عِنْدِي`/`عِنْدَكَ`.
- ❌ **The five nouns** (`أَبُوكَ`, `أَخُوكَ` with long vowels) and **`مَعَ`** (with) — also **Lesson 10**.
- ❌ **Building relative clauses** with `الَّذِي`, and the feminine `الَّتِي`. `الَّذِي` is **recognition only** here; the productive system is **Lesson 12**.
- ❌ **The feminine "you" `أَنْتِ`** as a drilled item — mention it exists, defer real practice (the full pronoun table is Lesson 15).
- ❌ **The diptote (`غَيْر مُنْصَرِف`) rule** — even though the `فَعْلَان` state-adjectives (`جُوعَان`, `كَسْلَان`…) take no tanween. Same hands-off policy. Full rule is Lesson 14+.
- ❌ **Plurals / plural agreement** — all singular here. `هَؤُلَاءِ`, plural adjectives, etc. are Lesson 13+.
- ❌ **The `إِعْرَاب` case-names** (`رَفْع`/`نَصْب`/`جَرّ`) as a formal system. Keep saying "ending becomes kasra after a preposition" descriptively.

**Yaad rakho:** Sabse common sawaal: *"'mera naam' / 'mere paas' kaise kehte hain?"* (`اسْمِي` / `عِنْدِي`) — jawab: *"Lesson 10 mein."* Doosra: *"`الَّذِي` se jumla kaise banate hain?"* — *"sirf pehchaan abhi; banana Lesson 12 mein."* Teesra: *"`كَسْلَان` par tanween kyun nahin?"* — *"khaas sifat hai, baad mein."* `أَنْتِ` (aurat) — bata do, drill mat karo. Ek waqt mein ek hi cheez.

## Self-check before teaching

Before the class, make sure:

- [ ] You can place an adjective correctly: noun first, then adjective, agreeing in gender + definiteness + ending (`بَيْتٌ كَبِيرٌ`, `الْمَدِينَةُ الْكَبِيرَةُ`, `فِي الْغُرْفَةِ الْكَبِيرَةِ`).
- [ ] You can explain, in one Urdu sentence, the difference between `الْمُدَرِّسُ جَدِيدٌ` and `الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ`. *(The chapter's core decision.)*
- [ ] You can match gender on the adjective every time (`لُغَةٌ سَهْلَةٌ`, not `سَهْلٌ`).
- [ ] You can use `أَنَا` / `أَنْتَ` in a nominal sentence with no word for "am/are" (`أَنَا طَالِبٌ`, `أَنْتَ طَالِبٌ جَدِيدٌ ؟`).
- [ ] You can put the kasra after `عِنْدَ` (`عِنْدَ الْمُدِيرِ`) and place it in the location family.
- [ ] You can read a sentence with `الَّذِي` and explain it means "who/which" — *without* trying to teach how to build one.
- [ ] You have decided your **pacing** (long session or split) and have the Ch 8 review ready if needed.

If yes to all seven — *tum tayar ho* (but give yourself extra prep time; this is a big one).

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Do they place the adjective **after** the noun, not before? *(Word-order flip.)*
2. Do they read `الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ` as a **phrase** ("the new teacher") and `الْمُدَرِّسُ جَدِيدٌ` as a **sentence** ("the teacher is new")? *(The chapter's core — and the Ch 8 link.)*
3. Do they match **gender** on the adjective (`فَاكِهَةٌ لَذِيذَةٌ`)? *(Ch 6 rule inside naʿt.)*
4. Do they use `أَنَا`/`أَنْتَ` without inventing a word for "am/are"? *(Nominal sentence.)*
5. Do they put the **kasra** after `عِنْدَ`? *(Location family.)*
6. Do they *recognise* `الَّذِي` as "who/which" — without over-reaching to build clauses? *(Recognition scope held.)*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh chhe points batayenge ke Lesson 10 (attached pronouns) par jaana hai ya `النَّعْت` dohraana hai. Agar minimal pair (`جَدِيدٌ` vs `الْجَدِيدُ`) clear nahin, ya sifat ism se pehle aa rahi hai — to ek aur `النَّعْت` round do. Ch 9 ka core: sifat ism ke **peeche** + uski **naqal** (gender + `الـ` + ending), aur `الـ`-match ka phrase-vs-jumla signal. Yeh pakka, baqi sab uske baad.
