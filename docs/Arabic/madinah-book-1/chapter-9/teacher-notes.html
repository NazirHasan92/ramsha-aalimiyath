How Ch 9 connects back

Chapter 9 is the descriptive turning-point, and it leans hard on the last three chapters:

  • It generalises Ch 8's هَذَا الْبَيْتُ. The "open switch" idea (definite noun + الـ = a phrase needing a predicate) now applies to adjectives: الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ ("the new teacher") is a phrase; الْمُدَرِّسُ جَدِيدٌ ("the teacher is new") is a sentence.
  • It re-uses Ch 6's gender agreement. The adjective copies the noun's ة (فَاكِهَةٌ لَذِيذَةٌ) — now formally named النَّعْت.
  • عِنْدَ joins the location family. After تَحْتَ/أَمَامَ/خَلْفَ, another word that puts the next noun in kasra.
  • أَنَا/أَنْتَ extend Ch 5's pronouns (هُوَ/هِيَ) — same nominal-sentence behaviour.

⏱️ This is a HEAVY chapter — plan your pacing

Lesson 9 carries four new things, one of them a major rule (النَّعْت). It does not fit a comfortable 60 minutes. Either run a 75–90 minute session, or split it: Class 1 = النَّعْت only; Class 2 = أَنَا/أَنْتَ + عِنْدَ + الَّذِي + reading. If Ch 8's هَذَا الْبَيْتُ is shaky, review it first — النَّعْت's definiteness rule rests on it.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Place an adjective (النَّعْت) after its noun (المَنْعُوت): بَيْتٌ كَبِيرٌ, طَالِبٌ مُجْتَهِدٌ.
  2. Match the adjective to the noun in gender, definiteness, and ending (لُغَةٌ سَهْلَةٌ, الْمَدِينَةُ الْكَبِيرَةُ, فِي الْغُرْفَةِ الْكَبِيرَةِ).
  3. Tell apart الْمُدَرِّسُ جَدِيدٌ ("the teacher is new" — sentence) and الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ ("the new teacher" — phrase).
  4. Use أَنَا ("I") and أَنْتَ ("you", m) in nominal sentences (أَنَا طَالِبٌ, مَنْ أَنْتَ ؟).
  5. Use عِنْدَ ("at someone's place"), putting the next noun in kasra (عِنْدَ الْمُدِيرِ).
  6. Recognise الَّذِي ("who/which") in a reading passage — without yet building relative clauses.

1 · النَّعْت — the adjective & its noun (the big rule)

An adjective (النَّعْت / الصِّفَة) comes after the noun it describes (المَنْعُوت / المَوْصُوف) and copies it in gender, definiteness, and ending. The textbook prints the diagram بَيْتٌ (مَنْعُوت) · جَدِيدٌ (نَعْت) on p. 53.

Where this fits: Ch 6 taught feminine agreement; Ch 8 taught the demonstrative + definite noun. Ch 9 names the adjective relationship (النَّعْت) and adds the definiteness rule that turns a description on or off.

النَّعْت · an-naʿt · the adjective

Meaning
The describing word (adjective). It comes after the noun it describes — the opposite of English/Urdu order — and agrees with it in gender, definiteness, and ending.
Roman Urdu
Sifat — ism ke peeche aati hai (English/Urdu se ulta) aur uski naqal karti hai: gender, الـ, aur ending.
Rule
Noun first, then adjective: بَيْتٌ جَدِيدٌ ("a house new" = a new house). The noun is the مَنْعُوت; the adjective is the نَعْت.
Example
عَبَّاسٌ تَاجِرٌ غَنِيٌّ ʿabbāsun tājirun ghaniyyun · "Abbas is a rich merchant" (تاجر = noun, غني = adjective after it)

The big one: الْمُدَرِّسُ جَدِيدٌ vs الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ

Meaning
The الـ on the adjective decides everything. Definite + indefinite = a sentence ("the teacher is new"). Definite + definite = a phrase ("the new teacher"), which still needs a predicate.
Roman Urdu
الْمُدَرِّسُ جَدِيدٌ = "ustad naya hai" (JUMLA). الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ = "naya ustad" (PHRASE — Ch 8 wala "open switch" phir se). Poora karo: … فِي الْفَصْلِ.
Rule
Mismatched الـ → sentence (subject + predicate). Matched الـ → description (phrase, open until a khabar is added).
Example
الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ فِي الْفَصْلِ al-mudarrisu l-jadīdu fi l-faṣl(i) · "the new teacher is in the classroom" (phrase الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ + khabar فِي الْفَصْلِ)

Gender agreement inside النَّعْت (Ch 6, now named)

Meaning
A feminine noun takes a feminine adjective (the ة appears on the adjective too) — the Chapter 6 rule, operating inside the adjective relationship.
Roman Urdu
Sifat apne ism ke kapde pehnti hai: ism feminine → sifat bhi ة legi. لُغَةٌ سَهْلَةٌ ✓, لُغَةٌ سَهْلٌ ✗.
Rule
Masc noun → masc adjective; fem noun (ة or a Ch 6 ة-less feminine) → fem adjective.
Example
الْعَرَبِيَّةُ لُغَةٌ سَهْلَةٌ · التُّفَّاحُ فَاكِهَةٌ لَذِيذَةٌ "Arabic is an easy language · the apple is a delicious fruit" (both adjectives carry the ة)

💡 Teaching tip — drill the minimal pair until it's reflex

Put الْمُدَرِّسُ جَدِيدٌ and الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ side by side on the board and switch between them a dozen times: "sentence … phrase … sentence … phrase". Then make them finish each phrase: الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ … → … فِي الْفَصْلِ. This single pair is the whole chapter.

⚠️ Adjective goes AFTER the noun

English/Urdu say "new house" / "naya ghar" (adjective first). Arabic says بَيْتٌ جَدِيدٌ ("house new"). Classmates will reach for جَدِيدٌ بَيْتٌ ✗. Drill noun-first, like the iḍāfa flip in Ch 4.

2 · أَنَا / أَنْتَ — the speaker pronouns

After Ch 5's هُوَ/هِيَ ("he/she"), the lesson adds the people in the conversation: أَنَا ("I") and أَنْتَ ("you", masculine).

Where this fits: These extend the detached-pronoun set begun in Ch 5. They behave identically — sit at the front of a nominal sentence as the mubtadaʾ, with no word for "am/are".

أَنَا / أَنْتَ · anā / anta

Meaning
أَنَا = "I" (same for a man or a woman). أَنْتَ = "you" (speaking to a male). Both are subjects of nominal sentences.
Roman Urdu
أَنَا = "main" (mard/aurat dono). أَنْتَ = "tum/aap" (mard ke liye). هُوَ/هِيَ ki tarah jumle ke shuru mein.
Rule
No word for "am/are" — أَنَا طَالِبٌ = "I am a student". The feminine "you" (أَنْتِ) exists but is deferred (full set in Lesson 15).
Example
أَأَنْتَ طَالِبٌ جَدِيدٌ ؟ — نَعَمْ . أَنَا طَالِبٌ جَدِيدٌ a-anta ṭālibun jadīdun? — naʿam. anā ṭālibun jadīdun · "Are you a new student? — Yes, I am a new student"

⚠️ No word for "am/are"

أَنَا طَالِبٌ = "I am a student" — but nothing stands for "am". It's a nominal sentence (Ch 1): two nouns, the "is/am/are" lives in the gap. Don't let classmates hunt for a copula.

3 · عِنْدَ — "at someone's place / with"

A new location-type word: عِنْدَ ("at the place of / with"). It joins the تَحْتَ/أَمَامَ/خَلْفَ family — the next noun takes kasra.

Where this fits: Another member of the "location word + kasra" family that has grown every few chapters (تَحْتَ Ch 5, أَمَامَ/خَلْفَ Ch 8). Group it with them on the board.

عِنْدَ · ʿinda · "at / with"

Meaning
"At (the place of) / with / in the possession of". The word ends in fatḥa; the noun after it takes kasra.
Roman Urdu
"Ke paas / ke yahan". Jagah-batane wale khaandaan ka naya member — agla lafz zer leta hai.
Rule
Next noun → kasra: عِنْدَ الْمُدِيرِ. (The attached-pronoun forms عِنْدِي/عِنْدَكَ are Lesson 10 — not yet.)
Example
أَيْنَ الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ ؟ — هُوَ عِنْدَ الْمُدِيرِ ayna l-mudarrisu l-jadīd? — huwa ʿinda l-mudīr(i) · "Where is the new teacher? — He is at the director's"

4 · الَّذِي — the relative pronoun (recognition only)

The reading passages use الَّذِي ("who/which") to attach a describing clause to a definite noun. Teach it as recognition only — the full system is Lesson 12.

Where this fits: Think of الَّذِي as a "super-adjective": a one-word نَعْت describes with a word; الَّذِي describes with a whole clause. The class only needs to recognise it here.

الَّذِي · alladhī · "who / which"

Meaning
Links a describing clause to a definite, masculine, singular noun. Where a one-word adjective gives a quality, الَّذِي gives a whole "who did / which is …" clause.
Roman Urdu
"Jo / jis" — ek poora describing-phrase jodta hai definite (mard) ism ke saath. Sirf pehchaan — banana abhi nahin.
Rule
Recognition only. Read it as "who/which"; don't make students build clauses. Feminine الَّتِي and the full system are Lesson 12.
Example
الْكِتَابُ الَّذِي عَلَى الْمَكْتَبِ لِلْمُدَرِّسِ al-kitābu lladhī ʿala l-maktabi li-l-mudarris(i) · "The book which is on the desk is the teacher's"

⚠️ Recognition, not production

Don't open the relative-clause system here. If a classmate asks "how do I make a 'who' sentence?", answer: "Lesson 12 — abhi sirf pehchaan lo ke الَّذِي ka matlab 'jo/jis' hai."

⚠️ The فَعْلَان state-adjectives are diptote — no tanween

جُوعَانُ، عَطْشَانُ، غَضْبَانُ، كَسْلَانُ، مَلْآنُ take no tanween (a single ـُ, not ـٌ) — like the diptote names زَيْنَب/خَدِيجَة. Teach them as useful "state" words ("I'm hungry / thirsty / angry"); if asked about the missing tanween, say "khaas qism ki sifat hai" and don't open the diptote rule (Lesson 14+).

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Putting the adjective before the noun

English/Urdu order leaks in: جَدِيدٌ بَيْتٌ ✗. Arabic is noun-first: بَيْتٌ جَدِيدٌ ✓. Drill noun-then-adjective, like the iḍāfa flip in Ch 4.

2. Confusing الْمُدَرِّسُ جَدِيدٌ and الْمُدَرِّسُ الْجَدِيدُ (the big one)

With الـ on the adjective it's a phrase ("the new teacher"); without, it's a sentence ("the teacher is new"). Use the Ch 8 "open switch" image. Matched الـ = description; mismatched الـ = statement.

3. Forgetting the gender match on the adjective

لُغَةٌ سَهْلٌ ✗ → لُغَةٌ سَهْلَةٌ ✓. The adjective copies the noun's ة. This is Ch 6's rule, now inside النَّعْت.

4. Hunting for a word for "am/are"

أَنَا طَالِبٌ = "I am a student" with no copula. Same nominal-sentence rule as هُوَ طَالِبٌ (Ch 5) and هَذَا بَيْتٌ (Ch 1).

5. Trying to build الَّذِي clauses (not yet!)

الَّذِي is recognition-only here. If asked to produce relative clauses, defer: "Lesson 12." And don't introduce the feminine الَّتِي yet.

📿 Connect to the Qur'an

The adjective (النَّعْت) is everywhere in the Qur'an. Two clean examples — one definite, one indefinite — that the class can now parse.

Surah Al-Fātiḥah · 1:6 — a definite النَّعْت they recite daily

اهْدِنَا الصِّرَاطَ الْمُسْتَقِيمَ

ihdina ṣ-ṣirāṭa l-mustaqīm — "Guide us to the straight path."

Students recognise:

  • الصِّرَاطَ الْمُسْتَقِيمَ — a perfect النَّعْت: الصِّرَاط (the path — مَنْعُوت) + الْمُسْتَقِيم (the straight — نَعْت). Both carry الـ → matched definiteness → a description ("the straight path"), exactly today's rule.

Honest note (for you): both words end in fatḥa here (they're the object of "guide") — a case-ending the class hasn't formally met. Don't dwell on the ending; point only at the الـ-on-both, which is the النَّعْت signal. They recite this 17 times a day — a powerful payoff.

Surah Yūsuf · 12:2 — an indefinite النَّعْت (and it's about Arabic!)

إِنَّا أَنزَلْنَاهُ قُرْآنًا عَرَبِيًّا

innā anzalnāhu qurʾānan ʿarabiyyā — "Indeed We sent it down as an Arabic Qur'an."

Students recognise: قُرْآنًا عَرَبِيًّا — قُرْآنًا (a Qur'an — مَنْعُوت) + عَرَبِيًّا (Arabic — نَعْت). Neither has الـ → matched indefiniteness → a description ("an Arabic Qur'an"). The mirror image of the Fātiḥah example, and it ties straight to the lesson's own الْعَرَبِيَّةُ لُغَةٌ سَهْلَةٌ.
