How Ch 7 connects back

Chapter 7 is the keystone of the demonstrative arch built over six chapters:

  • The demonstrative grid completes. Ch 1's هَذَا, Ch 2's ذَلِكَ, Ch 3/6's هَذِهِ — and now Ch 7's تِلْكَ fills the last empty corner (far + feminine). No gaps remain.
  • The feminine system carries over unchanged. Ch 6's agreement (تِلْكَ مُمَرِّضَةٌ, تِلْكَ قَصِيرَةٌ) runs identically — تِلْكَ is just a pointer; it changes nothing behind it.
  • Iḍāfa and prepositions run with تِلْكَ. Ch 4's iḍāfa (تِلْكَ سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ) and Ch 3's مِنْ + kasra (تِلْكَ مِنَ الْيَابَانِ) — all review, now with the new word.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Use تِلْكَ ("that") for a far feminine thing or person (تِلْكَ فَاطِمَةُ, تِلْكَ بَطَّةٌ).
  2. Place all four singular demonstratives correctly by deciding two things — near/far and masculine/feminine.
  3. Contrast ذَلِكَ ↔ تِلْكَ (same distance, gender flips) and هَذِهِ ↔ تِلْكَ (same gender, distance flips).
  4. Ask and answer with تِلْكَ: مَنْ تِلْكَ ؟, مَا تِلْكَ ؟, أَتِلْكَ ... ؟.
  5. Keep the Ch 6 agreement and Ch 4 iḍāfa rules when the subject is تِلْكَ.
  6. Pronounce تِلْكَ correctly (til-ka, plain ت) and read the grid table.
  7. Use the new vocabulary: مُمَرِّضَة، بَطَّة، بَيْضَة، مُؤَذِّن، نَاقَة، حَدِيقَة (+ طَوِيلَة / قَصِيرَة).

1 · تِلْكَ — the far feminine "that"

The chapter's one new word. تِلْكَ (tilka) points at a far feminine thing — the partner of ذَلِكَ (far masculine) and the far-twin of هَذِهِ (near feminine).

Where this fits: The class already has three pointers — هَذَا (near masc, Ch 1), ذَلِكَ (far masc, Ch 2), هَذِهِ (near fem, Ch 3/6). There was one obvious gap: a far feminine pointer. تِلْكَ fills it.

تِلْكَ · tilka · "that" (far, feminine)

Meaning
The demonstrative for a far feminine thing or person — English "that". Used exactly where هَذِهِ is used, except the thing pointed at is distant, not near.
Roman Urdu
"Woh" — magar door wali feminine cheez/aurat ke liye. هَذِهِ ki door wali behen, aur ذَلِكَ ki feminine behen.
Rule
Far + feminine → تِلْكَ. Read it til-ka (ت with kasra, ل sukun, ك fatḥa). Shares the ك ending with ذَلِكَ — both far-words end in ك.
Example (from the lesson)
مَنْ هَذِهِ ؟ — هَذِهِ آمِنَةُ . وَمَنْ تِلْكَ ؟ — تِلْكَ فَاطِمَةُ man hādhihi? — hādhihi Āminatu. wa-man tilka? — tilka Fāṭimatu · "Who is this? — This is Aamina. And who is that? — That is Faatima"

The two far-pointers, side by side

Masculine far
ذَلِكَ dhaalika — starts with ذ (dh) · Ch 2
Feminine far
تِلْكَ tilka — starts with ت (t) · Ch 7
Hook
dh for the masculine one, t for the feminine one — and both end in the ك that marks "far".
Roman Urdu
Mard = ذَلِكَ (dh se), aurat = تِلْكَ (t se). Dono door-wale lafz ك par khatam.

💡 Teaching tip — let them feel the gap first

Before you give the word, point at near/far and masc/fem objects and ask for the pointer. They'll have three — and stall on the far female. Let them feel the hole, then fill it: "تِلْكَ". A word that answers a question they already have sticks far better than a word handed to them cold.

⚠️ It's til-ka, not dhilka

Primed by هَذَا/هَذِهِ/ذَلِكَ (all with ذ), classmates may mis-read the opening of تِلْكَ as a ذ/د sound. It's a clean ت (t). Model it slowly three times: til … ka … tilka.

2 · The complete demonstrative grid — the payoff

With تِلْكَ in place, all four singular demonstratives exist. Two questions — near/far and masculine/feminine — pick the right one every time. The lesson prints this exact contrast on page 41.

Where this fits: This is the emotional centre of the chapter. The grid had a hole for six lessons; now it's symmetrical — every "this" has its "that", every masculine its feminine. Symmetry is easy to drill.

The two questions that pick the word

Question 1
Near or far? Near → the هـ words (هَذَا / هَذِهِ). Far → the ك words (ذَلِكَ / تِلْكَ).
Question 2
Masculine or feminine? Look at the noun (does it end in ة, or is it a Ch 6 ة-less feminine?). Masc → هَذَا / ذَلِكَ. Fem → هَذِهِ / تِلْكَ.
Rule
Two independent choices → exactly one of four words. No gaps, no overlaps.
Roman Urdu
Do alag sawaal → char mein se ek lafz. (1) paas/door? (2) mard/aurat? Bas.

💡 Teaching tip — draw the empty 2×2 and fill it live

Draw the grid empty on the board. Fill three cells with what they know (هَذَا, ذَلِكَ, هَذِهِ), leave the far-feminine cell blank, then drop in تِلْكَ with a flourish. The visual "last piece" lands the keystone feeling — the system is now whole.

3 · تِلْكَ in action — questions & the review machinery

Because the grammar is one word, the lesson spends its energy using تِلْكَ inside everything already learned — questions, feminine agreement, iḍāfa, prepositions. None of it is new; all of it is re-drilled with the new pointer.

Where this fits: A consolidation block. The single most useful contrast for class is ذَلِكَ ... وَتِلْكَ ... (same distance, gender flips) — it isolates the one decision the chapter is really teaching.

Questions with تِلْكَ

Who? (far female)
مَنْ تِلْكَ ؟ — تِلْكَ فَاطِمَةُ man tilka? — tilka Fāṭimatu · "Who is that? — That is Faatima"
What? (far fem thing)
مَا تِلْكَ ؟ — تِلْكَ بَيْضَةٌ mā tilka? — tilka bayḍatun · "What is that? — That is an egg"
Is that …? (أَ + تِلْكَ)
أَتِلْكَ دَجَاجَةٌ ؟ — لَا، تِلْكَ بَطَّةٌ a-tilka dajājatun? — lā, tilka baṭṭatun · "Is that a hen? — No, that is a duck"
Roman Urdu
Sare sawaal purane — bas تِلْكَ lag gaya door-feminine ke liye: مَنْ تِلْكَ؟ (woh kaun?), مَا تِلْكَ؟ (woh kya?), أَتِلْكَ...؟ (kya woh...?).

The review machinery runs with تِلْكَ

Agreement (Ch 6)
هَذِهِ طَبِيبَةٌ وَتِلْكَ مُمَرِّضَةٌ · هَذِهِ طَوِيلَةٌ وَتِلْكَ قَصِيرَةٌ "This is a doctor and that is a nurse · this is tall and that is short" — the ة flows through under تِلْكَ too
Iḍāfa (Ch 4)
هَذِهِ سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدَرِّسِ وَتِلْكَ سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ "This is the teacher's car and that is the director's car" — first noun bare, kasra on the second
مِنْ + kasra (Ch 3)
هَذِهِ مِنَ الْهِنْدِ وَتِلْكَ مِنَ الْيَابَانِ "This (woman) is from India and that is from Japan" — the مِنَ الْـ bridge again
Best class contrast
ذَلِكَ دِيكٌ وَتِلْكَ دَجَاجَةٌ dhālika dīkun wa-tilka dajājatun · "That is a rooster and that is a hen" — same distance, only gender flips

💡 Teaching tip — drill the two axes separately

Don't mix them at first. Gender axis (fix distance): ذَلِكَ دِيكٌ / تِلْكَ دَجَاجَةٌ, ذَلِكَ جَمَلٌ / تِلْكَ نَاقَةٌ — only ذَلِكَ vs تِلْكَ changes. Then the distance axis (fix gender): هَذِهِ طَبِيبَةٌ / تِلْكَ مُمَرِّضَةٌ — only near vs far changes. Isolating one decision at a time is what makes a light chapter land cleanly.

⚠️ Nothing new lives behind تِلْكَ

After Ch 6's busy feminine lesson, classmates may brace for تِلْكَ to carry its own pile of rules. It doesn't. Say it openly: "Aaj sirf ek lafz — تِلْكَ. Baqi sab pehle se aata hai." Then prove it by swapping a far one into Ch 6 sentences they already built.

⚠️ زَيْنَب is a diptote — no tanween

The name زَيْنَبُ takes no tanween (a single ـُ, not ـٌ) — same as خَدِيجَةُ back in Ch 5. Handle it in passing: "it's a special name that doesn't take tanween." Don't open the diptote (غَيْر مُنْصَرِف) rule — that's Lesson 14+.

💡 The animal pairs are gender-teaching gold

The lesson hands you ready-made masc/fem animal pairs at a fixed distance: دِيك (rooster) / دَجَاجَة (hen), جَمَل (camel) / نَاقَة (she-camel). Use them for the ذَلِكَ ↔ تِلْكَ drill — the meaning makes the gender obvious, so the only thing to decide is the pointer.

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Using ذَلِكَ for a far feminine thing (the big one)

Urdu has one "woh" for both genders, so classmates reach for ذَلِكَ (met first) even for a far female: ذَلِكَ دَجَاجَةٌ. Wrong — a far feminine is تِلْكَ دَجَاجَةٌ. Drill the gender axis at fixed distance until ذَلِكَ↔تِلْكَ is automatic.

2. Using هَذِهِ for a far feminine thing

After a whole Ch 6 of هَذِهِ, classmates keep saying it for far things too: هَذِهِ نَافِذَةٌ for a window across the room. The distance is wrong — far = تِلْكَ نَافِذَةٌ. Physicalise near/far (hold close vs point across the room).

3. Mis-reading تِلْكَ as dhilka / dilka

The opening letter is a plain ت (t), not ذ. It's til-ka. Model slowly; use the first-letter hook (ذ=masc, ت=fem).

4. Dropping the Ch 6 agreement under تِلْكَ

Classmates focused on the new pointer forget the ة on the predicate: تِلْكَ مُمَرِّضٌ. The agreement still holds — تِلْكَ points at a female, so تِلْكَ مُمَرِّضَةٌ. Nothing new behind تِلْكَ; the old rule still runs.

5. Reaching for plural "those" (not yet!)

Once the singular grid is complete, classmates will ask how to point at a group ("those people"). That's هَؤُلَاءِ / أُولَٰئِكَ — Lesson 13. Don't pre-teach it. Say: "Plural pointers baad mein — Lesson 13."

📿 Connect to the Qur'an

The word تِلْكَ opens several surahs and famous verses. Use one in class as a payoff — but read the honest note on how the Qur'an uses it.

Surah Yusuf · 12:1 — تِلْكَ opens the surah

الٓر ۚ تِلْكَ آيَاتُ الْكِتَابِ الْمُبِينِ

alif-lām-rā · tilka āyātu l-kitābi l-mubīn — "Alif Lām Rā. These are the verses of the clear Book."

Students recognise:

  • تِلْكَ — today's new word, right at the start of a surah.
  • آيَاتُ الْكِتَابِ — an iḍāfa (Ch 4): "the verses of the Book" — first noun bare, second in kasra. They can fully parse it.

Honest note (for you): here تِلْكَ points at a whole group of verses (a plural). In Arabic, a plural of non-rational things is treated as feminine-singular, so تِلْكَ ("those/these") is used. The class learns تِلْكَ as singular far-feminine; this "group" use is a preview of the plural treatment in Lesson 13. Frame it as: "you can read تِلْكَ and the iḍāfa right now — the 'group' meaning you'll meet fully later."

Surah Al-Baqarah · 2:229 — تِلْكَ حُدُودُ اللَّهِ

تِلْكَ حُدُودُ اللَّهِ فَلَا تَعْتَدُوهَا

tilka ḥudūdu llāhi fa-lā taʿtadūhā — "These are the limits of Allah, so do not transgress them."

Students recognise: تِلْكَ (today) + حُدُودُ اللَّهِ — another iḍāfa ("the limits of Allah"), اللَّه in kasra. Same honest note: تِلْكَ here points at the set of limits (a group). A second clean example of the new word in the Qur'an.
