# Part A · Understanding the chapter

Chapter 7 is the smallest chapter so far — and that's by design. It adds **one** word: `تِلْكَ` (*tilka*), the feminine version of `ذَلِكَ`. Where `ذَلِكَ` points at a **far masculine** thing ("that man over there"), `تِلْكَ` points at a **far feminine** thing ("that woman over there", "that car over there").

That's the whole grammar of the lesson. Everything else — feminine agreement (`طَبِيبَة`, `طَوِيلَة`), iḍāfa (`سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ`), prepositions (`مِنَ الْهِنْدِ`), the questions `مَنْ` / `مَا` — is **review from Chapters 1–6**, now running with `تِلْكَ` in the mix. The textbook deliberately recycles the Chapter 6 feminine machinery so that the only *new* thing your classmates have to hold is the single word `تِلْكَ`.

In one sentence: **Chapter 7 completes the four-cell demonstrative grid by adding `تِلْكَ` (far feminine "that") — the partner of `ذَلِكَ`, the far-twin of `هَذِهِ` — and then drills the near↔far and masculine↔feminine contrasts until the whole pointing system is automatic.**

Here is what's actually in the lesson:

1. **`تِلْكَ` (*tilka*) — "that" (far, feminine).** The one new word. Used exactly where `هَذِهِ` would be used, except the thing is *far* instead of *near*: `هَذِهِ آمِنَةُ` (this is Aamina, near) vs `تِلْكَ فَاطِمَةُ` (that is Faatima, far).
2. **The complete demonstrative grid.** With `تِلْكَ` in place, all four singular pointing-words exist: near/far × masculine/feminine. The lesson itself prints this as a table (p. 41) — *near* (`هَذَا` / `هَذِهِ`) vs *far* (`ذَلِكَ` / `تِلْكَ`). This is the chapter's payoff.
3. **The contrasts.** The whole lesson is built on side-by-side pairs: `هَذِهِ ... وَتِلْكَ ...` (near-fem vs far-fem) and `ذَلِكَ ... وَتِلْكَ ...` (far-masc vs far-fem). Questions too: `مَنْ تِلْكَ؟` (who is that woman?), `مَا تِلْكَ؟` (what is that thing?), `أَتِلْكَ ... ؟` (is that ... ?).

Plus a small batch of new vocabulary — a nurse (`مُمَرِّضَة`), a duck (`بَطَّة`), an egg (`بَيْضَة`), a muezzin (`مُؤَذِّن`), a she-camel (`نَاقَة`), and the garden (`حَدِيقَة`, review from Ch 3) — most of them paired with animals and people the contrasts use.

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter 7 sirf **ek lafz** sikhaata hai — `تِلْكَ` (*tilka*) = "woh" (door wali **feminine** cheez). Jaise `ذَلِكَ` mard ke liye door se "woh", waise `تِلْكَ` aurat/feminine cheez ke liye door se "woh". Baqi sab — agreement, iḍāfa, sawaal — purana hai, bas `تِلْكَ` ke saath chal raha hai. Asli kamaal: ab pointing ka poora **char-khaana grid** mukammal ho gaya (`هَذَا`/`ذَلِكَ`/`هَذِهِ`/`تِلْكَ`).

## 1 · `تِلْكَ` — the far feminine pointer

You already know three pointing words, taught one per chapter:

- `هَذَا` (*haadhaa*) — "this", **near masculine** (Ch 1)
- `ذَلِكَ` (*dhaalika*) — "that", **far masculine** (Ch 2)
- `هَذِهِ` (*haadhihi*) — "this", **near feminine** (Ch 3, then the whole feminine system in Ch 6)

There is an obvious gap: a *far feminine* pointer. That gap is exactly what `تِلْكَ` fills.

> `مَنْ هَذِهِ ؟ — هَذِهِ آمِنَةُ . وَمَنْ تِلْكَ ؟ — تِلْكَ فَاطِمَةُ .`
> *"Who is this? — This is Aamina. And who is that? — That is Faatima."*

`آمِنَة` is **near** (so `هَذِهِ`); `فَاطِمَة` is **far** (so `تِلْكَ`). Same as the masculine pair the class already knows: `هَذَا حَامِدٌ` (this is Hamid, near) vs `ذَلِكَ عَلِيٌّ` (that is Ali, far).

### How `تِلْكَ` is built and pronounced

- **Spelling:** `تِلْكَ` = `ت` (with kasra, *ti-*) + `ل` (with sukun, *l*) + `ك` (with fatḥa, *ka*). Read it *til-ka*.
- It has the **same `لـكَ` ending as `ذَلِكَ`** (*dhaa-li-ka* / *til-ka*) — that shared `ـلِكَ`/`ـلْكَ` "tail" is a useful hook: both far-pointers end in that **`ك`** sound. (`هَذَا` and `هَذِهِ`, the *near* pair, do **not** have it.)
- Like `ذَلِكَ`, `تِلْكَ` carries a **silent dagger-alif** in fully-vowelled texts (you may see it written `تِلْكَ` with a small alif over the `ل`). Your classmates met this same silent-alif convention in `هَذَا`, `ذَلِكَ`, `هَذِهِ` — it is not a new spelling idea, just the same one again.

### The simplest way to hold it

Think of a 2×2 box. One axis is **distance** (near vs far); the other is **gender** (masculine vs feminine). `تِلْكَ` is the **far + feminine** corner — and it was the only empty corner left.

**Yaad rakho:** `تِلْكَ` = `هَذِهِ` ki **door wali** behen, aur `ذَلِكَ` ki **feminine** behen. Bolne mein *til-ka* (ت par zer, ل saakin, ك par zabar). Hook: dono door-wale lafz (`ذَلِكَ`, `تِلْكَ`) `ك` par khatam hote hain; dono nazdeek-wale (`هَذَا`, `هَذِهِ`) `هـ` se shuru. `تِلْكَ` = 2×2 box ka aakhri khaali khaana — door + feminine.

## 2 · The complete demonstrative grid — the payoff

This is the moment the whole pointing system clicks shut. After this lesson there are exactly **four** singular demonstratives, and every one of them has a clear job:

| | **Masculine (مُذَكَّر)** | **Feminine (مُؤَنَّث)** |
|---|---|---|
| **Near (قَرِيب) — "this"** | `هَذَا` *haadhaa* (Ch 1) | `هَذِهِ` *haadhihi* (Ch 3/6) |
| **Far (بَعِيد) — "that"** | `ذَلِكَ` *dhaalika* (Ch 2) | `تِلْكَ` *tilka* (Ch 7) |

The textbook prints this exact contrast on page 41 as two columns — *the near demonstratives* (`أَسْمَاءُ الإِشَارَةِ لِلْقَرِيبِ`) against *the far demonstratives* (`أَسْمَاءُ الإِشَارَةِ لِلْبَعِيدِ`):

> القَرِيب: `هَذَا مُحَمَّدٌ` · `هَذِهِ آمِنَةُ`
> البَعِيد: `ذَلِكَ حَامِدٌ` · `تِلْكَ زَيْنَبُ`

Two questions decide which word you use:

1. **Near or far?** Near → the `هـ` words (`هَذَا` / `هَذِهِ`). Far → the `ك` words (`ذَلِكَ` / `تِلْكَ`).
2. **Masculine or feminine?** Look at the noun (does it end in `ة`, or is it one of the `ة`-less feminines from Ch 6?). Masculine → `هَذَا` / `ذَلِكَ`. Feminine → `هَذِهِ` / `تِلْكَ`.

That's it. The same two questions your classmates have been answering since Chapter 3 — only now there's a clean four-cell answer instead of three.

### Why this matters more than "one new word"

Up to now the grid had a hole in it, and a hole is hard to teach around. Now the system is **symmetrical**: every "this" has a matching "that", and every masculine has a matching feminine. Symmetry is easy to memorise and easy to drill. The chapter *feels* tiny, but it's the keystone that locks the demonstrative arch.

**Yaad rakho:** Ab pointing ka poora grid taiyaar — char lafz, do sawaal. (1) **Nazdeek ya door?** Nazdeek → `هـ` wale (`هَذَا`/`هَذِهِ`); door → `ك` wale (`ذَلِكَ`/`تِلْكَ`). (2) **Mard ya aurat?** Noun dekho (`ة` hai? Ch 6 wale `ة`-less feminine hain?). Mard → `هَذَا`/`ذَلِكَ`; aurat → `هَذِهِ`/`تِلْكَ`. Textbook khud p.41 par yeh do-column table chhaapti hai (قريب vs بعيد). Yeh chapter chhota lagta hai par yahi grid ko mukammal karta hai.

## 3 · `تِلْكَ` in action — questions and the review machinery

Because the grammar is one word, the lesson spends its energy *using* `تِلْكَ` inside everything the class already knows. Three things to notice — none of them new, all of them re-drilled with the new word.

### (a) Questions with `تِلْكَ`

- `مَنْ تِلْكَ ؟` — *"Who is that?"* (for a **far female person**). Parallel to `مَنْ هَذِهِ؟` (near) and `مَنْ ذَلِكَ؟` (far male).
- `مَا تِلْكَ ؟ — تِلْكَ بَيْضَةٌ .` — *"What is that? — That is an egg."* (for a **far feminine thing**).
- `أَتِلْكَ دَجَاجَةٌ ؟ — لَا، تِلْكَ بَطَّةٌ .` — *"Is that a hen? — No, that is a duck."* (the Ch 1 question-hamza `أَ` glued to `تِلْكَ`).

### (b) Feminine agreement runs with `تِلْكَ`

The Chapter 6 rule — feminine subject takes a feminine predicate — works identically whether the subject is `هَذِهِ` or `تِلْكَ`:

> `هَذِهِ طَبِيبَةٌ وَتِلْكَ مُمَرِّضَةٌ .`
> *"This (woman) is a doctor and that (woman) is a nurse."*

> `هَذِهِ طَوِيلَةٌ وَتِلْكَ قَصِيرَةٌ .`
> *"This (one) is tall and that (one) is short."*

Both `مُمَرِّضَة` and `قَصِيرَة` carry the `ة` because the subject (`تِلْكَ`, pointing at a female) is feminine — exactly Ch 6's "the `ة` flows through" rule.

### (c) Iḍāfa and prepositions run with `تِلْكَ`

> `هَذِهِ سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدَرِّسِ وَتِلْكَ سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ .`
> *"This is the teacher's car and that is the director's car."* (two feminine iḍāfas, Ch 4 rules intact)

> `هَذِهِ مِنَ الْهِنْدِ وَتِلْكَ مِنَ الْيَابَانِ .`
> *"This (woman) is from India and that (woman) is from Japan."* (Ch 3 `مِنْ` + kasra, plus the `مِنَ الْـ` bridge)

The lesson's most useful contrast for class is the **far-masculine ↔ far-feminine** pair, because it isolates the *gender* axis at a fixed distance:

> `ذَلِكَ دِيكٌ وَتِلْكَ دَجَاجَةٌ .`
> *"That is a rooster and that is a hen."* — `ذَلِكَ` (masc far) vs `تِلْكَ` (fem far), same distance, only gender differs.

**Yaad rakho:** `تِلْكَ` ka istemaal sab purani cheezon ke saath: sawaal (`مَنْ تِلْكَ؟`, `مَا تِلْكَ؟`, `أَتِلْكَ...؟`), agreement (`تِلْكَ مُمَرِّضَةٌ`, `تِلْكَ قَصِيرَةٌ` — `ة` har jagah), iḍāfa (`تِلْكَ سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ`), preposition (`تِلْكَ مِنَ الْيَابَانِ`). Class ke liye sabse achha contrast: `ذَلِكَ دِيكٌ وَتِلْكَ دَجَاجَةٌ` — distance same, sirf gender badla, to `ذَلِكَ` ↔ `تِلْكَ` saaf dikh jaata hai.

## Vocabulary spotlight — the new words

Chapter 7's new-words box (p. 41) is short — six words — and most arrive paired with the animals and people the contrasts use.

**The new-words box (`الكَلِمَاتُ الجَدِيدَة`):**
- `مُمَرِّضَة` (*mumarriḍah*) — nurse (f) — the contrast partner of `طَبِيبَة` (doctor)
- `بَطَّة` (*baṭṭah*) — duck (f)
- `بَيْضَة` (*bayḍah*) — egg (f)
- `مُؤَذِّن` (*mu'adhdhin*) — muezzin, the one who calls the adhān (m)
- `نَاقَة` (*nāqah*) — she-camel (f) — the feminine partner of `جَمَل` (camel)
- `حَدِيقَة` (*ḥadīqah*) — garden (f) — **review from Ch 3**, re-listed here

**Other words the lesson uses (mostly review, some easy new):**
- *Adjective pair (new):* `طَوِيلَة` (tall, f) / `قَصِيرَة` (short, f) — and their masculine `طَوِيل` / `قَصِير`.
- *Animals in the contrasts:* `دِيك` (rooster, m), `دَجَاجَة` (hen, f), `حِمَار` (donkey, m), `جَمَل` (camel, m), `بَقَرَة` (cow, f — Ch 6), `كَلْب` (dog, m), `قِطّ` (cat, m).
- *Country names (used, not the lesson's focus):* `الْهِنْد` (India), `الْيَابَان` (Japan). *Note: country names get their proper treatment in Lesson 8 — here they're just things to point at.*
- *Feminine names (rose):* `آمِنَة` (Aamina), `فَاطِمَة` (Faatima), `زَيْنَب` (Zaynab).

> **⚠️ Watch-out for you (not class):** `زَيْنَب` is *ghayr munṣarif* (a diptote) — it takes **no tanween** (`زَيْنَبُ`, not `زَيْنَبٌ`). You saw the same thing with `خَدِيجَة` in Ch 5. Handle it in passing — "it's a special name that doesn't take tanween" — and **don't open the diptote rule**. That whole system is later (Lesson 14+).

**Yaad rakho:** Naye lafz sirf chhe (box mein): `مُمَرِّضَة` (nurse), `بَطَّة` (batakh), `بَيْضَة` (anda), `مُؤَذِّن` (azaan dene wala), `نَاقَة` (oontni), `حَدِيقَة` (baagh — Ch 3 se review). In ke ilaawa do naye adjective `طَوِيلَة`/`قَصِيرَة` (lambi/chhoti), aur kuch jaanwar (`دِيك` murgha, `دَجَاجَة` murghi, `حِمَار` gadha, `جَمَل` oont) jo contrasts mein aate hain. `زَيْنَب` diptote hai (tanween nahin) — passing mein, rule mat kholo.

## The big picture — what Chapter 7 silently does

Step back. Chapters 1–6 built the nominal sentence, the iḍāfa, and the feminine system. Chapter 7 adds a single word — but that word **closes a system**.

1. **The demonstrative grid is now complete.** Four words, two axes, no gaps. Every "this" has its "that"; every masculine has its feminine. From here, pointing is a *solved problem* (until plurals — `هَؤُلَاءِ` / `أُولَٰئِكَ` — arrive in Lesson 13).
2. **It is a consolidation chapter wearing the disguise of a new-word chapter.** The real work is re-firing Ch 6's agreement, Ch 4's iḍāfa, and Ch 3's prepositions — all with `تِلْكَ` threaded through. If those earlier rules are shaky, this lesson quietly reveals it.
3. **The near↔far contrast becomes teachable as a clean pair.** Before, you could only contrast `هَذَا`↔`ذَلِكَ` (masculine). Now you can also contrast `هَذِهِ`↔`تِلْكَ` (feminine) — and the lesson's dialogue does exactly that, sentence after sentence.

The chapter feels light because it *is* light — one word. But it's the word that makes the pointing system whole.

**Yaad rakho:** Ek lafz — par poora system band ho jaata hai. Pointing ka grid mukammal (char lafz, koi khaali khaana nahin) — agle plural pointers (`هَؤُلَاء`/`أُولَٰئِك`) Lesson 13 mein. Yeh asal mein **consolidation** chapter hai naye-lafz ke bhes mein: Ch 6 agreement + Ch 4 iḍāfa + Ch 3 preposition sab `تِلْكَ` ke saath dohraaye jaate hain. Agar woh purane rule kamzor hain to yeh chapter zaahir kar dega.

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

Now you understand what the chapter is doing. This section is how to *deliver* it to your classmates. Because it's a light chapter, the class is mostly **drilling and contrast**, not new theory.

---

## The landmines for Urdu-speaking adult learners

### Landmine 1 · Urdu's single "woh" (the big one)

This is the heart of the difficulty. Urdu uses **one** word — *woh* — for "that", regardless of gender, and *yeh* for "this". Arabic splits each of these by gender. So where Urdu says *woh* for both a far man and a far woman, Arabic insists on `ذَلِكَ` (man) vs `تِلْكَ` (woman). Classmates will reach for one far-word for everything — usually `ذَلِكَ`, because they met it first.

**Counter-strategy:** Drill the **gender axis at a fixed distance**. Put `ذَلِكَ` and `تِلْكَ` side by side and only switch the noun's gender: `ذَلِكَ دِيكٌ` (rooster) → `تِلْكَ دَجَاجَةٌ` (hen); `ذَلِكَ جَمَلٌ` (camel) → `تِلْكَ نَاقَةٌ` (she-camel); `ذَلِكَ مُؤَذِّنٌ` → `تِلْكَ مُمَرِّضَةٌ`. Same distance, only gender flips — so the *only* thing they have to decide is `ذَلِكَ` vs `تِلْكَ`.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Urdu mein 'woh' ek hi lafz hai — mard ho ya aurat, 'woh' hi kehte hain. Arabic mein aisa nahin. Door wala mard = `ذَلِكَ`, door wali aurat/feminine cheez = `تِلْكَ`. Dekho: `ذَلِكَ دِيكٌ` (woh murgha) magar `تِلْكَ دَجَاجَةٌ` (woh murghi). Donon 'woh' hain Urdu mein — par Arabic mein alag. Noun ki gender dekho, phir `ذَلِكَ` ya `تِلْكَ` chuno."*

### Landmine 2 · Near vs far — `هَذِهِ` vs `تِلْكَ`

The other axis. Both `هَذِهِ` and `تِلْكَ` are feminine — the difference is *distance*. Classmates who have drilled `هَذِهِ` all through Chapter 6 may keep saying `هَذِهِ` for far things too, simply out of habit.

**Counter-strategy:** Physicalise distance. Hold an object **close** — `هَذِهِ سَاعَةٌ`; point at something **across the room** — `تِلْكَ نَافِذَةٌ`. Walk it: near = `هَذِهِ`, far = `تِلْكَ`. Use the lesson's own near↔far feminine pairs: `هَذِهِ طَبِيبَةٌ وَتِلْكَ مُمَرِّضَةٌ`, `هَذِهِ طَوِيلَةٌ وَتِلْكَ قَصِيرَةٌ`.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Dono feminine hain — `هَذِهِ` aur `تِلْكَ`. Farq sirf doori ka hai. Paas wali cheez = `هَذِهِ` (yeh). Door wali cheez = `تِلْكَ` (woh). Haath mein ghadi ho to `هَذِهِ سَاعَةٌ`; deewaar par door khidki ho to `تِلْكَ نَافِذَةٌ`. Gender to dono mein feminine — bas yeh socho: paas ya door?"*

### Landmine 3 · Reading `تِلْكَ` correctly (the `ت`, not `ث`/`د`)

A small but real reading slip: `تِلْكَ` starts with plain `ت` (*t*). Some classmates, primed by `هَذَا`/`هَذِهِ`/`ذَلِكَ` which contain `ذ` (*dh*), will mis-read the opening as a `ذ`/`د` sound (*dilka* / *dhilka*). It is *til-ka*, clean `t`.

**Counter-strategy:** Model it slowly three times — *til … ka … tilka* — and have them mirror. Contrast the two far-words by their **first letter**: `ذَلِكَ` starts with `ذ` (*dh*, masculine), `تِلْكَ` starts with `ت` (*t*, feminine). "*dh* for the masculine one, *t* for the feminine one" is a clean auditory hook.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`تِلْكَ` ka pehla harf saaf `ت` hai — *til-ka*. `ذ` (*dh*) nahin. Yaad rakhne ka tareeqa: door wala **mard** `ذَلِكَ` (*dh* se), door wali **aurat** `تِلْكَ` (*t* se). Mard = *dh*, aurat = *t*."*

### Landmine 4 · "Is there a whole new feminine system to learn?" (there isn't)

After Chapter 6's busy feminine lesson, some classmates will brace for `تِلْكَ` to bring its own pile of rules. It doesn't. The agreement, the `ة`, the iḍāfa, the body-part feminines — all of that is **Chapter 6, already learned**. `تِلْكَ` is just a pointer; it sits in front and changes nothing behind it.

**Counter-strategy:** Say it out loud at the start of class: *"Aaj sirf ek lafz — `تِلْكَ`. Baqi sab aapko pehle se aata hai."* Then prove it by taking sentences they already built in Ch 6 with `هَذِهِ` and simply swapping a far one to `تِلْكَ`. Relief is a good teaching mood.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Ghabrao mat — aaj naya sirf ek lafz hai: `تِلْكَ`. Agreement, `ة`, iḍāfa — yeh sab Chapter 6 mein seekh chuke ho, kuch naya nahin. `تِلْكَ` bas 'woh' (feminine, door) kehne ka tareeqa hai — uske peeche ka jumla wahi purana."*

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–6 min | Greetings · recap the three known pointers (`هَذَا`/`ذَلِكَ`/`هَذِهِ`) by pointing at near/far, masc/fem objects. Ask: "what's missing?" — let them feel the gap. |
| 6–16 min | **Introduce `تِلْكَ`** — the far feminine. Fill the last cell of the grid on the board. Pronounce *til-ka* slowly; the `ت` (not `ذ`). Compare the two far-words by first letter (`ذ`=masc, `ت`=fem). |
| 16–28 min | **The gender axis (fixed distance).** `ذَلِكَ` ↔ `تِلْكَ` drills: `ذَلِكَ دِيكٌ / تِلْكَ دَجَاجَةٌ`, `ذَلِكَ جَمَلٌ / تِلْكَ نَاقَةٌ`, `ذَلِكَ مُؤَذِّنٌ / تِلْكَ مُمَرِّضَةٌ`. Only the gender flips. |
| 28–40 min | **The distance axis (fixed gender).** `هَذِهِ` ↔ `تِلْكَ` drills: `هَذِهِ طَبِيبَةٌ وَتِلْكَ مُمَرِّضَةٌ`, `هَذِهِ طَوِيلَةٌ وَتِلْكَ قَصِيرَةٌ`. Physicalise near/far. |
| 40–50 min | **`تِلْكَ` inside the review machinery.** Questions (`مَنْ تِلْكَ؟`, `مَا تِلْكَ؟`, `أَتِلْكَ بَطَّةٌ؟`); one feminine iḍāfa (`تِلْكَ سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ`); one preposition (`تِلْكَ مِنَ الْيَابَانِ`). Stress: nothing new behind `تِلْكَ`. |
| 50–58 min | **The full grid read-aloud** (p. 41 table): `هَذَا مُحَمَّدٌ / هَذِهِ آمِنَةُ` (near) · `ذَلِكَ حَامِدٌ / تِلْكَ زَيْنَبُ` (far). New vocabulary recap. |
| 58–60 min | Wrap up · the Qur'an payoff (`تِلْكَ آيَاتُ الْكِتَابِ`) · assign exercises. |

**Yaad rakho:** Ch 7 ka pacing **halka** hai — ek hi naya lafz. Sabse zyaada waqt do contrast-axis par lagao: (1) gender axis `ذَلِكَ`↔`تِلْكَ` (distance same), (2) distance axis `هَذِهِ`↔`تِلْكَ` (gender same). Yeh do drills hi chapter ka dil hain. Theory thodi, drill zyaada.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 7

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum Chapter X mein dekhenge"*:

- ❌ **Plural demonstratives** — `هَؤُلَاءِ` ("these", people) and `أُولَٰئِكَ` ("those", people). That's **Lesson 13**. Keep pointing strictly singular.
- ❌ **The formal adjective rule** (`النَّعْت` / `مَنْعُوت`, the full four-way agreement). Still **Lesson 9**. `طَوِيلَة`/`قَصِيرَة` are taught here descriptively ("the adjective copies the `ة`"), exactly as in Ch 6.
- ❌ **`أَمَامَ` / `خَلْفَ`** (in front of / behind) and the **country-name treatment** — those belong to **Lesson 8**, the very next lesson. `الْهِنْد`/`الْيَابَان` appear here only as things to point at; don't build the location grammar.
- ❌ **Other pronouns** — `أَنَا`, `أَنْتَ`, `نَحْنُ`, `هُمْ`. Stick to `هُوَ`/`هِيَ` from Ch 5.
- ❌ **The diptote (*ghayr munṣarif*) rule** — even though `زَيْنَب` (and `خَدِيجَة` from Ch 5) sit right there with no tanween. Same hands-off policy: name them "special names", move on. Full rule is Lesson 14+.
- ❌ **Re-teaching the whole feminine system.** It's Chapter 6. Reference it, don't repeat it — `تِلْكَ` changes nothing behind it.

**Yaad rakho:** Sabse common sawaal: *"plural 'woh log' kaise kehte hain?"* (`هَؤُلَاء`/`أُولَٰئِك`) — jawab: *"Lesson 13 mein."* Doosra: *"`زَيْنَب` par tanween kyun nahin?"* — *"yeh khaas naam hai, tanween nahin leta — poora rule baad mein."* `أَمَامَ`/`خَلْفَ` aur country grammar = Lesson 8, abhi nahin. Ek waqt mein ek hi cheez.

## Self-check before teaching

Before the class, make sure:

- [ ] You can place any of the four pointers instantly given a noun + a distance (`تِلْكَ سَيَّارَةٌ` far-fem, `ذَلِكَ بَابٌ` far-masc, `هَذِهِ بِنْتٌ` near-fem, `هَذَا قَلَمٌ` near-masc). *(The grid — reflex.)*
- [ ] You can run the **gender axis** drill cleanly: `ذَلِكَ دِيكٌ / تِلْكَ دَجَاجَةٌ`, `ذَلِكَ جَمَلٌ / تِلْكَ نَاقَةٌ`. *(Masc↔fem at fixed distance.)*
- [ ] You can run the **distance axis** drill cleanly: `هَذِهِ طَبِيبَةٌ / تِلْكَ مُمَرِّضَةٌ`. *(Near↔far at fixed gender.)*
- [ ] You can ask all three `تِلْكَ` questions (`مَنْ تِلْكَ؟`, `مَا تِلْكَ؟`, `أَتِلْكَ ... ؟`) and answer them. *(Questions + `تِلْكَ`.)*
- [ ] You can pronounce `تِلْكَ` cleanly — *til-ka*, plain `ت` — and explain the `ذ`=masc / `ت`=fem first-letter hook.
- [ ] You can say in one Urdu sentence why Urdu's single *woh* doesn't map (*"Urdu mein ek 'woh', Arabic mein mard `ذَلِكَ` aur aurat `تِلْكَ`"*).
- [ ] You can answer "what about *woh log* (plural)?" with calm confidence: *"Lesson 13."*

If yes to all seven — *tum tayar ho.*

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Do they pick `تِلْكَ` (not `ذَلِكَ`) for a **far feminine** thing? *(The gender axis — the chapter's core decision.)*
2. Do they pick `تِلْكَ` (not `هَذِهِ`) for a **far** feminine thing? *(The distance axis.)*
3. Do they keep the Ch 6 agreement when the subject is `تِلْكَ` (`تِلْكَ مُمَرِّضَةٌ`, not `تِلْكَ مُمَرِّضٌ`)? *(Old rule, new pointer.)*
4. Do they keep the iḍāfa rules with `تِلْكَ` (`تِلْكَ سَيَّارَةُ الْمُدِيرِ` — first noun bare)? *(Ch 4 under the new word.)*
5. Are they reading `تِلْكَ` as *til-ka* (clean `ت`), not *dhilka*? *(Pronunciation.)*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh paanch points batayenge ke Lesson 8 (`أَمَامَ`/`خَلْفَ`) par jaana hai ya `تِلْكَ`/grid dohraana hai. Agar classmate door-feminine ke liye `ذَلِكَ` ya `هَذِهِ` keh raha hai, to grid abhi pakka nahin — ek aur drill round do. Ch 7 ka core: do axis (gender + distance) saaf, aur purani machinery `تِلْكَ` ke saath barqaraar.
