How Ch 10 connects back

Chapter 10 is where Arabic becomes personal, and it leans on several earlier chapters:

  • It adds the fifth route to definiteness. After الـ, names, iḍāfa (Ch 4), and detached pronouns (Ch 5), the attached tail makes a noun definite — which is why اسْمُكَ takes no الـ and no tanween, exactly like the first noun of an iḍāfa.
  • It extends the pronoun set from Ch 5/9. هُوَ/هِيَ/أَنَا/أَنْتَ were detached; these new ones are attached tails.
  • عِنْدَ (Ch 9) and لِـ (Ch 4) now take tails — and split the job of "have": عِنْدَ for things, لِـ for people.

⏱️ This is a HEAVY chapter — plan your pacing

Lesson 10 introduces a whole new system (attached pronouns) plus the five-nouns twist and the عِنْدَ/لِـ "have" split. It does not fit a comfortable 60 minutes. Either run a 75–90 minute session, or split it: Class 1 = the four tails on ordinary nouns until reflex; Class 2 = أَبُوكَ/أَخُوكَ + عِنْدَ/لِـ + مَعَ + reading. If Ch 4's لِـ or Ch 9's عِنْدَ is shaky, review those first.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Attach the four pronouns to a noun: اسْمِي (my name), اسْمُكَ (your name), اسْمُهُ (his name), اسْمُهَا (her name).
  2. Know a tailed noun is definite — so it takes no الـ and no tanween (بَيْتِي, not الْبَيْتِي).
  3. Use the five-nouns pattern: أَبُوكَ/أَخُوكَ (long و) but أَبِي/أَخِي for "my".
  4. Say "I have" two ways and choose correctly: عِنْدِي for things, لِي for people (عِنْدِي كِتَابٌ · لِي أَخٌ).
  5. Use مَعَ + a tail (مَعِي, مَعَكَ) and with a visible noun (مَعَ خَالِدٍ).
  6. Pick ـهُ vs ـها by the owner's gender.

1 · The four attached pronouns — ـِي / ـكَ / ـهُ / ـها (the engine)

An attached pronoun (الضَّمِير المُتَّصِل) is a tail that fuses onto the end of a word and means "my / your / his / her". The book teaches four — and even labels them (Exercise 5, p. 58): المُتَكَلِّم (my) · المُخَاطَب (your) · الغَائِب (his) · الغَائِبَة (her).

Where this fits: Ch 5/9 gave the detached pronouns (هُوَ/هِيَ/أَنَا/أَنْتَ, separate words in front). Ch 10 gives the attached ones — tails on the end — and a fifth route to definiteness.

The four tails · ـِي · ـكَ · ـهُ · ـها

Meaning
ـِي = my · ـكَ = your (to a male) · ـهُ = his · ـها = her. They glue onto the end of a noun to show who owns it.
Roman Urdu
Chipakne wali dumein: ـِي (mera), ـكَ (tera/aapka — mard), ـهُ (uska — mard), ـها (uski — aurat).
Rule
The ـِي ("my") tail pulls a kasra (اسْمِي); the other three sit on the noun's normal ḍamma (اسْمُكَ). Pick ـهُ/ـها by the owner's gender.
Example
اسْمِي مُحَمَّدٌ · اسْمُهُ حَمْزَةُ · اسْمُهَا زَيْنَبُ ismī muḥammad · ismuhu ḥamzah · ismuhā zaynab · "my name is Muhammad · his name is Hamza · her name is Zaynab"

A tail makes the noun definite — no الـ, no tanween

Meaning
"My name" is already a specific name, so the noun is definite. You never add الـ and never add tanween to a tailed noun — exactly like the first noun of an iḍāfa.
Roman Urdu
Dum khud "the" ka kaam karti hai. اسْمِي pehle se ma'rifa — isliye na الـ, na tanween. (الاسْمِي GHALAT.)
Rule
Tailed noun = definite. بَيْتِي ✓ · الْبَيْتِي ✗ · بَيْتٌي ✗. One owner-mark is enough.
Example
بَيْتِي · بَيْتُكَ · بَيْتُهُ · بَيْتُهَا baytī · baytuka · baytuhu · baytuhā · "my / your / his / her house" (no الـ on any of them)

💡 Teaching tip — drill the four tails on one noun until reflex

Pick اسْم, then بَيْت, then لُغَة, and run the four tails each time: ismī, ismuka, ismuhu, ismuhā. This regular pattern is the engine of the whole lesson — once it's automatic, عِنْدَ/لِـ/مَعَ are just the same four tails on three new words.

⚠️ ـهُ (his) vs ـها (her) — by the owner's gender

Urdu's uska is genderless, so classmates blur these. The tail follows the owner: Hamza → اسْمُهُ, Zaynab → اسْمُهَا. (Same gender-drill as هُوَ/هِيَ in Ch 5.)

2 · The five nouns — أَبُوكَ / أَخُوكَ (the long و)

Most nouns take the tail straight onto a ḍamma (بَيْتُكَ). But أَبٌ (father) and أَخٌ (brother) grow a long و before a tail (Exercise 8, p. 59). Teach it as a pattern to memorise, not a rule.

Where this fits: The class's first taste of a noun that changes its shape, not just its ending. أَبٌ/أَخٌ belong to a small club ("the five nouns") — but only the أَبُو form is shown here; don't name or derive the system.

أَبٌ / أَخٌ + a tail · the long و

Meaning
"Father" and "brother" insert a long و before the tail: أَبُوكَ (your father), أَخُوهُ (his brother). The و is their special subject-marker.
Roman Urdu
أَب aur أَخ zid'di hain — dum se pehle lamba و: أَبُوكَ (tera baap), أَخُوهُ (uska bhai).
Rule
Only "my" is different — the و disappears: أَبِي (my father), أَخِي (my brother). Normal nouns (ابْنٌ → ابْنُهُ) have no و.
Example
أَبِي فِي الْكُوَيْتِ · أَبُوهُ فِي الْيَابَانِ abī fi l-kuwayt(i) · abūhu fi l-yābān(i) · "my father is in Kuwait · his father is in Japan"

⚠️ Don't teach the "five nouns" system — just the pattern

The book only shows the أَبُو (subject) shape. The object/after-preposition forms (أَبَاكَ/أَبِيكَ) and the label الأَسْمَاء الخَمْسَة come much later. Teach the grid as a chunk to chant: abī · abūka · abūhu · abūhā.

3 · "To have" — عِنْدَ (things) vs لِـ (people)

Arabic has no verb "to have". It uses عِنْدَ or لِـ + a tail — and the book makes the choice explicit (p. 59): نَقُولُ: عِنْدِي كِتَابٌ، وَنَقُولُ: لِي أَخٌ، وَلَا نَقُولُ: عِنْدِي أَخٌ.

Where this fits: عِنْدَ arrived in Ch 9 and لِـ in Ch 4, both with visible nouns. Now they take the four tails and divide the labour of "have": objects vs relationships.

عِنْدَ + tail · "to have" (things)

Meaning
عِنْدِي = "I have", عِنْدَكَ = "you have", عِنْدَهُ = "he has" — used for objects you possess.
Roman Urdu
CHEEZ ke liye عِنْدَ + dum: عِنْدِي سَيَّارَةٌ (mere paas car hai). Negative: مَا عِنْدِي.
Rule
عِنْدِي takes kasra ("my" tail); عِنْدَكَ/عِنْدَهُ keep the fatḥa on عِنْدَ. Negate with مَا.
Example
أَعِنْدَكَ سَيَّارَةٌ ؟ — لَا، مَا عِنْدِي سَيَّارَةٌ . عِنْدِي دَرَّاجَةٌ a-ʿindaka sayyārah? — lā, mā ʿindī sayyārah. ʿindī darrājah · "Do you have a car? — No, I don't have a car. I have a bicycle."

لِـ + tail · "to have" (people / relations)

Meaning
لِي = "I have", لَكَ = "you have", لَهُ = "he has", لَهَا = "she has" — used for people you're related to.
Roman Urdu
RISHTE ke liye لِـ + dum: لِي أَخٌ (mera ek bhai hai), لَهَا طِفْلٌ (uske bachcha hai).
Rule
The book's rule (p. 59): say لِي أَخٌ ✓, never عِنْدِي أَخٌ ✗. Watch the vowel: لِي (kasra) but لَكَ/لَهُ/لَهَا (fatḥa).
Example
لِي أَخٌ وَاحِدٌ · أُخْتِي لَهَا طِفْلٌ صَغِيرٌ lī akhun wāḥid · ukhtī lahā ṭiflun ṣaghīr · "I have one brother · my sister has a small child"

⚠️ The big trap: عِنْدِي أَخٌ ✗

Classmates learn عِنْدِي first and over-use it for relatives. A brother is not a possession — use لِـ: لِي أَخٌ ✓. Put two boxes on the board — CHEEZEN (عِنْدَ) and RISHTE (لِـ) — and sort words into them live.

مَعَ ("with") is one of the new words. It takes the same four tails — and with a visible noun it's just مَعَ + the noun.

مَعَ · maʿa · "with"

Meaning
"With / in the company of". Takes the four tails (مَعِي = with me, مَعَكَ = with you …) or sits before a visible noun (مَعَ خَالِدٍ).
Roman Urdu
"Ke saath". Wahi char dumein: مَعِي (mere saath), مَعَكَ (tere saath), مَعَهُ (uske saath).
Rule
مَعِي takes kasra ("my"); مَعَكَ/مَعَهُ/مَعَهَا keep the fatḥa. With a noun, that noun takes kasra: مَعَ خَالِدٍ.
Example
مَنْ مَعَكَ يَا عَلِيُّ ؟ — مَعِي زَمِيلٌ man maʿaka yā ʿaliyy? — maʿī zamīl · "Who is with you, Ali? — A colleague is with me"

⚠️ مَعَهُ, not مَعَ هُوَ

For "with him", the pronoun attaches: مَعَهُ ✓. Don't use the detached هُوَ after it (مَعَ هُوَ ✗). Same for عِنْدَهُ, لَهُ.

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Putting الـ on a tailed noun

الاسْمِي ✗ → اسْمِي ✓. The tail already makes it definite (like the first noun of an iḍāfa). One owner-mark is enough.

2. عِنْدِي أَخٌ instead of لِي أَخٌ (the big one)

A relative is not a possession. Use لِـ for people: لِي أَخٌ ✓; use عِنْدَ for things: عِنْدِي كِتَابٌ ✓. The book itself forbids عِنْدِي أَخٌ (p. 59).

3. أَبُوكَ — dropping the و, or keeping it for "my"

أَبُكَ ✗ → أَبُوكَ ✓ (your father). And "my father" drops the و: أَبِي ✓, not أَبُويِ ✗. Chant the grid: abī · abūka · abūhu · abūhā.

4. Mixing ـهُ (his) and ـها (her)

Urdu's uska is genderless. The tail follows the owner: a man → اسْمُهُ, a woman → اسْمُهَا. Drill with named people (Hamza / Zaynab).

5. Using a detached pronoun after مَعَ/عِنْدَ

مَعَ هُوَ ✗ → مَعَهُ ✓. The pronoun attaches as a tail; don't leave it as the separate word هُوَ.

📿 Connect to the Qur'an

Lesson 10 prints no verse of its own — but the attached pronouns are everywhere in the Qur'an. Three verses your class can now decode, one for each tail they learned.

Surah Al-ʿAlaq · 96:1 — the tail ـكَ ("your")

اقْرَأْ بِاسْمِ رَبِّكَ الَّذِي خَلَقَ

iqraʾ bi-smi rabbika lladhī khalaq — "Read in the name of your Lord who created."

Students recognise: رَبِّكَ = رَبّ (Lord) + the tail ـكَ ("your") — exactly today's اسْمُكَ/لُغَتُكَ. The very first revealed words. (بِاسْمِ is review — preposition + iḍāfa; الَّذِي is recognition-only from Ch 9.)

Surah Aṣ-Ṣaff · 61:6 — the tail ـهُ ("his")

… وَمُبَشِّرًا بِرَسُولٍ يَأْتِي مِنْ بَعْدِي اسْمُهُ أَحْمَدُ

… wa-mubashshiran bi-rasūlin yaʾtī min baʿdi-y-smuhu aḥmad — "… and giving glad tidings of a messenger to come after me whose name is Ahmad."

Students recognise: اسْمُهُ = "his name" — اسْم + the tail ـهُ — the same structure as the lesson's own اسْمُهُ حَمْزَةُ ("his name is Hamza"). Here: "his name is Ahmad" ﷺ.

Surah Ṭā-Hā · 20:25 — the tail ـِي ("my") and لِي

رَبِّ اشْرَحْ لِي صَدْرِي

rabbi-shraḥ lī ṣadrī — "My Lord, expand for me my chest." (Mūsā's duʿā ﷺ.)

Students recognise: صَدْرِي = "my chest" — the tail ـِي ("my", pulling a kasra) — and لِي = "for me / I have", today's لِـ + ـِي. A short, famous duʿā built entirely from this lesson's tails.
