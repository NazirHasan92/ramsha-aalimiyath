# Part A · Understanding the chapter

For nine chapters, every word your class met was either a bare noun (`بَيْتٌ`), a noun made definite by `الـ` (`الْبَيْتُ`), a name (`مُحَمَّدٌ`), or a noun made definite by *another* noun (iḍāfa: `بَيْتُ الْمُدَرِّسِ` = "the teacher's house"). Chapter 10 adds the **last and most-used route to definiteness**: gluing a tiny *pronoun* onto the end of the word.

In one sentence: **Chapter 10 teaches the attached pronouns — `ـِي` (my), `ـكَ` (your, m), `ـهُ` (his), `ـها` (her) — which stick onto the end of a noun (`اسْمِي` = "my name"), onto `عِنْدَ` and `لِـ` to say "I have" (`عِنْدِي` / `لِي`), and onto `مَعَ` to say "with me" (`مَعِي`) — plus the special "five nouns" `أَبٌ`/`أَخٌ` that grow a long `و` (`أَبُوكَ`, `أَخُوهُ`).**

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter 10 **bhaari** hai — ek bada naya nizaam: chhoti **dumein** (tails) jo lafz ke aakhir mein chipakti hain. `ـِي` (mera), `ـكَ` (tera/aapka — mard), `ـهُ` (uska — mard), `ـها` (uski — aurat). `اسْمِي` = "mera naam". Yehi dumein `عِنْدَ`/`لِـ` par lag kar "mere paas hai" (`عِنْدِي`/`لِي`) aur `مَعَ` par lag kar "mere saath" (`مَعِي`) ban jaati hain. `أَبٌ`/`أَخٌ` thoda khaas — dum se pehle lamba `و` ugaate hain (`أَبُوكَ`, `أَخُوهُ`). Pehle char dumein pakki karo; phir baaki sab aasaan.

## 1 · The attached pronouns — `ـِي` / `ـكَ` / `ـهُ` / `ـها` (the engine)

This is the heart of the chapter. So far a pronoun has always been a **separate word**: `هُوَ` (he), `هِيَ` (she), `أَنَا` (I), `أَنْتَ` (you) — these sit *in front* and are called **detached** pronouns (`الضَّمَائِر المُنْفَصِلَة`). Lesson 10 introduces the other kind: the **attached** pronoun (`الضَّمِير المُتَّصِل`) — a tail that fuses onto the **end** of a word and means "my / your / his / her".

The textbook teaches exactly **four** of them, and even gives them names (you'll see these labels in Exercise 5, p. 58):

| Tail | Means | The book's label | Roman Urdu |
|---|---|---|---|
| `ـِي` | my | `المُتَكَلِّم` (the speaker) | mera/meri |
| `ـكَ` | your (to a male) | `المُخَاطَب` (the one addressed) | tera/aapka (mard) |
| `ـهُ` | his | `الغَائِب` (the absent male) | uska (mard) |
| `ـها` | her | `الغَائِبَة` (the absent female) | uski (aurat) |

Take a noun like `اسْمٌ` ("a name") and attach the four tails:

> `اسْمِي` (my name) · `اسْمُكَ` (your name) · `اسْمُهُ` (his name) · `اسْمُهَا` (her name)
> `لُغَتِي` (my language) · `لُغَتُكَ` (your language) · `لُغَتُهُ` (his language)
> `بَيْتِي` (my house) · `بَيْتُكَ` (your house) · `بَيْتُهُ` (his house) · `بَيْتُهَا` (her house)

Two things to notice — both important:

### (a) The tail makes the noun definite — so no `الـ`, no tanween

A noun with a tail is **already definite** ("*my* name" — a specific name). So you never put `الـ` on it, and it never takes tanween. You can't say `الاسْمِي` ✗ or `اسْمٌي` ✗ — just `اسْمِي` ✓. This is the **fifth route to definiteness**, joining the four your class already knows: `الـ`, names, iḍāfa, and (from Ch 5) being a pronoun.

In fact, a tailed noun *is* a kind of iḍāfa — `اسْمُكَ` is literally "the-name-of-you". That's why it behaves like the first noun of an iḍāfa (no `الـ`, no tanween). You don't need to teach that link, but it's why the grammar works.

### (b) The vowel before the tail

Look carefully at the endings:

> `اسْمُكَ` · `اسْمُهُ` · `اسْمُهَا` — the noun keeps a **ḍamma** (`ـُ`) before `ـكَ`/`ـهُ`/`ـها`.
> `اسْمِي` — but before `ـِي` ("my"), the noun takes a **kasra** (`ـِ`). The "my" tail always pulls a kasra.

So the simple rule for class: **`ـِي` ("my") sits on a kasra; the other three sit on the word's normal ḍamma.** (The ḍamma is there because these nouns are the subject/mubtadaʾ — *rafʿ* — but you don't name that.)

**Yaad rakho:** Char dumein: `ـِي` (mera — **zer** kheenchti hai: `اسْمِي`), `ـكَ` (tera — `اسْمُكَ`), `ـهُ` (uska — `اسْمُهُ`), `ـها` (uski — `اسْمُهَا`). Dum lagte hi lafz **ma'rifa** (definite) ho jaata hai → na `الـ`, na tanween (`الاسْمِي` GHALAT, `اسْمٌي` GHALAT). Book ke naam: `المُتَكَلِّم`/`المُخَاطَب`/`الغَائِب`/`الغَائِبَة` — sirf pehchaan, ratta mat lagwao.

## 2 · The "five nouns" — `أَبٌ` / `أَخٌ` grow a long `و`

Now a twist the book drills in Exercise 8 (p. 59). Most nouns just take the tail straight onto their ḍamma (`بَيْتُكَ`). But `أَبٌ` ("father") and `أَخٌ` ("brother") are special: before a tail (or before another noun), they **grow a long `و`**:

> `أَبٌ` → `أَبُوكَ` (your father) · `أَبُوهُ` (his father) · `أَبُوهَا` (her father)
> `أَخٌ` → `أَخُوكَ` (your brother) · `أَخُوهُ` (his brother) · `أَخُوهَا` (her brother)

**Exception — "my":** with the `ـِي` tail, the `و` disappears and you just get a long `ي`:

> `أَبِي` (my father) — *not* `أَبُويِ` · `أَخِي` (my brother) — *not* `أَخُويِ`

These (`أَبٌ`, `أَخٌ`, and a few others) are part of a small club grammarians call **the five nouns** (`الأَسْمَاء الخَمْسَة`). You should know the name for yourself, but **do not** teach the label or the full system — the book only shows the `و` form here. (The other shapes `أَبَا`/`أَبِي` as object/after-preposition come much later; don't open that.)

> **For you, not the class:** you actually met `أَبُو`/`أَخُو` glancingly before — in names like *Abū Bakr* ("the father of Bakr"). Here it becomes systematic. The `و` is the noun's *rafʿ* (subject) marker, shown as a long vowel instead of a ḍamma. Teach it as a **pattern to memorise**, not a rule to derive: "`أَب` and `أَخ` put a `و` before the tail — except 'my', which is `أَبِي`/`أَخِي`."

**Yaad rakho:** `أَبٌ` aur `أَخٌ` zid'di hain — dum se pehle lamba **`و`** ugaate hain: `أَبُوكَ` (tera baap), `أَخُوهُ` (uska bhai). Lekin "mera" mein `و` ghaayab, sirf lamba `ي`: `أَبِي` (mera baap), `أَخِي` (mera bhai). Inhe **pattern** ki tarah yaad karwao — "panch ism" ka naam ya poora rule mat kholo (woh baad ke sabaq). `ابْن` (beta) aam noun hai: `ابْنُهُ` (uska beta) — `و` nahin.

## 3 · "To have" two ways — `عِنْدَ` + tail vs `لِـ` + tail

Arabic has **no verb "to have"**. Instead it uses a preposition-type word + a tail. Lesson 10 gives your class *both* of the everyday ones — and, crucially, **when to use which**.

### `عِنْدَ` + tail — for *things you possess*

`عِنْدَ` ("at the place of / with") arrived in Lesson 9 with a visible noun (`عِنْدَ الْمُدِيرِ`). Now it takes the four tails:

> `عِنْدِي` (I have) · `عِنْدَكَ` (you have) · `عِنْدَهُ` (he has) · `عِنْدَهَا` (she has)
> `أَعِنْدَكَ سَيَّارَةٌ ؟ — لَا، مَا عِنْدِي سَيَّارَةٌ . عِنْدِي دَرَّاجَةٌ .`
> "Do you have a car? — No, I don't have a car. I have a bicycle."

Note `عِنْدِي` (with kasra, the "my" tail) but `عِنْدَكَ`/`عِنْدَهُ` (keep the fatḥa on `عِنْدَ`). The negative is `مَا عِنْدِي …` ("I don't have …").

### `لِـ` + tail — for *people and relationships*

`لِـ` ("for / belonging to") arrived back in Lesson 4. With the tails it also means "have" — but for **people you're related to**:

> `لِي` (I have) · `لَكَ` (you have) · `لَهُ` (he has) · `لَهَا` (she has)
> `لِي أَخٌ وَاحِدٌ` — "I have one brother." · `أُخْتِي لَهَا طِفْلٌ صَغِيرٌ` — "My sister has a small child."

Watch the vowel: `لِي` keeps a **kasra** (the "my" tail), but `لَكَ`/`لَهُ`/`لَهَا` switch the `لـ` to a **fatḥa**. Just teach the set as a unit to read aloud: *lī, laka, lahu, lahā* — the book itself does this in Exercise 6 ("اقْرَأْ" = "read").

### The rule the book makes explicit (p. 59)

The textbook prints a usage note in its own words:

> `نَقُولُ: عِنْدِي كِتَابٌ . وَنَقُولُ: لِي أَخٌ . لَا نَقُولُ: عِنْدِي أَخٌ .`
> "We say: *I have a book.* And we say: *I have a brother.* We do **not** say: *ʿindī akh.*"

So:
- **A thing you own** → `عِنْدَ`: `عِنْدِي كِتَابٌ`, `عِنْدِي سَيَّارَةٌ`, `عِنْدَهُ قَلَمٌ`.
- **A person you're related to** → `لِـ`: `لِي أَخٌ`, `لَهَا طِفْلٌ`, `لَكَ أُخْتٌ`.

This is the chapter's **twin-trap hammer** (like `مَنْ`/`مِنْ` in Ch 3 or `لِلْـ` in Ch 4). Drill it hard.

**Yaad rakho:** Arabic mein "have/paas hona" ka koi alag verb nahin. Do tareeqe:
> **Cheez** (object) → `عِنْدَ` + dum: `عِنْدِي كِتَابٌ` (mere paas kitaab hai), `عِنْدَكَ سَيَّارَةٌ`. Negative: `مَا عِنْدِي`.
> **Rishta** (person/relation) → `لِـ` + dum: `لِي أَخٌ` (mera ek bhai hai), `لَهَا طِفْلٌ`.
Book ka usool (p.59): `عِنْدِي كِتَابٌ` ✓ aur `لِي أَخٌ` ✓ — lekin `عِنْدِي أَخٌ` GHALAT. Cheez ke liye `عِنْدَ`, rishte ke liye `لِـ`. Yehi is sabaq ka sabse bada "jodwa-jaal" — khoob drill karwao. (`لِي`=zer, `لَكَ/لَهُ/لَها`=zabar.)

## 4 · `مَعَ` + tail — "with me / with you"

`مَعَ` ("with") is one of the new words, and it takes the same four tails:

> `مَعِي` (with me) · `مَعَكَ` (with you) · `مَعَهُ` (with him) · `مَعَهَا` (with her)
> `مَنْ مَعَكَ يَا عَلِيُّ ؟ — مَعِي زَمِيلٌ .` — "Who is with you, Ali? — A colleague is with me."
> `خَرَجَ أَبِي مِنَ الْبَيْتِ . مَنْ خَرَجَ مَعَهُ ؟ — خَرَجَ مَعَهُ عَمِّي .` — "My father left the house. Who left with him? — My uncle left with him."

Same vowel pattern: `مَعِي` (kasra, "my") but `مَعَكَ`/`مَعَهُ`/`مَعَهَا` (keep the fatḥa). With a *visible* noun it's just `مَعَ` + the noun: `خَرَجَ حَامِدٌ مَعَ خَالِدٍ` ("Hamid left with Khalid").

> **For you, not the class:** `مَعَ` is a ẓarf like `تَحْتَ`/`عِنْدَ`, and the word/tail after it is technically its muḍāf ilayhi — which is again *why* the tails attach so naturally. Don't teach that; just say "`مَعَ` takes the same tails."

**Yaad rakho:** `مَعَ` = "ke saath". Wahi char dumein: `مَعِي` (mere saath), `مَعَكَ` (tere saath), `مَعَهُ` (uske saath), `مَعَهَا` (uske saath — aurat). Visible noun ho to seedha `مَعَ` + ism: `مَعَ خَالِدٍ`. (`مَعِي`=zer, baaki zabar.)

## The big picture — what Chapter 10 silently does

Step back. Chapter 10 is the chapter where Arabic becomes **personal**:

1. **You can now own things and name relationships.** Before, your class could say "a house" or "the teacher's house"; now they can say "*my* house", "*your* father", "*her* husband", "I *have* a brother". That is the language of real conversation.
2. **The fifth — and most-used — route to definiteness arrives.** `الـ` · names · iḍāfa · pronouns · **and now the attached tail**. After this, almost any noun in the Qur'an or a hadith that ends in `ـهُ`/`ـها`/`ـكَ`/`ـِي` is decodable.
3. **"Have" gets its Arabic shape.** There's no verb for it — `عِنْدَ` for things, `لِـ` for people. This single distinction unlocks a huge amount of everyday speech.
4. **The five nouns crack open.** `أَبُوكَ`/`أَخُوهُ` are the class's first taste of a noun that *changes shape*, not just its ending — a preview of the richer grammar ahead (the full five-nouns system comes later).

The chapter is heavy because the tail-system is genuinely new machinery — but it's *regular* machinery. Once the four tails are reflex, everything else (`عِنْدَ`/`لِـ`/`مَعَ`, even `أَبُوكَ`) is the same four tails reused. Spend your time there.

**Yaad rakho:** Ch 10 woh sabaq hai jahan Arabic **zaati** (personal) ho jaati hai — "mera ghar", "tera baap", "mere paas hai", "mera ek bhai hai". Definiteness ka **paanchwan** (aur sabse zyaada istemaal hone wala) raasta: chipakne wali dum. "Have" ki Arabic shakl: cheez ke liye `عِنْدَ`, rishte ke liye `لِـ`. Bhaari hai kyunke nayi machinery hai — lekin **ba-qaaida** machinery. Char dumein pakki ho jayein to baaki sab unhi ka dohraav.

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

Now you understand what the chapter is doing. This section is how to *deliver* it. Because the load is heavy, the single most important decision is **pacing** — read the note below first.

> **⏱️ Pacing — strongly recommended:** This lesson is too big for a comfortable 60 minutes. Either **(a)** run a longer 75–90 minute session, or **(b)** split it: **Class 1 = the four tails on ordinary nouns** (`اسْمِي`/`اسْمُكَ`/`اسْمُهُ`/`اسْمُها`) until they're reflex; **Class 2 = `أَبُوكَ`/`أَخُوهُ` + `عِنْدَ`/`لِـ` ("have") + `مَعَ` + reading.** If your classmates' Ch 4 `لِـ` or Ch 9 `عِنْدَ` is shaky, do a 5-minute review of those first — this lesson stacks the tails right on top of them.

## The landmines for Urdu-speaking adult learners

### Landmine 1 · Putting `الـ` on a tailed noun (the definiteness slip)

A tail already makes the noun definite, so `الـ` is wrong: `الاسْمِي` ✗, `الْبَيْتُهُ` ✗. Urdu speakers reach for it because "my name" *feels* specific so they want a "the".

**Counter-strategy:** Reuse the iḍāfa rule from Ch 4 — "the first noun of an iḍāfa never takes `الـ`." A tailed noun is exactly that: `اسْمُكَ` = "the-name-of-you", so no `الـ`. One owner-mark is enough.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Dum khud hi 'the' ka kaam kar deti hai. `اسْمِي` = 'mera naam' — pehle se khaas/ma'rifa. Isliye `الـ` mat lagao: `الاسْمِي` GHALAT, sirf `اسْمِي` SAHIH. Yaad karo iḍāfa (Ch 4) — pehle ism par kabhi `الـ` nahin. Dum-wala ism bhi wahi hai."*

### Landmine 2 · `عِنْدِي أَخٌ` instead of `لِي أَخٌ` (the big one)

The chapter's core trap. Classmates learn `عِنْدِي` first ("I have") and then over-use it for relatives: `عِنْدِي أَخٌ` ✗. The book explicitly forbids this.

**Counter-strategy:** Two boxes on the board — **CHEEZEN** (`عِنْدَ`) and **RISHTE** (`لِـ`). Sort words into them live: car, pen, book → `عِنْدَ`; brother, sister, child, husband → `لِـ`. Repeat the book's line: `نَقُولُ لِي أَخٌ، لَا نَقُولُ عِنْدِي أَخٌ`.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Do dabbe banao. CHEEZ (car, qalam, kitaab) → `عِنْدَ`: `عِنْدِي سَيَّارَةٌ`. RISHTA (bhai, behen, bachcha, shauhar) → `لِـ`: `لِي أَخٌ`. Book khud kehti hai: `لِي أَخٌ` SAHIH, `عِنْدِي أَخٌ` GHALAT. Bhai 'cheez' nahin hai — `لِـ` use karo!"*

### Landmine 3 · `أَبُوكَ` / `أَخُوكَ` — forgetting the long `و` (or using it for "my")

Two opposite errors: (a) `أَبُكَ` ✗ (dropping the `و`), and (b) `أَبُويِ` ✗ (keeping the `و` for "my" instead of `أَبِي`).

**Counter-strategy:** Teach the tiny grid as a single chunk and chant it: *abī · abūka · abūhu · abūhā*. Highlight that **only "my" is different** (`أَبِي`, no `و`). Contrast with a normal noun (`بَيْتِي · بَيْتُكَ`) so they see `أَب`/`أَخ` are the odd ones.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`أَب` aur `أَخ` zid'di hain. 'Tera/uska baap' mein lamba `و`: `أَبُوكَ`, `أَبُوهُ`. Lekin 'mera baap' mein `و` ghaayab — sirf `أَبِي`. Chant karo: abī, abūka, abūhu, abūhā. Sirf pehla (mera) alag hai. `بَيْت` jaise aam lafz mein aisa kuch nahin — `أَب`/`أَخ` hi khaas hain."*

### Landmine 4 · The `ـها` vs `ـهُ` mix-up (gender of the owner)

Urdu's possessive *uska* is genderless, so classmates blur `ـهُ` (his) and `ـها` (her): they'll say `اسْمُهُ` for a woman. The tail's gender follows the **owner**, not the thing owned.

**Counter-strategy:** Drill with named people: point at "Hamza" → `اسْمُهُ حَمْزَةُ`; point at "Zaynab" → `اسْمُهَا زَيْنَبُ`. The owner's gender picks the tail. (This mirrors the `هُوَ`/`هِيَ` gender drill from Ch 5.)

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`ـهُ` = uska (MARD), `ـها` = uski (AURAT). Urdu ka 'uska' dono ke liye chalta hai — Arabic mein nahin! Dum **maalik** (owner) ke gender se chunti hai, cheez ke nahin. Hamza → `اسْمُهُ`, Zaynab → `اسْمُهَا`. (Yeh Ch 5 ka `هُوَ`/`هِيَ` wala gender-drill phir se.)"*

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 10

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum Chapter X mein dekhenge"*:

- ❌ **The feminine "your" `ـكِ`** (with a kasra, addressing a female). The lesson uses only `ـكَ` (masculine). Mention it exists if asked, but **don't drill it** — the full pronoun set is **Lesson 15**.
- ❌ **The plural tails** `ـكُمْ` (your, pl), `ـهُمْ` (their), `ـنَا` (our). All singular here. Plurals are **Lessons 13–15**.
- ❌ **The full "five nouns" system** — the object/after-preposition shapes `أَبَاكَ`/`أَبِيكَ`. Only the `أَبُو` (subject) shape is shown. Teach the `أَبُوكَ`/`أَخُوكَ` pattern, not the declension.
- ❌ **Building `الَّذِي` clauses.** It appears in the dialogue (`الْفَتَى الَّذِي مَعَكَ`) but stays **recognition only** (as set in Ch 9); the productive system is **Lesson 12**. Don't introduce `الَّتِي`.
- ❌ **The diptote (`غَيْر مُنْصَرِف`) rule** — even though the names in Exercise 9 (`حَمْزَةُ`, `أُسَامَةُ`…) take no tanween. Same hands-off policy as `زَيْنَب`/`خَدِيجَة`. Full rule is **Lesson 14+**.
- ❌ **Conjugating the verbs** `ذَهَبَ`/`خَرَجَ`/`جَلَسَ` — they appear (`أَذَهَبْتَ ؟ — ذَهَبْتُ`) but stay **recognition only**, exactly as in Ch 5/8. Don't teach the `ـتَ`/`ـتُ` verb endings as a system.
- ❌ **The `إِعْرَاب` case-names** (`رَفْع`/`نَصْب`/`جَرّ`) as a formal system. Keep saying "the tail sits on a ḍamma; 'my' pulls a kasra" descriptively.

**Yaad rakho:** Sabse common sawaal: *"aurat ko 'tera' kaise kehte hain?"* (`ـكِ`) — jawab: *"Lesson 15 mein."* Doosra: *"'tum sabka' / 'hamara' kaise?"* (`ـكُمْ`/`ـنَا`) — *"Lessons 13–15."* Teesra: *"`أَبُوكَ` mein `و` kyun?"* — *"`أَب`/`أَخ` khaas hain, pattern yaad karo, poora rule baad mein."* `الَّذِي` sirf pehchaan (Lesson 12 mein banana). Diptote naam — bata do, rule mat kholo. Verbs (`ذَهَبَ`/`خَرَجَ`) sirf pehchaan. Ek waqt mein ek hi cheez.

## Self-check before teaching

Before the class, make sure:

- [ ] You can attach all four tails to a plain noun with correct vowels (`اسْمِي`, `اسْمُكَ`, `اسْمُهُ`, `اسْمُهَا`) and know `ـِي` pulls a kasra.
- [ ] You can explain, in one Urdu sentence, why a tailed noun takes no `الـ` and no tanween. *(It's already definite — like the first noun of an iḍāfa.)*
- [ ] You can produce the five-nouns grid from memory: `أَبِي · أَبُوكَ · أَبُوهُ · أَبُوهَا` (and `أَخِي · أَخُوكَ …`), remembering only "my" drops the `و`.
- [ ] You can state and apply the `عِنْدَ` (things) vs `لِـ` (people) rule, and say why `عِنْدِي أَخٌ` is wrong.
- [ ] You can use `مَعَ` + a tail (`مَعِي`, `مَعَكَ`).
- [ ] You can pick `ـهُ` vs `ـها` by the **owner's** gender, not the thing's.
- [ ] You have decided your **pacing** (long session or split) and have the Ch 4/Ch 9 review ready if needed.

If yes to all seven — *tum tayar ho* (but give yourself extra prep time; this is a big one).

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Do they attach the tail **without** adding `الـ` or tanween (`بَيْتِي`, not `الْبَيْتِي`)? *(Definiteness.)*
2. Do they use `لِـ` for relatives and `عِنْدَ` for things (`لِي أَخٌ`, `عِنْدِي كِتَابٌ`)? *(The big rule.)*
3. Do they put the long `و` on `أَبُوكَ`/`أَخُوكَ` — and drop it for `أَبِي`/`أَخِي`? *(Five nouns.)*
4. Do they pick `ـهُ`/`ـها` by the **owner's** gender? *(Owner, not thing.)*
5. Do they use `مَعَ` + tail correctly (`مَعَهُ`, not `مَعَ هُوَ`)? *(Attached, not detached.)*
6. Do they keep `الَّذِي` and the verbs (`ذَهَبَ`) at **recognition** level — without over-reaching? *(Scope held.)*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh chhe points batayenge ke Lesson 11 (reading lesson) par jaana hai ya tails dohraani hain. Agar `عِنْدِي أَخٌ` nikal raha hai, ya `أَبُوكَ` ka `و` ghaayab ho raha hai, ya `الـ` dum-wale ism par lag raha hai — to ek aur tails-round do. Ch 10 ka core: char chipakne wali dumein + "have" ka do-tareeqa (`عِنْدَ` cheez, `لِـ` rishta) + `أَبُوكَ`/`أَخُوكَ` ka `و`. Yeh pakka, baqi sab uske baad.
