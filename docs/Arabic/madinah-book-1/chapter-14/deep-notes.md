>
> Last lesson (13) was about talking **about** a group — *they / their* (`هُمْ`/`هُنَّ`, `ـهُمْ`/`ـهُنَّ`). It filled the **3rd-person** rows of the pronoun grid. Lesson 14 is the **direct continuation**: now you learn to talk **to** a group — *you all* (`أَنْتُمْ`) — and to speak **as** a group — *we* (`نَحْنُ`). With them come their two attached tails:
>
> - **`أَنْتُمْ`** ("you all", men) + tail **`ـكُمْ`** ("your", of you all) — `بَيْتُكُمْ` (your house), `رَبُّكُمْ` (your Lord).
> - **`نَحْنُ`** ("we") + tail **`ـنَا`** ("our / us") — `بَيْتُنَا` (our house), `رَبُّنَا` (our Lord).
>
> And the past verb grows the same two endings: **`ذَهَبْتُمْ`** ("you all went") and **`ذَهَبْنَا`** ("we went").
>
> The lesson's heart is one glorious sentence built entirely out of "`ـنَا`": **`نَحْنُ مُسْلِمُونَ . اللهُ رَبُّنَا وَالإِسْلَامُ دِينُنَا …`** — "We are Muslims. Allah is **our** Lord, Islam is **our** religion…". Once your class can say that sentence and mean it, this lesson has done its job.
>
> **One thing you must NOT do:** the women's "you all" and the **complete pronoun chart** are **Lesson 15** (next lesson, the payoff of the whole 15-lesson arc, p.91). Don't draw the full table yet — leave the class one row to look forward to.

# Part A · Understanding the chapter

For thirteen chapters your class has built the pronoun system one piece at a time: `هُوَ`/`هِيَ` (Ch 5), `أَنَا`/`أَنْتَ` (Ch 9), `أَنْتِ` (Ch 12), then last lesson the plurals `هُمْ`/`هُنَّ` ("they"). There are only two everyday pronouns still missing — **"we"** and **"you all"** — and Lesson 14 hands you both. After this, the only gap left is the women's "you all," which Lesson 15 fills as it draws the whole chart.

In one sentence: **Chapter 14 teaches `أَنْتُمْ` ("you all", men) and `نَحْنُ` ("we"), their attached tails `ـكُمْ` ("your") and `ـنَا` ("our"), and the matching past-verb endings `ذَهَبْتُمْ` / `ذَهَبْنَا` — all carried on a warm dialogue and a creedal sentence.**

**Yaad rakho:** Pichhla sabaq (13) tha "**woh log / unka**" (`هُمْ`/`هُنَّ`, `ـهُمْ`/`ـهُنَّ`) — kisi group ke **baare mein** baat. Aaj ka sabaq (14) seedha agla qadam hai: group se **baat karna** — "**tum sab**" (`أَنْتُمْ`) — aur apne aap ko group ke taur par kehna — "**hum**" (`نَحْنُ`). Saath unki do tail: `ـكُمْ` ("tumhara" — `بَيْتُكُمْ`) aur `ـنَا` ("hamara" — `بَيْتُنَا`). Verb bhi: `ذَهَبْتُمْ` (tum sab gaye), `ذَهَبْنَا` (hum gaye). Sabaq ka dil: `نَحْنُ مُسْلِمُونَ . اللهُ رَبُّنَا وَالإِسْلَامُ دِينُنَا …` — poora `ـنَا` se bana. (Aurton ka "tum sab" + poora chart — **Lesson 15**, abhi nahin.)

## The dialogue (read it first — everything grows out of it)

A man, **Ḥāmid**, meets a group of young brothers (`الفِتْيَة`, "the youths") — the sons of Dr. Mūsā — and chats with them. Because he is talking **to a group**, every "you" becomes "you all" (`ـكُمْ`), and because they answer **as a group**, every "we / our" is `نَحْنُ` / `ـنَا`. Read it slowly and watch the two tails light up.

> — **الفِتْيَةُ :** السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ .
> — **حَامِد :** وَعَلَيْكُمُ السَّلَامُ وَرَحْمَةُ اللهِ وَبَرَكَاتُهُ .
> — **أَحَدُ الْفِتْيَةِ :** كَيْفَ حَالُكَ يَا عَمِّي ؟
> — **حَامِد :** أَنَا بِخَيْرٍ وَالْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ . كَيْفَ حَالُ**كُمْ** ؟ مَنْ **أَنْتُمْ** ؟
> — **أَحَدُهُمْ :** **نَحْنُ** أَبْنَاءُ الدُّكْتُورِ مُوسَى .
> — **حَامِد :** أَهْلًا وَسَهْلًا وَمَرْحَبًا . أَبُو**كُمْ** صَدِيقِي . . . أَيْنَ عَمُّ**كُمْ** الشَّيْخُ عِيسَى ؟
> — **أَحَدُهُمْ :** هُوَ مَرِيضٌ . هُوَ الْآنَ فِي الْمُسْتَشْفَى .
> — **حَامِد :** شَفَاهُ اللهُ . . . مَنْ هَذِهِ الطِّفْلَةُ الَّتِي مَعَ**كُمْ** ؟
> — **أَحَدُهُمْ :** هِيَ أُخْتُ**نَا** .
> — **حَامِد :** مَا اسْمُهَا ؟ — **أَحَدُهُمْ :** اسْمُهَا لَيْلَى .
> — **حَامِد :** أَيْنَ بَيْتُ**كُمُ** الْجَدِيدُ ؟
> — **أَحَدُهُمْ :** بَيْتُ**نَا** الْجَدِيدُ قَرِيبٌ مِنَ الْمَطَارِ .
> — **حَامِد :** أَ**أَنْتُمْ** فِي الْمَدْرَسَةِ الثَّانَوِيَّةِ ؟
> — **أَحَدُهُمْ :** لَا . **نَحْنُ** بِالْجَامِعَةِ . أَنَا فِي كُلِّيَّةِ الْهَنْدَسَةِ ، وَمَحْمُودٌ فِي كُلِّيَّةِ الطِّبِّ ، وَإِبْرَاهِيمُ فِي كُلِّيَّةِ الشَّرِيعَةِ ، وَيُوسُفُ فِي كُلِّيَّةِ التِّجَارَةِ .

Then Ḥāmid asks about a young man (`الفَتَى`) sitting in their car — a foreigner named William — and at the end:

> — **حَامِد :** أَ**ذَهَبْتُمْ** إِلَى الْمُسْتَشْفَى لِزِيَارَةِ عَمِّ**كُمُ** الْيَوْمَ ؟
> — **يُوسُف :** نَعَمْ . **ذَهَبْنَا** .

Notice how little is genuinely new. The whole question-machine is old (`مَنْ`، `أَيْنَ`، `كَيْفَ حَالُ…`، prepositions + kasra، `الَّتِي`، the five nouns `أَبُوكُمْ`). The only new gears are the two tails `ـكُمْ`/`ـنَا`, the two pronouns `أَنْتُمْ`/`نَحْنُ`, and the two verb endings.

**Yaad rakho:** Pehle dialogue parho. Ḥāmid ek group (`الفِتْيَة`, naujawan bhai) se baat kar raha hai — isliye har "tum" → "**tum sab**" (`ـكُمْ`: `حَالُكُمْ`, `أَبُوكُمْ`, `بَيْتُكُمْ`), aur group jawab deta hai "**hum / hamara**" (`نَحْنُ`, `ـنَا`: `أُخْتُنَا`, `بَيْتُنَا`). Naya sirf: do tail (`ـكُمْ`/`ـنَا`), do zameer (`أَنْتُمْ`/`نَحْنُ`), do verb ending (`ذَهَبْتُمْ`/`ذَهَبْنَا`). Baaki sab purana.

## 1 · `أَنْتُمْ` ("you all") and the tail `ـكُمْ` ("your")

In Ch 9 your class met `أَنْتَ` ("you", one man). When you address **more than one** person, that single `أَنْتَ` becomes:

> **`أَنْتُمْ`** (antum) = "you all" — addressing a group (of men, or a mixed group).

And the attached tail — the "your" that hangs on the end of a noun — does the same. The Ch 10 tail `ـكَ` ("your", one man) grows to:

> **`ـكُمْ`** (-kum) = "your" / "you all's" — `بَيْتُكُمْ` (your house), `رَبُّكُمْ` (your Lord), `مَدْرَسَتُكُمْ` (your school), `لُغَتُكُمْ` (your language), `عِنْدَكُمْ` (you all have), `مَعَكُمْ` (with you all).

This is the tail every Muslim already says ten times a day: the **`ـكُمْ`** of **`السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ`** ("peace be upon you **all**"). Point that out and the whole concept lands in one second — they have been saying it their whole lives.

> **For you, not the class:** `ـكُمْ` attaches exactly like the singular tails of Ch 10 — and the **five nouns** behave the same special way they did there. `أَب` + `كُمْ` = **`أَبُوكُمْ`** ("your father", with the long `و`), just as `أَب` + `كَ` was `أَبُوكَ`. The dialogue uses `أَبُوكُمْ` and `أَخُوكُمْ`/`أَخُونَا`. You don't need to re-teach the five-nouns rule — just remind the class it's the same `أَبُو…` shape they met in Ch 10.

**Yaad rakho:** `أَنْتَ` (ek mard "tu/aap") ka group-version = **`أَنْتُمْ`** ("tum sab"). Tail `ـكَ` ("tera") ka group-version = **`ـكُمْ`** ("tumhara, tum sab ka"): `بَيْتُكُمْ` (tumhara ghar), `رَبُّكُمْ` (tumhara Rabb). **Sabse aasaan hook:** yeh wahi `ـكُمْ` hai jo `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ` mein rozana bolte ho! Pure jeevan se yaad hai. (Five nouns wala `أَبُوكُمْ` Ch 10 jaisa hi — naya nahin.)

## 2 · `نَحْنُ` ("we") and the tail `ـنَا` ("our / us")

The partner of "you all" is "we." For the speaker-plus-others, Arabic uses:

> **`نَحْنُ`** (naḥnu) = "we" — `نَحْنُ مُسْلِمُونَ` (we are Muslims), `نَحْنُ أَبْنَاءُ الدُّكْتُورِ مُوسَى` (we are Dr. Mūsā's sons), `نَحْنُ بِالْجَامِعَةِ` (we are at the university).

And its tail:

> **`ـنَا`** (-nā) = "our" (and, on a verb, "us / we") — `بَيْتُنَا` (our house), `رَبُّنَا` (our Lord), `دِينُنَا` (our religion), `أُخْتُنَا` (our sister), `لُغَتُنَا` (our language), `أَبُونَا`/`أَخُونَا` (our father/brother).

Two beautiful things about `ـنَا`:

1. **It never changes for gender.** `ـكُمْ` is for men (the women's form is next lesson), but `ـنَا` is the **same for everyone** — a group of men, women, or mixed all say `بَيْتُنَا`. One worry less.
2. **It does double duty.** The very same `ـنَا` that means "our" on a noun (`بَيْتُنَا`) means "we did" on a verb (`ذَهَبْنَا` — "we went"). So the moment they learn "our," they are halfway to "we went."

**Yaad rakho:** "Hum" = **`نَحْنُ`**. Tail `ـنَا` = "hamara": `بَيْتُنَا` (hamara ghar), `رَبُّنَا` (hamara Rabb), `دِينُنَا` (hamara deen). **Do khoobiyan:** (1) `ـنَا` gender se nahin badalta — mard, aurat, mixed, sab `بَيْتُنَا`; (2) wahi `ـنَا` noun par "hamara" (`بَيْتُنَا`) aur verb par "hum ne kiya" (`ذَهَبْنَا`). Ek tail, do kaam.

## 3 · The plural past verb — `ذَهَبْتُمْ` ("you all went") / `ذَهَبْنَا` ("we went")

In Ch 13 the verb learned its 3rd-person plurals: `ذَهَبُوا` (they-men went), `ذَهَبْنَ` (they-women went). Lesson 14 adds the **2nd-person "you all"** and the **1st-person "we"** on the same three known verbs (`ذَهَبَ`، `خَرَجَ`، `جَلَسَ`):

> **"you all" → add `ـتُمْ`:** `ذَهَبَ` → **`ذَهَبْتُمْ`** ("you all went"), `خَرَجَ` → `خَرَجْتُمْ`, `جَلَسَ` → `جَلَسْتُمْ`.
> **"we" → add `ـنَا`:** `ذَهَبَ` → **`ذَهَبْنَا`** ("we went"), `خَرَجَ` → `خَرَجْنَا`, `جَلَسَ` → `جَلَسْنَا`.

The book drills it directly (Exercise 6, p.86): it gives the one-person form `أَنْتَ ذَهَبْتَ` ("you [one man] went") and asks the class to turn it into the group form `أَنْتُمْ ذَهَبْتُمْ` ("you all went"). And the dialogue ends on the pair perfectly: `أَذَهَبْتُمْ إِلَى الْمُسْتَشْفَى ؟` — `نَعَمْ ، ذَهَبْنَا` ("Did you all go to the hospital? — Yes, we went.").

The lovely symmetry to point out: the verb endings **echo the pronouns/tails**. "You all" is `أَنْتُمْ` / `ـكُمْ` / `ـتُمْ`; "we" is `نَحْنُ` / `ـنَا` / `ـنَا`. The `…م` family is "you all"; the `…نا` family is "we."

> **Important scope line:** teach **only** the "you all" (`ـتُمْ`) and "we" (`ـنَا`) endings, on the three verbs the class already knows. You are **not** teaching the present tense or the full conjugation table. Keep it to the spoken pair from the dialogue: *"you all went / we went."*

**Yaad rakho:** Ch 13 ne diya `ذَهَبُوا`/`ذَهَبْنَ` ("woh log gaye"). Ab Lesson 14: **"tum sab" → `ـتُمْ`** (`ذَهَبْتُمْ`), **"hum" → `ـنَا`** (`ذَهَبْنَا`). Kitaab (Exercise 6) seedha drill karti hai: `أَنْتَ ذَهَبْتَ` (tu gaya) → `أَنْتُمْ ذَهَبْتُمْ` (tum sab gaye). **Khoobsurat symmetry:** "tum sab" ka khandaan `…م` (`أَنْتُمْ`/`ـكُمْ`/`ـتُمْ`), "hum" ka khandaan `…نا` (`نَحْنُ`/`ـنَا`/`ـنَا`). **Sirf itna:** do ending, teen jaane-pehchaane verbs — poori conjugation nahin.

## 4 · The creedal sentence — `نَحْنُ مُسْلِمُونَ …` (the `ـنَا` showcase)

The lesson's exercises (p.84, Exercise 2) contain a sentence that is the emotional and spiritual centre of the whole chapter. It is built almost entirely out of today's tail `ـنَا` ("our"):

> **نَحْنُ مُسْلِمُونَ . اللهُ رَبُّنَا وَالإِسْلَامُ دِينُنَا وَالنَّبِيُّ مُحَمَّدٌ ﷺ رَسُولُنَا وَالقُرْآنُ الكَرِيمُ كِتَابُنَا وَالكَعْبَةُ قِبْلَتُنَا وَالعَرَبِيَّةُ لُغَتُنَا .**
>
> *naḥnu muslimūn. allāhu rabbunā wa-l-islāmu dīnunā wa-n-nabiyyu muḥammadun ﷺ rasūlunā wa-l-qurʾānu l-karīmu kitābunā wa-l-kaʿbatu qiblatunā wa-l-ʿarabiyyatu lughatunā.*
>
> "We are Muslims. Allah is **our** Lord, Islam is **our** religion, the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ is **our** Messenger, the Noble Qur'an is **our** Book, the Kaʿbah is **our** qiblah, and Arabic is **our** language."

Count the `ـنَا`s: `رَبُّنَا`، `دِينُنَا`، `رَسُولُنَا`، `كِتَابُنَا`، `قِبْلَتُنَا`، `لُغَتُنَا` — six times, on six nouns, in one breath. This is why `ـنَا` is worth teaching well: it lets a beginner state the entire foundation of their identity in a single Arabic sentence. Make this the sentence the class memorises and recites together. It is also a quiet motivation gift — *"the Arabic you came here to read is `لُغَتُنَا`, our own language."*

**Yaad rakho:** Sabaq ka dil yeh jumla hai (p.84, Exercise 2) — poora `ـنَا` ("hamara") se bana: `رَبُّنَا`، `دِينُنَا`، `رَسُولُنَا`، `كِتَابُنَا`، `قِبْلَتُنَا`، `لُغَتُنَا` — chha baar. Ek hi saans mein apni poori pehchaan Arabic mein. **Yehi jumla class ko zabani yaad karwao** aur mil kar parho — `ـنَا` isliye ahem hai. (Aur `العَرَبِيَّة لُغَتُنَا` — "Arabic hamari zabaan" — ek pyaara motivation tohfa.)

## 5 · Reading the names — foreigners and the Prophets (`اقْرَأْ وَاكْتُبْ`)

The lesson closes with a pure **reading drill** (Exercise 5, p.85): a list of names to read and copy. They fall into three groups:

- **Foreign place-names:** `لَنْدَنُ` (London), `بَارِيسُ` (Paris), `بَاكِسْتَانُ` (Pakistan), `إِصْطَنْبُولُ` (Istanbul).
- **Foreign personal names:** `وِلْيَمُ` (William), `إِدْوَرْدُ` (Edward).
- **Prophets, angels, and one tyrant:** `إِبْرَاهِيمُ`، `إِسْمَاعِيلُ`، `إِسْحَقُ`، `يَعْقُوبُ`، `أَيُّوبُ`، `سُلَيْمَانُ`، `دَاوُدُ`، `يُونُسُ`، `إِدْرِيسُ` (Prophets); `جِبْرَئِيلُ`، `مِيكَائِيلُ` (angels Jibrīl and Mīkāʾīl); `فِرْعَوْنُ` (Pharaoh).

This is genuinely valuable for the class — recognising the Prophets' names in their Arabic spelling is a small joy in its own right.

One thing your **sharp** eye will catch: every one of these names is printed with a **single ḍamma and no tanween** (`إِدْوَرْدُ`, not `إِدْوَرْدٌ`; `يَعْقُوبُ`, not `يَعْقُوبٌ`). For the class, treat that exactly as a **read-aloud habit**: *"read these names as printed — they end in a plain `ـُ`, no `-un`. Don't worry about why yet."*

> **For you, not the class — the "why" you keep to yourself:** these names take no tanween because they are **`غَيْر مُنْصَرِف`** (diptotes) — foreign names and certain proper-noun patterns are blocked from tanween. **The book has still not named this rule** (Exercise 5 is just "read and write," with no rule statement), so you must **not** teach it as a system yet. It is a recurring read-only drill (you saw the same with `أُسَامَةُ`/`حَمْزَةُ` earlier and the `ـَاء`-plurals in Ch 13). Keep doing what worked: read them right, say nothing about the missing tanween in class.

**Yaad rakho:** Aakhri exercise (5, p.85) ek **reading drill** hai — naam parho aur likho: foreign jagah (`لَنْدَن`, `بَارِيس`, `بَاكِسْتَان`, `إِصْطَنْبُول`), foreign log (`وِلْيَم`, `إِدْوَرْد`), aur **Anbiya** (`إِبْرَاهِيم`, `إِسْمَاعِيل`, `إِسْحَق`, `يَعْقُوب`, `أَيُّوب`, `سُلَيْمَان`, `دَاوُد`, `يُونُس`, `إِدْرِيس`), farishte (`جِبْرَئِيل`, `مِيكَائِيل`), aur `فِرْعَوْن`. Class ke liye qeemti — Anbiya ke naam Arabic mein pehchaanna. **Note:** yeh sab bina tanween (`إِدْوَرْدُ`, na ke `إِدْوَرْدٌ`) — bas jaise likha hai waise parho, "kyun" abhi mat batao (wajah `غَيْر مُنْصَرِف`/diptote hai — magar kitaab ne abhi naam nahin diya, isliye tum bhi nahin).

## 6 · The big picture — the pronoun grid, one row from complete

Step back and look at what the class now owns. Across the book they have collected the everyday pronouns one or two at a time, and Lesson 14 adds the last two common ones:

| | **singular** (one) | **plural** (a group) |
|---|---|---|
| **I / we** | `أَنَا` (Ch 9) | **`نَحْنُ`** *(new)* |
| **you (m)** | `أَنْتَ` (Ch 9) | **`أَنْتُمْ`** *(new)* |
| **you (f)** | `أَنْتِ` (Ch 12) | *(next lesson)* |
| **he / they (m)** | `هُوَ` (Ch 5) | `هُمْ` (Ch 13) |

After today there is exactly **one** common box still empty — the women's "you all" — and Lesson 15 fills it as it draws the **complete** detached-pronoun table (the payoff of the whole arc, p.91). So Chapter 14's quiet achievement is this: it brings the class to the *edge* of the finished system. Teach it that way — *"two pronouns to go… actually, one. Next lesson we close the whole chart."*

> **For you, not the class:** do **not** pre-draw the full table or write the women's-"you all" form in any classmate-facing file. Naming it now steals Lesson 15's payoff (and the dual `أَنْتُمَا`/`هُمَا` is a *different* number — Lesson 16+, not even part of L15). Leave the last row blank on purpose.

**Yaad rakho:** Dekho class ke paas ab kya hai: `أَنَا`/`نَحْنُ`، `أَنْتَ`/`أَنْتُمْ`، `أَنْتِ`/(agla sabaq)، `هُوَ`/`هُمْ`، `هِيَ`/`هُنَّ`. Aaj ke baad sirf **ek** rozmarra ka khaana khaali — aurton ka "tum sab" — aur **Lesson 15** usko bhar kar **poora** pronoun chart bana dega (p.91, poore arc ka inaam). Ch 14 ka chupa hua kamaal: class ko mukammal system ke **kinaare** tak le aana. (Poora table abhi mat banao, aakhri qatar jaan-boojh kar khaali chhoro.)

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

Now you understand it. This is how to *deliver* it. This is a friendly, dialogue-driven lesson — lighter than the plural marathon of Ch 13 — and it fits **one 60-minute class** comfortably. The spine is simple: **two new "people" (you-all, we), each with a pronoun + a tail + a verb ending**, all paid off by one creedal sentence.

> **⏱️ Pacing — one sitting (≈60 min).** Open with the dialogue, teach `أَنْتُمْ`/`ـكُمْ`, then `نَحْنُ`/`ـنَا`, then the two verb endings, then crown it with the `نَحْنُ مُسْلِمُونَ` sentence. Close with the Prophets'-names reading drill. No need to split.

---

## The landmines for Urdu-speaking adult learners

### Landmine 1 · Reaching for `ـكُمْ`/`ـنَا` but forgetting the five-nouns shape (`أَبُوكُمْ`)

The tails attach cleanly to ordinary nouns (`بَيْتُكُمْ`, `رَبُّنَا`) — but on `أَب` and `أَخ` they trigger the **five-nouns** long-vowel shape from Ch 10: `أَبُوكُمْ` (not `أَبْكُمْ`), `أَخُونَا` (not `أَخْنَا`).

**Counter-strategy:** Don't re-teach the rule — just **recall** it. Write `أَبُوكَ` (Ch 10) on the board next to today's `أَبُوكُمْ` and let them see it's the same `أَبُو…` shape, new tail. Drill the pair `أَبُوكُمْ` / `أَبُونَا` ("your father" / "our father") together.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`ـكُمْ` aur `ـنَا` aam lafzon par seedhe lagte hain: `بَيْتُكُمْ`, `رَبُّنَا`. Magar `أَب`/`أَخ` par wahi Ch 10 wala lamba shape: `أَبُوكُمْ` ('tumhara baap'), `أَخُونَا` ('hamara bhai') — `أَبُوكَ` jaisa hi. Naya qaida nahin, sirf yaad-dehani."*

### Landmine 2 · Mixing up `ـكُمْ` ("your") and `ـنَا` ("our")

"You all's" vs "ours" — two new tails arriving together. A beginner will say `بَيْتُكُمْ` when they mean `بَيْتُنَا`, especially in fast Q-and-A.

**Counter-strategy:** Anchor each to a feeling, not a translation. `ـكُمْ` = **pointing at them** ("YOUR house" — finger out); `ـنَا` = **hand on own chest** ("OUR house"). Drill a back-and-forth: you ask `أَيْنَ بَيْتُكُمْ ؟` (point at them), they answer `بَيْتُنَا قَرِيبٌ …` (hand on chest). The gesture fixes the tail.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`ـكُمْ` = **tumhara** (unki taraf ishaara, ungli baahar) — `بَيْتُكُمْ`. `ـنَا` = **hamara** (apne seene par haath) — `بَيْتُنَا`. Sawaal-jawab khelo: main poochhoon `أَيْنَ بَيْتُكُمْ ؟`, tum kaho `بَيْتُنَا قَرِيبٌ …`. Ishaara tail ko pakka kar dega."*

### Landmine 3 · The `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ` reflex spilling onto `أَنْتُمْ` verbs

Everyone knows `عَلَيْكُمْ`, so `ـكُمْ` comes easily — but the matching **verb** ending `ـتُمْ` (`ذَهَبْتُمْ`) is brand new and they'll forget to change the verb, saying `أَنْتُمْ ذَهَبَ` or `أَنْتُمْ ذَهَبْتَ`.

**Counter-strategy:** Teach the "`…م` family" as a set: when the doer is `أَنْتُمْ`, **three things** carry `…م`/`…تم` — the pronoun `أَنْتُمْ`, the tail `ـكُمْ`, the verb `ـتُمْ`. Put `أَنْتُمْ — بَيْتُكُمْ — ذَهَبْتُمْ` in a row on the board so the `…كم/…تم` rhyme is visible and audible.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`عَلَيْكُمْ` to sab ko aata hai, magar verb ka `ـتُمْ` naya hai. 'Tum sab' ka poora khandaan saath chalta hai: `أَنْتُمْ` (zameer) — `بَيْتُكُمْ` (tail) — `ذَهَبْتُمْ` (verb). Teeno mein `…م` ki awaaz. `أَنْتُمْ ذَهَبَ` ghalat — `أَنْتُمْ ذَهَبْتُمْ`."*

### Landmine 4 · Treating `ـنَا` as gendered like `ـكُمْ`

Because `ـكُمْ` is "for men" (and they sense a women's form is coming), classmates may assume `ـنَا` also has a separate "women's we." It does **not**.

**Counter-strategy:** Make the simplicity the headline: *"`ـنَا` is the easy one — `بَيْتُنَا` for men, women, mixed, everyone. There is no other 'we.'"* Sell it as a free win between the harder gendered forms.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"`ـنَا` sabse aasaan — `بَيْتُنَا` mardon ke liye, aurton ke liye, mile-jule group ke liye, **sab ke liye ek**. Koi alag 'hum' nahin. `ـكُمْ` mein gender hai, magar `ـنَا` mein bilkul nahin — yeh muft ki aasaani hai."*

### Landmine 5 · The Prophets'-names tanween trap

Reading the name list, a careful classmate will *add* a tanween out of habit (`دَاوُدٌ` instead of `دَاوُدُ`), because every noun they've met for 13 chapters took one.

**Counter-strategy:** Frame it as a read-aloud habit, exactly like the silent `و` of `أُولَئِكَ` last lesson: *"these names end in a plain `ـُ` — read `دَاوُدُ`, not `دَاوُدٌ`. Why? We'll see later — for now, just read what's printed."* Do **not** open the diptote rule.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"In naamon ke aakhir mein sirf `ـُ` hai — `دَاوُدُ` parho, `دَاوُدٌ` nahin. Kyun? Baad mein dekhenge — abhi sirf jo likha hai woh parho. (Yeh pichhle sabaq ke khaamosh `و` jaisa — parhne ki aadat, qaida nahin.)"*

## Suggested class plan (one sitting, ≈60 min)

*Board before class:* the title (`الدَّرْسُ الرَّابِعَ عَشَرَ`); the two "families" — `أَنْتُمْ · ـكُمْ · ذَهَبْتُمْ` (you all) and `نَحْنُ · ـنَا · ذَهَبْنَا` (we); and the `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ` hook circled.

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–6 min | Recap last lesson's `هُمْ`/`هُنَّ` ("they"). Set up: "today — talking **to** a group, and **as** a group." |
| 6–18 min | **`أَنْتُمْ` + `ـكُمْ`** — start from `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ` (they already know `ـكُمْ`!). Drill `بَيْتُكُمْ`, `رَبُّكُمْ`, `حَالُكُمْ`. Recall `أَبُوكُمْ` (five nouns). Read the first half of the dialogue. |
| 18–30 min | **`نَحْنُ` + `ـنَا`** — `بَيْتُنَا`, `أُخْتُنَا`, `رَبُّنَا`. Flag: `ـنَا` is gender-free. The chest-vs-point gesture for `ـنَا` vs `ـكُمْ`. |
| 30–40 min | **The verb endings** — `أَنْتَ ذَهَبْتَ → أَنْتُمْ ذَهَبْتُمْ`; `نَحْنُ ذَهَبْنَا`. The dialogue's closing pair `أَذَهَبْتُمْ ؟ — ذَهَبْنَا`. Book Exercise 6. |
| 40–50 min | **The creedal sentence** `نَحْنُ مُسْلِمُونَ . اللهُ رَبُّنَا …` — recite together, line by line, until memorised. Count the six `ـنَا`s. |
| 50–58 min | **Reading drill** — the Prophets'/foreign names (Exercise 5). Read aloud; note "no tanween, just read it." |
| 58–60 min | Recap the two families; the pronoun grid (one box left for next lesson). Homework. |

**Yaad rakho:** Ek baithak kaafi hai (~60 min). Spine: do naye "log" (tum-sab, hum), har ek ke saath zameer + tail + verb ending, aur sab ka inaam `نَحْنُ مُسْلِمُونَ` jumla. Shuruaat `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ` se (`ـكُمْ` pehle se aata hai!), aakhir Anbiya ke naamon ki reading se.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 14

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum agle sabaq mein dekhenge"*:

- ❌ **The women's "you all" and its tail** — that is the **Lesson 15** payoff. Don't write the form; just say "talking to a group of women comes next lesson."
- ❌ **The COMPLETE detached-pronoun table** — Lesson 15 draws it (p.91). Leave the last box of your grid blank on purpose; don't pre-draw the finished chart.
- ❌ **The dual (`الْمُثَنَّى`) — "you two / they two"** — a *separate* number from the plural, **Lesson 16+**. Lesson 14 is plural (3+) "you all / we" only; don't mention `أَنْتُمَا`/`هُمَا`.
- ❌ **The diptote (`غَيْر مُنْصَرِف`) rule as a named system** — the Prophets'/foreign names print with no tanween for this reason, but the book still hasn't named it. Read them as printed; say nothing about the missing tanween.
- ❌ **The full verb-conjugation table / the present tense** — teach only the "you all" (`ـتُمْ`) and "we" (`ـنَا`) past endings on the three known verbs (`ذَهَبَ`/`خَرَجَ`/`جَلَسَ`).
- ❌ **Re-teaching the five nouns** — `أَبُوكُمْ`/`أَخُونَا` are *recalled* from Ch 10, not taught fresh. Don't reopen the whole five-nouns system.

**Yaad rakho:** Aurton ka "tum sab" + poora pronoun chart — **Lesson 15** (form abhi mat likho, aakhri khaana khaali). Dual (do log, `أَنْتُمَا`/`هُمَا`) — **Lesson 16+**, bilkul mat chhero. Diptote rule — bata mat, sirf jaise likha hai parho. Poori verb conjugation / present tense mat. Five nouns (`أَبُوكُمْ`) sirf yaad-dehani, naya nahin.

## Self-check before teaching

Before the class, make sure:

- [ ] You can use `أَنْتُمْ` ("you all") and the tail `ـكُمْ` on any noun (`بَيْتُكُمْ`, `رَبُّكُمْ`), and you connect it to `السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ`.
- [ ] You can use `نَحْنُ` ("we") and the tail `ـنَا` (`بَيْتُنَا`, `رَبُّنَا`), and you know `ـنَا` is gender-free and doubles as "we did."
- [ ] You can turn `أَنْتَ ذَهَبْتَ` into `أَنْتُمْ ذَهَبْتُمْ`, and say `نَحْنُ ذَهَبْنَا`.
- [ ] You can recite the whole `نَحْنُ مُسْلِمُونَ . اللهُ رَبُّنَا …` sentence from memory and name each `ـنَا`.
- [ ] You can read the Prophets'/foreign names list without adding tanween — and you know to *defer* the diptote "why."
- [ ] You can draw the pronoun grid with the last box deliberately left blank for Lesson 15.

If yes to all six — *tum tayar ho*. This lesson brings the class to the very edge of the finished pronoun system — let them feel how close it is.

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Do they use `أَنْتُمْ`/`ـكُمْ` for a group they're talking **to**, and `نَحْنُ`/`ـنَا` for **themselves** as a group? *(Concepts 1–2.)*
2. Do they handle the five-nouns shape `أَبُوكُمْ`/`أَخُونَا` (not `أَبْكُمْ`)? *(Landmine 1.)*
3. Do they keep `ـكُمْ` ("your") and `ـنَا` ("our") apart in fast Q-and-A? *(Landmine 2.)*
4. Do they change the **verb** for "you all" (`ذَهَبْتُمْ`, not `ذَهَبَ`) and "we" (`ذَهَبْنَا`)? *(Concept 3.)*
5. Can they recite the `نَحْنُ مُسْلِمُونَ …` sentence and feel it? *(Concept 4.)*
6. Do they read the Prophets'/foreign names without inventing a tanween — and avoid the women's-"you all" form (the L15 leak)? *(Concept 5 + the deferral line.)*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh sabaq class ko mukammal pronoun system ke **kinaare** tak le aata hai. Diagnosis: `أَنْتُمْ`/`ـكُمْ` (tum sab) aur `نَحْنُ`/`ـنَا` (hum) sahi jagah? `أَبُوكُمْ`/`أَخُونَا` (five nouns) theek? `ـكُمْ` vs `ـنَا` alag rakhte hain? Verb badalte hain (`ذَهَبْتُمْ`/`ذَهَبْنَا`)? `نَحْنُ مُسْلِمُونَ` jumla yaad + mehsoos? Anbiya ke naam bina tanween? Agla sabaq (Lesson 15): aurton ka "tum sab" + **poora** pronoun table — aaj uska form nahin likha.
