Last lesson filled the 3rd-person plural rows (هُمْ/هُنَّ = "they / their"). Chapter 14 fills the 2nd / 1st-person rows: أَنْتُمْ ("you all") and نَحْنُ ("we"), each with its tail and its verb ending. Lighter than Ch 13 — it fits one 60-minute class, crowned by the creedal sentence نَحْنُ مُسْلِمُونَ…

★ Master Overview — نقشۂ الدرس

One bird's-eye map: Chapter 14 adds the last two everyday pronouns — "you all" (أَنْتُمْ) and "we" (نَحْنُ). Each comes as a little family: a pronoun, an attached tail, and a past-verb ending. The "you all" family carries …م; the "we" family carries …نا. It all pays off in one creedal sentence.

How Ch 14 connects back — and nearly closes the system

Every new piece is an old singular tool, now in the plural:

  • The pronouns (Ch 5/9/12): أَنْتَ → أَنْتُمْ ("you all"), أَنَا → نَحْنُ ("we") — joining last lesson's هُمْ/هُنَّ.
  • The tails (Ch 10): ـكَ → ـكُمْ ("your"), and the gender-free ـنَا ("our") — the same five-nouns shape still applies (أَبُوكُمْ, أَخُونَا).
  • The verb (Ch 12/13): grows ذَهَبْتُمْ ("you all went") and ذَهَبْنَا ("we went") on the three known verbs.

After today, exactly one common pronoun box is still empty — the women's "you all" — which Lesson 15 fills as it draws the complete table (Concept 6). Leave that box blank on purpose.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Use أَنْتُمْ ("you all", men) and the attached tail ـكُمْ ("your") — بَيْتُكُمْ, رَبُّكُمْ — connecting it to the daily السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ.
  2. Use نَحْنُ ("we") and the gender-free tail ـنَا ("our") — بَيْتُنَا, رَبُّنَا.
  3. Make a past verb agree with "you all" (ذَهَبْتُمْ) and "we" (ذَهَبْنَا).
  4. Recite the creedal sentence نَحْنُ مُسْلِمُونَ . اللهُ رَبُّنَا … and identify each ـنَا.
  5. Read the Prophets'/foreign names list aloud (as printed, no added tanween).

1 · أَنْتُمْ ("you all") & the tail ـكُمْ ("your")

Addressing more than one person, أَنْتَ ("you", one man, Ch 9) becomes أَنْتُمْ ("you all"). Its attached tail ـكَ ("your", Ch 10) becomes ـكُمْ — the very tail of السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ.

Where this fits: أَنْتَ (Ch 9) and the tail ـكَ (Ch 10) scaled up to a group. The dialogue is full of it: مَنْ أَنْتُمْ ؟, كَيْفَ حَالُكُمْ ؟, أَيْنَ بَيْتُكُمْ ؟.

أَنْتُمْ / ـكُمْ · "you all" / "your"

Meaning
أَنْتُمْ = "you all" (a group of men, or mixed). The tail ـكُمْ = "your" (of you all): بَيْتُكُمْ (your house), رَبُّكُمْ (your Lord), مَدْرَسَتُكُمْ (your school), عِنْدَكُمْ (you all have), مَعَكُمْ (with you all).
Roman Urdu
أَنْتُمْ = "tum sab". Tail ـكُمْ = "tumhara": بَيْتُكُمْ (tumhara ghar). Yeh wahi ـكُمْ hai jo عَلَيْكُمْ mein rozana bolte ho.
Rule
The tail attaches like the Ch 10 tails. On the five nouns it keeps the long-vowel shape: أَب + كُمْ = أَبُوكُمْ ("your father"), أَخُوكُمْ — same أَبُو… shape as Ch 10's أَبُوكَ (recall, don't re-teach).
Example
مَنْ أَنْتُمْ ؟ — أَيْنَ بَيْتُكُمُ الْجَدِيدُ ؟ man antum? — ayna baytukumu l-jadīd? · "Who are you (all)? — Where is your new house?"

💡 Start from السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ

The class has said عَلَيْكُمْ their whole lives — so they already own ـكُمْ. Open the concept by circling the ـكُمْ of السَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُمْ on the board: "peace upon you all — that كم is today's lesson."

2 · نَحْنُ ("we") & the tail ـنَا ("our")

The partner of "you all" is "we". أَنَا ("I", Ch 9) becomes نَحْنُ ("we"), and its tail is ـنَا ("our") — the easy one: it never changes for gender, and it doubles as "we" on a verb.

Where this fits: أَنَا (Ch 9) and the tail family (Ch 10) → the group. The group answers with it throughout: نَحْنُ أَبْنَاءُ الدُّكْتُورِ مُوسَى, هِيَ أُخْتُنَا, بَيْتُنَا قَرِيبٌ مِنَ الْمَطَارِ.

نَحْنُ / ـنَا · "we" / "our"

Meaning
نَحْنُ = "we". The tail ـنَا = "our": بَيْتُنَا (our house), رَبُّنَا (our Lord), دِينُنَا (our religion), أُخْتُنَا (our sister), لُغَتُنَا (our language).
Roman Urdu
نَحْنُ = "hum". Tail ـنَا = "hamara": بَيْتُنَا (hamara ghar), رَبُّنَا (hamara Rabb).
Rule
ـنَا is the easy tail: gender-free (men, women, mixed all say بَيْتُنَا) and it doubles as "we" on a verb (ذَهَبْنَا = "we went"). On the five nouns: أَبُونَا, أَخُونَا.
Example
هِيَ أُخْتُنَا . — بَيْتُنَا الْجَدِيدُ قَرِيبٌ مِنَ الْمَطَارِ . hiya ukhtunā. — baytunā l-jadīdu qarībun mina l-maṭār. · "She is our sister. — Our new house is near the airport."

💡 The chest-vs-point gesture

To keep ـكُمْ and ـنَا apart, anchor each to a gesture: ـكُمْ = finger pointing out at them ("YOUR house"); ـنَا = hand on own chest ("OUR house"). Ask أَيْنَ بَيْتُكُمْ ؟ (point), they answer بَيْتُنَا … (hand on chest).

3 · The plural past verb — ذَهَبْتُمْ / ذَهَبْنَا

Last lesson the verb gained its 3rd-person plurals (ذَهَبُوا/ذَهَبْنَ). Lesson 14 adds "you all" (ـتُمْ) and "we" (ـنَا) on the same three known verbs. The endings echo the pronouns: the …م family is "you all", the …نا family is "we".

Where this fits: Extends the past-verb agreement of Ch 12–13. Teach only the two new endings on ذَهَبَ/خَرَجَ/جَلَسَ — no present tense, no full table. Book Exercise 6 (p.86) drills أَنْتَ ذَهَبْتَ → أَنْتُمْ ذَهَبْتُمْ.

ذَهَبْتُمْ / ذَهَبْنَا · "you all went" / "we went"

Meaning
"You all": add ـتُمْ — ذَهَبْتُمْ, خَرَجْتُمْ, جَلَسْتُمْ. "We": add ـنَا — ذَهَبْنَا, خَرَجْنَا, جَلَسْنَا.
Roman Urdu
"Tum sab" → ـتُمْ (ذَهَبْتُمْ — tum sab gaye); "hum" → ـنَا (ذَهَبْنَا — hum gaye).
Rule
The verb ending echoes the pronoun: "you all" = أَنْتُمْ/ـكُمْ/ـتُمْ (the …م family); "we" = نَحْنُ/ـنَا/ـنَا (the …نا family). Only these two endings, only the three known verbs.
Example
أَنْتَ ذَهَبْتَ → أَنْتُمْ ذَهَبْتُمْ . — أَذَهَبْتُمْ إِلَى الْمُسْتَشْفَى ؟ — نَعَمْ ، ذَهَبْنَا . anta dhahabta → antum dhahabtum. — a-dhahabtum ilā l-mustashfā? — naʿam, dhahabnā. · "You went → you all went. — Did you all go to the hospital? — Yes, we went."

4 · ★ The creedal sentence — نَحْنُ مُسْلِمُونَ …

The lesson's centrepiece (Exercise 2, p.84): a sentence built almost entirely out of today's tail ـنَا ("our") — six times in one breath. It lets a beginner state the whole foundation of their faith in a single Arabic sentence. Make this the line the class memorises and recites together.

Where this fits: The payoff of Concept 2. Every ـنَا the class just learned, used at once: رَبُّنَا, دِينُنَا, رَسُولُنَا, كِتَابُنَا, قِبْلَتُنَا, لُغَتُنَا.

The lesson's creedal sentence (from Exercise 2)

نَحْنُ مُسْلِمُونَ . اللهُ رَبُّنَا وَالإِسْلَامُ دِينُنَا وَالنَّبِيُّ مُحَمَّدٌ ﷺ رَسُولُنَا وَالقُرْآنُ الكَرِيمُ كِتَابُنَا وَالكَعْبَةُ قِبْلَتُنَا وَالعَرَبِيَّةُ لُغَتُنَا .

naḥnu muslimūn. allāhu rabbunā wa-l-islāmu dīnunā wa-n-nabiyyu muḥammadun ﷺ rasūlunā wa-l-qurʾānu l-karīmu kitābunā wa-l-kaʿbatu qiblatunā wa-l-ʿarabiyyatu lughatunā.

"We are Muslims. Allah is our Lord, Islam is our religion, the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ is our Messenger, the Noble Qur'an is our Book, the Kaʿbah is our qiblah, and Arabic is our language."

💡 Recite it line by line, then count the ـنَاs

Build it up phrase by phrase (اللهُ رَبُّنَا … وَالإِسْلَامُ دِينُنَا …) until the whole class can say it together. Then have them count: six ـنَاs. End on the quiet gift — وَالعَرَبِيَّةُ لُغَتُنَا: "the Arabic you came here to learn is our language." Yehi jumla zabani yaad karwao.

5 · Reading the names — Prophets & foreign (اقْرَأْ وَاكْتُبْ)

The lesson closes with a pure reading-and-copying drill (Exercise 5, p.85): foreign place-names, two foreign personal names, and a roll-call of Prophets and angels. Recognising the Prophets' names in Arabic is a small joy in itself — and a natural ـكُمْ/ـنَا break.

⚠️ Common Student Mistakes — Watch For These

1. Forgetting the five-nouns shape on أَب/أَخ

The tails attach cleanly to ordinary nouns (بَيْتُكُمْ), but أَب/أَخ keep the Ch 10 long-vowel shape: أَبُوكُمْ (not أَبْكُمْ), أَخُونَا. Recall Ch 10's أَبُوكَ; don't re-teach.

2. Mixing up ـكُمْ ("your") and ـنَا ("our")

Two new tails arriving together. Anchor each to a gesture — ـكُمْ = point out at them, ـنَا = hand on chest — and drill back-and-forth Q-and-A.

3. Not changing the verb for "you all"

عَلَيْكُمْ makes ـكُمْ easy, but the verb ending ـتُمْ is new — they say أَنْتُمْ ذَهَبَ ✗. Teach the "…م family" together: أَنْتُمْ — بَيْتُكُمْ — ذَهَبْتُمْ.

4. Treating ـنَا as gendered

Because ـكُمْ is "for men," they expect a separate "women's we." There isn't one — ـنَا is the same for everyone. Sell it as a free win.

5. Adding a tanween to the Prophets'/foreign names

13 chapters of tanween-taking nouns make them say دَاوُدٌ ✗. These names end in a plain ـُ — read دَاوُدُ as printed. Read-aloud habit; the "why" is deferred.

6. Leaking the women's "you all" (the L15 form)

A classmate may guess a feminine "you all" by analogy. Park it warmly: "that one's next lesson — we'll finish the whole chart then."

📿 Connect to the Qur'an

Today's forms — أَنْتُمْ, ـكُمْ, نَحْنُ, ـنَا — fill the Qur'an. Three verses your class can connect to the lesson's own words (they echo the creedal sentence's رَبُّنَا / دِينُنَا).

Surah Al-Kāfirūn · 109:3, 6 — أَنْتُمْ ("you all") + ـكُمْ ("your")

وَلَا أَنتُمْ عَابِدُونَ مَا أَعْبُدُ … لَكُمْ دِينُكُمْ وَلِيَ دِينِ

wa-lā antum ʿābidūna mā aʿbud … lakum dīnukum wa-liya dīn — "…nor are you (all) worshippers of what I worship … For you is your religion, and for me is my religion."

Students recognise: أَنْتُمْ = "you all" (Concept 1), and دِينُكُمْ = "your religion" — the tail ـكُمْ, the mirror of the creedal دِينُنَا ("our religion").

Surah Al-Ḥijr · 15:9 — نَحْنُ ("we") + the verb ـنَا

إِنَّا نَحْنُ نَزَّلْنَا الذِّكْرَ وَإِنَّا لَهُ لَحَافِظُونَ

innā naḥnu nazzalnā dh-dhikra wa-innā lahu la-ḥāfiẓūn — "Indeed, it is We who sent down the Reminder, and indeed We will be its guardian."

Students recognise: نَحْنُ = "we" (Concept 2), and نَزَّلْنَا carries the verb-tail ـنَا ("we sent down") — the same ـنَا as ذَهَبْنَا (Concept 3). Plus a sound plural حَافِظُونَ (Ch 13).

Surah Al-Baqarah · 2:201 — the tail ـنَا ("our / us")

رَبَّنَا آتِنَا فِي الدُّنْيَا حَسَنَةً وَفِي الْآخِرَةِ حَسَنَةً وَقِنَا عَذَابَ النَّارِ

rabbanā ātinā fī d-dunyā ḥasanatan wa-fī l-ākhirati ḥasanatan wa-qinā ʿadhāba n-nār — "Our Lord, give us good in this world and good in the Hereafter, and protect us from the punishment of the Fire." (the duʿāʾ within 2:201)

Students recognise: رَبَّنَا = "our Lord" — the very phrase from the creedal sentence (اللهُ رَبُّنَا) — and ـنَا again on آتِنَا / قِنَا ("give us / protect us").
