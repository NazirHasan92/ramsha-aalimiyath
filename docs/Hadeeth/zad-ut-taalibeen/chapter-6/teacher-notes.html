Teaching arc: the chapter trains the believer in three movements. First, aim your hunger — be insatiable for knowledge, not the world (#51); spend your courage on a word of truth (#52) and your time in Allah's path (#53); arm yourself with understanding, which Satan fears most (#54). Then, honour your ties — Allah's pleasure runs through your father's (#56), and the elder brother carries a father-like right (#57). Finally, guard your heart — fill your record with istighfār (#55), keep returning because all of us fall (#58), make your worship sincere not empty (#59), and prune away what does not concern you (#60).

Chapter ka safar: bhook (sahi rukh do) → ʿizzat (rishtay nibhaao) → dil (saaf aur sacha rakho). In sab ke neeche ek dhaaga: ikhlāṣ — sab kuch Allah ke liye.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Recite each of the 10 aḥādīth in Arabic (the matn of #57 is read from the book), with correct meaning in English + Roman Urdu.
  2. Name the source of each ḥadīth (Bayhaqī, Abū Dāwūd, Tirmidhī, Ibn Mājah, Bukhārī, Muslim, Dārimī, Aḥmad).
  3. Explain #51's two directions of one appetite — hunger for knowledge ennobles, hunger for the world destroys — and that even knowledge must be sought for Allah.
  4. State why #52 calls a word of truth before a tyrant the highest jihād — and why it is not a license for chaos or rebellion.
  5. Give the two readings of #53 (the reward lasts; it outweighs even total charity).
  6. Explain #54's narrow comparison — understanding is the sharper weapon against Satan — without belittling worship.
  7. State #56's principle (Allah's pleasure runs through the father's) and its two limits — no obedience in sin, and the mother is not lowered.
  8. Explain #57 — the elder brother carries a father-like right of respect and care.
  9. Explain #55 (rejoice in abundant, sincere istighfār) and #58 (all sin; the best return — not a permission to sin).
  10. Use #59 as a mirror for sincerity (not a verdict on others), and state #60 as the focused, pruned life — the deep thread of the chapter being ikhlāṣ.

Ḥadīth 51 · مَنْهُومَانِ لَا يَشْبَعَانِ · Two greedy ones are never satisfied

Theme 1 — Aim your hunger. One appetite, two opposite destinations.

مَنْهُومَانِ لَا يَشْبَعَانِ، مَنْهُومٌ فِي الْعِلْمِ لَا يَشْبَعُ مِنْهُ، وَمَنْهُومٌ فِي الدُّنْيَا لَا يَشْبَعُ مِنْهَا

manhūmāni lā yashbaʿān, manhūmun fī l-ʿilmi lā yashbaʿu minhu, wa manhūmun fī l-dunyā lā yashbaʿu minhā

"Two greedy people are never satiated: one who is greedy for knowledge can never get enough of it, and one who is greedy for worldly possessions can never get enough of them."

"Do (manhūm) log kabhi nahin bharte: ʿilm ka talabgaar, aur dunya ka talabgaar."

Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān)
Where this fits: Theme 1, node 1 — opens the chapter and links back to Ch 5 #49 (love of the world = root of every sin). Here is its mechanism: a hunger that can never be filled.

مَنْهُوم · manhūm — "one with an insatiable appetite"

Meaning
A person who can never be filled. The ḥadīth takes this single image and points it in two opposite directions: a hunger for ʿilm that lifts you, and a hunger for dunyā that ruins you. Same restlessness, opposite destination.
Roman Urdu
Woh shakhs jiska pet kabhi na bhare. Hadith ek hi tasveer ko do ulti samton mein modti hai: ʿilm ki bhook jo buland kare, aur dunya ki bhook jo barbaad kare. Wahi bechaini, ulti manzil.
The redirection
The lesson is not to kill the appetite but to re-aim it. Take that same bottomless drive and point it at knowledge of Allah. It links straight to Ch 5 #49 — the worldly hunger is the love of the world that roots every sin.
⚠️ The catch
Even the praised hunger must be for Allah. Knowledge gathered for pride, debate, or worldly position is just dunyā in a scholar's cloak. The chapter's deep thread — ikhlāṣ — is already here.

⚠️ Landmine 1 — "so greed is good?"

Pre-empt: "Yeh laalach ki taʿreef nahin. Ek bhook jo kabhi nahin bharti — do raste: ʿilm ki bhook tumhe buland karti hai, dunya ki bhook (yahi pichle chapter wali 'dunya ki mohabbat') barbaad karti hai. Bhook ko maaro mat — uska rukh badlo. Aur ʿilm bhi tab achha jab Allah ke liye ho, ghuroor ya maqaam ke liye nahin."

وَقُلْ رَبِّ زِدْنِي عِلْمًا

"And say: My Lord, increase me in knowledge."

Why this fits: Allah teaches His Prophet ﷺ to ask for more knowledge — the one hunger the believer is taught to never stop feeding. The very prayer of the insatiable seeker of #51.

Urdu mein: Allah apne Nabi ﷺ ko sikhaata hai: "aur ʿilm maang." Yahi #51 ki achhi bhook ki duʿā hai.

Ḥadīth 52 · أَفْضَلُ الْجِهَادِ مَنْ قَالَ كَلِمَةَ حَقٍّ عِنْدَ سُلْطَانٍ جَائِرٍ · The best jihād is a word of truth before a tyrant

Theme 1 — Aim your hunger. Moral courage, for Allah's sake.

أَفْضَلُ الْجِهَادِ مَنْ قَالَ كَلِمَةَ حَقٍّ عِنْدَ سُلْطَانٍ جَائِرٍ

afḍalu l-jihādi man qāla kalimata ḥaqqin ʿinda sulṭānin jāʾir

"The most virtuous jihād is when one speaks a word of truth before an unjust ruler."

"Sab se afzal jihād zaalim hukmraan ke saamne haq baat kehna hai."

Abū Dāwūd, Tirmidhī, Ibn Mājah
Where this fits: Theme 1 — the believer's courage, rightly aimed. A quiet jihād that can shelter a whole people.

Why this is the highest jihād

Meaning
We picture jihād as the battlefield; the Prophet ﷺ names something quieter and, in one respect, higher — speaking the truth to a tyrant's face. The book's two reasons: (1) the speaker places himself in real danger, like a warrior; (2) if his word stops the oppression, the benefit reaches a far larger group than killing combatants ever could.
Roman Urdu
Jihād se maydaan-e-jang yaad aata hai; Rasool ﷺ ne ek khaamosh magar buland jihād bataaya — zaalim ke saamne haq kehna. Do wajhain: (1) kehne wala jaan khatre mein daalta hai, sipaahi ki tarah; (2) agar baat zulm rok de, faayda bohot baray logon tak pohanchta hai.
⚠️ The boundary
This is about moral courage, not reckless confrontation or rebellion. The fuller fiqh of how/when to correct a ruler is a large subject — stay with the moral point: truthful speech, when genuinely for Allah and shielding people, is the noblest struggle.
The ikhlāṣ thread
The merit is in saying it for Allah and for the oppressed — accepting the risk — not to be seen as a hero. The chapter's deep current runs through this ḥadīth.

⚠️ Landmine 2 — "truth before a tyrant" = license to rebel

Smart or fiery classmates may weaponise this. "Yeh akhlaqi himmat ki taʿreef hai — fasaad ya baghaawat ka naara nahin. Fazīlat us haq mein hai jo Allah ke liye, khatra qubool karte hue, zaalim ke saamne kaha jaaye — taake zulm ruke. Yeh hangaama khara karne, ya kisi ko zaleel karne, ya hikmat chhodne ki ijaazat nahin."

Ḥadīth 53 · لَغَدْوَةٌ فِي سَبِيلِ اللهِ · A morning or evening in Allah's path is better than the world

Theme 1 — Aim your hunger. Time given to Allah outweighs the whole world.

لَغَدْوَةٌ فِي سَبِيلِ اللهِ أَوْ رَوْحَةٌ خَيْرٌ مِنَ الدُّنْيَا وَمَا فِيهَا

la-ghadwatun fī sabīli Llāhi aw rawḥatun khayrun mina l-dunyā wa mā fīhā

"A morning [journey] or an evening [journey] in the path of Allah is better than the world and whatever it contains."

"Allah ke raaste mein ek subah ya ek shaam guzaarna dunya aur jo kuch usme hai, us se behtar hai."

Bukhārī, Muslim
Where this fits: Theme 1 — pairs with #52: both elevate effort for Allah over the comfort and goods of the world.

غَدْوَة / رَوْحَة · ghadwah / rawḥah — a morning / an evening spent for Allah

Meaning
A ghadwah (a morning's going-out) or a rawḥah (an evening's) spent in the path of Allah is declared better than the entire world. The book gives two readings: it lasts where the world perishes; and it out-weighs even giving away the whole world in charity.
Roman Urdu
Allah ke raaste mein ek subah (ghadwah) ya ek shaam (rawḥah) — poori dunya se behtar. Do wajhain: ajr hamesha rehta hai (dunya fanaa ho jaati hai); aur agar koi poori dunya sadaqah kar de, tab bhi yeh us se bara ajr hai.
What "Allah's path" includes
Its primary sense is striving/jihād in His cause; the spirit the chapter builds is broader — any stretch of time genuinely spent for Allah (striving, seeking knowledge, serving the dīn) is being weighed against the whole dunyā, and winning.
The takeaway
Allah ko diya waqt, Allah ke liye diye maal se bhi qeemti. Dunya ke aaraam par Allah ke liye mehnat ko tarjeeh do.

"Say: the enjoyment of this world is little, and the Hereafter is better for the one who fears Allah."

Why this fits: The Qurʾān weighs the dunyā's enjoyment against the Ākhirah and calls it little — exactly the scale on which #53 places a morning or evening for Allah above the whole world.

Ḥadīth 54 · فَقِيهٌ وَاحِدٌ أَشَدُّ عَلَى الشَّيْطَانِ مِنْ أَلْفِ عَابِدٍ · One jurist is harder on Satan than a thousand worshippers

Theme 1 — Aim your hunger. Understanding is eyesight in spiritual warfare.

فَقِيهٌ وَاحِدٌ أَشَدُّ عَلَى الشَّيْطَانِ مِنْ أَلْفِ عَابِدٍ

faqīhun wāḥidun ashaddu ʿalā l-shayṭāni min alfi ʿābid

"A single jurist [person of understanding in the dīn] is more severe upon Satan than a thousand worshippers."

"Ek faqīh (dīn ki samajh wala) Shaytaan par hazaar ʿaabidon se zyada bhaari hai."

Tirmidhī, Ibn Mājah
Where this fits: Theme 1 — completes the case for knowledge: it is the believer's weapon against Satan. The most misused ḥadīth in the chapter — teach the boundary carefully.

فَقِيه · faqīh — "one with understanding of the dīn"

Meaning
Read the exact battleground: against Satan. The person of understanding can recognise Satan's ploys, warn others, and enjoin good. The ordinary worshipper — sincere, but without that understanding — is more easily caught because he cannot see the traps coming. Knowledge is eyesight in spiritual warfare.
Roman Urdu
Maydaan dekho: Shaytaan ke khilaaf. Samajh wala Shaytaan ke daav pehchaan leta hai, doosron ko khabardaar karta hai, neki ka hukm deta hai. ʿAam ʿaabid — mukhlis magar samajh ke baghair — aasani se phans jaata hai, kyunki jaal use dikhta hi nahin. ʿIlm rooh ki jang mein aankh hai.
⚠️ What it does NOT say
Not that worship is worthless, nor that the worshipper is lesser as a person, nor that knowledge without worship suffices. The comparison is narrow: against Satan, understanding is the sharper weapon. ʿIlm and ʿibādah travel together.
Who is a "faqīh"
One who understands, acts, and warns — not someone holding a title or hoarding information (recall #51's warning). It is living understanding, not a credential.

⚠️ Landmine 3 — "worship is worthless / the worshipper is lesser"

The most misused ḥadīth here. "Yeh ʿibādat ko bekaar nahin keh rahi, na ʿaabid ko kam-tar. Maydaan ek hi: Shaytaan ke khilaaf. Faqīh Shaytaan ke daav pehchaan kar bachaata hai; mukhlis magar samajh ke baghair ʿaabid phans jaata hai kyunki jaal dikhta nahin. ʿIlm aur ʿibādat saath chalte hain — yeh bas batati hai Shaytaan kis se zyada darta hai."

Ḥadīth 56 · رِضَى الرَّبِّ فِي رِضَى الْوَالِدِ · The Lord's pleasure is in the father's pleasure

Theme 2 — Honour your ties. Allah ties His own pleasure to a parent's — hold the two limits.

رِضَى الرَّبِّ فِي رِضَى الْوَالِدِ وَسَخَطُ الرَّبِّ فِي سَخَطِ الْوَالِدِ

riḍā l-rabbi fī riḍā l-wālid, wa sakhaṭu l-rabbi fī sakhaṭi l-wālid

"The pleasure of the Lord (Allah) is in the pleasure of the father, and the displeasure of the Lord is in the displeasure of the father."

"Rabb ki khushi baap ki khushi mein hai, aur Rabb ki naaraazi baap ki naaraazi mein hai."

Tirmidhī
Where this fits: Theme 2 opens — the rights Allah ties to His own. (The authentic matn says al-Rabb, "the Lord", which the translator renders "Allah" — both correct.)

رِضَى الرَّبِّ · riḍā l-rabb — "the Lord's pleasure"

Meaning
Not merely "be kind to your father" — Allah's own riḍā (pleasure) is bound up with the father's, and His sakhaṭ (displeasure) with the father's. Pleasing your father becomes a route to pleasing Allah; angering him, a route to His anger. The relationship is lifted to a matter of your standing with Allah.
Roman Urdu
Sirf "baap se achha salook" nahin — Allah ki apni riḍā baap ki riḍā ke saath bandhi hai. Baap ko khush karna Allah ko khush karne ka raasta; naaraaz karna Allah ki naaraazi ka raasta. Maamla Allah ke saath taʿalluq ka ban jaata hai.
⚠️ Limit 1 — no obedience in sin
If a parent commands something sinful, that command is not obeyed — there is no obedience to a creature in disobedience of the Creator (itself a ḥadīth of this book, at #75 — a later chapter). You still treat the parent with kindness and respect; you simply do not obey the sin.
⚠️ Limit 2 — the mother is not lowered
Naming the father is not a ranking of father over mother. Other authentic narrations place the mother's right above the father's ("your mother" three times, then "your father"). Both parents' pleasure is a treasure.

⚠️ Landmine 4 — "obey the father in everything" / "the mother counts less"

Two errors at once. "Pehli: Allah ki khushi baap ki khushi mein — magar agar baap gunaah ka hukm de, woh hukm nahin maana jaayega (Khaaliq ki naafarmaani mein kisi ki itaaʿat nahin); phir bhi narmi aur ehtiraam baaqi. Doosri: 'baap' kehna maa ko kam nahin karta — doosri sahih hadith mein maa ka haq baap se zyada hai (teen baar 'teri maa', phir 'tera baap')."

📿 Connect to Qurʾān · 17:23

"Your Lord has decreed that you worship none but Him, and to parents, good treatment."

Seniority within the family

Meaning
Just as the father has a great right over his children, the eldest brother — who in many households helps raise, protect, and provide for the younger ones — carries a father-like right of respect over them.

The unstated flip side: the elder owes the younger the care a father owes — honour flows up, care flows down. Not a one-way demand.

Ḥadīth 55 · طُوبَى لِمَنْ وَجَدَ فِي صَحِيفَتِهِ اسْتِغْفَارًا كَثِيرًا · Glad tidings for him with abundant istighfār in his record

طُوبَى / اسْتِغْفَار · ṭūbā / istighfār — "glad tidings / seeking forgiveness"

Meaning
Ṭūbā is a word of glad tidings ("blessedness"; the scholars say also a tree in Paradise). The Prophet ﷺ gives it to whoever finds his book of deeds full of istighfār — the sincere forgiveness-asking Allah accepted, presented to him on the Day of Judgement as a source of joy.
Roman Urdu
Ṭūbā khushkhabri ka lafz (Jannat ke darakht ka naam bhi). Rasool ﷺ yeh us ko de rahe hain jo Qayaamat ke din apna aʿmaal-naama istighfār se bhara paaye — woh sacha istighfār jo Allah ne qubool kiya.
"Abundant" + "sincere"
Istighfār is not points racked up while heedless — it is a turning of the heart, repeated often. Make it a daily habit: after every prayer, when you slip, before sleep.
The image
Picture the most terrifying day, the records opened — and there, page after page, your "astaghfiru-Llāh." The cheapest investment with the richest return.

خَطَّاء / تَوَّاب · khaṭṭāʾ / tawwāb — "constant sinner / constant repenter"

Meaning
Half humbling: all children of Adam are khaṭṭāʾ — prone to error by nature (the book's one exception: only the prophets are maʿṣūm, infallible). Half hopeful: the best of those sinners are al-tawwābūn — those who keep returning. The door is repentance, open to all.
Roman Urdu
Aadhi aajzi: sab bani Aadam khaṭṭāʾ hain (sirf anbiyā maʿṣūm). Aadhi umeed: in mein behtareen al-tawwābūn — jo baar baar palatte hain. Darwaza tawba hai, sab ke liye khula.
⚠️ Not a permission slip
"All sin" is a statement of our weakness — given so we don't despair and don't judge each other harshly — not a relaxing of the standard. The praise (khayr) is on the returning, never on the sinning.
Recall #20
From an earlier chapter: one who repents from sin is like one who has no sin. Repentance genuinely wipes the slate. So: don't despair when you fall, but don't make peace with the fall — return.

⚠️ Landmine 5 — "everyone sins anyway, so what's the harm?"

The hopeful ḥadīth's trap. "'Sab gunaah karte hain' ka matlab yeh nahin ke 'gunaah theek hai.' Taʿreef gunaah par nahin — tawba par hai. Yeh baat isliye kahi gayi taake hum girne par maayoos na hon aur ek doosre ko sakhti se judge na karein — meyaar narm karne ke liye nahin. (Aur #20: sache dil se tawba karne wala aise hai jaise gunaah kiya hi nahin.)"

إِنَّ اللَّهَ يُحِبُّ التَّوَّابِينَ وَيُحِبُّ الْمُتَطَهِّرِينَ

"Indeed, Allah loves those who turn to Him in repentance, and loves those who purify themselves."

Why this fits: The Qurʾān names the very ones #58 calls "the best" — al-tawwābūn — as beloved to Allah. Returning is not merely accepted; it is loved.

Urdu mein: Quran unhi ko jinhe #58 "behtareen" kehti hai — al-tawwābūn — Allah ka mehboob qaraar deta hai. Tawba sirf qubool nahin — pyaari hai.

Ḥadīth 59 · كَمْ مِنْ صَائِمٍ · How many who fast gain nothing but hunger

Theme 3 — Guard your heart. The chapter's clearest statement of the ikhlāṣ thread.

كَمْ مِنْ صَائِمٍ لَيْسَ لَهُ مِنْ صِيَامِهِ إِلَّا الظَّمَأُ وَكَمْ مِنْ قَائِمٍ لَيْسَ لَهُ مِنْ قِيَامِهِ إِلَّا السَّهَرُ

kam min ṣāʾimin laysa lahu min ṣiyāmihi illā l-ẓamaʾ, wa kam min qāʾimin laysa lahu min qiyāmihi illā l-sahar

"How many there are who fast but gain nothing from it but hunger, and how many there are who stand in prayer at night but gain nothing from it but sleeplessness."

"Kitne roza-daar hain jinhe rozay se sirf bhook milti hai, aur kitne raat ko khaday rehne wale jinhe sirf bedaari milti hai."

Dārimī

📖 Matn note — "thirst" vs "hunger"

The book's printed Arabic reads إِلَّا الظَّمَأُ (illā l-ẓamaʾ, "nothing but thirst"), while its English renders it "hunger." We keep the Arabic exactly as the book prints it and the English exactly as the book translates it; both are authentic wordings of this ḥadīth and the lesson is identical. (Kitaab ki Arabic mein ẓamaʾ = pyaas, English mein "hunger" = bhook — dono asal, baat aik.)

Where this fits: Theme 3 — the sharpest warning in the chapter: the form of worship can be full while the soul of it is empty.

When the form is full but the heart is empty

Meaning
A person fasts all day, another stands all night — and the Prophet ﷺ says some walk away with nothing but hunger and nothing but sleeplessness. The book names the two ways: (1) the faster who fasts without seeking Allah's reward or without abstaining from sin; (2) the night-worshipper whose motive is to show off (riyāʾ). The form is complete; the soul — sincerity — is missing.
Roman Urdu
Ek din bhar roza, doosra raat bhar namaz — magar kuch ke haath sirf bhook aur sirf bedaari. Kitaab do soorat batati hai: (1) roza bila niyyat-e-ajr ya gunaah na chhodte hue; (2) raat ki ʿibādat dikhaave (riyāʾ) ke liye. ʿIbādat ki shakl poori, rooh — ikhlāṣ — ghaayab.
⚠️ Don't despair
The book is careful: the minimal obligation is still discharged by such a fast — you are not commanded to repeat it. This is a warning to raise your sincerity, not a verdict that your worship is void.
⚠️ A mirror, not a telescope
You cannot see another's intention — only Allah can. Turn the ḥadīth on yourself: am I doing this for Allah, or for hunger's sake, or for eyes?

⚠️ Landmine 6 — "my worship is worthless" / "look how fake he is"

Two opposite misreadings. "Ek: 'Shaayad meri saari ibādat bekaar' — nahin, farz to ada ho gaya; yeh tambeeh hai ke ikhlāṣ barhaao. Doosri: 'Woh to dikhaava karta hai' — yeh hadith aaina hai, door-been nahin; doosre ki niyyat sirf Allah jaanta hai. Ise apne aap par mod do: 'Main yeh Allah ke liye kar raha hoon, ya bhook/nazaron ke liye?'"

مَا لَا يَعْنِيهِ · mā lā yaʿnīh — "what does not concern him"

Meaning
Ḥusn al-islām — the excellence/beauty of a person's Islam — shows in one habit: he leaves the nonessential — talk, involvement, matters that are simply not his business and bring him no nearer to Allah.
Roman Urdu
Ḥusn al-islām — kisi ke Islam ki khoobsurati — ek ʿaadat se zaahir: woh chhod deta hai jo us se taʿalluq nahin — faaltu baatein, faaltu dakhal, woh maamlaat jo Allah ke qareeb nahin laate.
The book's framing
A person should be constantly occupied with fulfilling Allah's commands, and let go of everything that does not earn Allah's pleasure and is not essential for this world or the next. The reward named is enormous: endless bliss and salvation in the Hereafter.
⚠️ Focus, not withdrawal
"What does not concern you" means the futile — gossip, prying, meddling — not your real duties (family, enjoining good where it is your concern). The ḥadīth is about pruning the wasteful so your energy goes to what matters — not becoming cold or isolated.

⚠️ Common student confusions — watch for these

1. "Greed is being praised" (#51)

One insatiable appetite, two directions: hunger for knowledge ennobles; hunger for the world destroys (= Ch 5's love of the world). Re-aim the drive, don't kill it — and even knowledge must be for Allah.

2. "Truth before a tyrant" = rebellion (#52)

A ḥadīth of moral courage, not a slogan for chaos, humiliation, or revolt. The virtue is truth said for Allah and for the oppressed, accepting the risk.

3. "Worship is worthless" (#54)

The comparison is narrow: against Satan, understanding is the sharper weapon. It does not belittle worship or the worshipper. ʿIlm and ʿibādah travel together.

4. "Obey the father in everything" / "the mother counts less" (#56)

Two limits: no obedience to a parent in sin (kindness remains), and naming the father does not lower the mother — her right is even greater in other narrations.

5. "Everyone sins anyway, so what's the harm?" (#58)

The praise is on returning (tawba), never on the sinning. "All sin" guards against despair and harsh judgement — it is not a relaxing of the standard.

6. "My worship is worthless" / judging others (#59)

The minimal obligation is discharged; this is a call to raise sincerity, not a verdict of void. And it is a mirror for yourself — not a telescope for others' intentions.

7. "Leave what doesn't concern you" = withdraw from people (#60)

Mā lā yaʿnīh is the futile — gossip, prying, meddling — not real duties to family or the dīn. It is focus, not isolation.

8. Forgetting the thread — ikhlāṣ

The knowledge of #51, the truth of #52, the worship of #59, the letting-go of #60 are each only worth anything when done for Allah. Keep returning to the thread so the ten don't feel scattered.

وَمَا أُمِرُوا إِلَّا لِيَعْبُدُوا اللَّهَ مُخْلِصِينَ لَهُ الدِّينَ

"They were commanded only to worship Allah, [being] sincere to Him in religion."

Why this fits: The Qurʾānic statement of ikhlāṣ — the soul of worship #59 warns is missing in the empty fast and the show-off vigil. The thread of the whole chapter.

Urdu mein: Ikhlāṣ ka Qurʾānic bayan — wahi rooh jo #59 ke khaali rozay aur dikhaave ki ibādat mein ghaayab hai. Poore chapter ka dhaaga.
