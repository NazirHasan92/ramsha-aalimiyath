Chapter 6 — Student Handout

Aḥādīth 51–60 from Part 1 of Zad ut-Taalibeen · aim your hunger, honour your ties, guard your heart

👋 Welcome — what we'll learn today

Today we cover ten short aḥādīth of the Prophet ﷺ that, read together, answer one question: where should a believer point his hunger, his honour, and his heart? They move through three movements: aim your hunger (what to strive for), honour your ties (parents and elders), and guard your heart (sincerity and repentance).

Aaj ka sabaq — das choti hadithein jo ek sawaal ka jawaab deti hain: momin apni bhook, ʿizzat, aur dil kahaan lagaaye? Teen harkatein: bhook (kis ke piche bhaago), ʿizzat (kis ka haq pehchaano), dil (use saaf aur sacha kaise rakho).

The 10 fall into three themes:

  1. Aim your hunger (#51, #52, #53, #54) — be greedy for knowledge not the world, the best jihād is truth before a tyrant, a morning in Allah's path beats the world, and one person of understanding is harder on Satan than a thousand worshippers.
  2. Honour your ties (#56, #57) — Allah's pleasure runs through the father's, and the elder brother carries a father-like right.
  3. Guard your heart (#55, #58, #59, #60) — rejoice in abundant istighfār, all sin but the best repent, worship without sincerity gains nothing, and excellence is leaving what does not concern you.

The deep thread beneath all ten: ikhlāṣ — doing it for Allah, sincerely.

(We take the aḥādīth by theme, so #55 sits with the "heart" group at the end. The number and source travel with each one.)

Ḥadīth 51 · Two greedy ones are never satisfied

مَنْهُومَانِ لَا يَشْبَعَانِ، مَنْهُومٌ فِي الْعِلْمِ لَا يَشْبَعُ مِنْهُ، وَمَنْهُومٌ فِي الدُّنْيَا لَا يَشْبَعُ مِنْهَا

manhūmāni lā yashbaʿān, manhūmun fī l-ʿilmi lā yashbaʿu minhu, wa manhūmun fī l-dunyā lā yashbaʿu minhā

"Two greedy people are never satiated: one who is greedy for knowledge can never get enough of it, and one who is greedy for worldly possessions can never get enough of them."

"Do (manhūm) log kabhi nahin bharte: ʿilm ka talabgaar, aur dunya ka talabgaar."

Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān)

One appetite, two destinations. The ḥadīth takes one image — a hunger that can never be filled — and points it two opposite ways. A hunger for knowledge lifts you; a hunger for the world destroys you (this is the "love of the world" from last chapter). The lesson is not to kill your appetite — it is to re-aim it toward knowledge of Allah.

⚠️ The catch

Even the praised hunger must be for Allah. Knowledge collected for pride, debate, or worldly position is just the world wearing a scholar's cloak.

ʿIlm bhi tab achha jab Allah ke liye ho — ghuroor ya maqaam ke liye nahin.

Ḥadīth 52 · The best jihād is a word of truth before a tyrant

أَفْضَلُ الْجِهَادِ مَنْ قَالَ كَلِمَةَ حَقٍّ عِنْدَ سُلْطَانٍ جَائِرٍ

afḍalu l-jihādi man qāla kalimata ḥaqqin ʿinda sulṭānin jāʾir

"The most virtuous jihād is when one speaks a word of truth before an unjust ruler."

"Sab se afzal jihād zaalim hukmraan ke saamne haq baat kehna hai."

Abū Dāwūd, Tirmidhī, Ibn Mājah

A quiet jihād that shelters many. We think of jihād as the battlefield; here the Prophet ﷺ names something quieter and higher — telling the truth to a tyrant's face. Two reasons: the speaker puts himself in real danger, like a warrior; and if his word stops the oppression, the benefit reaches a far larger group than a battlefield victory. One brave sentence can shelter a whole people.

⚠️ Courage, not chaos

This is about moral courage for Allah's sake — not a license to stir trouble, humiliate, or rebel. The virtue is the truth, said sincerely for Allah and for the oppressed, accepting the risk.

Yeh akhlaqi himmat hai — fasaad ya baghaawat nahin. Haq, Allah ke liye, mazloom ke liye.

Ḥadīth 53 · A morning or evening in Allah's path beats the world

لَغَدْوَةٌ فِي سَبِيلِ اللهِ أَوْ رَوْحَةٌ خَيْرٌ مِنَ الدُّنْيَا وَمَا فِيهَا

la-ghadwatun fī sabīli Llāhi aw rawḥatun khayrun mina l-dunyā wa mā fīhā

"A morning [journey] or an evening [journey] in the path of Allah is better than the world and whatever it contains."

"Allah ke raaste mein ek subah ya ek shaam dunya aur jo kuch usme hai, us se behtar hai."

Bukhārī, Muslim

Two ways to read it. A morning (ghadwah) or evening (rawḥah) spent in Allah's path beats the whole world — because (1) its reward is eternal while the world is transient; and (2) even if a person gave the entire world away in charity, this stretch of time for Allah would be greater. Time given to Allah outweighs even wealth given for Allah.

💡 The point

"Allah's path" centres on striving in His cause, but the spirit is broad: any time genuinely spent for Allah — striving, learning, serving His dīn — is being weighed against the whole dunyā, and winning.

Allah ko diya waqt, Allah ke liye diye maal se bhi qeemti.

Ḥadīth 54 · One jurist is harder on Satan than a thousand worshippers

فَقِيهٌ وَاحِدٌ أَشَدُّ عَلَى الشَّيْطَانِ مِنْ أَلْفِ عَابِدٍ

faqīhun wāḥidun ashaddu ʿalā l-shayṭāni min alfi ʿābid

"A single jurist [person of understanding] is more severe upon Satan than a thousand worshippers."

"Ek faqīh (dīn ki samajh wala) Shaytaan par hazaar ʿaabidon se zyada bhaari hai."

Tirmidhī, Ibn Mājah

Understanding is eyesight. Note the battleground: against Satan. A person of understanding can recognise Satan's tricks, warn others, and enjoin good. The ordinary worshipper — sincere, but without that understanding — is more easily caught, because he cannot see the traps coming. In spiritual warfare, knowledge is sight.

⚠️ This does NOT belittle worship

The comparison is narrow — against Satan, understanding is the sharper weapon. It does not say worship is worthless or the worshipper is a lesser person. Knowledge and worship belong together; a "faqīh" is one who understands, acts, and warns — not a title-holder.

ʿIbādat bekaar nahin — bas Shaytaan samajh wale se zyada darta hai. ʿIlm aur ʿibādat saath chalte hain.

Ḥadīth 56 · The Lord's pleasure is in the father's pleasure

رِضَى الرَّبِّ فِي رِضَى الْوَالِدِ وَسَخَطُ الرَّبِّ فِي سَخَطِ الْوَالِدِ

riḍā l-rabbi fī riḍā l-wālid, wa sakhaṭu l-rabbi fī sakhaṭi l-wālid

"The pleasure of the Lord (Allah) is in the pleasure of the father, and the displeasure of the Lord is in the displeasure of the father."

"Rabb ki khushi baap ki khushi mein hai, aur Rabb ki naaraazi baap ki naaraazi mein hai."

Tirmidhī

Your standing with Allah runs through your parent. The Prophet ﷺ does not merely say "be kind to your father" — he says Allah's own pleasure is bound up with the father's. Pleasing him becomes a route to pleasing Allah; angering him, a route to Allah's anger. (The authentic wording says "the Lord" — one of Allah's names — which the translator renders "Allah".)

⚠️ Two limits you must hold

1. No obedience in sin. If a parent commands something ḥarām, that command is not obeyed — there is no obedience to a creature in disobedience of the Creator — though you still treat them with kindness and respect.

2. The mother is not lowered. Naming the father is not a ranking; other authentic narrations place the mother's right above the father's ("your mother" three times, then "your father").

Do kinaare: (1) gunaah mein itaaʿat nahin, magar narmi baaqi; (2) "baap" kehna maa ko kam nahin karta — maa ka haq aur bhi zyada hai.

Ḥadīth 57 · The elder brother's right is like a father's

"The right of an elder brother over the younger ones is like the right of the father over his children."

"Baray bhai ka chhoton par haq waisa hai jaisa baap ka apni aulaad par haq."

Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān)

(The Arabic wording is read directly from the book — we don't reproduce a near-correct version here.)

Seniority within the family carries weight. Just as the father has a great right over his children, the eldest brother — who in many homes helps raise, protect, and provide for the younger ones — carries a father-like right of respect. Honour him; don't treat him as "just another sibling."

💡 Keep the proportion right

"Like a father's right" is about respect and care — not that he is a parent in every ruling. And it is two-way: the elder owes the younger the care a father owes.

"Baap jaisa haq" = ehtiraam aur shafqat; aur yeh do-tarfa hai — bara bhai bhi khayaal rakhe.

Ḥadīth 55 · Glad tidings for him with abundant istighfār

طُوبَى لِمَنْ وَجَدَ فِي صَحِيفَتِهِ اسْتِغْفَارًا كَثِيرًا

ṭūbā li-man wajada fī ṣaḥīfatihi istighfāran kathīrā

"Glad tidings for the one who finds in his record [of deeds] abundant seeking of forgiveness."

"Khushkhabri us ke liye jo apne aʿmaal-naame mein kasrat se istighfār paaye."

Ibn Mājah

The page that delights you on the worst day. Ṭūbā is a word of glad tidings (the scholars say also a tree in Paradise). On the Day of Judgement, the most terrifying day, the records are opened — and blessed is the one who finds his pages full of sincere istighfār that Allah accepted. It is the cheapest investment with the richest return: "astaghfiru-Llāh," repeated through the day.

💡 Make it a habit

Istighfār is not points racked up while heedless — it's a turning of the heart, repeated often. After every prayer, when you slip, before sleep. (And it pairs with the next ḥadīth: because we all sin, we all keep needing to return.)

Istighfār ko rozana ki ʿaadat banao — har namaz ke baad, phisalne par, sone se pehle.

Ḥadīth 58 · All sin; the best are those who repent

كُلُّ بَنِي آدَمَ خَطَّاءٌ وَخَيْرُ الْخَطَّائِينَ التَّوَّابُونَ

kullu banī ādama khaṭṭāʾun wa khayru l-khaṭṭāʾīna l-tawwābūn

"All the children of Adam are sinners, and the best of the sinners are those who repent."

"Tamaam bani Aadam khataa-kaar hain, aur khataa-karon mein behtareen woh jo tawba karte hain."

Tirmidhī

A diagnosis and a door in one line. Half humbling: all of us are prone to error — only the prophets are infallible (maʿṣūm). So if you sin, that does not make you a monster; it makes you a child of Adam. Half hopeful: the best of those who sin are al-tawwābūn — the ones who keep returning. The door of repentance is open to everyone.

⚠️ Not a permission slip

"Everyone sins anyway" is not "so the harm is small." The praise is on the returning, never on the sinning. This guards us against despair and against judging each other — it does not relax the standard. (And remember Ḥadīth 20: one who truly repents is like one who never sinned.)

"Sab gunaah karte hain" ≠ "gunaah theek hai." Taʿreef tawba par hai. Giro to maayoos mat ho — palto; magar gunaah se dosti mat karo.

Ḥadīth 59 · Some who fast gain nothing but hunger

كَمْ مِنْ صَائِمٍ لَيْسَ لَهُ مِنْ صِيَامِهِ إِلَّا الظَّمَأُ وَكَمْ مِنْ قَائِمٍ لَيْسَ لَهُ مِنْ قِيَامِهِ إِلَّا السَّهَرُ

kam min ṣāʾimin laysa lahu min ṣiyāmihi illā l-ẓamaʾ, wa kam min qāʾimin laysa lahu min qiyāmihi illā l-sahar

"How many there are who fast but gain nothing from it but hunger, and how many there are who stand at night in prayer but gain nothing from it but sleeplessness."

"Kitne roza-daar jinhe rozay se sirf bhook milti hai, aur kitne raat ko khaday rehne wale jinhe sirf bedaari."

Dārimī

Note: the book prints the Arabic as إِلَّا الظَّمَأُ ("nothing but thirst") and translates it "hunger" — both are authentic; the meaning is the same.

Full form, empty heart. The body does the work; the heart gets no reward. How? (1) The one who fasts without seeking Allah's reward, or who does not leave sin while fasting; (2) the one whose night prayer is really to show off (riyāʾ). In both, the shape of worship is complete but its soul — sincerity (ikhlāṣ) — is missing. Sincerity is essential in every act of worship.

⚠️ A mirror — not a telescope

Don't despair (the minimal obligation is fulfilled — this is a push to raise sincerity, not a verdict that your worship is void). And don't use it to judge others — only Allah sees intentions. Turn it on yourself: am I doing this for Allah, or for hunger, or for eyes?

Maayoosi nahin (farz ada ho gaya) — ikhlāṣ barhaao. Doosron ko judge nahin — yeh aaina hai, door-been nahin.

Ḥadīth 60 · Excellence of Islam = leaving what does not concern you

مِنْ حُسْنِ إِسْلَامِ الْمَرْءِ تَرْكُهُ مَا لَا يَعْنِيهِ

min ḥusni islāmi l-marʾi tarkuhu mā lā yaʿnīh

"Part of the excellence of a person's Islam is his leaving what does not concern him."

"Aadmi ke Islam ki khoobi mein se hai ke woh use chhod de jo us se taʿalluq nahin rakhta."

Tirmidhī, Aḥmad

A clean, focused life. The beauty of a person's Islam shows in one habit: he leaves the nonessential — idle talk, prying, meddling, anything that brings him no nearer to Allah — and stays occupied with what Allah loves. The reward named is enormous: endless bliss and salvation in the Hereafter. This ḥadīth is so beloved that scholars made it one of the Forty foundational traditions.

⚠️ Focus, not withdrawal

"What does not concern you" means the futile — not your real duties to family, or to enjoin good where it is your concern. The ḥadīth is about pruning the wasteful so your energy goes to what matters — not becoming cold or cut off.

"Jo taʿalluq na rakhe" = faaltu cheezein — na ke asal zimmedaariyan. Yeh tawajjuh hai, kinaara-kashi nahin.

💡 Why it closes the chapter

You cannot chase knowledge, speak truth, honour your parents, and keep a sincere, repenting heart if your hours bleed into what does not concern you. #60 is the one habit that protects all the rest — and the thread under the whole chapter is ikhlāṣ: everything for Allah.

Exercise 4 · True or False

  1. Ḥadīth 51 praises greed in general. —
  2. "A word of truth before a tyrant" is a license to start a rebellion. —
  3. Ḥadīth 54 means that worship is worthless and worshippers are inferior. —
  4. If a father commands something sinful, his command must still be obeyed. —
  5. "All sin" (Ḥadīth 58) means sinning is acceptable since everyone does it. —
  6. A fast done without sincerity still discharges the minimal obligation. —
  7. Ḥadīth 59 should be used to judge whether other people are sincere. —
  8. "Leave what does not concern you" means abandon your family duties. —

Exercise 5 · Reflection — write a sentence or two each

  1. What is one worldly thing you feel an endless hunger for? By Ḥadīth 51, how could you re-aim that same drive toward knowledge of Allah?
  2. Name a small truth you've been afraid to say to someone with power over you. By Ḥadīth 52 — and with wisdom — what is the right thing to do?
  3. Pick one short stretch of time this week to spend purely "in Allah's path" (Ḥadīth 53). When and what?
  4. Is there something your father (or mother) wants from you that you keep delaying? By Ḥadīth 56, what's your next step — and is it free of any disobedience to Allah?
  5. Build a daily istighfār habit (Ḥadīth 55) — when in your day will you say it? Commit to it.
  6. Take one act of worship you do. By Ḥadīth 59, ask honestly: who is it really for? Write what you find — privately.
  7. Name one "thing that does not concern you" that eats your time (Ḥadīth 60). What will you prune this week?
