# Part A · Understanding the chapter

These ten aḥādīth (51–60) are still inside the **"Comprehensive Sayings"** run of Part 1 — short, pithy, free-standing. But like every chapter we've built, they organise around a quiet spine. Last chapter (41–50) walked the believer through *mind → heart → hands*, and its diagnostic centre was Ḥadīth 49: **love of the world is the root of every sin.** This chapter picks up right where that left off. Its very first ḥadīth (#51) names two appetites — a hunger for *knowledge* and a hunger for *the world* — and the rest of the chapter is, in effect, the answer to one question: **where should a believer point his hunger, his honour, and his heart?**

So the spine here is: **aim your hunger → honour your ties → guard your heart.**

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter 6 ka naqsha — *pehle bhook* (kis cheez ke piche bhaago — ʿilm aur Allah ka raasta, dunya nahin), *phir ʿizzat* (kis ka haq pehchaano — maa-baap aur baray), *aakhir mein dil* (use kaise saaf aur sacha rakho — istighfār, tawba, ikhlāṣ). Bhook → ʿizzat → dil. Aur in sab ke neeche ek hi dhaaga: *sab kuch Allah ke liye, sacchaai ke saath (ikhlāṣ).*

Three natural themes — keep this in your head as the spine of the chapter:

1. **Aim your hunger** (Aḥādīth 51, 52, 53, 54) — be insatiable, but for the *right* things: knowledge, not worldly stuff (#51); the highest struggle is a *word of truth before a tyrant* (#52); a single morning or evening *in Allah's path* outweighs the whole world (#53); and one person of real *understanding* (faqīh) is harder on Satan than a thousand mere worshippers (#54). This is the believer's *drive*, rightly directed.
2. **Honour your ties** (Aḥādīth 56, 57) — Allah binds His own pleasure to the *father's* pleasure (#56); and the elder brother carries a father-like *right* over the younger ones (#57). The believer who chases the right things must not trample the people closest to him on the way.
3. **Guard your heart** (Aḥādīth 55, 58, 59, 60) — the inner life before Allah: rejoice if your record is full of *istighfār* (#55); know that *all* of us sin, and the best are those who keep *returning* (#58); beware worship that is all *form* and no *sincerity* — it yields only hunger and sleeplessness (#59); and the mark of a *complete* Muslim is that he *leaves what does not concern him* (#60).

Notice the flow: **a rightly-aimed drive (the will) → honoured relationships (the ties) → a sincere, returning heart (the self before Allah).** And the deepest current — the thing that ties #52 (truth despite danger), #59 (sincerity vs empty form), #55 (sincere istighfār), and #60 (dropping the trivial *for what pleases Allah*) into one — is **ikhlāṣ**: doing it *for Allah*, not for the world, not for show. Where Chapter 5's hidden thread was *ḥubb al-dunyā*, this chapter's hidden thread is its cure: *ikhlāṣ*.

**Yaad rakho:** Teeno theme ek dhaage se bandhe hain — *ikhlāṣ* (sab kuch sirf Allah ke liye). #52 (khatre mein bhi haq), #59 (dikhaawe ki ibādat bekaar), #55 (sacha istighfār), #60 (faaltu cheezein chhodo Allah ki khushi ke liye) — sab ikhlāṣ par ghoomte hain. Pichle chapter ka chhupa dhaaga *dunya ki mohabbat* tha; is chapter ka chhupa dhaaga uska *ilaaj* hai — *ikhlāṣ*.

Work through each ḥadīth below. For each, ask yourself: *can I explain this hadith to a classmate in one sentence, in Urdu, without reading the book?* (*Kya main yeh hadith ek classmate ko ek line mein Urdu mein samjha sakti hoon, bina kitaab dekhe?*) If yes — move on. If not — re-read.

> **A note on order:** the book gives these in numeric order (51 → 60). Below — and in all four deliverables — I present them *by theme*, so #55 (istighfār) sits with the "heart" aḥādīth at the end rather than in the middle. The number and source travel with each ḥadīth, so nothing is lost; the theme order just makes the chapter *teach* better. Presentation order: **51, 52, 53, 54 · 56, 57 · 55, 58, 59, 60.**

## Theme 1 · Aim your hunger

## 51 · مَنْهُومَانِ لَا يَشْبَعَانِ — "Two greedy ones are never satisfied"

**Full wording:** *Two greedy people are never satiated: one who is greedy for knowledge can never get enough of it, and one who is greedy for worldly possessions can never get enough of them.*
**Source:** Bayhaqī (*Shuʿab al-Īmān*).

### One word, two opposite directions

The ḥadīth uses one striking image — *manhūm*, a person with an *insatiable* appetite, someone who can never be filled — and then points it in **two opposite directions**. There is a hunger that never stops and *ennobles* you (hunger for *ʿilm*), and a hunger that never stops and *destroys* you (hunger for *dunyā*). Same restlessness; completely different destination.

This is the perfect opening for the chapter, and it links straight back to Chapter 5's diagnosis. There we learned *love of the world is the root of every sin* (#49). Here the Prophet ﷺ shows the *mechanics* of that love: it is a hunger that **can never be filled** — the more the worldly person gets, the more he wants. By pairing it with the hunger for knowledge, the ḥadīth doesn't tell you to *kill* your appetite; it tells you to **re-aim** it. Take that same bottomless drive and point it at knowledge of Allah.

**Yaad rakho:** Ek lafz — *manhūm* (jiska pet kabhi na bhare) — aur do ulti samtein. Ek bhook jo kabhi khatam nahin hoti aur tumhe *buland* karti hai (ʿilm ki bhook), aur ek jo kabhi khatam nahin hoti aur tumhe *barbaad* karti hai (dunya ki bhook). Bhook ko maaro mat — uska *rukh* badlo. Wahi bemisaal lagan, Allah ke ʿilm ki taraf mod do.

### The catch — even the hunger for knowledge must be for Allah

Don't over-romanticise the first half. The praised hunger is for *beneficial, sacred* knowledge — knowledge that brings you closer to Allah and is sought *for His sake*. Knowledge collected for pride, debate, or worldly position is just *dunyā* wearing a scholar's cloak. So the deep thread of this chapter — *ikhlāṣ* — is already here: even the noblest appetite is only noble when it is *for Allah*.

**Yaad rakho:** Pehle hisse ko bhi sambhaal kar samjho. Achhi bhook *naafiʿ, dīni* ʿilm ki hai — jo Allah ke qareeb laaye aur *Allah ke liye* ho. Ghuroor, behes, ya dunyawi maqaam ke liye jamaa kiya ʿilm — woh bhi dunya hi hai, bas ʿilm ka libaas pehne. Ikhlāṣ ka dhaaga yahaan se shuru.

## 52 · أَفْضَلُ الْجِهَادِ كَلِمَةُ حَقٍّ عِنْدَ سُلْطَانٍ جَائِرٍ — "The most virtuous jihād is a word of truth before a tyrant ruler"

**Source:** Abū Dāwūd, Tirmidhī, Ibn Mājah.

### Why *this* is called the highest jihād

We hear "jihād" and think of the battlefield. The Prophet ﷺ here names something quieter and, in one respect, *higher*: speaking a **word of truth** (*kalimatu ḥaqq*) in front of an **unjust ruler** (*sulṭān jāʾir*). The book gives the reasoning precisely: the person who tells the truth to a tyrant's face puts himself in *real danger* — exactly like a warrior in battle. And there is an added weight: if that word *stops the ruler from oppression*, the benefit reaches a *far larger group of people* than killing combatants on a battlefield ever could. One brave sentence can shelter a whole population from injustice.

**Yaad rakho:** "Jihād" sun-te hi maidaan-e-jang yaad aata hai. Magar Rasool ﷺ ne yahaan ek *khaamosh* magar *buland* jihād bataaya: zaalim hukmraan ke saamne *haq baat* kehna. Wajah do — (1) aisa kehne wala apni jaan khatre mein daal-ta hai, bilkul ek sipaahi ki tarah; (2) agar woh baat zulm rok de, to uska faayda *bohot baray* logon tak pohanchta hai. Ek bahaadur jumla poori riʿaaya ko zulm se bacha sakta hai.

### The boundary — courage, not chaos

This is a ḥadīth about *moral courage*, and it must be taught as such — **not** as a slogan for reckless confrontation or rebellion. The virtue is in the *truth* spoken with *sincerity* for Allah's sake, accepting the personal risk. It is not a license to stir public chaos, to humiliate for sport, or to abandon wisdom. (The fuller fiqh of *how* and *when* to correct a ruler is a large subject — stay with the moral point the book makes: that truthful speech, when it is genuinely for Allah and shields people from injustice, is the noblest of struggles.) The *ikhlāṣ* thread again: the merit is in saying it *for Allah and for the oppressed*, not to be seen as a hero.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh *akhlaqi himmat* ki hadith hai — *be-soche tasaadum ya baghaawat* ka naara nahin. Fazīlat us *haq* mein hai jo *Allah ke liye*, khatra qubool karte hue kaha jaaye. Yeh fasaad phailaane, ya kisi ko zaleel karne ki ijaazat nahin. Ikhlāṣ phir yahaan: ajr us baat mein hai jo *Allah aur mazloom ke liye* ho, hero banne ke liye nahin.

## 53 · غَدْوَةٌ فِي سَبِيلِ اللهِ — "A morning or an evening in the path of Allah…"

**Full wording:** *A morning or an evening spent in the path of Allah is more superior than the world and whatever it contains.*
**Source:** Bukhārī, Muslim.

### Two ways to read "better than the world"

A *ghadwah* (a morning's journey out) or a *rawḥah* (an evening's journey) spent **in the path of Allah** is declared better than *the world and everything in it*. The book offers two clean explanations — give your class both:

1. **It lasts; the world doesn't.** The reward of that morning or evening is *eternal*; the goods of this world are *transient*. You are trading something that perishes for something that never ends.
2. **It out-weighs even total charity.** Even if a person owned the *whole world* and gave it *all* away in charity, the reward of that single stretch of time spent for Allah would be *greater*. Time given to Allah is worth more than wealth given for Allah.

**Yaad rakho:** *Ghadwah* (subah ka nikalna) ya *rawḥah* (shaam ka) jo *Allah ke raaste* mein ho — woh poori dunya aur jo kuch usme hai, us se behtar hai. Kitaab do wajhain deti hai: (1) us ka ajr *hamesha* rehta hai, dunya *fanaa* ho jaati hai; (2) agar koi poori dunya bhi sadaqah kar de, to bhi Allah ke liye guzaara woh thora sa waqt us se *baray* ajr ka hai. *Allah ko diya waqt, Allah ke liye diye maal se bhi qeemti.*

### What "the path of Allah" includes

Don't shrink "*fī sabīli Llāh*" to one narrow meaning. Its primary sense is striving and jihād in His cause, but the spirit the chapter is building is broader: *any* stretch of time genuinely spent *for Allah* — striving, seeking knowledge, serving His dīn — is being weighed here against the entire dunyā, and winning. This sits perfectly beside #52: both elevate *effort for Allah* over the comfort and goods of the world.

**Yaad rakho:** "*Fī sabīli Llāh*" ko ek tang maʿna tak mat samateo. Asal maʿna Allah ke raaste mein jihād/koshish hai, magar rooh wasiʿ hai: *jo bhi waqt sach-much Allah ke liye* guzre — koshish, ʿilm, dīn ki khidmat — woh poori dunya ke muqaable mein taula ja raha hai, aur jeet raha hai. #52 ke saath bilkul fit: dono *Allah ke liye mehnat* ko dunya ke aaraam par tarjeeh dete hain.

## 54 · لَفَقِيهٌ وَاحِدٌ أَشَدُّ عَلَى الشَّيْطَانِ — "A single jurist is harder on Satan than a thousand worshippers"

**Source:** Tirmidhī, Ibn Mājah.

### Why understanding beats raw devotion *against Satan*

Read this one carefully — it is the easiest in the chapter to misuse. The Prophet ﷺ says **one faqīh** (a person of *understanding* in the dīn) is *more severe upon Satan* (*ashaddu ʿalā l-shayṭān*) than a *thousand* ordinary worshippers. Note the exact battleground: it is *against Satan*. The book explains the mechanism: a person of understanding can *recognise* Satan's evil ploys, can *warn* others about his tricks, and can *enjoin good*. The ordinary worshipper — sincere, but without that understanding — is *more easily caught* in Satan's traps precisely because he cannot *see* them coming. Knowledge is *eyesight* in spiritual warfare; without it even a devoted worshipper walks in the dark.

**Yaad rakho:** Is hadith ko ehtiyaat se parho — ghalat istemaal sab se aasan isi ka hai. Rasool ﷺ ne kaha *ek faqīh* (dīn ki *samajh* wala) *Shaytaan par* hazaar ʿaam ʿaabidon se zyada *bhaari* hai. Maydaan dekho: *Shaytaan ke khilaaf*. Wajah — samajh wala Shaytaan ke daav *pehchaan* leta hai, doosron ko *khabardaar* karta hai, aur neki ka *hukm* deta hai. ʿAam ʿaabid — mukhlis magar samajh ke baghair — *aasani se phans* jaata hai, kyunki use jaal *dikhta hi nahin*. ʿIlm rooh ki jang mein *aankh* hai; uske baghair mukhlis ʿaabid bhi andheray mein chalta hai.

### What it does NOT say

It does **not** say worship is worthless, nor that the worshipper is inferior *as a person*, nor that knowledge *without* worship is enough. The comparison is narrow and specific: *in the contest against Satan*, understanding is a more powerful weapon than devotion alone. And "faqīh" here means one who *understands and acts and warns* — not someone holding a title or hoarding information (recall #51's warning). Knowledge and worship are meant to travel *together*; this ḥadīth simply tells you which one Satan fears more.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh *nahin* keh rahi ke ʿibādat bekaar hai, ya ʿaabid kam-tar insaan hai, ya ʿilm akela kaafi hai. Muqaabla tang aur khaas hai: *Shaytaan ke khilaaf*, samajh akeli ʿibādat se zyada kaargar hathiyaar hai. Aur "faqīh" yahaan woh hai jo *samjhe, amal kare, khabardaar kare* — sirf khitaab/maʿlūmaat wala nahin (#51 ki tambeeh yaad). ʿIlm aur ʿibādat saath chalte hain; yeh hadith bas batati hai Shaytaan kis se zyada darta hai.

## Theme 2 · Honour your ties

## 56 · رِضَا الرَّبِّ فِي رِضَا الْوَالِدِ — "The Lord's pleasure is in the father's pleasure"

**Full wording:** *The pleasure of the Lord (Allah) is in the pleasure of the father, and the displeasure of the Lord is in the displeasure of the father.*
**Source:** Tirmidhī.

*(The authentic wording uses* al-Rabb *— "the Lord" — which the translator renders as "Allah". Both are correct; al-Rabb is one of Allah's names.)*

### Allah ties His own pleasure to a parent's

This is one of the most weighty ḥadīths on parents in the whole tradition. The Prophet ﷺ does not merely say "be kind to your father" — he says Allah's own *riḍā* (pleasure) is *bound up with* the father's pleasure, and Allah's *sakhaṭ* (displeasure) with the father's displeasure. Pleasing your father becomes a *route to pleasing Allah*; angering him becomes a *route to Allah's anger*. The relationship is lifted from mere good manners to a matter of your standing *with Allah*.

**Yaad rakho:** Maa-baap ke baare mein yeh sab se bhaari hadithon mein se hai. Rasool ﷺ ne sirf yeh nahin kaha "baap se achha salook karo" — balke yeh ke Allah ki apni *riḍā* baap ki riḍā ke *saath bandhi* hai, aur Allah ki *naaraazi* baap ki naaraazi ke saath. Baap ko khush karna *Allah ko khush karne ka raasta* ban jaata hai; use naaraaz karna *Allah ki naaraazi ka raasta*. Maamla akhlaaq se uth kar *Allah ke saath taʿalluq* ka ban jaata hai.

### Two boundaries you MUST hold

This ḥadīth has two edges that, if missed, turn it into something harmful — so hold them firmly:

1. **It does not over-rule obedience to Allah.** If a parent commands something *sinful* — to abandon prayer, to cut off a spouse unjustly, to do something ḥarām — that command is *not* to be obeyed, because there is **no obedience to any creature in disobedience of the Creator** (this is itself a ḥadīth of this very book — it appears later, at #75; *that's a later chapter*, so just reference the principle). You still treat the parent with kindness and respect — you simply do not *obey the sin*.
2. **It does not diminish the mother.** The ḥadīth names the *father*, but this is *not* a ranking of father over mother. Other authentic narrations place the mother's right *above* the father's (the man asked who is most deserving of his good company, and the answer was "your mother" three times, then "your father"). Naming the father here does not exclude or lower the mother — both parents' pleasure is a treasure.

**Yaad rakho:** Is hadith ke do kinaare hain — chhoot jaayein to yeh nuqsaan-deh ban jaati hai:
1. *Yeh Allah ki itaaʿat ko nahin kaat-ti.* Agar maa-baap koi *gunaah* ka hukm dein (namaz chhodna, kuch ḥarām) to woh hukm *nahin* maana jaayega — kyunki *Khaaliq ki naafarmaani mein kisi makhlūq ki itaaʿat nahin* (yeh isi kitaab ki hadith hai, #75 — *woh aage ka chapter hai*). Phir bhi maa-baap se narmi aur ehtiraam baaqi — bas *gunaah* mein itaaʿat nahin.
2. *Yeh maa ka darja kam nahin karti.* Hadith *baap* ka naam leti hai, magar yeh baap ko maa par tarjeeh dena *nahin*. Doosri sahih hadithon mein maa ka haq baap se *zyada* hai (teen baar "teri maa", phir "tera baap"). Yahaan baap ka zikr maa ko nikaalta ya kam nahin karta.

## 57 · The elder brother's right is like a father's

**Full wording (English):** *The right of an elder brother over the younger ones is like the right of the father over his children.*
**Source:** Bayhaqī (*Shuʿab al-Īmān*).

> **Arabic matn omitted on purpose.** I am not 100% certain of the exact Arabic wording and harakaat of this narration, and our rule is firm: *never write a near-correct matn.* Teach it from the English meaning and the source above. If you want to display the Arabic in class, look it up in the book itself (it is printed there) and copy it exactly.

### The principle — seniority within the family carries weight

This ḥadīth extends the honour of #56 one step sideways. Just as the *father* has a great right over his children, the *eldest brother* — who, in many households, helps raise, protect, and provide for the younger siblings — carries a *father-like* right over them. He is to be respected, not treated as just another sibling. In Urdu-speaking, family-centred culture this lands immediately: the *baray bhai* often stands in a near-parental role, and the dīn affirms that standing.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh hadith #56 ki ʿizzat ko ek qadam aur barhaati hai. Jaise *baap* ka apni aulaad par bara haq hai, waise *baray bhai* ka — jo aksar chhoton ko paalne, bachaane, aur kharch mein madad karta hai — *baap jaisa* haq hai. Use sirf ek aur bhai-behen ki tarah nahin, balke ehtiraam se dekho. Hamari khaandaani tehzeeb mein yeh foran samajh aata hai: *baray bhai* ka maqaam aksar walidain jaisa hota hai, aur dīn us maqaam ki tasdeeq karta hai.

### Keep the proportion right

"*Like* the right of the father" is an *analogy of respect and care*, not a claim that an elder brother *is* a parent in every ruling. Don't stretch it into questions of inheritance, guardianship, or obedience-in-everything. The point is the *attitude*: honour, defer to, and care for the elder; and (the unstated flip side) the elder owes the younger the *care* a father owes — this is a two-way bond, not a one-way demand.

**Yaad rakho:** "Baap *jaisa* haq" — yeh *ehtiraam aur shafqat ki misaal* hai, yeh daʿwa nahin ke bara bhai har hukm mein *baap hi* hai. Ise wiraasat, wilaayat, ya har baat mein itaaʿat tak mat khencho. Asal baat *rawayya* hai: baray ka ehtiraam aur khidmat; aur (jo nahin kaha gaya woh bhi) bara bhai chhoton ka utna hi *khayaal* rakhe jitna baap rakhta — yeh do-tarfa rishta hai, ek-tarfa mutaalba nahin.

## Theme 3 · Guard your heart

## 55 · طُوبَى لِمَنْ وَجَدَ فِي صَحِيفَتِهِ اسْتِغْفَارًا كَثِيرًا — "Glad tidings for him who finds abundant istighfār in his record"

**Source:** Ibn Mājah.

### The deeds-page that delights you on the worst day

*Ṭūbā* is a word of glad tidings — "blessedness", "what a joy", and (the scholars say) the name of a tree in Paradise. The Prophet ﷺ gives this glad tiding to the one who, on the Day of Judgement, **finds his book of deeds full of istighfār** (sincere asking of forgiveness). The book is exact: these are the *sincere* invocations for forgiveness that Allah *accepted* — and they will be *presented to the believer* on that Day as a source of blessing and joy. Picture the scene: the most terrifying day, the records opened — and there, page after page, your *"astaghfiru-Llāh."* It is the cheapest investment with the richest return.

**Yaad rakho:** *Ṭūbā* khushkhabri ka lafz hai — "kya hi achha", "mubarak" (ʿulamā kehte hain Jannat mein ek darakht ka naam bhi). Rasool ﷺ yeh khushkhabri us ko de rahe hain jo Qayaamat ke din apna *aʿmaal-naama istighfār se bhara* paaye — woh *sacha* istighfār jo Allah ne *qubool* kiya, aur us din *uske saamne pesh* hoga, khushi ka sabab ban kar. Tasawwur karo: sab se khauf-naak din, daftar khulte hain — aur wahaan, safha-dar-safha, tumhara *"astaghfiru-Llāh."* Sab se sasta sauda, sab se bara nafa.

### "Abundant" and "sincere" together

Two words carry the ḥadīth: *kathīr* (abundant) and the book's gloss, *sincere*. Istighfār is not a magic phrase you rack up like points while heedless; it is a *turning* of the heart, repeated *often*. The point is to make istighfār a *daily habit* — after every prayer, in moments of slipping, before sleep — so that your record quietly fills with it. (And it pairs with #58 next: *because* we all keep sinning, we all keep needing to return.)

**Yaad rakho:** Do lafz hadith ko uthaate hain: *kathīr* (kasrat se) aur kitaab ka izaafa, *sacha*. Istighfār koi jaadui jumla nahin jo ghaflat mein points ki tarah jamaa karo — yeh dil ka *paltna* hai, baar baar. Maqsad: istighfār ko *rozana ki ʿaadat* banao — har namaz ke baad, phisalne ke lamhon mein, sone se pehle — taake aʿmaal-naama chupke chupke isi se bhar jaaye. (Aur agli hadith #58 se jurta hai: *kyunki* hum sab gunaah karte rehte hain, isi liye sab ko paltna parta hai.)

## 58 · كُلُّ بَنِي آدَمَ خَطَّاءٌ — "All the children of Adam are sinners, and the best of sinners are those who repent"

**Source:** Tirmidhī (and Ibn Mājah).

### A diagnosis and a door, in one sentence

Half of this ḥadīth is humbling; half is the most hopeful line in the chapter. **"All the children of Adam are *khaṭṭāʾ*"** — *constant* sinners, prone to error by their very nature. The book adds the one exception: only the *prophets and messengers* (upon them be peace) are *maʿṣūm* (infallible) and excluded; *every other human* has the potential to sin. So if you sin — that does not make you a monster; it makes you a *child of Adam*. But the ḥadīth does not stop at the diagnosis. **"…and the best of those sinners are those who repent (*al-tawwābūn*)."** The door is repentance, and it is open to all.

**Yaad rakho:** Aadhi hadith aajzi sikhaati hai, aadhi sab se ziyaada umeed deti hai. *"Tamaam bani Aadam khaṭṭāʾ hain"* — taba-an khataa karne waale. Kitaab ek istisnaa deti hai: sirf *anbiyā aur rusul* (ʿalayhimu s-salām) *maʿṣūm* hain; baaqi *har insaan* se gunaah mumkin hai. To agar tum se gunaah ho gaya — tum koi raakshas nahin, tum *bani Aadam* ho. Magar hadith tashkhīs par nahin ruk-ti: *"…aur in khataa-karon mein behtareen woh hain jo tawba karein (al-tawwābūn)."* Darwaza *tawba* hai, aur sab ke liye khula hai.

### The landmine — this is not a permission slip

Here is the trap, and you must close it before anyone opens it: *"everyone sins anyway, so what's the harm?"* That inverts the ḥadīth. The praise (*khayr*) is **not** on the sinning — it is on the *returning*. "All sin" is a statement of our *weakness*, given so we don't *despair* and so we don't *judge each other harshly*; it is not a relaxation of the standard. And recall #20 from an earlier chapter: *one who repents from sin is like one who has no sin* — repentance genuinely wipes the slate. So the ḥadīth's real message is: *don't despair when you fall, but don't make peace with the fall — return.*

**Yaad rakho:** Yahaan jaal hai, ise koi kholay us se pehle band karo: *"sab to gunaah karte hi hain, kya farq parta hai?"* — yeh hadith ko ulta deta hai. Taʿreef (*khayr*) gunaah par *nahin* — *paltne* par hai. "Sab gunaah karte hain" hamari *kamzori* ka bayan hai, taake hum *maayoos* na hon aur ek doosre ko *sakhti se na judge karein* — yeh meyaar mein narmi nahin. Aur #20 yaad karo (pichla chapter): *gunaah se tawba karne wala aise hai jaise us ne gunaah kiya hi nahin*. To asal payaam: *girne par maayoos mat ho, magar girne se sulah bhi mat karo — palto.*

## 59 · كَمْ مِنْ صَائِمٍ — "How many who fast gain nothing but hunger…"

**Full wording:** *How many there are who fast but do not gain anything from it but hunger, and how many there are who pass the night standing in prayer but do not gain anything from it but sleeplessness.*
**Source:** Dārimī.

### When the form is full but the heart is empty

This is a sharp, sobering ḥadīth — and the chapter's clearest statement of the *ikhlāṣ* thread. A person fasts all day; another stands in prayer all night — and the Prophet ﷺ says some of them walk away with *nothing but hunger* and *nothing but sleeplessness*. The body did the work; the heart got no reward. The book names the two ways this happens: (1) the faster who fasts *without seeking reward from Allah*, or who *does not abstain from sin* even while fasting; and (2) the night-worshipper whose real motive is *to show off* (*riyāʾ*). In both, the *form* of worship is complete but the *soul* of it — sincerity — is missing. As the book puts it: **sincerity (*ikhlāṣ*) is essential in all acts of worship.**

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh ek tez, jhinjhodne wali hadith hai — aur is chapter mein *ikhlāṣ* ke dhaage ka sab se saaf bayan. Ek shakhs din bhar roza rakhta hai, doosra raat bhar namaz mein khara rehta hai — aur Rasool ﷺ kehte hain kuch logon ke haath *sirf bhook* aur *sirf bedaari* aati hai. Jism ne mehnat ki; dil ko ajr na mila. Kitaab do soorat batati hai: (1) woh roza-daar jo *Allah se ajr ki niyyat ke baghair* rakhe, ya rozay mein bhi *gunaah na chhode*; (2) woh raat ka ʿaabid jiska asal maqsad *dikhaava* (*riyāʾ*) ho. Dono mein ʿibādat ki *shakl* poori, magar uski *rooh* — ikhlāṣ — ghaayab. Kitaab kehti hai: *har ʿibādat mein ikhlāṣ laazmi hai.*

### Two readings to keep apart — fear, not despair

This ḥadīth can be *misheard* in two opposite ways, and both are wrong:
- **Misuse 1 — despair:** *"So maybe all my fasts and prayers are worthless."* No. The book is careful: the *minimal obligation is still discharged* by such a fast — you are not commanded to repeat it. The ḥadīth is a warning to *raise your sincerity*, not a verdict that your worship is void.
- **Misuse 2 — judging others:** *"Look at him, all show."* The ḥadīth is a *mirror*, not a *telescope*. You cannot see another person's intention — only Allah can. Turn it on *yourself*: *am I doing this for Allah, or for hunger's sake, or for eyes?*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh hadith do ulti tarah *galat* suni ja sakti hai, dono ghalat:
- *Galat 1 — maayoosi:* "Shaayad meri saari namazein-rozey bekaar." Nahin. Kitaab ehtiyaat se kehti hai: aise rozay se *farz to ada ho gaya* — dobaara ka hukm nahin. Yeh hadith *ikhlāṣ barhaane* ki tambeeh hai, ʿibādat ke baatil hone ka faisla nahin.
- *Galat 2 — doosron ko judge karna:* "Dekho, sab dikhaava." Yeh hadith *aaina* hai, *door-been* nahin. Doosre ki niyyat sirf Allah jaanta hai. Ise *apne aap* par mod do: *main yeh Allah ke liye kar raha hoon, ya bhook ke liye, ya nazaron ke liye?*

## 60 · مِنْ حُسْنِ إِسْلَامِ الْمَرْءِ تَرْكُهُ مَا لَا يَعْنِيهِ — "Part of the excellence of one's Islam is leaving what does not concern him"

**Source:** Tirmidhī, Aḥmad.

### The closing note — a clean, focused life

The chapter closes on a ḥadīth so loved that scholars made it one of the *Forty* foundational traditions. *Ḥusn al-islām* — the *excellence*, the *beauty*, of a person's Islam — is shown by one habit: **he leaves (*tark*) what does not concern him (*mā lā yaʿnīh*)** — the nonessential talk, the nonessential involvement, the matters that are simply *not his business* and bring him no nearer to Allah. The book's framing is beautiful: a person should be *constantly occupied with fulfilling Allah's commands*, and should *let go of everything that does not earn Allah's pleasure and is not essential for this world or the next.* The reward named is enormous: this is what brings *endless bliss and salvation in the Hereafter.*

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter ek aisi hadith par band hota hai jise ʿulamā ne *Chaalees* bunyaadi hadithon mein shaamil kiya. *Ḥusn al-islām* — kisi ke Islam ka *husn, khoobsurati* — ek ʿaadat se zaahir hoti hai: woh *chhod deta hai jo us se taʿalluq nahin rakhta* (*mā lā yaʿnīh*) — faaltu baatein, faaltu dakhal, woh maamlaat jo *uske matlab ke nahin* aur Allah ke qareeb nahin laate. Kitaab ka andaaz pyaara hai: insaan ko *hamesha Allah ke ahkaam mein masroof* rehna chahiye, aur *har woh cheez chhod deni chahiye jo Allah ki khushi na de aur dunya ya Aakhirat ke liye zaroori na ho.* Ajr bohot bara: yahi *Aakhirat ki abadi kaamyaabi* ka sabab hai.

### The boundary — focus, not withdrawal

Don't let "*leave what does not concern you*" become an excuse to neglect *real* responsibilities or to withdraw from people who genuinely have a claim on you. "*What does not concern you*" means the *futile and nonessential* — idle gossip, prying, meddling, chasing what gives no benefit. It does **not** mean abandoning family duties, dropping the obligation to enjoin good and forbid evil where it *is* your concern, or becoming cold and isolated. The ḥadīth is about *focus* — pruning the wasteful so your energy goes to what matters — not about *withdrawal*.

**Yaad rakho:** "*Jo tumse taʿalluq na rakhe use chhodo*" ko *asal* zimmedaariyon se ghaflat ya logon se kat-ne ka bahaana mat banao. "*Jo taʿalluq na rakhe*" ka matlab *faaltu aur ghair-zaroori* hai — bekaar gheebat, taak-jhaank, dakhal-andaazi, bila-faayda cheezon ka peechha. Yeh *nahin* ke khaandaani haqooq chhod do, ya jahaan amr bil maʿrūf *tumhara* kaam hai wahaan se hatt jaao, ya thanday aur tanha ho jaao. Hadith *tawajjuh* ki baat hai — faaltu cheezein kaat-na taake taaqat asal cheez par lage — *kinaara-kashi* ki nahin.

### Why it is the perfect close

Look at the whole chapter through #60. The believer was told to *aim his hunger* (51–54), *honour his ties* (56–57), and *guard his heart* (55, 58, 59). Ḥadīth 60 is the *operating principle* that makes all of it possible: a life *pruned* of the trivial. You cannot chase knowledge, speak truth, honour your parents, and keep a sincere, repenting heart if your hours bleed away into what *does not concern you*. The chapter ends by handing you the one habit that protects all the rest.

**Yaad rakho:** Poore chapter ko #60 ki ainak se dekho. Momin ko kaha gaya: *bhook sahi rukh do* (51–54), *rishtay nibhaao* (56–57), *dil ki hifaazat karo* (55, 58, 59). Hadith 60 woh *usool* hai jo yeh sab mumkin banata hai: ek aisi zindagi jo *faaltu se paak* ho. Tum ʿilm, haq, maa-baap ka ehtiraam, aur sacha tawba karne wala dil tab tak nahin sambhaal sakte jab tak tumhare ghantay *us cheez mein* bah jaayein jo *tumse taʿalluq nahin*. Chapter wahi ek ʿaadat de kar khatam hota hai jo baaqi sab ki hifaazat karti hai.

---

## The big picture — what these 10 aḥādīth are silently building

Stand back. The chapter trains the believer in **three movements**:

1. **Aim your hunger** — be insatiable for knowledge, not the world (#51); spend your courage on a word of truth (#52) and your time in Allah's path (#53); and arm yourself with *understanding*, which Satan fears most (#54).
2. **Honour your ties** — Allah's pleasure runs through your father's (#56), and the elder brother carries a father-like right (#57). Don't trample the people closest to you while chasing the heights.
3. **Guard your heart** — fill your record with istighfār (#55), keep *returning* because all of us fall (#58), and make your worship *sincere*, not empty form (#59) — then prune away everything that does not concern you (#60), so the whole life stays focused on Allah.

The deepest current under all ten is **ikhlāṣ** — *for Allah, sincerely*. The knowledge in #51, the truth in #52, the worship in #59, the letting-go in #60 — each is only worth anything when it is *for Him*. Chapter 5 named the disease (love of the world); Chapter 6 hands you the cure (a sincere heart, rightly aimed).

**Yaad rakho:** 10 hadith — magar 3 harkatein, ek tarbiyat. *Bhook sahi rukh do* (51–54) → *rishtay nibhaao* (56–57) → *dil ki hifaazat karo* (55, 58, 59, 60). Sab ke neeche ek hi dhaaga: *ikhlāṣ — sab kuch Allah ke liye, sacchaai se.* Chapter 5 ne bīmārī batayi (dunya ki mohabbat); Chapter 6 ilaaj deta hai (saaf, sahi-rukh dil). Class ko aakhir mein yeh ek-tasveer dikhao.

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

Now you understand the chapter. This section is how to *deliver* it to your classmates.

---

## The 6 landmines to watch for

### Landmine 1 · #51 — "So greed is good?"

Classmates may hear *"two greedy people are never satisfied"* and either think greed itself is being praised, or miss that the *second* half (greed for the world) is a *warning*. Clarify the redirection — and that even knowledge must be for Allah.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh hadith laalach ki taʿreef nahin kar rahi. Yeh ek bhook ki baat kar rahi hai jo kabhi nahin bharti — aur do raste dikhaati hai. Ek bhook tumhe buland karti hai: ʿilm ki bhook. Doosri tumhe barbaad karti hai: dunya ki bhook (yahi pichle chapter wali 'dunya ki mohabbat' hai). Rasool ﷺ keh rahe hain bhook ko maaro mat — uska rukh badlo, Allah ke ʿilm ki taraf. Aur dhyan rakho: ʿilm bhi tab achha hai jab Allah ke liye ho, ghuroor ya dunyawi maqaam ke liye nahin."*

### Landmine 2 · #52 — "Word of truth before a tyrant" = reckless rebellion

This is a courage ḥadīth, not a slogan for stirring chaos, public humiliation, or revolt. Smart or fiery classmates may weaponise it. Keep it on the moral point the book makes.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh hadith *akhlaqi himmat* ki taʿreef hai — fasaad ya baghaawat ka naara nahin. Fazīlat us haq mein hai jo *Allah ke liye*, khatra qubool karte hue, zaalim ke saamne kaha jaaye — taake zulm ruke aur logon ka bhala ho. Yeh ijaazat nahin ke hum hangaama khara karein, ya kisi ko zaleel karein, ya hikmat chhod dein. Asal cheez: sacchaai, ikhlāṣ ke saath, mazloom ke liye — hero banne ke liye nahin."*

### Landmine 3 · #54 — "One faqīh > 1000 worshippers" = worship is worthless

The most misused ḥadīth in the chapter. Classmates may belittle worship or worshippers. The comparison is *narrow* — it is about being *harder on Satan*, where understanding is the sharper weapon.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Hoshiyaar — yeh hadith ʿibādat ko bekaar nahin keh rahi, na ʿaabid ko kam-tar insaan. Maydaan ek hi hai: *Shaytaan ke khilaaf*. Samajh wala (faqīh) Shaytaan ke daav *pehchaan* leta hai aur doosron ko bachaata hai; mukhlis magar samajh ke baghair ʿaabid aasani se phans jaata hai kyunki use jaal dikhta hi nahin. ʿIlm rooh ki jang mein aankh hai. ʿIlm aur ʿibādat saath chalte hain — yeh bas batati hai Shaytaan kis se zyada darta hai. Aur 'faqīh' woh hai jo samjhe, amal kare, aur khabardaar kare — sirf degree wala nahin."*

### Landmine 4 · #56 — "Obey your father in everything" / "the mother counts less"

Two errors at once. Pious classmates may think a parent must be obeyed even in sin; others may read the naming of "father" as lowering the mother. Close both.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Do baatein saaf karo. Pehli: Allah ki khushi baap ki khushi mein hai — magar agar baap *gunaah* ka hukm de (namaz chhodo, koi ḥarām karo), to woh hukm *nahin* maana jaayega, kyunki Khaaliq ki naafarmaani mein kisi ki itaaʿat nahin. Phir bhi baap se narmi aur ehtiraam baaqi — bas gunaah mein itaaʿat nahin. Doosri: hadith ne 'baap' kaha, magar yeh maa ko kam nahin karti — doosri sahih hadith mein maa ka haq baap se *zyada* hai (teen baar 'teri maa', phir 'tera baap'). Dono ka maqaam bara hai."*

### Landmine 5 · #58 — "Everyone sins anyway, so what's the harm?"

The hopeful ḥadīth has a trap: turning "all sin" into a permission slip. The praise is on *returning*, never on the sinning.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh hadith bohot umeed deti hai — magar usay ulta mat samjho. 'Sab gunaah karte hain' ka matlab yeh *nahin* ke 'to chalo gunaah theek hai.' Taʿreef gunaah par nahin — *tawba* par hai: 'in mein behtareen woh jo palatte hain.' Yeh baat isliye kahi gayi taake hum girne par *maayoos* na hon aur ek doosre ko *sakhti se judge* na karein — meyaar narm karne ke liye nahin. Aur yaad rakho (#20): sache dil se tawba karne wala aise hai jaise gunaah kiya hi nahin. To: giro to maayoos mat ho — palto; magar girne se dosti mat karo."*

### Landmine 6 · #59 — "My worship is worthless" / "look how fake he is"

Two opposite misreadings — despair about your own worship, or judging others'. Aim it as a *mirror*.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh hadith do tarah galat suni jaati hai. Ek: 'Shaayad meri saari ibādat bekaar.' Nahin — kitaab kehti hai aise rozay se *farz to ada ho gaya*; yeh tambeeh hai ke *ikhlāṣ barhaao*, faisla nahin ke ibādat baatil. Doosri: 'Dekho, woh to sirf dikhaava karta hai.' Yeh hadith *aaina* hai, *door-been* nahin — doosre ki niyyat sirf Allah jaanta hai. Ise apne aap par mod do: 'Main yeh Allah ke liye kar raha hoon, ya bhook/nazaron ke liye?' Ibādat ki shakl kaafi nahin — uski rooh, ikhlāṣ, zaroori hai."*

> **Bonus watch-point · #60 — "leave what doesn't concern you" ≠ withdrawal.** If a classmate reads #60 as "ignore people / drop responsibilities", correct gently: *"Mā lā yaʿnīh"* means the *futile* — gossip, prying, meddling — not your real duties to family or the dīn. It is *focus*, not isolation.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 6

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum aage dekhenge"* or *"yeh book ke scope se bahar hai"*:

- ❌ **The full fiqh and politics of correcting rulers** triggered by #52 — the conditions of *khurūj*, when public vs private naṣīḥa applies, the scholarly debates on rebellion. Stay with the *moral virtue* of courageous, sincere truth.
- ❌ **A ranking contest of ʿilm vs ʿibādah** triggered by #54 — don't turn it into "scholars are better than worshippers" as people. The point is narrow: understanding is the sharper weapon *against Satan*.
- ❌ **The detailed fiqh of obedience to parents** triggered by #56 — every edge case (parents and marriage choice, travel, money). Teach the *principle* (Allah's pleasure runs through the father's) and the *one limit* (no obedience in sin). The full principle of "no obedience to creation in disobedience of the Creator" is **Ḥadīth 75 — a later chapter**; reference it, don't unpack it.
- ❌ **The full science of tawba and its conditions** triggered by #58 — the technical *shurūṭ al-tawba*, whether sins recur, the categories of major/minor. Keep #58 to its clean message: all fall; the best return; don't despair, don't normalise sin.
- ❌ **The fiqh of riyāʾ and which acts are invalidated** triggered by #59 — the detailed rulings on showing-off, hidden shirk, repeating worship. Teach the *spirit*: sincerity is the soul of worship; use it as a mirror on yourself.
- ❌ **The full ādāb of speech / the sciences of the tongue** triggered by #60 — keep it to the simple, liberating principle: prune the futile, focus on what brings you near Allah.

**Yaad rakho:** Pre-loading aur tangents madad nahin karte. 10 hadithein hain — har ek apne aap mein kaafi hai. Aur cheezein mat daalo. Class ka focus = *yeh 10 hadithein dil mein bait jaayein* — khaas taur par #59 ka ikhlāṣ aur #60 ki saaf zindagi. Bas.

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Did they catch the *redirection* in #51 — hunger re-aimed, not killed; and the world-half as a warning? *(Bhook ka rukh — aur dunya wala hissa tambeeh?)*
2. Are they reading #52 as a license for chaos/rebellion, or as moral courage for Allah? *(Himmat — ya hangaama?)*
3. Did they mishear #54 as "worship is worthless"? *(ʿIbādat bekaar — ya Shaytaan ke khilaaf samajh ka hathiyaar?)*
4. Do they hold #56's two limits — no obedience in sin, mother not lowered? *(Do kinaare yaad?)*
5. Are they turning #58 into a permission slip? *(Sab gunaah karte hain → bahaana, ya tawba ki daʿwat?)*
6. Are they despairing over their own worship, or judging others', after #59? *(Maayoosi / doosron ko judge — ya apna aaina?)*
7. Are they reading #60 as withdrawal instead of focus? *(Faaltu chhodna — ya logon se kat-na?)*
8. Can they state the arc — *bhook → ʿizzat → dil* — and the **ikhlāṣ** thread? *(Chapter ka safar aur asal dhaaga yaad hai?)*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh 8 points tumhe batayenge kahaan revision chahiye. Agar 3+ points par cohort phans rahi hai, agle session se pehle yeh hadithein dohrao — khaas taur par #54 (ʿibādat bekaar nahin) aur #59 (aaina, door-been nahin), kyunki inhi do ko ghalat samajhne par poora chapter ka rukh bigad jaata hai.
