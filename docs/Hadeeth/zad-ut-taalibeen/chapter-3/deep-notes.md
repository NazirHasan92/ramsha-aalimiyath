# Part A · Understanding the chapter

These 10 aḥādīth continue Part 1 of Zad ut-Taalibeen — still the "Comprehensive Sayings" section, still short pithy one-liners. Bulandshehrī groups them by Arabic grammatical form, but for *you* as the teacher, this chapter is doing something tighter than Chapter 1: it is building the **portrait of an intelligent, socially-present, spiritually-alert believer** — and naming the traps that pull a person away from that portrait.

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter 3 mein 10 hadithein ek "smart momin" ki tasveer banati hain. Aql wala, log se ghulnay wala, sachhai mein khada, aur jaal se khabardar. Yeh sirf ehkaam ki list nahin — yeh ek personality hai jise tum aapne classmates ke saamne dikhana hai.

Four themes emerge naturally — keep these in your head as the spine of the chapter:

1. **The intelligent believer** (Aḥādīth 21, 22, 29) — moderation, mastering the nafs, defining birr by character.
2. **Social presence** (Aḥādīth 23, 30) — sociability as a sign of īmān; nearness to Allah through benefit to His ʿiyāl.
3. **Trade & truthfulness** (Aḥādīth 25, 26) — the warning to traders, and the company promised to honest ones.
4. **Spiritual traps** (Aḥādīth 24, 27, 28) — what corrodes the heart silently: songs, nifāq-of-action, the major sins.

The arc: **what makes a believer intelligent → how that intelligence shows up among people → how it is tested in transactions → how it is undone by hidden traps**. By the end, the classmate should leave with a sharper picture of *what an aware Muslim looks like* — and a clearer map of *what quietly pulls a person off that picture*.

**Yaad rakho:** Char themes — *aqlmandi · meeljol · tijaarat · jaal*. Yeh chapter ka safar hai. Pehle "smart momin" ki tasveer banao, phir bataao kya cheez us tasveer ko dhundla karti hai.

Work through each ḥadīth below. For each, ask yourself: *can I explain this in one sentence, in Urdu, to a classmate?* (*Kya main yeh hadith ek classmate ko ek line mein Urdu mein samjha sakti hoon?*) If yes — move on. If not — re-read.

## 21 · الاقْتِصَادُ فِي النَّفَقَةِ نِصْفُ الْمَعِيشَةِ — "Three halves"

**Source:** Bayhaqī, *Shuʿab al-Īmān*.

The full matn — three parallel statements — is one of the most quoted sayings of the Messenger ﷺ in classical adab literature:

> *"Moderation in spending is half of one's sustenance · Friendliness toward people is half of one's intelligence · Asking good questions is half of one's knowledge."*

### The three "halves" — why "half"?

Notice the structure: each clause names a virtue and equates it with *half* of something larger. The Arabic *niṣf* (half) is not literal — it means "the bulk of, the larger part of, the determining share of." This is a classical Arabic rhetorical idiom: *"half"* = "the decisive portion".

So the Prophet ﷺ is saying: in three different domains of life (provision · intellect · knowledge), there is one specific virtue that, all by itself, accounts for most of the outcome.

**Yaad rakho:** "Nisf" (half) yahaan literal nahin — *"sabse bara hissa"* matlab hai. Teen alag-alag zindagi ke shoʿbon mein, ek ek aisi adat hai jo us pure domain ko bana ya bigaad sakti hai. Yeh tareeqa-e-bayaan ki khoobsurati hai.

### Half 1 — moderation in spending = half of provision

Income is mostly *outside* a person's control — Allah is the *Razzāq* (Provider). But *spending* is almost entirely within control. So if a person learns to be moderate — not extravagant, not miserly — their provision *feels* doubled, even if their income is unchanged.

This is exactly what the Qurʾān describes in 25:67: *"Those who, when they spend, are not extravagant and not miserly, but hold a just balance between the two."* The verse is describing *ʿibād al-Raḥmān* — the servants of the Most Merciful.

Notice the trap on both sides:
- **Israaf** (extravagance, *fuzool kharchi*) — spending beyond need. Empties the wallet, then the heart. Then comes anxiety, dispute, and chasing more income to fund what was never needed.
- **Bukhl** (miserliness, *kanjoosi*) — withholding what should be spent on family, on need, on Allah's cause. Empties the soul of generosity.

Moderation (*iqtiṣād*) sits between. It's not a fixed monetary line — it's an *attitude* of measured restraint and willing release.

**Yaad rakho:** Rizq Allah deta hai — magar uska istimal tum karti ho. Agar tum *iqtiṣād* (mizaaj wali moderation) seekh lo, to thodi cheez bhi barkat wali ho jaati hai. Israaf aur kanjoosi — dono ek hi sikkay ke do rukh hain. Moderation darmiyaan hai.

### Half 2 — friendliness toward people = half of intelligence

The Arabic is *al-tawaddud ilā al-nās* — to *make oneself beloved* to people, to *seek their warmth*, to *put oneself out* to win their affection. Not just polite — *actively warm*.

Why is this *half of intelligence*? Because intelligence in Islam is never solo. The *ʿaql* of one person is limited; the collaboration of two minds is more than twice one mind. A person who is friendly *gets access to* the wisdom of others. A person who is cold *cuts themselves off* from it — and stays at their own ceiling.

The book quotes *Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ* 8:795 on this: "a person's intelligence is perfected through interaction with others, in that the collaboration of two minds is better than one."

**Yaad rakho:** Akeli aql adhoori hai. *Tawaddud* (logon se ghul mil ke rehna) tumhe doosre dimagh tak pohnchata hai. Jo banda taakabbur mein akela rehta hai, woh apne hi level pe phansa rehta hai — chahe woh kitna bhi padha-likha ho. Logon ko admire karo, unse seekho — yahi aqlmandi hai.

### Half 3 — asking good questions = half of knowledge

The Arabic *ḥusn al-suʾāl* — "beauty of asking" — not just *asking*, but **asking well**. Two readings, both important:

1. **A well-thought-out question contains half the answer.** When you've thought through *what exactly you don't understand*, you've already done half the work. Random questioning gives random answers.
2. **Knowledge is structured by questions.** A scholar who can't form the right question doesn't yet have the issue clearly in their mind. The shape of your question determines what you can find.

This is why the Companions — when they asked the Prophet ﷺ — often asked very precisely. *"What is the best deed?"* *"What act admits one to Paradise?"* *"What removes hatred from the heart?"* The questions are sharp; the answers are precise.

**Yaad rakho:** Sawal *poochna* alag baat hai — sawal *banana* alag. Pehle apne aap se poochho: *main kya nahin samajhti? Sirf aam confusion, ya koi specific ginthi?* Jab sawal saaf banta hai, aadha jawab apne aap mil jaata hai. Random sawal random jawab laata hai.

### The three halves form one personality

Standing back: a person who **manages money with measure**, **gives warmth to people**, and **asks questions well** — this is a person whose life works on three different axes simultaneously. They are *intelligent in living*. This is the opening shot of Chapter 3 — the *intelligent believer* sketch.

**Yaad rakho:** Teen halves — *paisa, log, ilm* — milkar ek aqlmand zindagi ki tasveer banati hain. Yeh hadith Chapter 3 ki theme set karti hai: *smart momin kaisa hota hai*. Aage ki hadithein is portrait ko gehra karti hain.

## 22 · الْكَيِّسُ مَنْ دَانَ نَفْسَهُ — "The intelligent one is he who has subdued his nafs"

**Source:** Tirmidhī, Ibn Mājah.

The full ḥadīth: *"The intelligent one (al-kayyis) is he who has subdued his lower self (dāna nafsahu) and worked for what comes after death; and the foolish one (al-ʿājiz) is he who has put his lower self in pursuit of its desires and has vain hopes about Allah."*

### Two redefinitions in one ḥadīth

This ḥadīth quietly **redefines two words** that every culture thinks it understands: *intelligence* and *foolishness*.

In worldly parlance:
- *Intelligent* = clever, productive, ahead of others.
- *Foolish* = naïve, slow, easily fooled.

The Prophet ﷺ takes both words and reassigns them along a completely different axis — the axis of *self-mastery and what one is preparing for*. By the end of the ḥadīth, the words have new content.

**Yaad rakho:** Ek hi hadith mein do lafz ki definitions badal rahi hain — *aqlmand* aur *bewakoof*. Dunya kehti hai: aqlmand woh hai jo zyada kamaaye. Rasool ﷺ kehte hain: aqlmand woh hai jo apne nafs ka maalik ho aur Akhirah ke liye kaam kare. Yeh shift bohot bara hai.

### *Al-kayyis* — "the intelligent one"

The Arabic word *kayyis* doesn't mean "clever / smart in school" — it means *sharp-witted, alert, in command of one's faculties*. The Prophet ﷺ defines it by **two conditions**:

1. **Dāna nafsahu** — has subjected his lower self, brought it under command. The verb *dāna* means "to have called to account, to have judged, to have brought into obedience". So this is the person who has made their *nafs* a servant, not a master.
2. **Worked for what comes after death** — meaning every choice is weighed against the Akhirah. The horizon of decision is not "what will benefit me tomorrow" but "what will benefit me when I meet Allah".

These two conditions together = real intelligence. Anything less is, by this ḥadīth's measure, deficiency.

**Yaad rakho:** *Kayyis* = jisne apne *nafs* ko zer kiya aur Akhirah ke liye kaam kar raha hai. Yeh do shartein hain. Tumhare paas duniya ka koi bhi ilm ho — agar yeh do shartein nahin, to hadith ke nazar mein tum aql wala nahin.

### The horse and child metaphors

The book quotes ʿAllāma Būṣīrī's famous *Qaṣīdat al-Burda*:

> *"The self is like a child who, if you let it alone, would grow up loving suckling — but if you weaned it, it would be weaned. So keep its desires in check, and beware that it does not overpower you — for lust, when it overpowers, humiliates or destroys."*

Two metaphors are running here:
- **The horse:** the nafs is a powerful animal. Reined in, it carries you. Without reins, it goes where it wants — and probably destroys you.
- **The child:** the nafs is a needy child. Let alone, it never grows up. Disciplined, it can mature.

These are not "be harsh on yourself" metaphors. They are *parental* metaphors — *firm, loving, structured restraint*. Not cruelty.

**Yaad rakho:** Nafs ghoda bhi hai aur bachcha bhi. Ghoda hai — agar lagaam nahin, to tumhe phenk dega. Bachcha hai — agar discipline nahin, to bigad jaayega. *Dāna* matlab "control kiya, discipline kiya" — torture nahin. Yeh tarbiyat hai.

### *Al-ʿājiz* — the inverse

The opposite portrait: *al-ʿājiz* — "the impotent one, the incapable one". Also defined by two conditions:

1. **Put his nafs in pursuit of its desires** — the nafs is in the driver's seat. The person is being driven, not driving.
2. **Vain hopes about Allah** (*tamannā ʿalā Allāh*) — relying on "Allah is Most Merciful, He will forgive" as a license, while doing nothing to deserve it.

The trap of the second condition is subtle. Allah's mercy is real — but quoting it to excuse one's choices is what classical scholars call **deceptive hope**, *al-tamannī al-fāsid*. It's not *rajāʾ* (hope grounded in effort) — it's wishful thinking masquerading as faith.

The book points exactly to this: it quotes *Qurʾān 82:6* — *"O man! What has seduced you from your Lord, Most Beneficent?"* — and *Qurʾān 15:49–50* — *"Tell My servants that I am indeed the Oft-Forgiving, Most Merciful, AND that My chastisement is indeed the most grievous chastisement."* Both. Together. Not just one.

**Yaad rakho:** *Tamannā ʿalā Allāh* (Allah pe khaali ummeed rakhna bina amal ke) ek subtle dhokha hai. "Allah Rehman hai" yeh sach hai — magar yeh license nahin. Quran khud dono saath laata hai: Ghafoor aur Azaab dene wala. Sirf rehmat ka hawala dena aur kuch na karna — yeh "ʿajiz" wala kaam hai, "kayyis" wala nahin.

## 23 · الْمُؤْمِنُ مَأْلَفٌ — "The believer is sociable"

**Source:** Mustadrak, Bayhaqī, *Shuʿab al-Īmān*.

The matn: *"The believer is one who is sociable [with others] (maʾlaf); and there is no good in one who is not sociable [with others] (yaʾlafu) nor in one who is not met sociably [by them] (yuʾlafu)."*

### *Maʾlaf* — and the two-directional *ulfa* in the negation

The believer is described by a single word: **maʾlaf** (مَأْلَف) — from the root *alifa* (to be intimate, to find ease with). A *maʾlaf* is *a locus of affinity*: someone with whom people feel at home, easy to be near, easy to warm to. The believer **is** that.

Then the matn names what its *absence* looks like — and here the same root appears in **two forms**:
- **Yaʾlafu** (active) — *he is at ease with people; he goes toward them.*
- **Yuʾlafu** (passive) — *people are at ease with him; they come toward him.*

*"There is no good in one who **neither** yaʾlafu **nor** yuʾlafu"* — neither warm from his end, nor warmed-to from theirs. So warmth must flow in *both* directions, and the believer (the *maʾlaf*) is precisely the one in whom it does.

The negation is severe: *"there is no good in"* (*lā khayra fīman*) the one who lacks this. Not "less good" — *no good*. This is a strong rhetorical assertion. Bulandshehrī notes this aligns directly with the Qurʾān 3:103: *"Hold fast, all together, to the rope of Allah, and be not divided among yourselves..."*

**Yaad rakho:** Momin *maʾlaf* hai — woh insaan jisse log raahat paayein, ghul-mil ke rehne wala. Aur jo *na khud milta* (*yaʾlafu*) *na log uss se milein* (*yuʾlafu*) — uss mein "koi khair nahin". Dono direction mein garmi chahiye. Yeh zubaan bohot sakht hai — koi naram lafz nahin. Islam ek social deen hai.

### Why this ḥadīth lands hard on introverted classmates

You will have classmates who say *"I'm just an introvert"*, *"I don't like crowds"*, *"meeting people drains me"*. Take their reality seriously — introversion is real. The ḥadīth is not demanding that everyone become a social butterfly.

The ḥadīth is naming **the absence of warmth, not the absence of socialising**. A quiet person who, when met, is warm — fulfils *yuʾlafu*. A reserved person who, when needed, shows up — fulfils *yaʾlafu* in deed. The opposite — someone who is *cold* in disposition, who *avoids* people because they look down on them, who *makes others feel unwelcome* — *this* is what the ḥadīth is condemning.

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith *introvert* ko mana nahin kar rahi — *takabbur* aur *thandi-mizaaji* ko mana kar rahi hai. Aram-pasand banda agar mile to garmi se mile, kabhi-kabhi pohnche, dil saaf rakhe — to hadith poori ho jaati hai. Asal masla *dil ka cold hona* hai, sirf "main quiet hoon" nahin.

### *Ulfa* engenders community

Bulandshehrī adds (the [A] note in the book): "meeting with others in a sociable manner encourages others to do the same and also engenders within the community feelings of love and warmth." This is the practical mechanism.

Warmth is *contagious*. If you greet someone with warmth, they greet the next person with warmth. If you are cold, you cool the room. The believer's job in any room is to *raise the temperature of warmth*, not lower it. This is *daʿwa* before any words are spoken.

**Yaad rakho:** Garmi *infectious* hai. Tum garmi se milti ho — agla insan agle ko garmi se milta hai. Tumhari thandi-mizaaji bhi spread hoti hai. Har jagah jahaan tum jaati ho, *ulfa* ka temperature thoda barhao — yeh hi daʿwa hai, bina ek lafz ke.

## 24 · الْغِنَاءُ يُنْبِتُ النِّفَاقَ — "Songs cultivate hypocrisy"

**Source:** Bayhaqī, *Shuʿab al-Īmān*.

The matn: *"Songs cause hypocrisy to grow in the heart just as water makes crops grow."*

### Handle this one with extra care

This is **the hardest ḥadīth of the chapter for the modern classroom**. Music is woven into the daily fabric of every classmate's life — through phones, cars, social media, weddings, films. Don't lead with condemnation. Lead with **the spiritual mechanism the ḥadīth is naming** — that's what they can hear.

Also: this is *not* a fiqh class. Don't go into the detailed legal discussion of which instruments are which, which kinds of singing are *makrūh* vs *ḥarām*. The book itself goes into this depth — but for your class, the *spirit* of the ḥadīth is the lesson, not the technical jurisprudence. (If they push for fiqh detail, point to the book and to Imam Nawawi's discussion in *al-Rawḍa*.)

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh hadith mushkil hai modern class mein. Music har jagah hai. Pehle hukm mat sunaao — pehle hadith ka *spiritual logic* explain karo: *yeh kyun nifāq grow karta hai dil mein?* Jab logic samjh aaye, hukm apne aap pareshaani nahin deta. Fiqh ki detail mein mat jao — yeh fiqh class nahin.

### The plant metaphor — *yunbitu*

The Arabic *yunbitu* = "causes to sprout, grow, vegetate". The same verb used in the Qurʾān for plants growing after rain. The metaphor is striking:

- Rain doesn't *force* a plant to grow — it provides the medium in which whatever seed is there will grow.
- Songs don't *make* hypocrisy from nothing — they provide the medium in which *whatever seeds of nifāq are already in the heart* will grow.

So the ḥadīth isn't saying every song produces a full hypocrite. It's saying that *prolonged immersion in songs* is **water for the seeds of nifāq** — those seeds being the small inconsistencies between what we believe and how we feel.

**Yaad rakho:** *Yunbitu* matlab "ugaata hai" — jaise paani ugaata hai. Paani plant nahin banaata — woh medium deta hai jisme jo seed hai woh ugay. Gaane *banate nahin* nifāq — woh paani hain. Tumhare dil mein jo seeds hain — woh paani ke saath barhte hain.

### What is *nifāq* here?

The book is careful: this *nifāq* is **not** the kufr-level hypocrisy (the kind condemned to the lowest level of Hell). It is the *practical* nifāq — the small inconsistency between what one professes and how one actually feels. This is the *ʿamalī nifāq* of Ḥadīth 27 of this chapter (we'll cover that below).

So the chain is: songs water the seeds → small inconsistencies grow → the gap between profession and feeling widens. Over years, this gap is a real spiritual problem — even if the person still prays, still fasts, still calls themselves a Muslim.

**Yaad rakho:** "Nifāq" yahaan kafir wala nifāq nahin — *ʿamali nifāq* (action wala). Yani dil aur amal ka farq. Banda kehta hai "Allah sab kuch hai" magar dil par koi aur asar zyada hota hai. Yeh gap badhta jaata hai gaane sun-sun ke. Aur woh gap khud ek bemari hai.

### Why specifically songs?

What is it about *songs* (as opposed to, say, food or sleep) that makes them *water for nifāq*?

The classical answer — which is worth understanding — is that songs **bypass the ʿaql and act directly on the qalb and the desires**. They smuggle messages, emotional states, longings, attachments past the rational filter. A film teaches with words; a song teaches with feeling. And feeling, repeated, *becomes* belief — without the person consciously noticing.

So songs are uniquely powerful at growing *unconscious commitments* — to a particular lover, a particular grief, a particular worldview, a particular self-image. Most are not aligned with what Islam wants in the heart. Hence: water on seeds of nifāq.

**Yaad rakho:** Gaane *ʿaql* ke filter ko skip karte hain — seedhe dil aur jazbaat pe asar karte hain. Film lafzon se sikhati hai — gaana feel se sikhata hai. Aur feel bar bar mile to woh "yaqeen" ban jaata hai, bina banda samjhe. Isi liye yeh khaas khatra hai.

### What to do with this in class

Don't ban anything from the classmates' lives in class. Just plant the spiritual logic. Ask each one to **observe** for a week: *which songs do I return to most? What do they teach my heart to long for? Is that longing aligned with what I'm trying to become as a Muslim?* That's the lesson. Behaviour change follows insight; it doesn't follow lecture.

**Yaad rakho:** Class mein "music chhodo" wali tone na use karo — observation ka kaam de do. Ek hafta apne suntey hue gaane note karo: *yeh dil ko kya sikha rahe hain? Kis cheez ki tamanna paida kar rahe hain?* Yeh self-awareness behaviour se ziyada barkat-wali hai.

## 25 · التُّجَّارُ يُحْشَرُونَ يَوْمَ الْقِيَامَةِ فُجَّارًا — "Traders will be gathered as transgressors"

**Source:** Tirmidhī, Ibn Mājah, Dārimī.

The matn: *"Traders will be gathered on the Day of Judgement as transgressors (fujjār) — except those who feared Allah, were righteous, and spoke the truth."*

### A general warning + a narrow exception

This ḥadīth is structured as a **general indictment** with a **narrow exception clause**. The Messenger ﷺ doesn't say *"some traders"* — he says *"the traders"* (with the definite article *al-tujjār*), implying the category as a whole. Then he carves out: *"except those who feared Allah, were righteous, and spoke the truth."*

Why such a sweeping start? Because the **default tendencies of commerce** — competition, persuasion, market positioning, profit pressure — push naturally toward small dishonesties: exaggeration, concealment of defects, false oaths, inflated claims, breaking of agreements. These pressures are real and constant. Without active *taqwā*, even a sincere person drifts into them.

The Prophet ﷺ is not condemning commerce — he is **naming its pressure-field**. Commerce is permitted; it is even praised; but it has a specific gravity that pulls toward fujūr, and only deliberate effort resists.

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith ka structure dekho — *general warning* phir *narrow exception*. Kyun? Kyunki tijaarat ka maahaul khud-ba-khud chhoti chhoti be-imaaniyon ki taraf le jaata hai — competition, profit pressure, persuasion. Banda chahe sincere ho, bina taqwa ke yeh pressure usay kheech leta hai. Hadith yeh pressure name kar rahi hai, tijaarat ko ban nahin kar rahi.

### Three conditions of the exception

The Prophet ﷺ names *three* qualities in the trader who escapes the general verdict:

1. **Ittaqā Allāh** — feared Allah. Active God-consciousness in the moment of transaction.
2. **Barra** — was righteous, was kind, dealt well. The verb has the sense of "completing one's duty with goodness" — beyond strict justice, into kindness.
3. **Ṣadaqa** — spoke the truth. About the goods, about the price, about availability, about defects.

Notice: these aren't three of "many" virtues — they are the **three specific virtues that the trading environment most tests**. The temptation in trade is to deceive (countered by *ṣadaqa*), to cut corners on quality (countered by *barr*), and to act as if no one is watching (countered by *taqwā*). The list is precise.

**Yaad rakho:** Teen shartein hain — *taqwa, ihsaan (barr), sach*. Yeh teenon usi maahaul ke counter hain jismein tijaarat hoti hai. Tijaarat *deceive* karne ko, *corner cut* karne ko, *guard utarne* ko bulati hai. Yeh teen virtues us bulawe ka jawab hain. Random list nahin — targeted counter hain.

### "Fujjār" — the word

*Fājir* (plural: *fujjār*) — this is the same word that appeared in Chapter 1, Ḥadīth 8 (*al-fājir khibbun laʾīm*). It means *one who has broken out of obedience, a transgressor, a dissolute*. Strong word. The Prophet ﷺ is using it of *traders by default* — to shock the audience and to wake them up to the pressure they're under.

This isn't a polite warning. It's a thunder-clap. The Prophet ﷺ wanted his community of traders (Madinan commerce was substantial) to feel the gravity.

**Yaad rakho:** *Fujjār* bohot sakht lafz hai. Banda taajir hai, Muslim hai, namaz parhta hai — magar agar woh taqwa nahin rakhta apne lain-deen mein, to is hadith ki nazr mein woh "fujjār" mein gina gaya. Yeh shock karne ke liye hi kaha gaya — taake taajir log apne aap ko samjhen.

## 26 · التَّاجِرُ الصَّدُوقُ الأَمِينُ — "The truthful, trustworthy trader"

**Source:** Tirmidhī, Dārimī, Dāraquṭnī.

The matn: *"The truthful and trustworthy trader will be in the company of the prophets, the very truthful (ṣiddīqūn), and the martyrs."*

### The pair to Ḥadīth 25

Ḥadīth 25 is the warning; Ḥadīth 26 is the promise. These are **a pair** — read them together. Don't deliver one without the other.

The same trader who, by default, would be raised as *fujjār* — if they hold to *ṣidq* (truth-telling) and *amāna* (trustworthiness) — is raised among the **highest classes of the believers**. The category jumps from *fujjār* to *the company of prophets*. This is one of the **largest moral promotions** in the entire ḥadīth corpus.

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith 25 aur 26 jodi hain — ek dusre ke baghair na padhai jaayein. 25 dhamki hai. 26 sila hai. Aur jo *jump* hai woh extraordinary hai — *fujjār* se seedha *anbiyā ki sangat tak*. Itni badi promotion deen mein bohot kam jagah milti hai. Saaf-dil tijaarat ka maqaam yeh hai.

### The two qualities — *ṣadūq* and *amīn*

- **Ṣadūq** — habitually truthful. The intensive form of *ṣādiq*. Not "doesn't lie much" — *always tells the truth*, even when it costs.
- **Amīn** — trustworthy. The intensive form of *āmin*. Discharges every trust given — money, goods, promises, secrets.

Together, these are *exactly* the two titles by which the Quraysh called the young Muhammad ﷺ before prophethood: *al-Ṣādiq al-Amīn* (the Truthful, the Trustworthy). The ḥadīth is therefore promising the trader who holds these qualities *the company that resembles the Prophet ﷺ himself* — because they are practising his pre-prophetic character.

This is a stunning detail. The Prophet ﷺ began his life as a *trader* (with Khadīja raḍiyallāhu ʿanhā's caravan) and was known as al-Ṣādiq al-Amīn. The promise of this ḥadīth quietly invokes that legacy.

**Yaad rakho:** *Ṣadūq* aur *amīn* — yeh wahi do alqab hain jin se Quraysh Nabī ﷺ ko prophethood se pehle bulate the. Yani hadith promise yeh nahin keh raha *"prophets jaisa banoge"* — kah raha hai *"Prophet ﷺ jo pehle tha (taajir) — uska kirdaar tum mein hai — to uski sangat tum mein hai"*. Yeh subtle hai magar gehra.

### Where this lands — both promotions and burdens

In the company of *al-nabiyyīn, al-ṣiddīqīn, al-shuhadāʾ* — the same list as Qurʾān 4:69 (which appeared in Chapter 1, Ḥadīth 3). The ranks of those whom Allah has favoured. The shopkeeper who is truthful and trustworthy is promised entry to this rank — by faithfulness in transactions, not by formal scholarship or martyrdom.

This should both **encourage** every classmate who does business — and **weigh on them**. The reward is enormous; therefore the responsibility is enormous. *Every transaction* is loaded with eternal consequence.

**Yaad rakho:** Sangat — *anbiyā, siddiqīn, shuhadā*. Wahi list jo Quran 4:69 mein hai. Yeh ahem hai — kyunki yeh batata hai ke deen mein *darja* sirf scholar ya martyr ka nahin hota — taajir ka bhi hota hai, agar uska kirdaar saaf ho. Yeh dilasa bhi hai, magar zimmedaari bhi.

## 27 · آيَةُ الْمُنَافِقِ ثَلاثٌ — "Three signs of a hypocrite"

**Source:** Bukhārī, Muslim. One of the most famous aḥādīth in the entire corpus.

The matn: *"The signs of the hypocrite are three: when he speaks, he lies; when he promises, he breaks the promise; when he is trusted, he betrays the trust."*

### The most-misused ḥadīth in modern Muslim life

This ḥadīth is **misused more often than almost any other**. Classmates will hear it and start *labelling other people* — "he's a hypocrite, look, he lied", "she's a hypocrite, she didn't keep her promise". This is the **exact opposite** of how the Prophet ﷺ intended this ḥadīth to be used.

Make this clear at the start, and keep returning to it:

> **This ḥadīth is a mirror for you, not a magnifying glass for others.**

The Prophet ﷺ named these signs *so that the believer can diagnose himself or herself*. *"Are any of these three growing in me? Where, when, with whom?"* That's the use. Not to police others.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh hadith *aaina* hai — *doosron par ungli rakhne ki cheez nahin*. Sabse zyada misuse hone wali hadith hai yeh. Class ko shuru se hi sakhti se yeh batao — *yeh apne andar dekhne ki cheez hai. Doosre ko "munafiq" kehne ke liye nahin.*

### Why "signs" (*āyat*) and not "definition"?

The Arabic *āya* = *sign, indication*. Not a definition. A definition says *what something IS*. A sign says *what to look for if you suspect it*. The Prophet ﷺ chose the word *āya* carefully — these are symptoms, not the disease itself.

This matters because:
- A person can lie once and not be a hypocrite. Even a believer slips.
- A person can break a promise once and not be a hypocrite. Circumstances change.
- A person can betray a trust once in weakness and not be a hypocrite. Repentance covers it.

The *signs* show up when these things become **habits** — when lying, promise-breaking, and betraying trust have become the person's default. Then they indicate something underneath.

**Yaad rakho:** *Āya* matlab "nishaani" — *taʿrīf* (definition) nahin. Ek baar koi jhoot bole, ek baar wadaa toḍe — woh "munafiq" nahin ban jaata. Hadith *aadat* ki baat kar rahi hai. Jab yeh teenon adatein bun jaayein — tab niche ek bemari hai. Sirf "one incident" ki taraf ungli mat uthao.

### The two readings — *ʿamalī nifāq* vs *iʿtiqādī nifāq*

The book gives the classical clarification carefully:

**Reading 1 — Hypocrisy in action (*nifāq ʿamalī*).** The person isn't a kāfir hiding belief — they really do believe. But their *behaviour* in these three areas resembles the behaviour of a true hypocrite. This is the dominant reading. *ʿAllāma al-Khaṭṭābī* (4th century AH ḥadīth master) holds this view.

**Reading 2 — A warning that these habits, if continuous, lead to hypocrisy of belief.** Practising these three over and over corrodes the belief itself. The signs are diagnostic *and* prognostic — they indicate present moral failure *and* warn of future faith-failure.

Both readings are valid. For your classmates, the takeaway is the same: **these three habits are spiritually dangerous, regardless of their technical classification**. Take them seriously in yourself.

**Yaad rakho:** Do reading hain — (1) *ʿamali nifāq* matlab amal mein hypocrisy, iman aakhri tarah nahin — yeh dominant reading hai. (2) Yeh adatein agar continuous hon to iman bhi corrode ho sakta hai — warning hai. Dono ka lesson ek hi hai: *yeh teen adatein gambheer hain, in se apne aap ko bachao*.

### The three habits — sharper definitions

**1. *Idhā ḥaddatha kadhab* — "When he speaks, he lies":** not just outright falsehood. Includes the *casual exaggerations*, the *story-telling that drifts from accuracy*, the *replies that suggest something the speaker knows isn't quite true*. Habitual lubricant in speech.

**2. *Idhā waʿada akhlaf* — "When he promises, he breaks it":** two sub-cases per the book (*Dalīl al-Fāliḥīn 5:159*) — (a) the person who promised *with no real intention to fulfil*, and (b) the person who promised *sincerely* but then breaks it *without valid reason*. Both count. Only (c) — promised sincerely, prevented by genuine cause — is excused.

**3. *Idhā 'tumina khān* — "When he is trusted, he betrays the trust":** any *amāna* — money entrusted, a secret entrusted, a responsibility entrusted. Includes the small daily trusts — workplace duties, family responsibilities, neighbour's possessions left in your care.

**Yaad rakho:** Teen adatein — *jhoot bolna, waadaa toḍna, amaanat ke saath khayanat*. Aur *jhoot* mein casual exaggeration aur "thodi badi baat" bhi shaamil hai. Waadaa toḍne mein woh waadaa bhi shaamil hai jo tum jaante huey ke nahin nibhao gi, kar do. Aur *khayanat* mein chhoti chhoti zimmedaariyon ki bhi laaprwahi shaamil hai — sirf paisa nahin. Yeh expand karna zaroori hai.

## 28 · الْكَبَائِرُ — "The major sins"

**Source:** Bukhārī, Muslim.

The matn (from the variant cited in Zad ut-Taalibeen): *"The major sins are: associating partners with Allah, disobeying parents, killing a human being [unlawfully], and the immersing oath (al-yamīn al-ghamūs)."*

### Why this short list — when other lists are longer?

There are multiple aḥādīth listing the *kabāʾir* (major sins). Some give 7, some give more. The list in *this* ḥadīth is **four**. Why these four specifically? Look at what they touch:

1. **Shirk** — violates the right of *Allah* (the greatest right).
2. **Disobedience to parents** — violates the right of *creatures* who occupy the highest human position over you.
3. **Killing a human** — violates the right of *life itself*, the foundation of all other rights.
4. **The immersing oath** — violates the right of *truth before Allah*, weaponising His name to deceive.

So these four cover: **the right of Allah**, **the right of creation** (parents as the gateway), **the right of life**, and **the right of speech sworn by Allah's name**. They are *foundational* — without these intact, all other rights collapse. That's why this list of four exists alongside longer lists.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh char khaas kyun? Char foundational haq tooḍte hain — *Allah ka haq* (shirk), *makhlooq ka haq* (waalidain ke zariye), *zindagi ka haq* (qatl), aur *Allah ke naam ka haq* (jhoothi qasm). Yeh foundation hai. Inhi pe baqi sab haq khade hain. Isi liye yeh char alag list mein hain.

### Don't moralise — clarify

This ḥadīth lists severe sins, and there is a strong temptation to *moralise* — to spend half a class warning about each one. Don't do this. Your classmates already know shirk is wrong, killing is wrong. They don't need a lecture on it.

Your job is to **clarify**, not to *moralise*:
- Clarify what counts as *minor shirk* (showing off, swearing by anything other than Allah).
- Clarify what counts as *ʿuqūq* of parents (not just hitting them — also *disregarding them*, *embarrassing them*, *not visiting*).
- Clarify what *al-yamīn al-ghamūs* is — most classmates haven't heard the term.

That is the value you add as a teacher. Otherwise the ḥadīth becomes a "don't sin" lecture and the classmates tune out.

**Yaad rakho:** Lecture mat do — *clarify* karo. Saari class ko pata hai "shirk haram hai", "qatl haram hai". Tumhara kaam hai un cheezon ko khulasa karna jo classmates nahin jaante: *minor shirk kya hai, parents ki ʿuqūq mein kya kya shaamil hai, yamīn al-ghamūs kya hai*. Yeh value tum doge — moralising nahin.

### *Al-yamīn al-ghamūs* — the immersing oath

The term most classmates won't have heard. Define carefully:

**Definition:** An oath sworn by Allah's name about a *past* event — affirming something happened or didn't happen — *while knowing the contrary is true*. So: deliberately lying under Allah's name, about a past fact.

**Why "immersing" (*ghamūs*)?** Because — as the book quotes from *Mirqāt* — this sin "immerses (sinks) the person in sin, then submerges him in the Hellfire". The metaphor is of being plunged in. The oath drags the swearer down.

**Examples in modern life:** swearing on the Qurʾān (or by Allah's name) that *"I didn't say it"* when you did; that *"I wasn't there"* when you were; that *"the goods are new"* when they're used; that *"I paid him"* when you didn't. This is the *yamīn ghamūs*.

This is distinct from a *future* oath (e.g., *"By Allah, I will visit you tomorrow"*) — those have different rules (expiation possible if broken). The *ghamūs* is specifically about the *past*, and is far more severe.

**Yaad rakho:** *Yamīn al-ghamūs* = jhoothi qasm Allah ke naam ki, kisi *guzri hui* baat ke baare mein, jaante hue. Future ka waada Allah ke naam pe lena alag baat hai (uska kaffara hai). Yeh *guzri baat ke baare mein* jhoothi qasm — sabse sangeen. *Ghamūs* matlab "doobne wali / dubaane wali" — qasm khud insaan ko dabaa deti hai. Yeh khaas khulasa karo class mein.

### Other major sins exist — this list isn't exhaustive

The book reminds (implicit in the listing) that there are *other* major sins not in this ḥadīth: *zinā, riba, theft, drinking khamr, fleeing from battle, falsely accusing a chaste woman, eating the property of an orphan*. These appear in other aḥādīth. The four in *this* ḥadīth are *the foundational set* — not the *exhaustive set*.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh char *complete* list nahin — *foundational* list hai. Doosre kabāʾir doosri hadithein mein aaye hain — zinā, riba, chori, sharab peena. Class ko clearly batao — *yeh char "selected" hain, baqi alag hadithein mein hain*. Confusion na ho.

## 29 · الْبِرُّ حُسْنُ الْخُلُقِ — "Righteousness is good character"

**Source:** Muslim, Tirmidhī.

The matn: *"Righteousness (al-birr) is good character (ḥusn al-khuluq), and sin is what causes unease in your chest and what you dislike others to become aware of."*

### Two definitions in one short ḥadīth

The Prophet ﷺ packs **two redefinitions** into this ḥadīth — of *birr* (righteousness) and of *ithm* (sin). Both are extraordinary.

**Definition 1 — Righteousness = good character.** Not *"righteousness includes good character"*. Not *"righteousness shows up as good character"*. *Righteousness IS good character.* This is the same definite-noun-equals-definite-noun construction we saw in Chapter 1 Ḥadīth 1 (*al-dīnu al-naṣīḥa*) — the strongest identification possible in Arabic.

This is a stunning equation. The whole edifice of *birr* — the umbrella term for all righteous deeds — is funnelled into *one thing*: **character**. Without good character, the deeds are empty. With good character, the deeds become alive.

**Definition 2 — Sin = what makes the chest tight, plus what one wouldn't want others to know.** Two markers — both subjective, both internal. The Prophet ﷺ is locating sin not just in the books of fiqh but **in the conscience**.

**Yaad rakho:** Ek hadith mein do redefinitions. *Birr (saara naik amal) = husn al-khuluq (acha akhlaq).* Aur *gunaah = jo dil mein chubhe + jo tum nahin chahti ke log jaane*. Dono internal hain — kitab se nahin, dil se diagnose hote hain. Yeh deen ka ek alag rukh hai.

### Why character is *all* of birr

A person can fast, pray, give zakāh, perform ḥajj — every external ritual — and **still be cruel, rude, dismissive, harsh, gossipy**. The Prophet ﷺ in another ḥadīth (Bukhārī) said of such a woman: *"She will be in the Fire"* — despite her abundant worship — *"because she harms her neighbours with her tongue."*

So the ḥadīth is correcting a deep misunderstanding: that *birr* is about quantity of *ʿibādah*. No — *birr* is about quality of *character*. The ʿibādah is essential, but it is meant to *produce* good character. If years of ʿibādah leave a person bad-tempered, judgmental, harsh, ungenerous — the ʿibādah hasn't done its job.

**Yaad rakho:** Banda namaz, roza, hajj sab kare — aur phir bhi *bad-mizaaj, kaḍwa, gheebat karne wala* ho — to uski ʿibādah ka asar nahin pohncha. *Birr* matlab woh saara naik amal — magar uski neechi tehmat *husn al-khuluq* hai. Agar ʿibādah ke baad bhi akhlaq nahin sudhra, to kuch missing hai. Yeh hadith yeh wake-up call hai.

### The two markers of sin — *ḥāka* and *karihta*

**Ḥāka fī ṣadrika** — *"caused unease in your chest"*. The verb *ḥāka* literally means "to weave back and forth" — like a stuck thread on a loom. So the image is: the action *snags* in your chest, doesn't pass smoothly, keeps tugging at you afterwards. *Conscience-flutter.*

**Karihta an yaṭṭaliʿa ʿalayhi al-nās** — *"you disliked that people should know about it"*. The privacy test. If you would be embarrassed for others to find out — that's a signal.

Together, these are an **internal diagnostic for situations where a clear ruling isn't obvious**. The book is careful to note (citing *Mazād al-Rāghibīn 16*): this applies *specifically when fiqh doesn't give a clear ruling*, or when the person doesn't know the ruling. If fiqh is clear that something is forbidden, you don't need the gut-check — the ruling settles it. The gut-check is for the *grey zones*.

**Yaad rakho:** Do internal nishaaniyaan — *seene mein chubhna* aur *log jaan jaayein to sharm aaye*. Magar yeh *grey areas* ke liye hai — jab fiqh ki ruling clear nahin ya tumhe pata nahin. Agar fiqh ne saaf mana kiya hai — to ruling kafi hai, gut feeling nahin chahiye. Yeh distinction zaroori hai.

### The fiṭra condition

The book adds a crucial caveat: this diagnostic *works only for one whose conscience is intact* — *"one who has returned to his innate purity (fiṭra) and whose heart has opened up to complete submission (islām)."*

A person drowned in sin has a *dead conscience* — their chest doesn't flutter at sin anymore, and they don't care who knows what they do. For such a person, this diagnostic is *broken*. They have to fix the *instrument* first (by repentance, by clean living, by company of the righteous) before they can use it to diagnose.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh diagnostic sirf saaf zameer wale par chalta hai. Agar banda gunaahon mein doob chuka hai — uska zameer mar gaya hai. Phir use chubhan nahin hoti, sharm nahin aati. Pehle zameer zinda karo — phir yeh hadith ka instrument kaam karega. Yeh subtle hai magar zaroori — warna class ka koi banda kahega "mere dil ko to chubhan nahin hoti, matlab gunaah nahin hai!".

## 30 · الْخَلْقُ عِيَالُ اللهِ — "All creation is Allah's family"

**Source:** Bayhaqī, *Shuʿab al-Īmān*.

The matn: *"All created beings are Allah's dependants (ʿiyāl), and the most beloved of creation to Allah is the one who is good (man aḥsana) to His dependants."*

### *ʿIyāl* — the family metaphor

The Arabic *ʿiyāl* — usually translated *"dependants"* — has a specific human feel. A man's *ʿiyāl* are his family members for whom he is materially and emotionally responsible. Wife, children, sometimes parents. Not just "people in his charge" abstractly — *people whose welfare he carries*.

The Prophet ﷺ uses this word for **all of creation** in relation to Allah. Every human, every animal, every tree, every fish — *ʿiyāl Allāh*. Allah carries their welfare. He feeds them, sustains them, knows them, loves them as a head-of-household loves his family.

This is one of the **softest images of Allah's relationship to creation** in the entire ḥadīth corpus. Not Allah as distant Sovereign — Allah as the One who has gathered all of creation under His care as a vast *family*.

**Yaad rakho:** *ʿIyāl* = "ghar wale, jin ki tum kafalat karte ho". Hadith mein Allah ne **saari makhlooq** ko apne *ʿiyāl* kaha hai. Sirf insaan nahin — janwar, paudhe, machhliyaan — sab. Yeh Allah ki softest tasveer hai — door wala Maalik nahin, sab ko apne kunbe mein samete hue Rabb.

### The Companion-test

The classical commentators ask: *how should we hear this metaphor?* The Prophet ﷺ doesn't mean Allah literally has *needs* like a head-of-household. The word is **borrowed** for its *relational quality* — Allah's nearness to creation, His care, His knowledge of their conditions.

A famous variant of this ḥadīth (also cited in *Mirqāt 8:731*) is: *"The best of you is the one most beneficial to people."* This variant strips the metaphor and gives the rule directly: **the most beloved to Allah = the one most useful to others.**

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith ka *ʿiyāl* lafz Allah ki *zaroorat* nahin batata — uske *taʿalluq aur shafqat* batata hai. Aur ek variant mein saaf kaha gaya — *"sabse acha tum mein woh hai jo logon ke sabse zyada kaam aaye"*. Yeh hi rule hai.

### The radical claim — doing good to creation is the highest

Look at the structure: *"the most beloved (aḥabbu) of creation to Allah is **the one who is good** (man aḥsana) to His dependants."*

The most beloved is defined not by a private quality but by a *relationship to others*: doing **iḥsān** — good — to Allah's family. So Allah's *most beloved* isn't necessarily the *most knowledgeable*, the *most worshipping*, the *most ascetic*, the *most articulate*. It is **the one who does the most good to His creation**. Service to the *ʿiyāl* of Allah = the highest possible station in Allah's love. (The book reinforces this with the related variant it cites — *"the best of you is the one most beneficial, anfaʿ, to people"* — same rule, plainer words.)

This is a re-orientation. Many classmates think Allah's love is won by *more rakʿahs*, more dhikr, more fasting. The Prophet ﷺ here points to a different — and harder — track: **being useful to people, animals, neighbours, the poor, the elderly, the sick, the lost.** *That* is the access route to being most-beloved.

**Yaad rakho:** Allah ka sabse ziada mahboob *zyada padha-likha* ya *zyada ʿibadat karne wala* nahin — *zyada kaam aane wala* hai. Yeh rerouting hai. Bohot log socht hain Allah ki mohabbat zyada rakaʿton se milti hai. Hadith batati hai — *logon ke kaam aana* sabse bara raasta hai. Aur yeh mushkil hai — kyunki yeh continuous hai, public hai, aur reward ka inkaar bhi karna parta hai.

### The chapter's closing image

Notice how the chapter *ends*. Ḥadīth 21 opened with *"friendliness toward people is half of intelligence"* — and now Ḥadīth 30 closes with *"the most beloved is the one who is good to people"*. The two ḥadīths frame the entire chapter:

- Open: *be sociable, learn from others, deal warmly.* (Theme: relational.)
- Close: *be useful, serve them, be the channel of their welfare.* (Theme: relational, deepened.)

The arc of the chapter is — among other things — a slow deepening of what it means to be **relationally alive as a Muslim**. From *friendliness* in #21, to *sociability as a sign of īmān* in #23, to *doing good (iḥsān) to creation* in #30. Each ḥadīth in this thread takes the previous one one level deeper.

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter shuru hoti hai #21 mein "logon se ghul mil ke rehna" se — aur khatm hoti hai #30 mein "logon ke kaam aana = Allah ki sabse zyada mohabbat" pe. Yeh ek arc hai — relational maturity ki seedhi. Class ko aakhir mein dikhao — *Chapter 3 ek tareeqe se yeh seekhata hai ke ek momin logon ke beech kaisa hota hai*.

## The big picture — what Chapter 3 has built

Stand back. By the end of these 10 aḥādīth, your classmates should be carrying **a four-axis portrait** of the awakened Muslim:

1. **Inner intelligence** — measured spending (21), self-mastery (22), conscience-led action (29).
2. **Outer warmth** — sociability (23), usefulness to creation (30).
3. **Marketplace integrity** — fear, kindness, truth in dealings (25, 26).
4. **Internal vigilance against traps** — songs as silent corroders (24), the three signs of nifāq (27), the four foundational kabāʾir (28).

These aren't 10 isolated rulings. They form **one coherent person** — and that person is who the book is calling *al-ṭālib*, the seeker.

**Yaad rakho:** 10 hadithein → 1 portrait. *Char rukh hain* — *andar ki aql, baahar ki garmi, tijaarat mein deyanat, jaal ke khilaaf hoshiyari*. Class ko aakhri mein yeh char rukh dikhao. Jaise Chapter 1 ne *talib* ki tasveer banayi thi — Chapter 3 us tasveer mein gehraai daal raha hai.

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

Now you understand the chapter. This section is how to *deliver* it to your classmates.

---

## The 8 landmines to watch for

### Landmine 1 · "Half" being heard as literal (Ḥadīth 21)

Classmates will hear *"half of provision, half of intelligence, half of knowledge"* literally — and ask *"what's the other half?"* Pre-empt by explaining the Arabic idiom *niṣf*.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh 'half' (nisf) literal nahin hai — yeh Arabic ka mohaawra hai. 'Nisf' yahaan matlab 'sabse bara hissa, decisive hissa'. Yeh kehne ka tareeqa hai ke *teen alag domains mein, teen alag adatein hain, har ek apne domain ka bulk hai*. Doosri half kya hai — yeh poochhna ghalat sawaal hai. Hadith yeh bata rahi hai ke yeh teen cheezein agar tum karoge to bohot zyada hisab tay ho jaayega."*

### Landmine 2 · "I'm an introvert" as objection to Ḥadīth 23

Classmates with introvert tendencies will object. Don't dismiss; redefine.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Hadith introvert ko mana nahin kar rahi — thandi-mizaaji aur takabbur ko mana kar rahi hai. Tum chup rahne wali ho? Bilkul theek hai. Magar jab tumhari mulaaqat hoti hai kisi se — kya tumhare chehre par garmi hai? Kya banda tumse mil ke khush hota hai? Yeh hai *yuʾlafu*. Aur kabhi-kabhi pohnchna apne maa-baap se, behan se, hamsaayi se — yeh hai *yaʾlafu*. Crowd mein har waqt rehna zaroori nahin — par dil ka thanda hona nahin chahiye. Yeh farq samjho."*

### Landmine 3 · Music ḥadīth — turning to fiqh debate (Ḥadīth 24)

Classmates will pull you into "but what about naat?", "what about duff?", "is this instrument haram?". **Don't go there.** This is not a fiqh class.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Hum aaj fiqh nahin kar rahe — agar tumhe specific halat ki ruling chahiye, kitab dekho ya kisi aalim se poochho. Yeh class hadith ke *spirit* ke liye hai. Spirit yeh hai — gaane *paani* hain dil mein. Dil mein jo seeds hain, woh barhte hain. Sawaal yeh nahin 'kaunsa gaana haram', sawaal yeh hai *'mere dil mein kya seeds hain, aur main kahaan paani daal rahi hoon?'* Yeh apne aap se poochho. Specific ruling alag baat hai — woh kitaab dekho."*

### Landmine 4 · Trader ḥadīth heard as condemnation of business (Ḥadīth 25)

Classmates who run businesses may feel personally attacked. Soften without dilluting.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Hadith tijaarat ko ban nahin kar rahi — Allah ne tijaarat halaal qarar di hai, Rasool ﷺ khud taajir thay. Hadith yeh keh rahi hai ke *tijaarat ka maahaul khud pressure laata hai* — competition, profit, marketing. Bina taqwa ke yeh pressure deceive karne ki taraf le jaata hai. Jo taajir taqwa rakhe, ihsaan kare, sach bole — woh agle hadith mein anbiya ki sangat ka wadaa rakhta hai. Yeh hadith taajir ke khilaaf nahin — taajir ko *jagaane* ke liye hai."*

### Landmine 5 · Three signs of nifāq used to label others (Ḥadīth 27)

This is **the biggest landmine of the chapter**. Classmates will go home and label half their relatives "munāfiq" using this ḥadīth. You must crush this on the spot.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh hadith *aaina* hai — *doosron pe ungli rakhne ki cheez nahin*. Rasool ﷺ ne yeh teen nishaaniyan isliye batayi hain ke tum apne andar dekho — *mujhme yeh teen kahaan, kab, kis ke saath ubhar rahi hain?* Yeh apni diagnosis hai. Agar tum apni saheli ya bhai ko 'munafiq' kehne ke liye yeh hadith use kar rahi ho — to tum hadith ka misuse kar rahi ho. Mein khud apne aap se poochhungi — *kya main kabhi waadaa karti hoon bina niyat nibhane ki? Kya main casual jhoot bolti hoon? Kya main koi amaanat halki tarah leti hoon?* Yahi sawaal hai."*

### Landmine 6 · Major sins → moralising lecture (Ḥadīth 28)

Resist the urge to give a "don't sin" lecture. Instead, clarify the *less-obvious* layers.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Saari class jaanti hai 'shirk haraam hai', 'qatl haraam hai'. Aaj yeh repeat nahin karenge. Aaj clarify karenge: *minor shirk* kya hai (riya, dikhawa, Allah ke alawa kisi ki qasm khaana), *waalidain ki ʿuqūq* mein kya kya shaamil hai (sirf maarna nahin — nazr-andaaz karna, embarrass karna, visit na karna, awaaz ooncha karna). Aur *yamīn al-ghamūs* — yeh shayad tumne pehle nahin suna ho — yeh hai *jhoothi qasm guzri baat pe Allah ke naam ki, jaante hue*. Yeh sabse khaufnaak qasm hai. Hum yeh definitions sikhayenge — lecture nahin."*

### Landmine 7 · "My heart doesn't sting, so it must be fine" (Ḥadīth 29)

Some classmates will use the second half of Ḥadīth 29 (*sin is what makes the chest uneasy*) as a license — *"this doesn't bother me, so it's not a sin."* Disarm.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh diagnostic sirf saaf zameer wale par chalta hai. Agar tumne saalon se ek cheez ki hai aur ab woh tumhe nahin chubhti — yeh matlab nahin ke woh sahi hai, yeh matlab hai ke tumhara zameer us cheez ke baare mein 'numb' ho gaya hai. Aur agar fiqh ki ruling saaf hai ke koi cheez haraam hai — to dil ki chubhan ka intazaar nahin karna. Ruling kafi hai. Yeh 'gut feeling' wala diagnostic sirf grey areas ke liye hai — jahaan fiqh saaf nahin ya pata nahin. Yeh distinction zaroori hai."*

### Landmine 8 · "Being useful = lots of social work" reduction (Ḥadīth 30)

Classmates may reduce this ḥadīth to *"do volunteer work, donate"* — missing the everyday application.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Logon ke kaam aana sirf bara bara kaam nahin — apni maa ka haath bantana, kisi pareshan saheli ko sun-na, hamsaaye ka khayaal rakhna, kisi bachche ko hosla dena — yeh sab 'anfaʿ li-ʿiyālihi' mein aata hai. Sabse bara kaam continuous chhote chhote kaam karne mein hai, jab koi nahin dekh raha. Yeh charity drive nahin — yeh aadat hai. Roz ki aadat. Tum kya choti se choti madad kar sakti ho kal jab uthhogi — yahi yeh hadith ka asal application hai."*

## Suggested 60-minute class plan

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–5 min | Salām, recitation of Surah Fātiḥah, intention. Brief recap: *we're in Chapter 3 of Zad ut-Taalibeen — Aḥādīth 21–30*. Last chapter built the foundations; this one sketches *the intelligent, socially-present, alert believer*. |
| 5–8 min | The 4 themes on the board. Tell them: 10 ḥadīths, four threads — *intelligence · social presence · trade integrity · spiritual traps*. |
| 8–22 min | **Theme 1 — The intelligent believer.** Aḥādīth 21, 22, 29. Read each, recite Arabic together, unpack quickly. For 21, drill the "three halves". For 22, emphasise the redefinition of *kayyis* / *ʿājiz*. Save 29 for after Theme 4 — it threads back to it (because *birr = good character* is the bridge to the warning ḥadīths). |
| 22–32 min | **Theme 2 — Social presence.** Aḥādīth 23 and 30. Read together as a pair — opening and closing of the relational arc. Handle Landmine 2 (the introvert objection). |
| 32–42 min | **Theme 3 — Trade & truthfulness.** Aḥādīth 25, 26 as a pair. Don't deliver one without the other. Handle Landmine 4 (business owners feeling attacked). |
| 42–55 min | **Theme 4 — Spiritual traps.** Aḥādīth 24, 27, 28. Most carefully. For 24, spend on the *spiritual mechanism*, not fiqh. For 27, crush the labelling-others trap *immediately*. For 28, clarify don't moralise. |
| 55–60 min | Re-read 29 in light of the chapter — *birr is good character*. Use as the closing: all of this is about the **character** we are building, internal and external. Distribute handout. Close with duʿāʾ. |

**Yaad rakho:** Pacing tighter hai is chapter mein — Chapter 1 mein 5 themes ki tarah nahin, yahaan 4 themes hain par 10 hadithein bantni hain. Theme 4 (jaal wali) sabse mushkil hai — wahaan zyada waqt do. Aur 29 ko theme 4 ke baad rakho — bridging effect ke liye.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 3

- ❌ **Detailed fiqh of music** triggered by Ḥadīth 24 — instrument-by-instrument rulings, *duff* vs other drums, naat with background music. Reference the book; stay with spirit.
- ❌ **Specific labelling of people** as munāfiq based on Ḥadīth 27. *No naming names.* Crush this immediately.
- ❌ **Long sermon on each major sin** in Ḥadīth 28. Clarify the *less-known* elements (minor shirk, *yamīn al-ghamūs*, scope of *ʿuqūq*); skip the obvious.
- ❌ **Modern political application** of the trader warning in Ḥadīth 25. Stay personal — *my* business, *my* shop, *my* online selling.
- ❌ **Sūfī states / maqāmāt** in the conscience ḥadīth (29). Don't take *ḥāka fī ṣadrika* into spiritual hierarchies. Plain text.
- ❌ **Extending Ḥadīth 30 into ecology / animal rights treatises** — true and beautiful as those applications are, this is not the time. Stay with the personal-relational application.
- ❌ **Cross-references to Chapter 4** — if classmates ask "what about ḥadīth 31, 32?", say *"yeh agle chapter mein dekhenge"*.

**Yaad rakho:** Tangents madad nahin karte. 10 hadithein hain — har ek apne aap mein dam rakhti hai. Aur cheezein mat daalo. Class ka focus = *yeh 10 hadithein dil mein bait jaayein, aur 4 themes pakke ho jaayein*. Bas.

## Self-check before teaching

- [ ] You can recite each of the 10 aḥādīth in Arabic from memory (or near-memory). *(Har hadith ki Arabic yaad ho.)*
- [ ] You can give the meaning of each + source. *(Har ek ka matlab aur source.)*
- [ ] You can name the **4 themes** and which ḥadīths go where. *(Char themes — aql, meeljol, tijaarat, jaal — aur kaun si hadith kahaan.)*
- [ ] For each ḥadīth, you can give a one-sentence Urdu summary without the book. *(Har ek ka ek-line Urdu khulasa.)*
- [ ] You have the 8 landmine counter-strategies — at least the Urdu phrasing. *(Aath landmines ki Urdu samjhauti.)*
- [ ] You can define *yamīn al-ghamūs* clearly. (Most classmates won't have heard it.)
- [ ] You can answer "but I'm an introvert" without softening Ḥadīth 23. *(Landmine 2 ka jawab tayar.)*
- [ ] You are ready to immediately crush any attempt to label other people as munāfiq from Ḥadīth 27. *(Yeh sabse zaroori — apne mind mein script ready ho.)*

If yes to all eight — *tum tayar ho.*

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Are they taking "half" literally in Ḥadīth 21? *(Niṣf ko literal le rahi hain?)*
2. Are they using "I'm an introvert" to dismiss Ḥadīth 23? *(Yeh objection aayi?)*
3. Did anyone fall into fiqh-debate on music? *(Music wali hadith fiqh debate mein chali gayi?)*
4. Are they labelling specific people as "munāfiq" based on Ḥadīth 27? *(Kisi ka naam le ke "munafiq" kaha?)*
5. Did the major-sins ḥadīth become a generic don't-sin lecture? *(Lecture ho gayi ya clarification?)*
6. Are they using "my chest doesn't sting" as license? *(Yeh excuse aayi?)*
7. Are they reducing "be useful" to big charitable acts only? *(Choti choti madad ka point miss hua?)*
8. Did they catch the **trader pair** (25+26) as a single unit? *(25 aur 26 ko jodi ki tarah samjha?)*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh 8 points tumhe batayenge kahaan revision chahiye. Khaas tor pe #4 — agar koi labelling kar raha hai, yeh foran agle hafte address karo. Yeh sabse bara misuse hota hai is hadith ka.
