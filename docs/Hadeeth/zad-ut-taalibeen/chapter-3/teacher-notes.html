🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Recite each of the 10 aḥādīth in Arabic, with meaning in English + Roman Urdu.
  2. Name the source of each (Bukhārī, Muslim, Tirmidhī, Ibn Mājah, Dārimī, Bayhaqī, Mustadrak).
  3. Explain the three "halves" (Ḥadīth 21) and why "niṣf" is idiomatic, not literal.
  4. State the two conditions of al-kayyis (Ḥadīth 22) and the trap of tamannā ʿalā Allāh.
  5. Articulate why ulfa (Ḥadīth 23) is two-directional and why "I'm an introvert" doesn't dismiss it.
  6. Explain the plant metaphor in Ḥadīth 24 — why songs grow rather than cause hypocrisy.
  7. Pair Ḥadīths 25 and 26 — the warning to traders and the promise to the truthful one.
  8. Use Ḥadīth 27 as a mirror, not a label — distinguish ʿamalī nifāq from iʿtiqādī nifāq.
  9. Name the four foundational kabāʾir in Ḥadīth 28 and define al-yamīn al-ghamūs.
  10. Apply both definitions in Ḥadīth 29 — birr = good character, and the two markers of ithm.
  11. Read Ḥadīth 30 as the chapter's closing — most-beloved = the one who does good to creation.

Ḥadīth 21 · الاقْتِصَادُ فِي النَّفَقَةِ · The three "halves"

Theme 1 — The intelligent believer. Three parallel clauses on spending, sociability, and questioning.

الاقْتِصَادُ فِي النَّفَقَةِ نِصْفُ الْمَعِيشَةِ، وَالتَّوَدُّدُ إِلَى النَّاسِ نِصْفُ الْعَقْلِ، وَحُسْنُ السُّؤَالِ نِصْفُ الْعِلْمِ

al-iqtiṣād fī'l-nafaqa niṣf al-maʿīsha · al-tawaddud ilā al-nās niṣf al-ʿaql · ḥusn al-suʾāl niṣf al-ʿilm

"Moderation in spending is half of one's sustenance; friendliness toward people is half of one's intelligence; asking good questions is half of one's knowledge."

"Mizaan se kharch karna rizq ka aadha hai; logon se ghul mil ke rehna aql ka aadha hai; acha sawal poochhna ilm ka aadha hai."

Bayhaqī · Shuʿab al-Īmān

الاقْتِصَاد · al-iqtiṣād — moderation in spending

Meaning
Income is mostly outside your control; spending is almost entirely within it. Moderation — between israaf (extravagance) and bukhl (miserliness) — accounts for most of how stable one's livelihood feels. The book points to Qurʾān 25:67.
Roman Urdu
Rizq Allah deta hai — istimal tum karte ho. Iqtiṣād matlab darmiyaan ki adat — *fuzool kharchi* aur *kanjoosi* dono ke beech. Yeh mizaaj hai, fixed amount nahin.
Example
Two families with the same income — one feels squeezed, the other feels comfortable. The difference is rarely the income; it's the spending posture. Iqtiṣād in action.

التَّوَدُّد · al-tawaddud — making oneself beloved

Meaning
Active warmth toward people — not just being polite, but seeking their affection, putting oneself out, showing up. The believer thereby gains access to others' intellect — and two minds together are more than twice one mind.
Roman Urdu
Logon se garmi se milna, unke saath ghul-mil ke rehna, unki madad lena. Akeli aql adhoori — doosre dimagh tak pohnchne se aql ka pooraa hissa milta hai.
Important
"Cleverness alone" is half-intelligence. Real intelligence is plugged into community.

حُسْنُ السُّؤَال · ḥusn al-suʾāl — beauty of asking

Meaning
A well-thought-out question contains half the answer. Random questioning yields random answers. The Companions famously asked the Prophet ﷺ very precise questions — and got precise answers.
Roman Urdu
Sawal poochna alag baat hai — sawal *banana* alag. Apne aap se poochho pehle: *main kya samajh nahin rahi, exactly?* Jab sawal saaf banta hai, aadha jawab apne aap mil jaata hai.
Example
"How do I get closer to Allah?" — too vague, will get a generic answer. "I find my mind wandering in ṣalāh; what specific habit changes the most for khushūʿ?" — precise question, useful answer.

⚠️ The big landmine (Landmine 1)

Classmates take "half" literally and ask "what's the other half?" — a wrong question. Pre-empt: "'Niṣf' Arabic ka mohaawra hai — matlab 'sabse bara hissa, decisive hissa'. Hadith teen alag adatein name kar rahi hai — har ek apne domain ka bulk hai. 'Doosri half' poochhna ghalat sawaal hai."

📿 Connect to Qurʾān · 25:67

وَالَّذِيْنَ إِذَا أَنْفَقُوْا لَمْ يُسْرِفُوْا وَلَمْ يَقْتُرُوْا وَكَانَ بَيْنَ ذَٰلِكَ قَوَامًا

"And those who, when they spend, are neither extravagant nor stingy, but hold a just balance between the two."

Why this fits: The verse names exactly the two poles — israaf and bukhl — and the middle (qawām). It describes the ʿibād al-Raḥmān (servants of the Most Merciful). The ḥadīth's iqtiṣād = the Qurʾān's qawām.

Ḥadīth 22 · الْكَيِّسُ مَنْ دَانَ نَفْسَهُ · The intelligent subdues the nafs

Theme 1 — Two redefinitions in one ḥadīth: kayyis (intelligent) and ʿājiz (foolish).

الْكَيِّسُ مَنْ دَانَ نَفْسَهُ وَعَمِلَ لِمَا بَعْدَ الْمَوْتِ، وَالْعَاجِزُ مَنْ أَتْبَعَ نَفْسَهُ هَوَاهَا وَتَمَنَّى عَلَى اللَّهِ

al-kayyisu man dāna nafsahu wa ʿamila li-mā baʿda al-mawt · wa-l-ʿājizu man atbaʿa nafsahu hawāhā wa-tamannā ʿalā Allāh

"The intelligent one is he who has subdued his lower self and worked for what comes after death; the foolish one is he who has put his lower self in pursuit of its desires and has vain hopes about Allah."

"Aqlmand woh hai jis ne apne nafs ko qaaboo kiya aur Akhirah ke liye kaam kiya; bewakoof woh hai jisne apne nafs ko khwaahishon ke peechhe lagaaya aur Allah pe khaali ummeedein rakheen."

Tirmidhī, Ibn Mājah
Where this fits: Theme 1, node 2 — the redefinition of "intelligence" along the axis of self-mastery + Akhirah-orientation.

الْكَيِّس · al-kayyis

Meaning
Not "clever in school" — sharp-witted, in command of one's faculties. The Prophet ﷺ defines it by two conditions: (1) has subdued the lower self, (2) works for what comes after death.
Roman Urdu
Sirf "padha-likha smart" nahin — *jisne apne nafs ko maalik banaya, naukar banakar rakha hai*. Aur jiska har faisla Akhirah ke taraazu pe tula hota hai. Yeh do shartein hain.
Verb dāna
From d-y-n — "to call to account, to bring into obedience". Not "to torture" — to discipline. The nafs becomes a trained servant, not a destroyed enemy.

The trap of tamannā ʿalā Allāh

What it is
Quoting "Allah is Most Merciful" to license sin or laziness — without effort to deserve mercy. The mercy is real; using it as a permit is false hope (al-tamannī al-fāsid).
Roman Urdu
"Allah Rehman hai, woh maaf kar dega" — yeh sach hai, magar yeh sentence *amal chhodne ka license* nahin. Quran khud dono saath rakhti hai — Ghafoor aur Azaab dene wala. Sirf rehmat ka hawala dena, kaam na karna — yeh "ʿajiz" wala raasta hai.
Qurʾān balance
15:49–50 — "Tell My servants that I am the Oft-Forgiving, Most Merciful, AND that My chastisement is indeed the most grievous." Both, together. 82:6 — "O man! What has deceived you concerning your Lord, the Generous?"

The horse and the child (Qaṣīdat al-Burda)

Quote
"The self is like a child who, if left alone, would grow up loving suckling — but if weaned, it would be weaned. So keep its desires in check, and beware lest it overpower you — for lust, when it overpowers, humiliates or destroys."
Roman Urdu
Nafs *ghoda* bhi hai aur *bachcha* bhi. Lagaam nahin to phenk dega. Discipline nahin to bigad jaayega. Parental sakhti — torture nahin. Yeh tarbiyat hai, sazaa nahin.

⚠️ Watch for the "Allah is forgiving" excuse

Some classmates use this — sincerely — to defer change. Don't moralise; just remind: "'Allah Ghafoor hai' yeh sach hai. Magar yeh sentence amal chhodne ka license nahin. Quran dono saath laata hai — rehmat aur warning. Sirf ek lena, doosre ko bhul jaana — yeh aql ka kaam nahin."

Ḥadīth 23 · الْمُؤْمِنُ مَأْلَفٌ · The believer is a source of affinity

Theme 2 — Social presence. The believer as maʾlaf — and the severe negation for the one warm in neither direction.

الْمُؤْمِنُ مَأْلَفٌ، وَلَا خَيْرَ فِيمَنْ لَا يَأْلَفُ وَلَا يُؤْلَفُ

al-muʾminu maʾlafun · wa lā khayra fīman lā yaʾlafu wa lā yuʾlafu

"The believer is sociable [with others]; and there is no good in one who is not sociable [with others] nor in one who is not met sociably [by them]."

"Momin maʾlaf hai — ghul-mil ke rehne wala, jisse log raahat paayein; aur uss mein koi khair nahin jo na khud milta hai (yaʾlafu) na log uss se milte hain (yuʾlafu)."

Mustadrak, Bayhaqī, Shuʿab al-Īmān
Where this fits: Theme 2, node 1 — the believer's social temperature, opening the relational arc that closes in Ḥadīth 30.

What the ḥadīth condemns — and what it doesn't

Condemns
Coldness of heart. Arrogance. Looking down on people. Avoiding them because they "drain" you. Making others feel unwelcome.
Does NOT condemn
Being reserved. Needing quiet. Choosing fewer but deeper friendships. Introversion. The ḥadīth attacks the absence of warmth, not the absence of socialising.
Roman Urdu
Hadith *introvert* ko ban nahin kar rahi — *takabbur aur thandi-mizaaji* ko mana kar rahi hai. Tum chup-chup rehne wali ho? Theek hai. Magar jab mile, garmi se milo. Kabhi-kabhi pohnchne wali bano. Dil thanda na ho — yeh asal sawaal hai.

The contagion of warmth

Logic
Warmth spreads. Coldness spreads. Whichever you bring into a room is what the room will return. The believer's job in any gathering is to raise the temperature of warmth — and this is daʿwa before any words are spoken.
Roman Urdu
Tumhari garmi infectious hai — agla insan agle ko garmi se milta hai. Tumhari thandi-mizaaji bhi spread hoti hai. Har jagah jahaan tum jaati ho, *ulfa* ka temperature thoda barhao — yeh bina ek lafz ke daʿwa hai.

⚠️ Pre-empt "I'm an introvert" (Landmine 2)

Don't dismiss the objection; redefine the ḥadīth's target. "Hadith *introvert* ko mana nahin kar rahi — *thandi-mizaaji* aur *takabbur* ko mana kar rahi hai. Tum chup rahti ho? Bilkul theek. Par jab mile, kya tumhare chehre par garmi hai? Kya log tumse mil ke khush hote hain? Yeh hai *yuʾlafu*. Aur tum kabhi-kabhi pohnchti ho — maa ko, bhai ko, hamsaaye ko? Yeh hai *yaʾlafu*."

📿 Connect to Qurʾān · 3:103

وَاعْتَصِمُوْا بِحَبْلِ اللَّهِ جَمِيْعًا وَلَا تَفَرَّقُوْا ۚ وَاذْكُرُوْا نِعْمَتَ اللَّهِ عَلَيْكُمْ إِذْ كُنْتُمْ أَعْدَاءً فَأَلَّفَ بَيْنَ قُلُوْبِكُمْ

"And hold fast, all together, to the rope of Allah and be not divided. Remember Allah's favour upon you when you were enemies and He brought your hearts together..."

Why this fits: The verb allafa ("joined together in love") is from the same root alifa as the ḥadīth's yaʾlafu / yuʾlafu. The Qurʾān frames ulfa as one of Allah's greatest gifts to the believers. The ḥadīth makes it a baseline expectation.

Ḥadīth 24 · الْغِنَاءُ يُنْبِتُ النِّفَاقَ · Songs cultivate hypocrisy

Theme 4 — Spiritual traps. Hardest ḥadīth of the chapter to deliver. Lead with the mechanism, not the ruling.

الْغِنَاءُ يُنْبِتُ النِّفَاقَ فِي الْقَلْبِ كَمَا يُنْبِتُ الْمَاءُ الزَّرْعَ

al-ghināʾu yunbitu al-nifāqa fī'l-qalbi kamā yunbitu al-māʾu al-zarʿ

Bayhaqī · Shuʿab al-Īmān
Where this fits: Theme 4, node 1 — the silent corroder. Pair this with Ḥadīth 27 (the three signs) when teaching.

The plant metaphor — yunbitu

Meaning
The verb yunbitu is the same one used in the Qurʾān for plants growing after rain. The metaphor is: songs don't create nifāq from nothing — they water seeds already in the heart. Whatever inconsistencies between your beliefs and your feelings exist — songs feed them.
Roman Urdu
Paani plant nahin banaata — woh medium deta hai jisme seed barhe. Gaane nifaaq banate nahin — woh *paani* hain. Tumhare dil mein jo seeds hain (chhoti chhoti inconsistencies), woh paani milne se barhte hain.

Which "nifāq" is meant here

Not
Iʿtiqādī nifāq — the kufr-level hypocrisy of the Madinan munāfiqūn. That's not what songs produce.
But
ʿAmalī nifāq — the practical inconsistency between what one professes and how one actually feels and acts. This is the same nifāq as Ḥadīth 27 below. Songs water this.
Roman Urdu
Yahaan *kafir wala* nifaaq nahin — *ʿamali* (amal wala). Yani dil aur amal ke beech ka farq. Banda kehta hai "Allah sab hai" — magar dil par koi aur cheez ki zyada pakad hai. Yeh gap badhta jaata hai.

Why specifically songs?

Mechanism
Songs bypass the ʿaql (rational filter) and act directly on the qalb and the desires. They smuggle longings, attachments, worldviews past conscious assessment. A film teaches with words; a song teaches with feeling. Feeling, repeated, becomes belief — without notice.
Roman Urdu
Gaane ʿaql ke filter ko skip karte hain — seedhe dil aur jazbaat pe asar karte hain. Film lafzon se sikhati, gaana feel se. Aur feel bar bar mile to woh "yaqeen" ban jaata hai — bina banda samjhe. Yahi gaane ka khaas khatra hai.

⚠️ Don't slide into fiqh debate (Landmine 3)

If classmates ask "what about duff?", "what about naat with background music?", "is this instrument haram?" — politely decline: "Aaj fiqh nahin kar rahe — yeh class hadith ke *spirit* ke liye hai. Specific halat ki ruling chahiye, kitab dekho ya kisi aalim se poochho. Spirit yeh hai — gaane *paani* hain, dil mein jo seeds hain woh barhate hain. Sawaal yeh hai *'mere dil mein kya seeds hain?'* — yeh poochho."

💡 The observation exercise (better than a ruling)

Ask classmates to observe for a week: "Kaunse gaane mujhe bar bar khichte hain? Yeh mere dil ko kya sikha rahe hain? Yeh kis cheez ki tamanna paida kar rahe hain? Why such a sweeping general warning?

Reasoning
The default pressures of commerce — competition, profit incentive, marketing — push naturally toward small dishonesties (exaggeration, concealing defects, false oaths, breaking agreements). Logic
Trade tempts to deceive (countered by ṣadaqa), to cut corners on quality (countered by barr — going beyond strict duty into kindness), and to act as if no one is watching (countered by taqwā). Targeted counters — not a random list of virtues.

Meaning
Plural of fājir — "transgressors, dissolute, those who break out of obedience". Same word as Chapter 1 Ḥadīth 8 (al-fājir khibbun laʾīm). A severe word, used here deliberately to shock traders into wakefulness.

Why "signs" (āya), not "definition"

Distinction
Āya = sign, indication. The Prophet ﷺ didn't say "the definition of a hypocrite is...". He said "the signs are...". A single slip doesn't make a hypocrite. The habit — these three becoming a person's default — is what the signs point to.

The two classical readings

Reading 1
ʿAmali nifāq (hypocrisy in action) — the person isn't a kāfir hiding belief; their behaviour resembles the hypocrite's. ʿAllāma al-Khaṭṭābī (4th c. AH) and the majority hold this reading.
Reading 2
Continuous practice corrodes belief — diagnostic and prognostic. The three habits, if entrenched, can eat the īmān they were planted alongside.

Do reading hain: (1) sirf *amali* nifaaq, iman aakhri tarah nahin — yeh dominant view. (2) Aadat continuous ho to iman tak ko corrode kar sakti hai — warning. Dono ka subq ek: yeh teen adatein gambheer hain.

Sharper definitions of the three

1. Lies
Not just outright falsehood. Casual exaggerations, story-drift, replies that suggest what the speaker knows isn't quite true. Habitual lubricant in speech.
2. Promise-breaking
Per Dalīl al-Fāliḥīn 5:159: (a) promising with no real intent to fulfil, or (b) promising sincerely then breaking without valid reason. Only being prevented by genuine cause is excused.
3. Betraying trust
Any amāna — money entrusted, secret entrusted, responsibility entrusted. Includes the small daily trusts — workplace duties, family responsibilities, neighbour's possessions, what people share in confidence.

Why these four?

Pattern
Four foundational rights are violated: (1) Allah's right, (2) creation's right via parents (highest human position over you), (3) life itself (foundation of all rights), (4) truth before Allah (Allah's name weaponised). Other rights collapse if these are unprotected.
Roman Urdu
Yeh char *foundational* haq tooḍte hain — Allah ka haq, makhlooq ka haq (waalidain ke zariye), zindagi ka haq, aur Allah ke naam ka haq. Inhi pe baqi sab haq khade hain. Isi liye yeh char alag list mein hain.

Less-known clarifications (the value-add for class)

Minor shirk
Riyāʾ (showing off in worship), swearing oaths by anything other than Allah, attributing power to other than Allah. Often overlooked because the classmate doesn't bow to an idol — but minor shirk is real.
ʿUqūq al-wālidayn
Not just hitting or shouting. Includes disregarding them, embarrassing them, not visiting them, not consulting them, making them feel a burden. The Qurʾān (17:23) forbids even saying "uff" to them.
Yamīn ghamūs
The defining clarification. A sworn oath by Allah's name about a past event — affirming it happened or didn't — while knowing the contrary is true. "Ghamūs" = "immersing / submerging": it plunges the swearer into sin and Hell-fire. Distinct from future oaths (which have expiation if broken). Modern examples: swearing "I didn't say it" when you did; "I wasn't there" when you were; "the goods are new" when used; "I paid him" when you didn't.
Roman Urdu
Class ke liye value yahi hai — minor shirk (riya, Allah ke alawa qasm), walidain ki ʿuqūq ki poori list (sirf maarna nahin — nazr-andaaz, embarrass, visit na karna), aur khaas tor pe yamīn al-ghamūs (guzri baat pe jhoothi qasm Allah ke naam ki). Yeh aakhri term shayad pehle nahin suna ho. Yeh khaas explain karo.

This list is foundational, NOT exhaustive

Other major sins exist
Other aḥādīth list: zinā, riba, theft, drinking khamr, fleeing from battle, falsely accusing a chaste woman, eating an orphan's property. These are major too — just not in this particular ḥadīth.
Roman Urdu
Yeh char *complete list nahin* — *foundational* list hai. Doosre kabaa'ir doosri hadithein mein. Class ko clear karo warna confusion ho jaayegi — *yeh char selected hain, baqi alag hadithein mein hain*.

⚠️ Don't slide into a "don't sin" sermon (Landmine 6)

Resist moralising. Your value is clarification, not warning. "Saari class jaanti hai 'shirk haraam hai', 'qatl haraam hai'. Aaj repeat nahin karenge. Aaj clarify karenge — minor shirk kya hai, ʿuqūq ki poori list, aur khaas tor pe yamīn al-ghamūs. Yeh teen cheezein jin ki definition shayad hazy hai."

عِيَال · ʿiyāl — the family metaphor

Meaning
A man's ʿiyāl are his family members for whom he is materially and emotionally responsible — wife, children, sometimes parents. Not abstract "people in his charge" — people whose welfare he carries. The Prophet ﷺ applies this word to all creation in relation to Allah.
Roman Urdu
*ʿIyāl* = ghar wale, jin ki tum kafalat karte ho. Aur hadith ke mutaabiq saari makhlooq Allah ki *ʿiyāl* hai — insaan, janwar, paudhe, machhliyaan, sab. Yeh Allah ki softest tasveer hai — door wala Maalik nahin, sab ko kunbe mein samete hue Rabb.
Important
Allah doesn't need like a head-of-household — the word is borrowed for its relational quality (nearness, care, knowledge of conditions), not for need.

The reroute — what wins Allah's love

Surprise
Allah's most beloved isn't named as "the most knowledgeable" or "the most worshipping". It is "the one who is good (does iḥsān) to His creation". Service to ʿiyāl Allāh = the highest station of Allah's love. (The book also relates the variant: "The best of you is the one most beneficial — anfaʿ — to people," Mirqāt 8:731.)
Roman Urdu
Bohot log socht hain Allah ki mohabbat zyada rakaʿton se milti hai. Hadith batati hai — *Allah ki makhlooq ke saath bhalai (ihsaan) karna sabse bara raasta hai*. Aur yeh continuous hai, public hai, aur reward ka inkaar bhi karna parta hai — mushkil rasta hai magar mahboob ka rasta hai.

The chapter's arc — opens with tawaddud, closes with iḥsān

Pattern
Ḥadīth 21 opened with tawaddud (friendliness = half of intelligence). Ḥadīth 23 deepened it (sociability is a sign of īmān). Now Ḥadīth 30 closes the arc (the one who does good to creation = most-beloved). The chapter slowly deepens what it means to be relationally alive as a Muslim.
Roman Urdu
Chapter shuru #21 mein "logon se ghul-mil ke rehna" se — khatm #30 mein "logon ke kaam aana = Allah ki mohabbat" pe. Yeh relational maturity ki seedhi hai. Class ke aakhir mein yeh arc dikhao — *Chapter 3 ek tareeqe se yeh seekhata hai ke ek momin logon ke beech kaisa hota hai*.

⚠️ Don't reduce to "social work" only (Landmine 8)

Classmates may hear "be useful" as "do volunteer work, donate". Expand it: "Logon ke kaam aana sirf bara bara kaam nahin — apni maa ka haath bantana, kisi pareshan saheli ko sun-na, hamsaaye ka khayaal rakhna, kisi bachche ko hosla dena — yeh sab is hadith mein hai. Sabse bara kaam continuous chhote chhote kaam karne mein hai, jab koi nahin dekh raha. Yeh aadat hai, charity drive nahin."

💡 Closing question for the class

End the chapter here. Ask: "Aaj raat se kal raat tak — koi ek chhota kaam tum kar sakti ho kisi Allah ke ʿiyāl ke liye? Bina batayey, bina shukria liya? Apne aap se waadaa karo — aur kal sochna kya hua. Yahi hadith ka asal application hai." Don't expect aloud answers — let the question land. Close with duʿāʾ.

⚠️ Common student confusions — watch for these

1. Taking "half" (niṣf) literally in Ḥadīth 21

Classmates ask "what's the other half?". It's idiomatic — "the decisive portion". Three halves means three habits, each accounting for the bulk of its domain.

2. Using "Allah is Most Merciful" as a license (Ḥadīth 22)

Classical commentators call this tamannī fāsid — corrupted hope. Mercy is real; using it to avoid action is misuse. The Qurʾān always pairs mercy with warning.

3. "I'm an introvert" as objection to Ḥadīth 23

The ḥadīth attacks coldness of heart, not introversion. A quiet warm person fulfils yuʾlafu; a reserved-but-reliable person fulfils yaʾlafu. Don't dismiss the objection — redefine the target.

4. Sliding into fiqh debate on Ḥadīth 24

Stay with the spiritual mechanism — songs water nifāq seeds. Don't argue about duff, naat with instruments, etc. Refer them to the book or a scholar.

5. Hearing Ḥadīth 25 as a condemnation of business

The ḥadīth names the pressure-field of trade. Pair it with Ḥadīth 26 — the trader who resists is promoted to the company of prophets. The Prophet ﷺ himself was a trader.

6. Using Ḥadīth 27 to label others as munāfiq

The biggest misuse. The ḥadīth is a mirror for self-examination. Crush any naming-of-others on the spot. The Prophet ﷺ said "signs" not "definition", and the dominant reading is ʿamali nifāq, not iʿtiqādī.

7. Reading Ḥadīth 28 as exhaustive list of major sins

Four foundational, not all. Other aḥādīth name more (zinā, riba, theft, khamr...). Clarify yamīn al-ghamūs in particular — most classmates haven't heard the term.

8. Using "no sting in my chest" as license (Ḥadīth 29)

The diagnostic only works on a heart still on fiṭra. Years of sin numb the chest. Plus, the diagnostic is for grey zones — if fiqh has ruled, ruling settles it.

9. Reducing "be useful" to formal charity only (Ḥadīth 30)

The hadith is about everyday usefulness — to parents, neighbours, the lost, the small. Continuous small kindnesses outweigh occasional big gestures.

📿 Connect to the Qurʾān

Verses that mirror the ḥadīths of this chapter. Use 2–3 in class — don't overload.

Sūrat al-Furqān · 25:67 — the spending verse

وَالَّذِيْنَ إِذَا أَنْفَقُوْا لَمْ يُسْرِفُوْا وَلَمْ يَقْتُرُوْا وَكَانَ بَيْنَ ذَٰلِكَ قَوَامًا

"And those who, when they spend, are neither extravagant nor stingy, but hold a just balance between the two."

Why this fits: Names both poles — isrāf and iqtār — and the middle, qawām. This is exactly the Qurʾānic version of the iqtiṣād in Ḥadīth 21. Description of ʿibād al-Raḥmān.

Sūrat al-Ḥijr · 15:49–50 — mercy and warning together

نَبِّئْ عِبَادِيْ أَنِّيْ أَنَا الْغَفُوْرُ الرَّحِيْمُ ۝ وَأَنَّ عَذَابِيْ هُوَ الْعَذَابُ الأَلِيْمُ

"Inform My servants that I am the Oft-Forgiving, the Most Merciful — and that My chastisement is the most grievous chastisement."

Why this fits: Two verses, one breath. Mercy and warning together — never one without the other. Direct counter to the tamannā ʿalā Allāh trap of Ḥadīth 22.

Sūrat Āl ʿImrān · 3:103 — Allah brought hearts together

وَاعْتَصِمُوْا بِحَبْلِ اللَّهِ جَمِيْعًا وَلَا تَفَرَّقُوْا ۚ ... فَأَلَّفَ بَيْنَ قُلُوْبِكُمْ فَأَصْبَحْتُمْ بِنِعْمَتِهِ إِخْوَانًا

"Hold fast, all together, to the rope of Allah, and be not divided... He brought your hearts together, so by His grace you became brothers."

Why this fits: The verb allafa (joined hearts in love) is from the same root as yaʾlafu / yuʾlafu in Ḥadīth 23. The Qurʾān frames ulfa as a major divine gift; the ḥadīth makes it a baseline expectation of the believer.

Sūrat al-Nisāʾ · 4:69 — the company of the prophets

وَمَنْ يُّطِعِ اللَّهَ وَالرَّسُولَ فَأُولَٰئِكَ مَعَ الَّذِيْنَ أَنْعَمَ اللَّهُ عَلَيْهِم مِّنَ النَّبِيِّيْنَ وَالصِّدِّيْقِيْنَ وَالشُّهَدَاءِ وَالصَّالِحِيْنَ

"Whoever obeys Allah and the Messenger — they will be with those upon whom Allah has bestowed favour: of the Prophets, the very truthful, the martyrs, and the righteous."

Why this fits: Same three ranks named in Ḥadīth 26's promise to the truthful trader. The verse describes the company; the ḥadīth specifies how a trader enters it — through ṣidq and amāna.

Sūrat al-Isrāʾ · 17:23 — the threshold for ʿuqūq

وَقَضَىٰ رَبُّكَ أَلَّا تَعْبُدُوا إِلَّا إِيَّاهُ وَبِالْوَالِدَيْنِ إِحْسَانًا ۚ ... فَلَا تَقُلْ لَهُمَا أُفٍّ

"Your Lord has decreed that you worship none but Him, and that you be kind to parents. ... Do not say to them even 'uff'..."

Why this fits: The verse pairs tawḥīd with parental iḥsān — the same two rights at the head of Ḥadīth 28. And the "uff" threshold defines the bar for ʿuqūq: even a syllable of dismissiveness is forbidden.

Sūrat Luqmān · 31:6 — the entertaining speech that misleads

وَمِنَ النَّاسِ مَنْ يَّشْتَرِيْ لَهْوَ الْحَدِيْثِ لِيُضِلَّ عَنْ سَبِيْلِ اللَّهِ بِغَيْرِ عِلْمٍ

"And of mankind is he who pays for diverting speech, that he may mislead from Allah's way without knowledge."

Why this fits: Many of the great exegetes (Ibn Masʿūd, Ibn ʿAbbās, Mujāhid, ʿIkrima, Maymūn ibn Mahrān) said this lahw al-ḥadīth ("diverting speech") refers specifically to songs and music. Direct Qurʾānic backing for Ḥadīth 24's warning.
