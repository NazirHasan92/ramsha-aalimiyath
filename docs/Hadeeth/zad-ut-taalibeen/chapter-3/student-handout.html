Chapter 3 — Student Handout

Aḥādīth 21–30 from Part 1 of Zad ut-Taalibeen

👋 Welcome — what we'll learn today

Today we continue Zad ut-Taalibeen (Provisions for the Seekers) — Aḥādīth 21–30. After Chapter 2 deepened the foundations of worship and conduct, this chapter sketches the awakened, alert, socially-present Muslim — and names the silent traps that pull a person off that portrait.

Ten aḥādīth fall into four themes:

  1. The intelligent believer (#21, #22, #29) — moderation, mastering the nafs, defining birr by character.
  2. Social presence (#23, #30) — sociability as a sign of īmān; nearness to Allah through usefulness to His creation.
  3. Trade & truthfulness (#25, #26) — the warning to traders, and the promise to honest ones.
  4. Spiritual traps (#24, #27, #28) — what corrodes the heart silently: songs, hypocrisy-in-action, the major sins.

Each ḥadīth section gives you: the Arabic, the meaning in English and Roman Urdu, the source, and a brief explanation. Then there are reflection questions and exercises at the end.

Ḥadīth 21 · The three "halves"

الاقْتِصَادُ فِي النَّفَقَةِ نِصْفُ الْمَعِيشَةِ، وَالتَّوَدُّدُ إِلَى النَّاسِ نِصْفُ الْعَقْلِ، وَحُسْنُ السُّؤَالِ نِصْفُ الْعِلْمِ

al-iqtiṣād fī'l-nafaqa niṣf al-maʿīsha · al-tawaddud ilā al-nās niṣf al-ʿaql · ḥusn al-suʾāl niṣf al-ʿilm

"Moderation in spending is half of one's sustenance; friendliness toward people is half of one's intelligence; asking good questions is half of one's knowledge."

"Mizaan se kharch karna rizq ka aadha hai; logon se ghul mil ke rehna aql ka aadha hai; acha sawal poochhna ilm ka aadha hai."

Bayhaqī · Shuʿab al-Īmān

About "half": The Arabic niṣf here is idiomatic, not literal. It means "the decisive portion, the bulk of it." So three habits, each of which accounts for the bulk of its domain.

Half 1 — moderation in spending. Income is mostly outside your control (Allah is the Provider). But spending is almost entirely within your control. A person who learns to spend with measure — not extravagant, not stingy — finds their provision feels doubled, even if their income hasn't changed. The Qurʾān (25:67) describes the servants of the Most Merciful as those who, when they spend, are neither wasteful nor miserly, but balanced in between.

Half 2 — friendliness toward people. The Arabic tawaddud means actively seeking others' warmth — not just being polite, but putting yourself out, showing up, building connection. Why is this "half of intelligence"? Because intelligence in Islam is never solo. The collaboration of two minds is more than twice one mind. A cold person stays at their own ceiling; a warm person gets access to others' insight.

Half 3 — asking good questions. The Arabic is ḥusn al-suʾāl — "beauty of asking". A well-formed question contains half the answer. Random questioning yields random answers. The Companions famously asked the Prophet ﷺ very precise questions — and got precise answers in return.

💡 Key insight

Three habits — spending with measure, warmth toward people, questioning well — account for most of what makes a person's life intelligent. They aren't impressive or showy. They're quiet, daily, decisive.

"Teen choti choti adatein — paisa, log, sawaal — milkar ek aqlmand zindagi ki tasveer banati hain. Yeh chapak-dhamak wali baat nahin — chup-chup, rozaana, decisive."

Ḥadīth 22 · The intelligent subdues the nafs

الْكَيِّسُ مَنْ دَانَ نَفْسَهُ وَعَمِلَ لِمَا بَعْدَ الْمَوْتِ، وَالْعَاجِزُ مَنْ أَتْبَعَ نَفْسَهُ هَوَاهَا وَتَمَنَّى عَلَى اللَّهِ

al-kayyisu man dāna nafsahu wa ʿamila li-mā baʿda al-mawt · wa-l-ʿājizu man atbaʿa nafsahu hawāhā wa-tamannā ʿalā Allāh

"The intelligent one is he who has subdued his lower self and worked for what comes after death; and the foolish one is he who has put his lower self in pursuit of its desires and has vain hopes about Allah."

"Aqlmand woh hai jis ne apne nafs ko qaaboo kiya aur Akhirah ke liye kaam kiya; bewakoof woh hai jis ne apne nafs ko khwaahishon ke peechhe lagaaya aur Allah pe khaali ummeedein rakheen."

Tirmidhī, Ibn Mājah

Two redefinitions in one ḥadīth:

The intelligent (al-kayyis)The foolish (al-ʿājiz)
Has subdued his nafs Has followed his nafs (nafs in the driver's seat)
Works for what comes after death Has vain hopes about Allah (without effort)

The "vain hopes" trap (tamannā ʿalā Allāh): This is when a person quotes "Allah is Most Merciful" as a license to keep doing wrong — without effort to deserve mercy. The mercy is real; using it as a permit is false. The Qurʾān always pairs mercy with warning:

نَبِّئْ عِبَادِيْ أَنِّيْ أَنَا الْغَفُوْرُ الرَّحِيْمُ ۝ وَأَنَّ عَذَابِيْ هُوَ الْعَذَابُ الأَلِيْمُ

"Inform My servants that I am the Oft-Forgiving, the Most Merciful — AND that My chastisement is the most grievous chastisement." (15:49–50)

💡 Key insight

The Prophet ﷺ redefines "intelligent". It isn't being clever in school or successful at business. It is — self-mastery + Akhirah-orientation. By that measure alone, all our familiar measures of intelligence need rechecking.

"Aqlmand kaun? Jo apne nafs ka maalik ho, aur jo Akhirah ke liye kaam karta ho. Tum kitne bhi padhi-likhi ho — agar yeh do shartein nahin, to is hadith ke meeyaar par poori nahin utharti."

Ḥadīth 23 · The believer is sociable (maʾlaf)

الْمُؤْمِنُ مَأْلَفٌ، وَلَا خَيْرَ فِيمَنْ لَا يَأْلَفُ وَلَا يُؤْلَفُ

al-muʾminu maʾlafun · wa lā khayra fīman lā yaʾlafu wa lā yuʾlafu

"The believer is sociable [with others]; and there is no good in one who is not sociable [with others] nor in one who is not met sociably [by them]."

"Momin maʾlaf hai — ghul-mil ke rehne wala, jisse log raahat paayein; aur uss mein koi khair nahin jo na khud milta hai na log uss se milte hain."

Mustadrak, Bayhaqī, Shuʿab al-Īmān

The believer is maʾlaf — a source of affinity. The matn describes the believer with one word, maʾlaf: someone with whom people feel at home, easy to warm to. Then it names what its absence looks like — and the same root (alifa) appears in two forms in the negation:

  • Yaʾlafu (active) — he goes toward others, greets, visits, helps.
  • Yuʾlafu (passive) — people feel at ease with him, seek him, return to him.

"No good in one who neither yaʾlafu nor yuʾlafu" — warmth must flow in both directions. The believer (the maʾlaf) is precisely the one in whom it does.

What this ḥadīth attacks — and what it doesn't: It does not condemn quiet people, reserved people, introverts. It attacks coldness of heart — arrogance, looking down on people, making others feel unwelcome. A quiet person who is warm when met fulfils yuʾlafu. A reserved person who shows up when needed fulfils yaʾlafu.

The Qurʾān on ulfa (3:103):

فَأَلَّفَ بَيْنَ قُلُوْبِكُمْ فَأَصْبَحْتُمْ بِنِعْمَتِهِ إِخْوَانًا

"He brought your hearts together (allafa), so by His grace you became brothers."

The verb allafa is from the same root as the ḥadīth's yaʾlafu / yuʾlafu. The Qurʾān frames ulfa as one of Allah's greatest gifts to believers. The ḥadīth makes it a baseline expectation.

💡 Reflection

Warmth is contagious. So is coldness. Whichever you bring into a room is what the room will return to the next person. The believer's job in any gathering: raise the temperature of warmth. This is daʿwa before a single word is spoken.

"Tumhari garmi infectious hai. Tumhari thandi-mizaaji bhi. Har jagah jahaan tum jaati ho, *ulfa* ka temperature thoda barhao — yeh bina lafz ke daʿwa hai."

Ḥadīth 24 · Songs grow hypocrisy

الْغِنَاءُ يُنْبِتُ النِّفَاقَ فِي الْقَلْبِ كَمَا يُنْبِتُ الْمَاءُ الزَّرْعَ

al-ghināʾu yunbitu al-nifāqa fī'l-qalbi kamā yunbitu al-māʾu al-zarʿ

"Songs cause hypocrisy to grow in the heart just as water makes crops grow."

"Gaane dil mein nifaaq ko ugaate hain, jaise paani fasal ko ugaata hai."

Bayhaqī · Shuʿab al-Īmān

The plant metaphor. The Arabic verb yunbitu is the same verb used in the Qurʾān for plants growing after rain. The metaphor is striking:

  • Rain doesn't force a plant to grow — it provides the medium in which whatever seed is there will grow.
  • Songs don't make hypocrisy from nothing — they provide the medium in which the seeds already in the heart sprout.

Which "nifāq"? Not the kufr-level hypocrisy of the Madinan munāfiqūn. This is ʿamali nifāq — the practical inconsistency between what one professes and how one actually feels and acts. The gap between belief and emotional reality.

Why specifically songs? Songs bypass the ʿaql (rational filter) and act directly on the qalb and the desires. They smuggle longings, attachments, and worldviews past your conscious assessment. A film teaches with words; a song teaches with feeling. And feeling, repeated, becomes belief — without notice.

The Qurʾān (31:6) is referenced by many of the great exegetes (Ibn Masʿūd, Ibn ʿAbbās, Mujāhid) as a warning about this very thing:

وَمِنَ النَّاسِ مَنْ يَّشْتَرِيْ لَهْوَ الْحَدِيْثِ لِيُضِلَّ عَنْ سَبِيْلِ اللَّهِ بِغَيْرِ عِلْمٍ

"And of mankind is he who pays for diverting speech, that he may mislead from Allah's way without knowledge."

💡 An observation exercise — not a ruling

This week, observe — don't change anything yet, just observe: Which songs do I return to most? What do they teach my heart to long for? Is that longing aligned with what I'm trying to become as a Muslim?

Behaviour change follows insight, not lecture. The first step is awareness of what's being watered.

"Yeh hafta apne sune hue gaane note karo — dil ko yeh kya sikha rahe hain? Kis cheez ki tamanna paida kar rahe hain? Stop karne ka hukm nahin — sirf observe karo. Awareness ke baad behaviour apne aap shift hota hai."

Ḥadīth 25 · Traders gathered as transgressors — except...

التُّجَّارُ يُحْشَرُونَ يَوْمَ الْقِيَامَةِ فُجَّارًا، إِلَّا مَنِ اتَّقَى وَبَرَّ وَصَدَقَ

al-tujjāru yuḥsharūna yawma al-qiyāmati fujjāran, illā man ittaqā wa-barra wa-ṣadaqa

"Traders will be gathered on the Day of Judgement as transgressors (fujjār) — except those who feared Allah, were righteous [in their oaths], and spoke the truth."

"Taajir Qiyamat ke din fujjaar bana ke jama kiye jaayenge — siwaay un ke jinhone Allah se daraa, ihsaan kiya aur sach bola."

Tirmidhī, Ibn Mājah, Dārimī

Read this with Ḥadīth 26 — they are a pair. Ḥadīth 25 is the warning; Ḥadīth 26 is the promise. Don't separate them.

Why such a sweeping warning? The default pressures of commerce — competition, profit incentive, persuasion — push naturally toward small dishonesties: exaggeration, concealing defects, false oaths, breaking agreements. Without active taqwā, even a sincere person drifts. The Prophet ﷺ is naming the pressure-field of trade, not condemning trade itself.

The three escape-conditions, and what they counter:

ConditionWhat it counters
Feared Allah (ittaqā Allāh)Acting as if no one is watching
Was righteous / kind (barra)Cutting corners on quality
Spoke the truth (ṣadaqa)The temptation to deceive in describing goods

⚠️ Read this honestly if you do business

If you own a shop, run a small business, or sell online — sit with this ḥadīth. The Prophet ﷺ is not insulting you; he is warning you. The trade environment will pull you. Do you feel the pull? In what specific moment? Taqwā, barr, ṣidq — these aren't abstract virtues; they're the exact armour for that pull.

"Agar tum koi business chalati ho — yeh hadith sakhti se nahin, mohabbat se kah rahi hai. Maahaul tum pe asar karega. Bachne ke liye yeh teen cheezein — Allah ka khauf, ihsaan, aur sach — exact dawai hain."

Ḥadīth 26 · The truthful, trustworthy trader

التَّاجِرُ الصَّدُوقُ الأَمِينُ مَعَ النَّبِيِّيْنَ وَالصِّدِّيْقِيْنَ وَالشُّهَدَاءِ

al-tājiru al-ṣadūqu al-amīnu maʿa al-nabiyyīn wa-l-ṣiddīqīn wa-l-shuhadāʾ

"The truthful and trustworthy trader will be in the company of the prophets, the very truthful, and the martyrs."

"Sachha aur amaanat-daar taajir Qiyamat ke din anbiya, siddiqeen aur shuhada ke saath hoga."

Tirmidhī, Dārimī, Dāraquṭnī

The pair to Ḥadīth 25 — the promise. The same trader who, by default, would be gathered as fujjār — if they hold to ṣidq (truth) and amāna (trustworthiness) — is raised among the highest classes of believers. This is one of the largest moral promotions in the entire ḥadīth corpus.

The hidden Prophetic echo. Notice the two titles: al-Ṣādiq and al-Amīn. These are exactly the two names by which the Quraysh called the young Muhammad ﷺ before prophethood — when he was himself a trader in Khadīja's caravans. So the ḥadīth quietly says: "the trader who has the Prophet ﷺ's pre-prophetic character will share his company."

The Qurʾān names the same company (4:69):

فَأُولَٰئِكَ مَعَ الَّذِيْنَ أَنْعَمَ اللَّهُ عَلَيْهِم مِّنَ النَّبِيِّيْنَ وَالصِّدِّيْقِيْنَ وَالشُّهَدَاءِ وَالصَّالِحِيْنَ

"...with those upon whom Allah has bestowed favour: of the Prophets, the very truthful, the martyrs, and the righteous."

💡 Reflection

Rank in dīn isn't only for scholars or martyrs. The honest shopkeeper, the truthful online seller, the trustworthy contractor — they have access to the same ranks. Through their transactions. Every honest deal is recorded.

"Anbiya ki sangat sirf ulama ke liye nahin. Imandari se kaam karne wale taajir bhi usi sangat ke ahl hain. Har sachha lain-deen Akhirah ke khaate mein darj hai."

Ḥadīth 27 · Three signs of a hypocrite

آيَةُ الْمُنَافِقِ ثَلاثٌ: إِذَا حَدَّثَ كَذَبَ، وَإِذَا وَعَدَ أَخْلَفَ، وَإِذَا اؤْتُمِنَ خَانَ

āyatu al-munāfiqi thalāth · idhā ḥaddatha kadhab · wa-idhā waʿada akhlaf · wa-idhā 'tumina khān

"The signs of a hypocrite are three: when he speaks, he lies; when he promises, he breaks it; and when he is trusted, he betrays the trust."

"Munaafiq ki teen nishaaniyaan hain — jab baat kare to jhoot bole, jab waadaa kare to toḍe, aur jab uss pe aitmaad kiya jaaye to khayanat kare."

Bukhārī, Muslim

⚠️ Before anything else — read this

This ḥadīth is a mirror for YOU. It is NOT a label for others.

The Prophet ﷺ named these signs so that the believer can diagnose himself or herself — "Are any of these three growing in me? Where, when, with whom?" That is the proper use of this ḥadīth.

Using this ḥadīth to call a relative, a friend, a colleague, or a spouse "munāfiq" is a misuse of the Prophet ﷺ's words. Don't do it.

"Yeh hadith aaina hai — doosre par ungli rakhne ki cheez nahin. Tum apne aap se poochho — mujh mein yeh teen kahaan ubhar rahi hain? Kisi rishte-daar ko 'munafiq' kehne ke liye yeh hadith use karna ghalat hai."

Why "signs" (āya) and not "definition"? A sign is a symptom, not the disease itself. A person can lie once and not be a hypocrite. A person can break one promise and not be a hypocrite. The signs show up when these things become habits — defaults in how a person operates.

Two classical readings:

  1. ʿAmali nifāq (hypocrisy in action) — the person isn't a kāfir hiding belief; their behaviour resembles the hypocrite's. This is the dominant reading.
  2. Warning — if these habits become continuous, they corrode the belief itself. Diagnostic and prognostic.

Sharper definitions of the three:

SignWhat it includes (often missed)
Speaks → liesNot just outright falsehood. Casual exaggerations, story-drift, replies that suggest what you know isn't true. Habitual lubricant in speech.
Promises → breaks(a) Promising with no real intent to fulfil, OR (b) promising sincerely then breaking without valid reason. Only being prevented by genuine cause is excused.
Trusted → betraysAny amāna — money, secret, responsibility. Includes small daily trusts — workplace duties, family responsibilities, neighbour's possessions. Not only "money entrusted."

Ḥadīth 28 · The four foundational major sins

الْكَبَائِرُ: الإِشْرَاكُ بِاللَّهِ، وَعُقُوقُ الْوَالِدَيْنِ، وَقَتْلُ النَّفْسِ، وَالْيَمِينُ الْغَمُوسُ

al-kabāʾir · al-ishrāku bi-llāh · wa-ʿuqūqu al-wālidayn · wa-qatlu al-nafs · wa-l-yamīnu al-ghamūs

"The major sins are: associating partners with Allah, disobeying parents, killing a human being [unlawfully], and the immersing oath."

"Kabaa'ir gunaah hain — Allah ke saath shirk karna, walidain ki naafarmaani, kisi ko qatl karna (na-haq), aur dubaane wali jhoothi qasm."

Bukhārī, Muslim

Why these four, specifically? Each violates a foundational right:

  1. Shirk — violates Allah's right (the greatest right).
  2. Disobedience to parents — violates the highest human position over you.
  3. Unlawful killing — violates the foundation of all other rights (life itself).
  4. The immersing oath — violates Allah's name (used to deceive).

Other major sins exist in other aḥādīth (zinā, riba, theft, drinking khamr, etc.). This list is foundational, not exhaustive.

The less-known clarifications:

TermWhat classmates often miss
Minor shirkRiyāʾ (showing off in worship), swearing oaths by anything other than Allah, attributing power to other than Allah. Easy to overlook because no one is bowing to an idol.
ʿUqūq al-wālidaynNot just hitting/shouting. Includes disregarding them, embarrassing them, not visiting, making them feel a burden. Qurʾān 17:23 forbids even saying "uff".
Yamīn ghamūsA sworn oath by Allah's name about a past event — affirming it happened or didn't — while knowing the contrary is true. "Ghamūs" means "immersing/submerging" — it plunges the swearer into sin and Hell-fire. Distinct from future oaths (which can be expiated).

Modern examples of yamīn al-ghamūs:

  • Swearing "I didn't say it" when you did.
  • Swearing "I wasn't there" when you were.
  • Swearing "the goods are new" when they're used.
  • Swearing "I paid him" when you didn't.

⚠️ Be careful with oaths

The casual habit of swearing "By Allah!" in conversation is dangerous precisely because of this ḥadīth. Use Allah's name only when you must — and only for the truth, exactly as you know it. Better: don't swear at all unless required.

"Allah ke naam ki qasm casual mein khaana — yeh khatra hai. Bina zaroorat qasm na khao. Aur agar khao to sirf such — bilkul wahi jo tum jaante ho. Yamīn al-ghamūs sabse khoufnaak qasm hai."

Ḥadīth 29 · Righteousness is good character

الْبِرُّ حُسْنُ الْخُلُقِ، وَالإِثْمُ مَا حَاكَ فِي صَدْرِكَ وَكَرِهْتَ أَنْ يَطَّلِعَ عَلَيْهِ النَّاسُ

al-birru ḥusnu al-khuluq · wa-l-ithmu mā ḥāka fī ṣadrika wa-karihta an yaṭṭaliʿa ʿalayhi al-nās

"Righteousness is good character; and sin is what causes unease in your chest and what you would dislike others to become aware of."

"Naik amal acha akhlaq hai; aur gunaah woh hai jo tumhare seene mein chubhe aur jis ka log jaan jaayein to tumhe bura lage."

Muslim, Tirmidhī

Two redefinitions packed into one ḥadīth:

(1) al-birr = ḥusn al-khuluq. Not "good character is part of birr". "Birr IS good character" — the strongest equation possible in Arabic. The whole edifice of birr (all righteous deeds) is funnelled into one thing: character.

This is a wake-up call. A person can fast, pray, give zakāh, perform ḥajj — every ritual — and still be cruel, rude, gossipy, harsh. In another ḥadīth (Bukhārī), the Prophet ﷺ said of such a woman that she would be in the Fire — because she harmed her neighbours with her tongue. Years of ʿibādah that don't soften the character haven't done their job.

(2) al-ithm has two internal markers when fiqh doesn't give you a clear ruling:

  • Ḥāka fī ṣadrika — the action snags in your chest, doesn't pass smoothly, keeps tugging at you. The conscience-flutter.
  • Karihta an yaṭṭaliʿa ʿalayhi al-nās — you would be embarrassed if others knew. The privacy test.

⚠️ Important caveat

This internal diagnostic only works for a heart still on fiṭra. If years of sin have made the conscience numb, the instrument is broken — fix the instrument first (through repentance, clean living, the company of the righteous) before relying on it.

Also: this is for grey areas only — where fiqh hasn't ruled or the ruling isn't known to you. If fiqh has clearly ruled something forbidden, you don't need to wait for your chest to sting. The ruling settles it.

"Yeh diagnostic sirf saaf zameer wale pe chalta hai. Aur sirf un cheezon ke liye jin ki ruling saaf nahin. Agar fiqh ne mana kiya hai — ruling kafi hai, gut feeling ka intazaar nahin."

Ḥadīth 30 · All creation is Allah's family

الْخَلْقُ عِيَالُ اللهِ فَأَحَبُّ الْخَلْقِ إِلَى اللهِ مَنْ أَحْسَنَ إِلَى عِيَالِهِ

al-khalqu ʿiyālu Allāh · fa-aḥabbu al-khalqi ilā Allāhi man aḥsana ilā ʿiyālihi

"All created beings are Allah's dependants (ʿiyāl), and the most beloved of creation to Allah is the one who is good to His dependants."

"Saari makhlooq Allah ka kunba hai, aur Allah ko sab se zyada mahboob woh hai jo uss kunbe ke saath bhalai (ihsaan) kare."

Bayhaqī · Shuʿab al-Īmān

The ʿiyāl metaphor. In Arabic, ʿiyāl are the family members you carry — wife, children, sometimes parents. Not abstract "people in your charge" — those whose welfare you hold. The Prophet ﷺ uses this word for all of creation in relation to Allah. Every human, every animal, every tree — ʿiyāl Allāh.

This is one of the softest images of Allah's relationship to creation in the entire ḥadīth corpus. Not Allah as distant Sovereign — Allah as the One who has gathered all of creation under His care as a vast family.

The radical claim about Allah's love. The Prophet ﷺ doesn't say Allah's most beloved is "the most knowledgeable" or "the most worshipping" or "the most ascetic". He says: "the one who is good (does iḥsān) to His creation". Service to Allah's ʿiyāl is the highest station of Allah's love.

The book also relates a variant of this ḥadīth: "The best of you is the one most beneficial (anfaʿ) to people." Same rule, less metaphor.

💡 What "being useful" actually looks like

Don't reduce this to formal charity work. Being useful includes:

  • Helping your mother in the kitchen.
  • Listening — really listening — to a stressed friend.
  • Checking on an elderly neighbour.
  • Reassuring a child.
  • A kind word to someone who looks lost.
  • A glass of water for a thirsty cat.

Continuous small kindnesses, when no one is watching — this is iḥsān ilā ʿiyālihi (goodness to His dependants). Not occasional grand gestures. Daily habit.

"Logon ke kaam aana sirf bara charity nahin. Maa ka haath bantana, pareshan saheli ko sun-na, hamsaaye ko poochna, kisi pyaase billi ko paani dena — yeh sab is hadith mein hai. Continuous, choti, chup-chup ki gayi madad — yahi sabse mahboob banati hai."
