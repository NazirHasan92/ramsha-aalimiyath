★ Master Overview — نَقْشَةُ الْبَابِ

Teaching arc: the chapter corrects the believer's three faculties in order. First the mind — know your dīn (#41), receive truth humbly (#42), pass on the Qurʾān (#43). Then the heart — act steadily (#44), guard what you love (#46), uproot the love of the world that is the source of every sin (#49). Finally the hands — pay debts (#45), be content with the sufficient (#47), earn lawfully (#48), and give the world away to the hungry (#50). A corrected mind shapes a corrected heart; a corrected heart governs corrected hands.

Chapter ka safar: zehn (jaano) → dil (pabandi, mohabbat, dunya se bachao) → haath (qarz, qanāʿat, halāl, sadaqah). In sab ke neeche ek muqabla: ḥubb al-dunyā banaam ḥubb al-Ākhirah.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Recite each of the 10 aḥādīth in Arabic, with correct meaning in English + Roman Urdu.
  2. Name the source of each ḥadīth (Ibn Mājah, Tirmidhī, Bukhārī, Muslim, Abū Dāwūd, Aḥmad, Ibn Ḥibbān, Bayhaqī).
  3. Distinguish farḍ ʿayn (knowledge every Muslim must have to do his own dīn) from farḍ kifāyah (scholarship the community must have) — and apply it to #41.
  4. State the narrow claim of #42 (truthful dreams at the pre-dawn hour) without turning it into dream-following or dream-interpretation.
  5. Explain why "the best" in #43 requires both learning and teaching the Qurʾān — and see their own student-teacher role inside it.
  6. Explain that the deeds Allah loves most are the most constant (#44), and why consistency beats intensity.
  7. Articulate the heart's law in #46 — love blinds and deafens — and connect it as the mechanism behind #49.
  8. State that #49 condemns love of the world (as goal), not the world itself or its lawful use.
  9. Apply #45's principle (maṭl al-ghaniyy ẓulm) only to the able debtor who stalls — not the genuinely broke.
  10. Explain #47 as contentment (sufficiency over distracting excess), not praise of poverty.
  11. State that lawful earning (#48) is an obligation on those responsible for support, and that feeding the hungry (#50) is the most virtuous charity — for Muslim, non-Muslim, or animal.

Ḥadīth 41 · طَلَبُ الْعِلْمِ فَرِيْضَةٌ عَلَى كُلِّ مُسْلِمٍ · Seeking knowledge is an obligation on every Muslim

Theme 1 — The mind. The gate to everything that follows.

طَلَبُ الْعِلْمِ فَرِيْضَةٌ عَلَى كُلِّ مُسْلِمٍ

ṭalabu l-ʿilmi farīḍatun ʿalā kulli muslim

"Seeking knowledge is an obligation upon every Muslim."

"ʿIlm haasil karna har musalman par farz hai."

Ibn Mājah
Where this fits: Theme 1, node 1 — opens the chapter. You cannot correct what you do not understand, so the chapter begins with the obligation to know.

فَرِيْضَة · farīḍah — "an obligation, a binding duty"

Meaning
Same root as farḍ. The knowledge made obligatory here is farḍ ʿayn — the knowledge every single Muslim needs to make his own worship valid: who Allah is, His oneness, the prophethood, and the rules of what he actually does (ṣalāh, ṣawm, etc.).
Roman Urdu
"Farīḍah" — yaani laazmi farz. Yahaan jo ʿilm farz hai woh farḍ ʿayn hai — jitna har musalman ko apni ʿibādat sahi karne ke liye chahiye: Allah ki maʿrifat, tawhīd, nubuwwat, aur jo amal woh karta hai uske ahkaam.
The other tier
Higher knowledge — issuing fatāwā, deep tafsīr, the ḥadīth sciences — is farḍ kifāyah: a communal obligation. It is enough that the community has scholars who carry it. It is not required of each Muslim individually.
Example
A man who has prayed for years but never learned the rules of wuḍūʾ and ṣalāh is neglecting a farḍ — not just missing an optional virtue. Learning those rules is the obligation #41 names.

⚠️ Landmine 1 — "every Muslim must become a scholar"

Classmates feel crushed ("I can never be a ʿālim") or over-reach. Pre-empt: "Yeh hadith har ek ko ʿālim/muftī/ḥāfiẓ banne ko nahin keh rahi. Yeh keh rahi hai: itna ʿilm seekho jitne se apni dīn sahi kar sako — Allah ko pehchaano, tawhīd jaano, namaz-roza ke masaail jaano. Yeh farḍ ʿayn hai. Gehra ʿilm farḍ kifāyah hai — kuch logon ka community mein hona kaafi. Dabaav mat lo."

📿 Connect to Qurʾān · 58:11

يَرْفَعِ اللَّهُ الَّذِيْنَ آمَنُوْا مِنْكُمْ وَالَّذِيْنَ أُوْتُوا الْعِلْمَ دَرَجَاتٍ

"Allah will raise those who believe among you, and those who were given knowledge, by degrees."

Why this fits: The Qurʾān ties rank with Allah to knowledge. Ḥadīth 41 makes the corresponding duty explicit — seeking that knowledge is obligatory.

Urdu mein: Quran ʿilm ko darjaat (buland maqaam) se jorta hai; hadith us ʿilm ko haasil karne ko farz qaraar deti hai.

Ḥadīth 42 · أَصْدَقُ الرُّؤْيَا بِالْأَسْحَارِ · The most truthful dreams are those before dawn

Theme 1 — The mind. A small, narrow claim — teach it small.

أَصْدَقُ الرُّؤْيَا بِالْأَسْحَارِ

aṣdaqu l-ruʾyā bi-l-asḥār

"The most truthful dreams are those [seen] at the times just before dawn."

"Sab se sachay khwaab woh hote hain jo seher ke waqt (fajr se thori pehle) aate hain."

Tirmidhī
Where this fits: Theme 1, node 2 — a faint, non-binding form of perception, set beside the binding knowledge of #41. Keep the two sharply distinct.

الْأَسْحَار · al-asḥār — "the pre-dawn times"

Meaning
The last portion of the night, just before dawn — the time of suḥūr and tahajjud. The ḥadīth observes that of all dreams, the truthful ones (ruʾyā ṣādiqah) most often come in this window.
Roman Urdu
Raat ka aakhri hissa, fajr se thori der pehle — suḥūr aur tahajjud ka waqt. Hadith batati hai ke sachay khwaab aksar isi waqt aate hain.
Two reasons (the book)
(1) After a night of rest the mind is calm — a clearer receiver. (2) This is the hour of the angels' descent who come to witness the morning prayer — a blessed window.
The boundary
A true dream is, the scholars say, a faint part of prophethood — a form of perception, not a binding source. ʿIlm (#41) is obligatory and binding; a dream is neither. Do not blur them.

⚠️ Landmine 2 — "so I should act on / interpret dreams"

Classmates love to inflate this. Shut it down gently: "Yeh hadith bohot choti baat keh rahi hai aur bas wahi. Yeh keh rahi hai ke sachay khwaab seher ke waqt aate hain — kyunki dimaagh pursukoon hota hai aur farishton ke nuzool ka waqt hai. Yeh nahin keh rahi ke khwaab pe amal karo, ya khwaab se masaail nikaalo, ya har khwaab ki taʿbīr dhoondo. ʿIlm laazim — khwaab nahin."

Ḥadīth 43 · خَيْرُكُمْ مَنْ تَعَلَّمَ الْقُرْآنَ وَعَلَّمَهُ · The best of you is the one who learns the Qurʾān and teaches it

Theme 1 — The mind. The charter of the student-teacher — Ramsha's own role.

خَيْرُكُمْ مَنْ تَعَلَّمَ الْقُرْآنَ وَعَلَّمَهُ

khayrukum man taʿallama l-qurʾāna wa ʿallamah

"The best among you is the one who learns the Qurʾān and teaches it."

"Tum mein behtareen woh hai jo Qurʾān seekhe aur (doosron ko) sikhaaye."

Bukhārī · ʿUthmān ibn ʿAffān (raḍiyallāhu ʿanhu)
Where this fits: Theme 1, node 3 — knowledge completed by transmission. Knowing is not enough; the "best" passes it on.

The two-part qualification — learned AND taught

Meaning
The "best" is not merely the one who knows the Qurʾān, nor merely the one who teaches — it is the one who does both: learns it, then passes it on. The two verbs are joined deliberately.
Roman Urdu
"Behtareen" woh nahin jo sirf jaanta hai, na woh jo sirf sikhaata hai — balke woh jo dono karta hai: seekha, phir sikhaaya. Dono lafz jaan-bujh kar saath hain.
Ramsha's role
This is exactly the student-teacher cycle: learn the material, then teach the cohort. The Qurʾān has travelled intact for 14 centuries through this exact loop: learn → teach → they learn → they teach.
Within reach of all
You need not be a great scholar. Teaching a child Sūrat al-Fātiḥah already puts you inside this ḥadīth. Learn something of the Qurʾān, and pass it on.

💡 Teaching tip — name what they are doing

Tell the cohort plainly: "Yeh hadith hamare apne kaam ki charter hai. Main seekhti hoon, phir aapko sikhaati hoon; aap seekhte ho, phir aagay sikhaate ho. Hum sab is hadith ke andar hain." It dignifies the whole class and motivates them to pass on what they learn.

Ḥadīth 44 · أَحَبُّ الْأَعْمَالِ إِلَى اللهِ أَدْوَمُهَا وَإِنْ قَلَّ · The most beloved deeds to Allah are the most constant, even if few

Theme 2 — The heart. Consistency beats intensity.

أَحَبُّ الْأَعْمَالِ إِلَى اللهِ أَدْوَمُهَا وَإِنْ قَلَّ

aḥabbu l-aʿmāli ilā Llāhi adwamuhā wa in qall

"The most beloved of deeds to Allah are the most constant of them, even if they are few."

"Allah ko sab se pyaare aʿmāl woh hain jo hamesha (pabandi se) kiye jaayein, chahe thoray hi hon."

Bukhārī, Muslim · ʿĀʾishah (raḍiyallāhu ʿanhā)
Where this fits: Theme 2 opens with how the heart should act — steadily. The "deeds" are the voluntary acts a person takes on regularly, not the obligatory five prayers.

أَدْوَم · adwam — "most continuous / most constant"

Meaning
From dawām (continuity). The most beloved deeds are not the biggest or most dramatic — they are the most regular, even if small (wa in qalla). A small wird done every day beats a huge burst that burns out in a week.
Roman Urdu
"Adwam" yaani sab se zyada dawāmi. Allah ko bara ya dramatic amal nahin — balke regular amal pyaara hai, chahe chhota ho. Roz ka chhota wird us bare josh se behtar jo ek hafte mein khatam.
What "deeds" means
The book clarifies: not the obligatory prayers (required regardless), but the voluntary acts — nafl prayer, daily recitation, dhikr awrād — that a person takes upon himself to do regularly.
The liberation
For the overwhelmed beginner this is freeing: don't aim for everything — pick one small thing and never drop it.

⚠️ Landmine 3 — "so a tiny bit is enough" (laziness license)

Don't let "even if few" become an excuse for minimum effort. "Yeh hadith thore ki taʿrīf nahin kar rahi — dawaam ki. Yeh nahin keh rahi 'thora karo aur khush raho' — keh rahi hai 'jo bhi karo, roz karo, chhodo mat.' Yeh sustī ki ijaazat nahin — istiqāmat ka sabaq hai."

📿 Connect to Qurʾān · 11:112

فَاسْتَقِمْ كَمَا أُمِرْتَ

"So remain steadfast [on the straight path], as you have been commanded."

Why this fits: The command of istiqāmah — steadfast constancy — is the Qurʾānic backbone of #44. Allah commands not a burst of zeal but a steady, ongoing uprightness.

Urdu mein: Istiqāmat ka hukm — yahi #44 ki bunyaad hai. Allah josh ka nahin, dawaam ka hukm deta hai.

Ḥadīth 46 · حُبُّكَ الشَّيْءَ يُعْمِيْ وَيُصِمُّ · Your love for a thing blinds you and deafens you

Theme 2 — The heart. A law of the heart — and the mechanism behind #49.

حُبُّكَ الشَّيْءَ يُعْمِيْ وَيُصِمُّ

ḥubbuka l-shayʾa yuʿmī wa yuṣimm

"Your love for a thing blinds you and deafens you."

"Kisi cheez se teri mohabbat tujhe andha aur behra kar deti hai."

Abū Dāwūd, Aḥmad · Abū al-Dardāʾ (raḍiyallāhu ʿanhu)
Where this fits: Theme 2, the heart's law. Hold this — it is the machinery of #49 (love of the world).

Love as a distorting lens — yuʿmī wa yuṣimm

Meaning
A law of the heart: when you love something, that love blinds you to its faults and deafens you to criticism of it. The love itself becomes a filter on reality.
Roman Urdu
Dil ka qanūn: jab tum kisi cheez se mohabbat karte ho, woh mohabbat us ke ʿaib dekhne se andha aur uski tanqeed sunne se behra kar deti hai. Mohabbat khud haqeeqat par parda ban jaati hai.
The hinge to #49
If love blinds and deafens, ask: what is the most dangerous thing to love? The chapter answers in #49 — the dunyā. #46 is the general law; #49 is its catastrophic application.
The positive inversion
Love is not the enemy — misplaced love is. This is why love of Allah and His Messenger ﷺ is so transformative: it re-tunes what you see and hear toward the truth. Aim the love right and the same power becomes a guide.

💡 Teaching tip — set up #49 here

Plant the seed now: "Yeh baat thaame rakho — agle hadith (#49) mein yeh kaam aayegi. Agar mohabbat andha aur behra kar deti hai, to sab se khatarnaak cheez kisse mohabbat hai? Dekhte hain." Teaching #46 immediately before #49 makes #49 land with full force.

Ḥadīth 49 · حُبُّ الدُّنْيَا رَأْسُ كُلِّ خَطِيْئَةٍ · Love of this world is the origin of every sin

Theme 2 — The heart. The diagnostic centre of the whole chapter.

حُبُّ الدُّنْيَا رَأْسُ كُلِّ خَطِيْئَةٍ

ḥubbu l-dunyā raʾsu kulli khaṭīʾah

"Love of this world is the head [origin] of every sin."

"Dunya ki mohabbat har gunaah ki jaḍ (sarchashma) hai."

Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān)
Where this fits: Theme 2, the root diagnosis. Raʾs = head / origin / fountainhead. Trace any sin to the bottom and you find love of the dunyā.

رَأْس · raʾs — "head, origin, fountainhead"

Meaning
The Prophet ﷺ traces every sin to a single root. Strip any sin down — lying, theft, backbiting, abandoning prayer, oppression — and at the bottom sits an excessive attachment to this world: wanting it, fearing to lose it, preferring it over the Ākhirah.
Roman Urdu
Rasool ﷺ ne har gunaah ki jaḍ ek cheez mein dikhaayi: dunya ki mohabbat. Koi bhi gunaah khol ke dekho — neeche dunya se had se zyada lagaav milega.
⚠️ The crucial qualification
The ḥadīth condemns ḥubb — the love of the dunyā — not the dunyā itself, and not using it. The world is the believer's field for the Ākhirah; wealth, family, food are gifts and tools. The Prophet ﷺ himself married, traded, ate, lived in the world.
The formula
Aasaan formula: dunya tumhare haath mein ho to theek hai; dunya tumhare dil mein baith jaaye to har gunaah ki jaḍ. Maal kashti mein paani jaisa — neeche theek, andar aa jaaye to doobne ka sabab.

The cure — woven across the chapter

If #49 is the disease
…the other aḥādīth prescribe the cure: knowledge (#41) re-orders priorities; consistent worship (#44) keeps the heart turned to Allah; contentment with little (#47); lawful earning without greed (#48); and giving the world away in charity (#50).
Roman Urdu
Agar dunya ki mohabbat bīmārī hai, to baaqi hadithein ilaaj batati hain: ʿilm, pabandi se ibādat, qanāʿat, halāl kamaai, aur sadaqah. Chapter sirf bīmārī nahin — ilaaj bhi deta hai.

⚠️ Landmine 4 — "so the world / wealth is evil" (world-hatred)

The chapter's most important landmine. Pious-minded classmates slide into world-hatred. Disarm firmly: "Hadith keh rahi hai dunya ki mohabbat har gunaah ki jaḍ — dunya khud nahin, na uska istemaal. Dunya momin ki kheti hai Ākhirah ke liye. Rasool ﷺ ne khud nikah kiya, tijaarat ki, achha khaaya. Mazammat mohabbat ki hai — dunya ko maqsad bana lena. Dunya haath mein theek; dunya dil mein har gunaah ki jaḍ."

📿 Connect to Qurʾān · 3:14

زُيِّنَ لِلنَّاسِ حُبُّ الشَّهَوَاتِ مِنَ النِّسَاءِ وَالْبَنِيْنَ وَالْقَنَاطِيْرِ الْمُقَنْطَرَةِ مِنَ الذَّهَبِ وَالْفِضَّةِ

"Beautified for people is the love of desires — women and sons, heaped-up sums of gold and silver…" (continues: …but with Allah is the best return.)

Why this fits: The verse names the love (ḥubb) of worldly desires as something made attractive to people — then redirects the heart to Allah as the better goal. Exactly #49's diagnosis and its cure.

Urdu mein: Ayat dunyawi khwaahishaat ki mohabbat ko "ziynat" (kashish) kehti hai, phir dil ko Allah ki taraf morti hai — yahi #49 ki tashkhīs aur ilaaj.

Ḥadīth 45 · مَطْلُ الْغَنِيِّ ظُلْمٌ · Stalling a debt by one who can pay is oppression

Theme 3 — The hands. A precise, weighty word: ẓulm.

مَطْلُ الْغَنِيِّ ظُلْمٌ

maṭlu l-ghaniyyi ẓulm

"The [deliberate] delay [in repayment] by a wealthy [debtor] is oppression."

"Maaldaar (jo de sakta hai) ka qarz taalna zulm hai."

Bukhārī, Muslim · Abū Hurayrah (raḍiyallāhu ʿanhu)
Where this fits: Theme 3 opens. Not a discourtesy — ẓulm, the word for grave injustice. The ruling targets the able debtor who stalls.

مَطْل · maṭl — "deliberate stalling of a debt"

Meaning
Putting off repayment — "I'll pay next month," "after Eid," "soon" — when you have the means to pay now. The Prophet ﷺ calls this ẓulm: the same word used for the gravest injustices.
Roman Urdu
Qarz ko jaan-bujh kar taalna — "agle maheene, Eid ke baad, jald" — jabke ada karne ki taaqat mojood hai. Rasool ﷺ ne isay zulm kaha, sirf be-adabi nahin.
The limit — al-ghaniyy
The ruling targets the able debtor. A genuinely broke person who cannot pay is not its subject — Allah commands patience with him. The ẓulm is specifically the rich man who could pay and chooses to stall.
Why so severe
The creditor parted with his own wealth to help you and trusted you. Making him beg for his own money — while you sit on it — turns his kindness into distress. The sharīʿah protects the lender. If you can pay, pay on time — it is part of your dīn, not just manners.

⚠️ Landmine 5 — applying ẓulm to the genuinely broke

Classmates feel guilt ("I owe money I can't pay — am I an oppressor?") or judge a poor debtor. "Lafz hai al-ghaniyy — woh jo ada karne ki taaqat rakhta hai magar taalta hai. Jo sach mein muflis hai, woh is hadith ka maudūʿ nahin — uske saath Allah ne sabr aur muhlat ka hukm diya. Agar tum tang-dast ho, tum zaalim nahin — magar jab taaqat aaye, foran ada karo."

Ḥadīth 47 · مَا قَلَّ وَكَفَى خَيْرٌ مِمَّا كَثُرَ وَأَلْهَى · A little that suffices is better than much that distracts

Theme 3 — The hands. Contentment (qanāʿah) — the practical antidote to #49.

مَا قَلَّ وَكَفَى خَيْرٌ مِمَّا كَثُرَ وَأَلْهَى

mā qalla wa kafā khayrun mimmā kathura wa alhā

"What is little but sufficient is better than what is abundant but distracts [from Allah]."

"Jo thora magar kaafi ho, woh us se behtar hai jo zyada magar (Allah se) ghaafil karne wala ho."

Ibn Ḥibbān
Where this fits: Theme 3 — the inner posture toward provision. The question is never the amount; it is the effect on the heart.

The hinge words — kafā (suffices) vs alhā (distracts)

Meaning
The ḥadīth weighs two provisions on one scale: little-but-sufficient vs much-but-distracting. It does not praise little as such, nor condemn much as such — it praises sufficiency and condemns distraction.
Roman Urdu
Hadith do rizq ek taraazu par taulti hai: thora-magar-kaafi banaam zyada-magar-ghaafil-karne-wala. Yeh thore ki taʿrīf nahin, na zyada ki mazammat — balke kifāyat ki taʿrīf aur ghaflat ki mazammat.
The real question
Never the amount — always the effect on your heart. A modest income that lets you worship in peace beats great wealth that swallows your time, nights, prayers, and peace.
Links to #44 & #49
This is qanāʿah. It is the antidote to #49 (love of the world), and it rhymes with #44: just as little-but-constant worship beats burst-and-burnout, little-but-sufficient provision beats much-but-heedless wealth.

⚠️ Landmine 3 (again) — "little is the goal"

Same trap as #44. "Yeh hadith 'kam kamao' nahin keh rahi — keh rahi hai 'jo kaafi ho aur Allah se ghaafil na kare, woh us zyada se behtar jo ghaafil kar de.' Yeh qanāʿat ka sabaq hai, sustī ki ijaazat nahin."

Ḥadīth 48 · طَلَبُ الْحَلَالِ فَرِيْضَةٌ بَعْدَ الْفَرِيْضَةِ · Seeking the lawful is an obligation after the [primary] obligation

Theme 3 — The hands. Earning lawfully is itself an act of dīn — note the echo of #41.

طَلَبُ الْحَلَالِ فَرِيْضَةٌ بَعْدَ الْفَرِيْضَةِ

ṭalabu l-ḥalāli farīḍatun baʿda l-farīḍah

"Seeking the lawful [earning] is an obligation after the [primary] obligation."

"Halāl (kamaai) talab karna bunyaadi farz ke baad (agla) farz hai."

Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān)
Where this fits: Theme 3 — the bookend of #41. Both ʿilm and ḥalāl rizq are called farīḍah. Lawful earning is worship, not a worldly distraction.

The echo of #41 — both are farīḍah

Meaning
The chapter bookends knowledge and wealth with the same word. Seeking knowledge (#41) is a farīḍah; seeking lawful earning (#48) is also a farīḍah — baʿda l-farīḍah, after the primary obligation.
Roman Urdu
"Farīḍah" ka jaan-bujh kar dohraav — #41 mein ʿilm farīḍah, yahaan halāl kamaai farīḍah. Bunyaadi farzon (namaz, roza) ke baad, halāl kamaana agla farz.
A real but lower tier
It is not the same rank as prayer (the book is careful — it is below the farḍ ʿayn of worship), but it is a genuine obligation, not a worldly distraction. The one who earns ḥalāl for his dependants is performing an act of dīn.
⚠️ Who is obligated
The duty falls on those who must support themselves and their dependants — not universally. One already provided for (the book's example: a woman under her father's or husband's support) is not obligated to earn. The duty attaches to responsibility, not to gender or people as such.

⚠️ Landmine 6 — "so everyone must go out and earn"

Don't over-generalise. "Halāl kamaana farz hai — magar kitaab ne saaf kiya: yeh farz us par hai jisko apna aur apne zer-e-kafālat logon ka kharch uthaana hai. Jiska kharch pehle se uth raha hai, us par farz nahin. Yeh zimmedaari ke saath juda hai, har shakhs ke saath nahin."

📿 Connect to Qurʾān · 2:168

يَا أَيُّهَا النَّاسُ كُلُوْا مِمَّا فِي الْأَرْضِ حَلَالًا طَيِّبًا

"O mankind, eat from whatever is on the earth [that is] lawful and good…"

Why this fits: The Qurʾān commands seeking and consuming the ḥalāl and ṭayyib. Ḥadīth 48 frames the pursuit of that lawful provision as an obligation in its own right.

Urdu mein: Quran halāl aur ṭayyib khaane ka hukm deta hai; hadith us halāl ko talab karne ko farz qaraar deti hai.

Ḥadīth 50 · أَفْضَلُ الصَّدَقَةِ أَنْ تُشْبِعَ كَبِدًا جَائِعَةً · The most virtuous charity is to satisfy a hungry stomach

Theme 3 — The hands. The closing note — wealth turned outward, within reach of everyone.

أَفْضَلُ الصَّدَقَةِ أَنْ تُشْبِعَ كَبِدًا جَائِعَةً

afḍalu l-ṣadaqati an tushbiʿa kabidan jāʾiʿah

"The most virtuous charity is that you satisfy a hungry stomach."

"Sab se afzal sadaqah yeh hai ke tum kisi bhooke ka pet bhar do."

Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān)
Where this fits: Theme 3, the chapter's close. The most concrete charity imaginable — filling an empty stomach — set as the afḍal (most virtuous) form.

كَبِد · kabid — "liver" (the seat of hunger)

Meaning
Literally liver — in classical Arabic idiom the organ of hunger (as we say "stomach"). To satisfy a hungry kabid is to feed someone who is actually hungry, right now. The most concrete charity there is.
Roman Urdu
Lafz kabid — lafzi maʿna "jigar", classical Arabic mein bhook ka ʿuzw (jaise hum "pet" kehte hain). Bhooke kabid ko ser karna = kisi sach-much ke bhooke ko abhi khilaana.
The book widens it
This includes feeding a hungry person — Muslim or non-Muslim — or even an animal. The merit is in relieving real hunger, wherever found. The recipient need not share your faith.
Why the perfect close
Charity is ḥubb al-dunyā (#49) reversed in action — taking what the world-lover hoards and giving it away. And the chapter chooses the humblest charity — a fed stomach — so its highest reward is within reach of even the poorest believer.

💡 Closing the chapter — land it here

End class on the architecture: "Chapter ʿilm se khula (#41), sadaqah par band hua (#50). Beech mein dil ko theek kiya. Aur dekho — sab se bara ajr sab se chhote kaam mein rakha: ek bhooke ka pet bharna. Shaayad tum kabhi masjid na banaa sako, magar kisi bhooke ko khilaana hamesha kar sakti ho. Yahi is chapter ka pyaara ikhtitām hai."

⚠️ Common student confusions — watch for these

1. "Every Muslim must become a scholar" (#41)

The obligation is farḍ ʿayn — enough knowledge to do your own dīn correctly. Scholarship is farḍ kifāyah — the community's duty, not each individual's. Relieve the pressure.

2. "Follow / interpret your dreams" (#42)

The dreams ḥadīth makes one narrow observation about the pre-dawn hour. It is not a basis for action, law, or dream-interpretation. Keep it small.

3. "A tiny bit is enough" — laziness license (#44, #47)

The praise is for constancy (#44) and sufficiency (#47), not for smallness. The lesson is steadiness and contentment — not minimum effort.

4. "The world / wealth is evil" (#49)

The chapter's biggest landmine. #49 condemns love of the world (as goal), not the world itself or its lawful use. Dunyā in the hand is fine; dunyā in the heart is the root of sin.

5. "Anyone in debt is an oppressor" (#45)

Maṭl al-ghaniyy ẓulm targets the one who can pay and stalls. The genuinely broke debtor is owed patience, not condemnation.

6. "Everyone must go out and earn" (#48)

Lawful earning is obligatory on those responsible for their own and dependants' support — not universally. The duty attaches to responsibility, not to gender or people as such.

7. Blurring ʿilm (#41) with a dream (#42)

Knowledge is obligatory and binding; a true dream is a faint, non-binding perception. They sit in the same theme but are not the same kind of thing.

8. Missing the #46 → #49 link

#46 (love blinds and deafens) is the general law; #49 (love of the world = root of sin) is its catastrophic application. Teach them together so #49 lands.
