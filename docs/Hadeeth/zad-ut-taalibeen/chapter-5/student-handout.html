Chapter 5 — Student Handout

Aḥādīth 41–50 from Part 1 of Zad ut-Taalibeen · the believer's mind, heart, and hands

👋 Welcome — what we'll learn today

Today we cover ten short aḥādīth of the Prophet ﷺ that, read together, form a single training of the self. They move through three faculties, in order: the mind (what you must know), the heart (what you love and how steadily you act), and the hands (how you earn, owe, and give).

Aaj ka sabaq — das choti hadithein jo milkar ek tarbiyat banti hain. Teen quwwatein, sahi tartīb mein: zehn (kya jaanna hai), dil (kya pasand karte ho, kitni pabandi se amal), aur haath (kaise kamao, qarz, sadaqah).

The 10 fall into three themes:

  1. The mind — sacred knowledge (#41, #42, #43) — seeking knowledge is obligatory, the truest dreams come before dawn, and the best person learns the Qurʾān and teaches it.
  2. The heart — steady deeds & where love leads (#44, #46, #49) — the most beloved deeds are the regular ones, love blinds and deafens, and love of the world is the root of every sin.
  3. The hands — wealth, debt & provision (#45, #47, #48, #50) — pay your debts, be content with the sufficient, earn lawfully, and feed the hungry.

The deep current beneath all ten: the contest between love of this world and love of the next.

Ḥadīth 41 · Seeking knowledge is an obligation on every Muslim

طَلَبُ الْعِلْمِ فَرِيْضَةٌ عَلَى كُلِّ مُسْلِمٍ

ṭalabu l-ʿilmi farīḍatun ʿalā kulli muslim

"Seeking knowledge is an obligation upon every Muslim."

"ʿIlm haasil karna har musalman par farz hai."

Ibn Mājah

What knowledge? This is one of the most famous aḥādīth — and the most misunderstood. It does not mean everyone must become a scholar. The knowledge made obligatory on every Muslim is the farḍ ʿayn knowledge: enough to make your own worship valid — who Allah is, His oneness, the prophethood, and the rules of what you actually do (how to pray, how to fast).

The other tier: Higher knowledge — issuing legal verdicts (fatāwā), deep tafsīr — is farḍ kifāyah: a communal duty. It is enough that the community has scholars. It is not required of each Muslim individually.

💡 The point

Learn enough of your dīn to do your dīn correctly. A person who has prayed for years but never learned the rules of wuḍūʾ and ṣalāh is neglecting a farḍ.

Itna ʿilm seekho jitne se apni dīn sahi kar sako. Dabaav mat lo — ʿālim banna farḍ kifāyah hai, har ek par nahin.

Ḥadīth 42 · The most truthful dreams are those before dawn

أَصْدَقُ الرُّؤْيَا بِالْأَسْحَارِ

aṣdaqu l-ruʾyā bi-l-asḥār

"The most truthful dreams are those [seen] at the times just before dawn."

"Sab se sachay khwaab woh hote hain jo seher ke waqt (fajr se thori pehle) aate hain."

Tirmidhī

A small, careful claim. Of all the dreams a person sees, the ones most likely to be truthful come in the asḥār — the last part of the night, just before dawn (the time of suḥūr and tahajjud). The book gives two reasons:

  • After a night of rest, the mind is calm — a clearer receiver.
  • It is the hour of the angels' descent, who come to witness the morning prayer — a blessed window.

⚠️ Keep it small

This ḥadīth is not a licence to act on dreams, draw rulings from them, or interpret every dream. It makes one narrow observation about a blessed hour — nothing more. ʿIlm (Ḥadīth 41) is binding; a dream is not.

Yeh hadith khwaab pe amal, masaail, ya har khwaab ki taʿbīr ki baat nahin kar rahi. Sirf ek choti baat — aur bas. Khwaab ko choti rakho.

Ḥadīth 43 · The best of you learns the Qurʾān and teaches it

خَيْرُكُمْ مَنْ تَعَلَّمَ الْقُرْآنَ وَعَلَّمَهُ

khayrukum man taʿallama l-qurʾāna wa ʿallamah

"The best among you is the one who learns the Qurʾān and teaches it."

"Tum mein behtareen woh hai jo Qurʾān seekhe aur (doosron ko) sikhaaye."

Bukhārī · ʿUthmān ibn ʿAffān (raḍiyallāhu ʿanhu)

Two parts, joined. The "best" is not merely the one who knows the Qurʾān, nor merely the one who teaches — it is the one who does both: learns it, then passes it on. This is exactly the cycle that has carried the Qurʾān intact for fourteen centuries: learn → teach → they learn → they teach.

Within reach of all. You need not be a great scholar. Teaching a child Sūrat al-Fātiḥah already puts you inside this ḥadīth. Learn something of the Qurʾān, and pass it on.

💡 This is what we are doing

Every one of us is inside this ḥadīth right now: we learn the material, then teach it to someone else. Pass on what you learn this week — even one ḥadīth, even one verse.

Hum sab abhi is hadith ke andar hain — seekhte hain, phir aagay sikhaate hain. Is hafte jo seekho, kisi ko sikhao.

Ḥadīth 44 · The most beloved deeds are the most constant

أَحَبُّ الْأَعْمَالِ إِلَى اللهِ أَدْوَمُهَا وَإِنْ قَلَّ

aḥabbu l-aʿmāli ilā Llāhi adwamuhā wa in qall

"The most beloved of deeds to Allah are the most constant of them, even if they are few."

"Allah ko sab se pyaare aʿmāl woh hain jo hamesha (pabandi se) kiye jaayein, chahe thoray hi hon."

Bukhārī, Muslim · ʿĀʾishah (raḍiyallāhu ʿanhā)

Consistency beats intensity. The deeds Allah loves most are not the biggest or most dramatic — they are the most regular, even if small. The "deeds" here are the voluntary ones (the obligatory prayers are required regardless): a daily wird of nafl prayer, Qurʾān recitation, or dhikr that you take upon yourself to keep up.

Why? A small deed done every day — including the days you don't feel like it — proves a settled relationship with Allah, not a passing mood. And it is sustainable: the burst-and-burnout pattern (an intense Ramadan, then eleven empty months) is fragile.

⚠️ Not a licence for laziness

"Even if few" praises constancy, not smallness. The lesson is steadiness, not minimum effort. Pick one small thing and never drop it.

"Chahe thoray hon" — yeh dawaam ki taʿrīf hai, thore ki nahin. Ek chhoti cheez pakdo aur kabhi mat chhodo.

Ḥadīth 46 · Your love for a thing blinds and deafens you

حُبُّكَ الشَّيْءَ يُعْمِيْ وَيُصِمُّ

ḥubbuka l-shayʾa yuʿmī wa yuṣimm

"Your love for a thing blinds you and deafens you."

"Kisi cheez se teri mohabbat tujhe andha aur behra kar deti hai."

Abū Dāwūd, Aḥmad · Abū al-Dardāʾ (raḍiyallāhu ʿanhu)

A law of the heart. When you love something, that love blinds you to its faults and deafens you to criticism of it. A parent who cannot see a flaw in their child; a person infatuated who cannot hear a single warning; someone attached to a leader, a group, a possession — who goes deaf the moment it is criticised. The love itself becomes a filter on reality.

The flip side: Love is not the enemy — misplaced love is. This is why love of Allah and His Messenger ﷺ is so transformative: it re-tunes what you see and hear toward the truth. Aim the love correctly, and the same power becomes a guide.

💡 Hold this thought

If love blinds and deafens — what is the most dangerous thing to love? The next ḥadīth answers: the dunyā. Ḥadīth 46 is the general law; Ḥadīth 49 is its worst case.

Agar mohabbat andha-behra kar deti hai, to sab se khatarnaak cheez kisse mohabbat hai? Agli hadith jawaab deti hai: dunya.

Ḥadīth 49 · Love of this world is the origin of every sin

حُبُّ الدُّنْيَا رَأْسُ كُلِّ خَطِيْئَةٍ

ḥubbu l-dunyā raʾsu kulli khaṭīʾah

"Love of this world is the head [origin] of every sin."

"Dunya ki mohabbat har gunaah ki jaḍ (sarchashma) hai."

Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān)

The root diagnosis. Raʾs means "head" — the source. The Prophet ﷺ traces every sin to one root: love of the dunyā. Trace any sin down and you find it: lying (to protect worldly standing), stealing (love of wealth), backbiting (love of status), abandoning prayer (love of comfort), oppression (love of power). At the bottom of each sits an excessive attachment to this world.

The crucial point — read carefully. The ḥadīth condemns the love of the world — not the world itself, and not using it. The world is the believer's field for the Ākhirah; wealth, family, food, work are gifts and tools. The Prophet ﷺ himself married, traded, ate, and lived in the world. What is condemned is making the dunyā the goal — the thing you would disobey Allah to keep.

⚠️ "The world is evil"? No.

This is the chapter's biggest trap. The condemnation is of love-as-goal, not of wealth, family, or enjoyment. Dunyā in your hand is fine; dunyā in your heart is the root of every sin. Like water: under the boat it carries you; inside the boat it sinks you.

Dunya ki mohabbat jaḍ hai — dunya khud nahin. Maal haath mein theek; dil mein baith jaaye to har gunaah ki jaḍ.

💡 The chapter is also the cure

If love of the world is the disease, the other aḥādīth are the medicine: knowledge (#41), steady worship (#44), contentment (#47), lawful earning (#48), and giving it away (#50). The whole chapter treats this one root.

Ḥadīth 45 · Stalling a debt by one who can pay is oppression

مَطْلُ الْغَنِيِّ ظُلْمٌ

maṭlu l-ghaniyyi ẓulm

"The [deliberate] delay [in repayment] by a wealthy [debtor] is oppression."

"Maaldaar (jo de sakta hai) ka qarz taalna zulm hai."

Bukhārī, Muslim · Abū Hurayrah (raḍiyallāhu ʿanhu)

A weighty word: ẓulm. The Prophet ﷺ does not call delayed repayment "rude" or "inconvenient" — he calls it oppression, the same word used for grave injustice. Maṭl is deliberately stalling — "I'll pay next month," "after Eid," "soon" — when you have the means to pay now.

The limit. The ruling targets al-ghaniyy — the one who can pay. A genuinely broke person who cannot pay is not its subject; he is owed patience. The ẓulm is specifically the able debtor who chooses to stall — making his creditor, who helped him with his own wealth, beg for it back.

⚠️ The genuinely broke are not the target

If you truly cannot pay, you are not an oppressor — but the moment you can, pay at once. And if you can pay and are stalling, know that this is not just bad manners — it is a sin.

Sach mein muflis ho to zaalim nahin — magar taaqat aate hi foran ada karo. De sakte ho aur taal rahe ho? Yeh sirf bad-akhlaqi nahin, zulm hai.

Ḥadīth 47 · A little that suffices beats much that distracts

مَا قَلَّ وَكَفَى خَيْرٌ مِمَّا كَثُرَ وَأَلْهَى

mā qalla wa kafā khayrun mimmā kathura wa alhā

"What is little but sufficient is better than what is abundant but distracts [from Allah]."

"Jo thora magar kaafi ho, woh us se behtar hai jo zyada magar (Allah se) ghaafil karne wala ho."

Ibn Ḥibbān

Contentment (qanāʿah). Notice the hinge words: kafā (it suffices) versus alhā (it distracts). The ḥadīth does not praise little as such, nor condemn much as such — it praises sufficiency and condemns distraction. The question is never the amount; it is the effect on your heart.

A modest income that lets you worship in peace is better than great wealth that swallows your time, your nights, your prayers, and your peace. This is the practical antidote to Ḥadīth 49: if you truly believe enough is better than distracting excess, the grip of love-of-the-world loosens.

💡 The same principle as #44

Just as little-but-constant worship (#44) beats burst-and-burnout, little-but-sufficient provision (#47) beats much-but-heedless wealth. Sustainable sufficiency over distracting excess — in worship and in provision.

Jaise thori-magar-musalsal ibadat behtar, waise thora-magar-kaafi rizq behtar. Ek hi usool — dono mein.

Ḥadīth 48 · Seeking the lawful is an obligation after the obligation

طَلَبُ الْحَلَالِ فَرِيْضَةٌ بَعْدَ الْفَرِيْضَةِ

ṭalabu l-ḥalāli farīḍatun baʿda l-farīḍah

"Seeking the lawful [earning] is an obligation after the [primary] obligation."

"Halāl (kamaai) talab karna bunyaadi farz ke baad (agla) farz hai."

Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān)

Earning lawfully is worship. Notice the echo of Ḥadīth 41: there, seeking knowledge was a farīḍah; here, seeking lawful earning is also a farīḍah. After you fulfil the primary obligations (ṣalāh, ṣawm), earning a lawful living becomes the next obligation. The Muslim who earns ḥalāl to support himself and his dependants is performing an act of dīn — not merely "working".

The limit. This obligation falls on those who must support themselves and their dependants — not on everyone. A person already provided for (the book's example: a woman under her father's or husband's support) is not obligated to earn. The duty attaches to responsibility, not to gender or to people as such.

⚠️ Not "everyone must earn"

The obligation tracks responsibility for support. Where there is no such responsibility, there is no obligation to earn. Don't over-generalise it.

Yeh farz zimmedaari ke saath juda hai. Jis par kharch ki zimmedaari nahin, us par kamaana farz nahin.

Ḥadīth 50 · The most virtuous charity is to feed the hungry

أَفْضَلُ الصَّدَقَةِ أَنْ تُشْبِعَ كَبِدًا جَائِعَةً

afḍalu l-ṣadaqati an tushbiʿa kabidan jāʾiʿah

"The most virtuous charity is that you satisfy a hungry stomach."

"Sab se afzal sadaqah yeh hai ke tum kisi bhooke ka pet bhar do."

Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān)

Wealth turned outward. The chapter that opened with knowledge closes with the most concrete charity imaginable: filling an empty stomach. The word is kabid — literally "liver", the organ of hunger in classical Arabic idiom (as we say "stomach"). The book widens it generously: this includes feeding a hungry person — Muslim or non-Muslim — or even an animal. The merit is in relieving real hunger, wherever it is found.

💡 Why this is the perfect ending

Charity is love of the world (#49) reversed in action — taking what the world-lover hoards and giving it away. And the chapter chooses the humblest charity — a fed stomach — so its highest reward is within reach of even the poorest believer. You may never build a masjid; you can always feed someone hungry.

Sab se bara ajr sab se chhote kaam mein: ek bhooke ka pet bharna. Shaayad masjid na banaa sako — magar kisi bhooke ko khilaana hamesha kar sakti ho.

Exercise 5 · Reflection — write a sentence or two each
