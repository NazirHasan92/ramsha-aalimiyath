# Part A · Understanding the chapter

With Ḥadīth 41 we **cross out of the "Comprehensive Sayings" section** that Chapters 1–4 lived in, and into a new run of one-line aḥādīth. The previous chapter (31–40) closed Part 1's first block on a tight theme — *who is the believer in community*. This chapter is looser, but a quiet **three-part architecture** holds it together: it opens with **knowledge** (what the believer must *know*), turns inward to the **heart** (what the believer *loves*, and how steadily he *acts*), and lands on **wealth and provision** (how the believer *earns, owes, and gives*).

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter 5 ka naqsha — *pehle ʿilm* (musalman ko kya jaanna chahiye), *phir dil* (kya pasand karta hai, kitni pabandi se amal karta hai), *aakhir mein maal* (kaise kamata hai, qarz kaise utaarta hai, kaise deta hai). ʿIlm → dil → dunya. Yeh teen theme poore chapter ko bandhte hain.

Three natural themes — keep this in your head as the spine of the chapter:

1. **Sacred knowledge** (Aḥādīth 41, 42, 43) — seeking knowledge is an *obligation* (#41); the most truthful dreams come at the pre-dawn hour (#42 — perception when the mind is clear); the *best* of people is the one who learns the Qurʾān and teaches it (#43). The believer's mind is the first thing the chapter addresses.
2. **The heart — steady deeds & where love leads** (Aḥādīth 44, 46, 49) — the deeds Allah loves most are the *regular* ones, even if small (#44); your *love* for a thing blinds and deafens you (#46); and love of *this world* is the origin of *every* sin (#49). This is the inner economy: what you cling to, and how consistently you act.
3. **Wealth, debt & provision** (Aḥādīth 45, 47, 48, 50) — a wealthy person delaying a debt is *oppression* (#45); a *little that suffices* beats *much that distracts* (#47); seeking *lawful* earning is an obligation in its own right (#48); and the best charity is to *fill a hungry stomach* (#50). The hands, after the heart.

Notice the flow: **know your dīn → guard your heart's love and stay consistent → handle wealth justly.** It mirrors the larger arc of the whole book (faith → character → world), compressed into ten sayings. Knowledge feeds the heart; a corrected heart handles wealth rightly. The order is not random — *you cannot fix the wealth chapter until the heart chapter is fixed, and you cannot fix the heart until you know.*

**Yaad rakho:** Teeno theme ek doosre par bante hain — *ʿilm* dil ko sahi karta hai, sahi *dil* maal ko sahi se sambhaalta hai. Isi liye tartīb yeh hai: pehle jaano, phir dil theek karo, phir haath (maal). Class ko yeh tartīb dikhao — yeh chapter ka asal payaam hai.

Work through each ḥadīth below. For each, ask yourself: *can I explain this hadith to a classmate in one sentence, in Urdu, without reading the book?* (*Kya main yeh hadith ek classmate ko ek line mein Urdu mein samjha sakti hoon, bina kitaab dekhe?*) If yes — move on. If not — re-read.

## 41 · طَلَبُ الْعِلْمِ فَرِيْضَةٌ عَلَى كُلِّ مُسْلِمٍ — "Seeking knowledge is an obligation on every Muslim"

**Source:** Ibn Mājah. (Widely narrated; the wording *ṭalabu l-ʿilmi farīḍatun ʿalā kulli muslim* is the famous one.)

### What "knowledge" means here — and what it doesn't

This is one of the most quoted aḥādīth in the whole tradition — and one of the most misunderstood. The Prophet ﷺ says seeking *ʿilm* is *farīḍah* (an obligation) on *every* Muslim. The danger is to hear "every Muslim must seek knowledge" and picture everyone becoming a scholar, a muftī, a ḥāfiẓ. **That is not what the ḥadīth means.**

The book is precise here. It draws the classic distinction:

- **Farḍ ʿayn** (individual obligation) — the knowledge *every single Muslim* must have: who his Creator is (*maʿrifah*), Allah's oneness (*waḥdāniyyah*), the prophethood of the messengers, and *how to perform what is required of him* — how to pray, how to fast, the rules of what he actually does. This is the *ʿilm* of Ḥadīth 41. It is the knowledge that makes your *own* worship valid.
- **Farḍ kifāyah** (communal obligation) — the higher knowledge: issuing legal verdicts (*fatāwā*), deep tafsīr, ḥadīth sciences. This is *not* required of each Muslim individually. It is enough that *the community* has scholars who carry it.

So the obligation of #41 is **practical and personal**: learn enough of your dīn to do your dīn correctly. A person who prays daily but has never learned the rules of wuḍūʾ and ṣalāh is neglecting a *farḍ* — not an optional virtue.

**Yaad rakho:** "ʿIlm har musalman par farz hai" — magar *kaunsa* ʿilm? Woh ʿilm jo tumhari apni ibādat ko sahi banaye: Allah ki maʿrifat, tawhīd, nubuwwat, aur jo amal tum karte ho uske ahkaam (namaz, roza kaise karna hai). Yeh *farḍ ʿayn* hai. Muftī banna, gehra tafsīr — yeh *farḍ kifāyah* hai, har ek par nahin. Yaani: *itna seekho jitna apni dīn sahi tareeqe se karne ke liye chahiye.*

### Why this ḥadīth opens the chapter

It is fitting that the chapter on the believer's mind, heart, and wealth opens with *knowledge*. You cannot correct what you do not understand. Every later ḥadīth in this chapter — what to love, how to earn, how to give — *presupposes* that you have first learned the rule. Knowledge is the gate.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh hadith chapter ke shuru mein kyun? Kyunki jo cheez samajh nahin, usay theek nahin kar sakte. Aage ki har hadith — dil, maal, sadaqah — pehle *jaanne* ka taqaaza karti hai. ʿIlm darwaza hai.

## 42 · أَصْدَقُ الرُّؤْيَا بِالْأَسْحَارِ — "The most truthful dreams are [those seen] just before dawn"

**Source:** Tirmidhī.

### A narrow, careful claim

This is a *small* ḥadīth and it must be taught *small*. It does **not** say "dreams are a source of religious law", nor "act on your dreams", nor "interpret everyone's dreams". It makes one narrow observation: of all the dreams a person sees, the ones most likely to be *truthful* (ṣādiqah) are those of the **asḥār** — the last portion of the night, just before dawn (the time of suḥūr and tahajjud).

The book gives two clean reasons:

1. **The mind is calm.** After a night of rest, just before waking, the mind is settled — not churning with the day's noise. A calm mind is a clearer receiver.
2. **It is the hour of the angels' descent.** This pre-dawn time is marked by the descent of the angels who come to witness the morning prayer. It is a blessed window of the night.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh choti hadith hai — isay choti hi rakho. Yeh nahin keh rahi ke khwaab pe amal karo ya khwaab se masaail nikaalo. Sirf yeh: sab khwaabon mein se *sachay* khwaab woh hote hain jo *seher* ke waqt aate hain (fajr se thori der pehle). Do wajhain — (1) dimaagh us waqt pursukoon hota hai, (2) yeh farishton ke nuzool ka waqt hai. Bas itna.

### Why it sits in the "knowledge" theme

A true dream (*ruʾyā ṣādiqah*) is, the scholars say, a small part of prophethood — a faint, non-binding form of *perception*. It belongs loosely with knowledge: both are ways the believer *receives* truth. But mark the difference sharply for your classmates: *ʿilm* (Ḥadīth 41) is *obligatory and binding*; a dream is *neither*. Don't let the two blur.

**Yaad rakho:** Sachay khwaab ko ʿulamā nubuwwat ka ek chhota hissa kehte hain — ek halki si *idraak* (perception). Isi liye yeh "ʿilm" wale theme ke saath aata hai. Magar farq saaf rakho: *ʿilm farz aur laazim hai; khwaab na farz hai na laazim.* Donon ko mat milao.

## 43 · خَيْرُكُمْ مَنْ تَعَلَّمَ الْقُرْآنَ وَعَلَّمَهُ — "The best among you is the one who learns the Qurʾān and teaches it"

**Source:** Bukhārī. Narrated by ʿUthmān ibn ʿAffān (raḍiyallāhu ʿanhu).

### The two-part qualification

This is a famous, beloved ḥadīth — and notice it has **two** clauses, joined: *taʿallama* (learned) **and** *ʿallamahu* (taught it). The "best of you" is not merely the one who *knows* the Qurʾān, nor merely the one who *teaches*. It is the one who does **both** — learns it, then passes it on.

That is exactly Ramsha's own position. She is a *student-teacher*: she learns the material, then teaches it to her cohort. This ḥadīth is, in a real sense, the charter of what she is doing. The cycle of *learn → teach → others learn → others teach* is how the Qurʾān has travelled intact for fourteen centuries.

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith ke do hisse hain — *seekha* aur *sikhaaya*. "Behtareen" woh nahin jo sirf jaanta hai, na woh jo sirf sikhaata hai — balke woh jo *dono* karta hai. Yeh Ramsha ka apna kaam hai: pehle khud seekho, phir cohort ko sikhao. Isi silsile se Qurʾān 1400 saal se mehfooz chala aa raha hai.

### Why the Qurʾān specifically

The "best" rank is tied to the *Qurʾān* in this ḥadīth — the speech of Allah itself. Teaching it isn't only transmitting information; it is being a link in the chain that carries Allah's words to the next person. Even teaching a child *Sūrat al-Fātiḥah* puts you inside this ḥadīth. You do not need to be a great scholar — you need to have learned *something* of the Qurʾān and to be passing it on.

**Yaad rakho:** Yahaan "behtareen" ka darja *Qurʾān* ke saath jura hai — Allah ke kalaam ke saath. Sikhaana sirf maʿlūmāt dena nahin — tum us zanjeer ki kaḍī ban-te ho jo Allah ke kalaam ko aagay le jaati hai. Kisi bachhe ko *Sūrat al-Fātiḥah* sikhaana bhi is hadith mein shamil hai. Bara ʿālim hona zaroori nahin — bas Qurʾān ka kuch seekho aur aagay pohnchao.

## 44 · أَحَبُّ الْأَعْمَالِ إِلَى اللهِ أَدْوَمُهَا وَإِنْ قَلَّ — "The most beloved deeds to Allah are the most constant, even if few"

**Source:** Bukhārī, Muslim. Narrated by ʿĀʾishah (raḍiyallāhu ʿanhā).

### Consistency beats intensity

Here begins Theme 2 — the heart. And the first lesson is about *how* the heart should act: **steadily**. The deeds Allah loves most are not the biggest or the most dramatic — they are the *most regular* (*adwam* = most continuous), *even if they are small in quantity* (*wa in qalla*).

The book clarifies what "deeds" means here: not the obligatory five prayers (those are required regardless), but the **voluntary** acts — set portions of *nafl* prayer, daily Qurʾān recitation, fixed *dhikr* litanies (*awrād*) — that a person *takes upon himself* to do regularly. The teaching: a small *wird* done every single day is more beloved to Allah than a huge burst of worship that burns out in a week.

**Yaad rakho:** Theme 2 (dil) yahaan se shuru. Pehla sabaq: *pabandi se* amal karo. Allah ko sab se zyada mehboob woh aʿmāl hain jo *hamesha* kiye jaayein — chahe thoray hi hon. "Aʿmāl" se murād farz namazein nahin (woh to har haal mein laazim hain) — balke *nafl*: rozana ka wird, thora Qurʾān, dhikr. Roz ka chhota wird Allah ko us bare josh se zyada pyaara hai jo ek hafte mein khatam ho jaaye.

### Why Allah loves the constant deed

Two reasons worth naming for classmates:

1. **It proves the heart's steadiness.** Anyone can do a great deed once, in a moment of emotion. Doing a small deed *every day*, including the days you don't feel like it, proves a *settled* relationship with Allah — not a mood.
2. **It is sustainable.** The burst-and-burnout pattern (a Ramadan of intense worship followed by eleven months of nothing) is fragile. The Prophet ﷺ pointed to a different model: *little and lasting*.

This is one of the most *practically liberating* aḥādīth in the chapter. It tells the overwhelmed beginner: *don't aim for everything — pick one small thing and never drop it.*

**Yaad rakho:** Allah constant amal kyun pasand karta hai? (1) Yeh dil ki *istiqāmat* sabit karta hai — bara kaam josh mein koi bhi ek baar kar leta hai, magar chhota kaam *roz* karna (un dinon bhi jab dil na chaahe) pakka taʿalluq dikhata hai. (2) Yeh *qaabil-e-dawaam* hai — Ramadan ka josh phir gyarah maheene khaali, yeh kamzor model hai. Rasool ﷺ ne doosra model diya: *thora magar hamesha.* Yeh hadith naye seekhne wale ke liye bohot raahat wali hai: sab kuch mat pakdo — ek chhoti cheez pakdo aur kabhi mat chhodo.

## 46 · حُبُّكَ الشَّيْءَ يُعْمِيْ وَيُصِمُّ — "Your love for a thing blinds you and deafens you"

**Source:** Abū Dāwūd, Aḥmad. (Narrated from Abū al-Dardāʾ, raḍiyallāhu ʿanhu.)

*(We take #46 before #45 here, because it belongs to the heart's theme with #44 and #49. In the book it appears in numerical order — but the underlying ideas group this way.)*

### Love as a distorting lens

This is a piercing piece of spiritual psychology. The Prophet ﷺ states a *law of the heart*: when you *love* something, that love **blinds** you to its faults and **deafens** you to criticism of it. The book is exact: love "blinds you from seeing the defects of the object of your love, and deafens you from hearing criticism of it."

We have all seen this. A parent who cannot see a flaw in their child. A person infatuated who cannot hear a single warning about the one they love. Someone attached to a leader, a group, a possession, an idea — who goes deaf the moment it is criticised. The love itself becomes a *filter* on reality.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh dil ki ek gehri haqeeqat hai. Jab tum kisi cheez se *mohabbat* karte ho, woh mohabbat tumhe us cheez ke *ʿaib* dekhne se *andha* kar deti hai, aur uski *tanqeed* sunne se *behra* kar deti hai. Maa-baap ko apne bachhe ka koi ʿaib nazar nahin aata; ʿaashiq ko mehboob ke khilaaf ek lafz bhi gawara nahin. Mohabbat khud *haqeeqat par ek parda* ban jaati hai.

### The hinge to the next ḥadīth

Hold this thought, because it is the *mechanism* behind Ḥadīth 49. If love blinds and deafens (#46), then ask: *what is the most dangerous thing to love?* The chapter's answer comes in #49 — **the dunyā**. Love of the world blinds you to its defects and deafens you to every warning about it. #46 is the general law; #49 is its most catastrophic application.

Note also the *positive* inversion the scholars draw: this is why love of Allah and His Messenger ﷺ is so transformative — it *re-tunes* what you see and hear toward the truth. Love is not the enemy; *misplaced* love is. Aim the love correctly and the same blinding/deafening power becomes a guide.

**Yaad rakho:** Is baat ko thaame rakho — yeh hadith #49 ki *machinery* hai. Agar mohabbat andha aur behra kar deti hai (#46), to sab se khatarnaak cheez kisse mohabbat hai? Jawaab #49 mein: *dunya*. Aur ek positive pehlu bhi: isi liye Allah aur uske Rasool ﷺ se mohabbat itni badalne wali hai — woh tumhari nazar aur sun-ne ko *haqq ki taraf* mod deti hai. Dushman mohabbat nahin — *ghalat jagah* ki mohabbat hai. Mohabbat sahi rukh kar do, to wahi taaqat rehnumā ban jaati hai.

## 49 · حُبُّ الدُّنْيَا رَأْسُ كُلِّ خَطِيْئَةٍ — "Love of this world is the head [origin] of every sin"

**Source:** Bayhaqī (*Shuʿab al-Īmān*).

### The root diagnosis

This is the diagnostic centre of the whole chapter. *Raʾs* literally means "head" — the source, the origin, the fountainhead. The Prophet ﷺ traces *every* sin back to a single root: **love of the dunyā** (*ḥubb al-dunyā*).

Trace any sin and you will find it. Why does a person lie? To protect worldly standing or gain. Why steal? Love of wealth. Why backbite? Love of status. Why abandon prayer? Love of comfort, sleep, leisure. Why oppress? Love of power. Strip each sin down and at the bottom sits an *excessive attachment to this world* — wanting it, fearing to lose it, preferring it over the Ākhirah.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh poore chapter ki *tashkhīs* (diagnosis) hai. *Raʾs* ka matlab "sar / jaḍ / sarchashma". Rasool ﷺ ne har gunaah ki jaḍ ek cheez mein dikhaayi: *dunya ki mohabbat*. Koi bhi gunaah lo aur khol ke dekho — neeche dunya ki mohabbat milegi. Jhoot? Dunyawi faayde ke liye. Chori? Maal ki mohabbat. Gheebat? ʿizzat/maqaam ki mohabbat. Namaz chhodna? Aaraam ki mohabbat. Har gunaah ke neeche dunya se *had se zyada lagaav* baitha hai.

### The crucial qualification — *love of*, not *the world itself*

This is the landmine, and you must defuse it carefully. The ḥadīth condemns *ḥubb* — the *love* of the dunyā — **not the dunyā itself**, and **not** using the dunyā. The world is the believer's *field* for the Ākhirah; wealth, family, food, work are all *gifts* and *tools*. The Prophet ﷺ himself married, traded, ate, and lived in the world.

What is condemned is *love* — making the dunyā the *goal*, the thing you live for, the thing you would disobey Allah to keep. A believer can *handle* a great deal of the world while *loving* the Ākhirah. (This is exactly the point Ḥadīth 76, later in the book, makes about wealth being no harm for the God-fearing — *that's a later chapter*; here we just need to get the qualification right.) The dunyā in your *hand* is fine; the dunyā in your *heart* is the root of every sin.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh landmine hai — ehtiyaat se khol do. Hadith *ḥubb* (mohabbat) ki mazammat kar rahi hai — *dunya ki mohabbat* — na ke dunya ki, na uske istemaal ki. Dunya to momin ki *kheti* hai Ākhirah ke liye; maal, ghar wale, khaana, kaam — yeh sab Allah ke *tohfe* aur *auzaar* hain. Rasool ﷺ ne khud nikah kiya, tijaarat ki, khaaya, dunya mein rahe. Mazammat *mohabbat* ki hai — dunya ko *maqsad* bana lena, usi ke liye jeena, usay rakhne ke liye Allah ki nāfarmāni kar dena. *Dunya haath mein theek hai; dunya dil mein har gunaah ki jaḍ hai.*

### The cure — implied across the chapter

If love of the dunyā is the disease (#49), the chapter quietly prescribes the cure across its other aḥādīth: *knowledge* (#41) that re-orders your priorities; *consistent worship* (#44) that keeps the heart turned to Allah; *contentment* with little (#47); *lawful earning* without greed (#48); and *giving the world away* in charity (#50). Read together, the chapter doesn't just diagnose — it treats.

**Yaad rakho:** Agar dunya ki mohabbat bīmārī hai (#49), to chapter ki baaqi hadithein *ilaaj* batati hain: *ʿilm* (#41) jo priorities theek kare; *pabandi se ibādat* (#44) jo dil ko Allah ki taraf rakhe; *qanāʿat* thoray par (#47); *halāl kamaai* bina laalach (#48); aur dunya ko *sadaqah* mein de dena (#50). Milakar yeh chapter sirf bīmārī nahin batata — *ilaaj* bhi deta hai.

## 45 · مَطْلُ الْغَنِيِّ ظُلْمٌ — "The delay [in repayment] of a wealthy [debtor] is oppression"

**Source:** Bukhārī, Muslim. Narrated by Abū Hurayrah (raḍiyallāhu ʿanhu).

*(Theme 3 — wealth — begins here. In the book #45 sits between #44 and #46; we group it with the wealth aḥādīth because that is its subject.)*

### A precise, weighty word: *ẓulm*

The Prophet ﷺ does not call delayed repayment "rude" or "inconvenient" — he calls it **ẓulm**, *oppression*: the same word used for the gravest injustices. *Maṭl* is the deliberate stalling of a debt — *"I'll pay you next month," "after Eid," "soon"* — when the person **has the means to pay now** (*al-ghaniyy* = the one who is *not poor*, who can afford it).

Read the precision: the ruling targets the *able* debtor. A genuinely broke person who *cannot* pay is not the subject of this ḥadīth — Allah commands *patience* with him (and the book's later aḥādīth and the Qurʾān treat his case). The ẓulm here is specifically the **rich man who could pay and chooses to stall.** That stalling — making your creditor chase you, wait, worry — is *injustice*, a sin, not a minor discourtesy.

**Yaad rakho:** Theme 3 (maal) yahaan se. Rasool ﷺ ne qarz ki taʾakheer ko "be-adabi" ya "takleef" nahin kaha — *ẓulm* kaha, yaani zulm. *Maṭl* matlab jaan-bujh kar qarz taalna — *"agle maheene," "Eid ke baad," "jald"* — jabke *ada karne ki taaqat mojood hai* (*al-ghaniyy* = jo tang-dast nahin). Dhyan do: hukm us par hai jo *ada kar sakta hai*. Jo sach mein muflis hai, woh is hadith ka maudūʿ nahin — uske saath Allah ne *sabr* ka hukm diya hai. Zulm yahaan us *maaldaar* ka hai jo de sakta hai magar taalta hai.

### Why so severe?

Because the creditor *parted with his own wealth to help you*. He trusted you. To then make him beg for his own money back — while you comfortably sit on it — turns his act of kindness into a source of distress. The sharīʿah protects the lender precisely because lending is an act of generosity that must not be punished by the borrower's negligence. For Urdu-speaking adults, many of whom lend and borrow within family and community, this ḥadīth is intensely practical: *if you can pay, pay — and paying on time is part of your dīn, not just good manners.*

**Yaad rakho:** Itni sakhti kyun? Kyunki qarz dene wale ne *apna maal* tumhari madad ke liye diya, tum par bharosa kiya. Ab usay apne hi paise ke liye peechay bhagwana — jabke tum aaram se baithe ho — uski neki ko takleef ka sabab bana deta hai. Sharīʿah qarz-khwāh ki hifaazat karti hai. Hum Urdu-speaking log aksar khaandaan aur mohalle mein lain-den karte hain — yeh hadith bohot ʿamali hai: *agar ada kar sakte ho, to ada karo — aur waqt par ada karna dīn ka hissa hai, sirf akhlaaq nahin.*

## 47 · مَا قَلَّ وَكَفَى خَيْرٌ مِمَّا كَثُرَ وَأَلْهَى — "A little that suffices is better than much that distracts"

**Source:** Ibn Ḥibbān.

### The balance of *kafā* against *alhā*

This ḥadīth weighs two kinds of provision against each other on a single scale. On one side: *mā qalla wa kafā* — what is *little* but **sufficient** (*kafā* = it is *enough*; it meets your need). On the other: *mā kathura wa alhā* — what is *much* but **distracts** (*alhā* = it makes you heedless, it pulls you away from Allah). The verdict: the little-but-sufficient is *better* (*khayr*).

Notice the hinge words. The ḥadīth does *not* praise *little* as such, nor condemn *much* as such. It praises *sufficiency* and condemns *distraction*. The question is never the *amount* — it is the *effect on your heart*. A modest income that lets you worship in peace is *better* than great wealth that swallows your time, your nights, your prayers, your peace.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh hadith do tarah ke rizq ko ek hi taraazu par taulti hai. Ek taraf: *thora magar **kaafi*** (*kafā* = zaroorat poori ho jaaye). Doosri taraf: *bohot magar **ghaafil karne wala*** (*alhā* = Allah se door kar de). Faisla: *thora-magar-kaafi behtar hai*. Lafz ghor se dekho — hadith *thore* ki taʿrīf nahin kar rahi, na *zyada* ki mazammat. Woh *kifāyat* ki taʿrīf aur *ghaflat* ki mazammat kar rahi hai. Sawaal *miqdaar* ka nahin — *dil par asar* ka hai. Mutawassit rizq jo sukoon se ʿibādat karne de, us bari daulat se behtar hai jo waqt, raatein, namazein, aur sukoon nigal jaaye.

### The link to #44 and #49

This is *qanāʿah* (contentment), and it sits beautifully between the chapter's other heart-aḥādīth. It is the practical antidote to #49 (love of the world): if you genuinely believe *enough is better than excess-that-distracts*, the grip of *ḥubb al-dunyā* loosens. And it pairs with #44: just as *little-but-constant* worship beats burst-and-burnout, *little-but-sufficient* provision beats much-but-heedless wealth. The same principle — **sustainable sufficiency over distracting excess** — runs through the believer's worship *and* his provision.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh *qanāʿat* hai, aur dil wali baaqi hadithon ke beech khoobsurati se baith-ti hai. Yeh #49 (dunya ki mohabbat) ka ʿamali toḍ hai: agar tum sach maano ke *kaafi behtar hai banisbat ghaafil karne wali zyaadati ke*, to dunya ki pakad dheeli par jaati hai. Aur yeh #44 se milti hai: jaise *thori-magar-musalsal* ʿibādat bare josh se behtar, waise *thora-magar-kaafi* rizq us zyada se behtar jo ghaafil kar de. Ek hi usool — *qaabil-e-dawaam kifāyat, ghaafil karne wali zyaadati se behtar* — momin ki ʿibādat *aur* rizq, dono mein chalta hai.

---

## 48 · طَلَبُ الْحَلَالِ فَرِيْضَةٌ بَعْدَ الْفَرِيْضَةِ — "Seeking the lawful is an obligation after the [primary] obligation"

**Source:** Bayhaqī (*Shuʿab al-Īmān*).

### Earning lawfully is itself an act of worship

Notice the deliberate echo of Ḥadīth 41. There, *seeking knowledge* was a *farīḍah*. Here, *seeking lawful earning* (*ṭalab al-ḥalāl*) is also a *farīḍah* — *baʿda l-farīḍah*, "after the obligation". The chapter bookends its knowledge theme and its wealth theme with the same word: both *ʿilm* and *ḥalāl rizq* are *obligations*, not optional extras.

The meaning of *baʿda l-farīḍah*: after you have fulfilled the *primary* individual obligations (ṣalāh, ṣawm, etc.), earning a *lawful* living becomes the *next* obligation. It is not of the *same* rank as prayer — the book is careful to say this is a lower tier than the *farḍ ʿayn* of worship — but it is a genuine obligation, not a worldly distraction. **The Muslim who earns ḥalāl to support himself and his dependants is performing an act of dīn, not merely "working".**

**Yaad rakho:** Lafz "farīḍah" ka jaan-bujh kar dohraav dekho — #41 mein *ʿilm* farīḍah tha, yahaan *ḥalāl kamaai* bhi farīḍah hai. *Baʿda l-farīḍah* ka matlab: pehli bunyaadi farzon (namaz, roza) ke *baad*, *halāl* kamaana *agla* farz ban jaata hai. Yeh namaz ke *baraabar* darja nahin (kitaab saaf kehti hai yeh us se neeche ka darja hai), magar yeh sacha farz hai, dunyawi mashghala nahin. *Jo musalman apne aur apne ahl-o-ʿiyāl ke liye halāl kamaata hai, woh dīn ka kaam kar raha hai — sirf "naukri" nahin.*

### The important limit — who is obligated

The book adds a crucial precision: this obligation falls on **those who must support themselves and their dependants** — not on everyone universally. A person who is *already provided for* — the book's example is *a woman under the protection of her father or her husband* — is **not** obligated to earn. The duty attaches to *responsibility*, not to gender or to people as such. Teach this precisely so no one over-generalises it into "every single person must go out and earn."

**Yaad rakho:** Kitaab ek aham baat barhaati hai: yeh farz un par hai jinhe *apna aur apne zer-e-kafālat logon ka* kharch uthaana hai — har ek par nahin. Jiska kharch pehle hi uth raha hai — kitaab ki misal: *woh ʿaurat jo apne waalid ya shauhar ki kafālat mein hai* — us par kamaana farz *nahin*. Yeh farz *zimmedaari* se juda hai, jins (gender) ya har shakhs se nahin. Yeh baat saaf sikhao taake koi ghalat ʿumūmiyat na bana le.

## 50 · أَفْضَلُ الصَّدَقَةِ أَنْ تُشْبِعَ كَبِدًا جَائِعَةً — "The most virtuous charity is to satisfy a hungry liver [stomach]"

**Source:** Bayhaqī (*Shuʿab al-Īmān*).

### The closing note — wealth turned outward

The chapter that opened with *knowledge* closes with *charity* — and not charity in the abstract, but the most concrete charity imaginable: **filling an empty stomach**. The word the ḥadīth uses is *kabid* — literally *liver*, the organ of hunger in classical Arabic idiom (as we say "stomach"). To *satisfy a hungry kabid* is to feed someone who is actually hungry, right now.

The book widens it generously: this includes feeding **a hungry person — Muslim or non-Muslim — or even an animal.** The merit is in *relieving real hunger*, wherever it is found. There is no requirement that the recipient share your faith. A hungry stranger, a hungry neighbour, a hungry creature — feeding any of them is named here as the *afḍal* (most virtuous) form of charity.

**Yaad rakho:** Jo chapter *ʿilm* se khula, woh *sadaqah* par band hota hai — aur woh bhi sab se thos sadaqah: *bhooka pet bharna*. Lafz *kabid* hai — lafzi maʿna "jigar", jo classical Arabic mein bhook ka ʿuzw hai (jaise hum "pet" kehte hain). *Bhooke kabid ko ser karna* matlab kisi sach-much ke bhooke ko abhi khilaana. Kitaab isay khulta hai: ismein *bhooka insaan — musalman ho ya ghair-musalman — ya jaanwar bhi* shamil hai. Fazīlat *asli bhook mitaane* mein hai, chahe jahaan ho. Lene wale ka tumhara dīn rakhna shart nahin.

### Why this is the perfect close

Look at the architecture. The chapter diagnosed *love of the world* as the root of all sin (#49). What is the most direct *act* against that love? To take the very thing the dunyā-lover hoards — food, wealth — and *give it away* to someone who needs it more. Charity is *ḥubb al-dunyā* reversed in action. And the chapter chooses the *humblest, most physical* charity — not grand endowments, but a fed stomach — to make it reachable for *everyone*. You may never build a masjid; you can always feed someone hungry. The chapter ends by putting its highest reward within reach of the poorest believer.

**Yaad rakho:** Naqsha dekho. Chapter ne *dunya ki mohabbat* ko har gunaah ki jaḍ bataaya (#49). Us mohabbat ke khilaaf sab se seedha *ʿamal* kya hai? Wahi cheez jo dunya-ka-ʿaashiq jamaʿ karta hai — khaana, maal — usay *de dena* kisi zaroortmand ko. Sadaqah, ʿamal mein, *ḥubb al-dunyā ka ulta* hai. Aur chapter ne sab se *ʿājizāna, jismāni* sadaqah chuna — bare auqāf nahin, balke ek bhara pet — taake yeh *har ek* ke liye mumkin ho. Shaayad tum kabhi masjid na banaa sako; magar kisi bhooke ko khilaana hamesha kar sakti ho. Chapter apna sab se bara ajr sab se ghareeb momin ki pohnch mein rakh kar khatam hota hai.

---

## The big picture — what these 10 aḥādīth are silently building

Stand back. The chapter walks the believer through **three faculties, in the right order**:

1. **The mind** — *know* your dīn (#41), receive truth humbly (#42), and pass on the Qurʾān (#43).
2. **The heart** — act *steadily* (#44), guard *what you love* because love blinds (#46), and uproot the love of the world that is the source of every sin (#49).
3. **The hands** — pay your debts on time (#45), be content with the sufficient (#47), earn *lawfully* (#48), and give the world away to the hungry (#50).

These are not ten scattered sayings. They are a single **discipline of the self**: a corrected mind shapes a corrected heart, and a corrected heart governs corrected hands. *ʿIlm → dil → maal.* And the deepest current beneath all ten is the contest between *ḥubb al-dunyā* and *ḥubb al-Ākhirah* — love of the world versus love of the next.

**Yaad rakho:** 10 hadith — par 1 tarbiyat. Teen quwwatein, sahi tartīb mein: *zehn* (jaano, #41–43) → *dil* (pabandi, mohabbat, dunya se bachao, #44/46/49) → *haath* (qarz, qanāʿat, halāl, sadaqah, #45/47/48/50). Sahi zehn sahi dil banata hai, sahi dil sahi haath chalata hai. Aur in sab ke neeche ek hi muqabla hai: *dunya ki mohabbat banaam Ākhirah ki mohabbat.* Class ko aakhir mein yeh ek-tasveer dikhao.

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

Now you understand the chapter. This section is how to *deliver* it to your classmates.

---

## The 6 landmines to watch for

### Landmine 1 · #41 Heard as "every Muslim must become a scholar"

Classmates will hear *"seeking knowledge is obligatory on every Muslim"* and either feel crushed (*"I can never be a scholar"*) or over-reach. The ḥadīth means the *farḍ ʿayn* knowledge — enough to do your own dīn correctly — not the *farḍ kifāyah* of scholarship.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh hadith yeh nahin keh rahi ke har musalman ʿālim, muftī ya ḥāfiẓ bane. Yeh keh rahi hai ke har musalman par itna ʿilm farz hai jitne se woh apni dīn *sahi* tareeqe se kar sake — Allah ko pehchaane, tawhīd jaane, aur jo ʿibādat woh karta hai uske masaail jaane (namaz, roza kaise karna hai). Yeh hai farḍ ʿayn. Aur gehra ʿilm — fatwa, tafsīr — woh farḍ kifāyah hai, yaani kuch logon ka community mein hona kaafi hai, har ek par nahin. To dabaav mat lo — bas itna seekho jo tumhe apni dīn theek karne ke liye chahiye."*

### Landmine 2 · #42 Heard as "follow your dreams / interpret dreams"

The dreams ḥadīth is small and classmates love to inflate it — *"so I should act on my dreams?"*, *"interpret this dream I had"*. Shut this down gently but firmly. The ḥadīth makes one narrow observation; it is **not** a basis for action or law.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh hadith bohot choti baat keh rahi hai — aur bas wahi. Yeh keh rahi hai ke sab khwaabon mein se sachay khwaab woh hote hain jo seher ke waqt aate hain — kyunki tab dimaagh pursukoon hota hai aur farishton ke nuzool ka waqt hai. Bas. Yeh hadith yeh *nahin* keh rahi ke khwaab pe amal karo, ya khwaab se masaail nikaalo, ya har khwaab ki taʿbīr dhoondo. ʿIlm farz hai aur laazim — khwaab na farz hai na laazim. Donon ko alag rakho."*

### Landmine 3 · #44 #47 — Heard as "small/little is the goal" (laziness license)

The "little-but-constant" (#44) and "little-but-sufficient" (#47) aḥādīth are a gift to the lazy if misheard: *"so I only need to do a tiny bit!"* Wrong. The praise is for *constancy* and *sufficiency*, not for *smallness*. The point is steadiness and contentment — not minimum effort.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Hoshiyaar raho — yeh do hadithein *thore* ki taʿrīf nahin kar rahin, balke *dawaam* (#44) aur *kifāyat* (#47) ki. #44 yeh nahin keh rahi 'thora karo aur khush raho' — woh keh rahi hai 'jo bhi karo, *roz* karo, chhodo mat.' #47 yeh nahin keh rahi 'kam kamao' — woh keh rahi hai 'jo *kaafi* ho aur Allah se ghaafil na kare, woh us *zyada* se behtar hai jo ghaafil kar de.' Yeh sustī ki ijaazat nahin — yeh *istiqāmat* aur *qanāʿat* ka sabaq hai. Farq samjho."*

### Landmine 4 · #49 — "Love of the world" misheard as "the world / wealth is evil"

This is the most important landmine of the chapter. Classmates — especially pious-minded ones — will hear *"love of the world is the root of all sin"* and slide into world-hatred: *"so wealth is bad, family is a distraction, enjoying anything is sinful."* That is **not** the ḥadīth. The condemnation is of *ḥubb* (love-as-goal), not of the dunyā or its lawful use.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh sab se aham point hai is chapter ka. Hadith keh rahi hai *dunya ki mohabbat* har gunaah ki jaḍ hai — *dunya khud* nahin, na uska istemaal. Dunya to momin ki *kheti* hai Ākhirah ke liye. Rasool ﷺ ne khud nikah kiya, tijaarat ki, achha khaana khaaya, achhi cheezein pasand kiin. Mazammat *mohabbat* ki hai — yaani dunya ko *maqsad* bana lena, usi ke liye jeena, usay rakhne ke liye Allah ki nāfarmāni kar dena. Ek aasaan formula: *dunya tumhare haath mein ho to theek hai; dunya tumhare dil mein baith jaaye to har gunaah ki jaḍ hai.* Maal kashti mein paani jaisa — kashti ke neeche theek, kashti ke andar aa jaaye to doobne ka sabab."*

### Landmine 5 · #45 — "Delaying a debt is oppression" applied to the genuinely broke

Classmates may hear #45 and feel guilt — *"I owe money I genuinely can't pay; am I an oppressor?"* — or harshly judge a poor debtor. The ḥadīth targets *al-ghaniyy*, the one **who can pay and stalls**. It does not condemn the person who genuinely cannot pay.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Hadith mein lafz hai *al-ghaniyy* — woh jo *ada karne ki taaqat rakhta hai* magar taalta hai. Yeh us par hai jo de sakta hai aur phir bhi *'agle maheene, Eid ke baad'* karta rehta hai. Jo sach mein muflis hai aur de hi nahin sakta — woh is hadith ka maudūʿ nahin. Uske saath to Allah ne *sabr* ka, aur muhlat dene ka hukm diya hai. To agar tum sach mein tang-dast ho, tum zaalim nahin — magar jab taaqat aaye, foran ada karo. Aur agar tum de *sakte* ho aur taal rahe ho — yeh sirf bad-akhlaqi nahin, yeh *zulm* hai aur gunaah hai."*

### Landmine 6 · #48 — "Everyone must earn" / ignoring the limit

Ḥadīth 48 (lawful earning is obligatory) can be over-generalised into *"so every single person, including those already provided for, must go out and earn."* The book is precise: the obligation falls on *those responsible for their own and dependants' support* — not universally.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Hadith keh rahi hai *halāl kamaana* farz hai — magar kitaab ne saaf kiya: yeh farz *us par* hai jisko apna aur apne zer-e-kafālat logon ka kharch uthaana hai. Jiska kharch pehle se uth raha hai — jaise woh ʿaurat jo apne waalid ya shauhar ki kafālat mein hai — us par kamaana farz nahin. Yeh farz *zimmedaari* ke saath juda hai, har shakhs ke saath nahin. To isay yeh mat banao ke 'har ek ko bahar nikal kar kamaana hi parega' — balke jis par kharch ki zimmedaari hai, us par halāl kamaana farz hai."*

## Suggested 60-minute class plan

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–5 min | Greeting, recitation of Sūrah Fātiḥah, intention. Connect to last chapter (4 — the believer in community). Tell them: *Part 1's "Comprehensive Sayings" block closed last week; today we move into the next run of aḥādīth, and they organise beautifully into mind → heart → hands.* |
| 5–10 min | The chapter's three themes (knowledge / the heart / wealth) — write the arc on the board: *ʿilm → dil → maal*. Tell them the deep current: *ḥubb al-dunyā vs ḥubb al-Ākhirah*. |
| 10–25 min | **Theme 1 — Aḥādīth 41, 42, 43** (knowledge). Read each, recite the Arabic together. | 25–45 min | **Theme 2 — Aḥādīth 44, 46, 49** (the heart). This is the heart of the lesson — give it the most time. #44 (constancy) → #46 (love blinds) → #49 (love of world = root of sin). Chapter ko #50 (bhooke ko khilaana) par *land* karne do — yeh ek thos, har ek ke liye mumkin, dil ko choo lene wala ikhtitām hai.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 5

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum aage dekhenge"* or *"yeh book ke scope se bahar hai"*:

- ❌ **The full ʿaqīdah curriculum** triggered by #41 — the detailed sciences of tawḥīd, the names and attributes, kalām. Stay with the *practical farḍ ʿayn* framing for this class.
- ❌ **Dream interpretation** triggered by #42 — the science of *taʿbīr al-ruʾyā*, categories of dreams, Yūsuf's dreams in detail. Resist requests to interpret classmates' actual dreams. Keep #42 *small*.
- ❌ **The fiqh of debt and bankruptcy** triggered by #45 — *ḥawālah*, *iflās*, what a judge may seize, imprisonment for debt. We teach the *moral principle* (*maṭl al-ghaniyy ẓulm*), not a procedural law course.
- ❌ **The detailed fiqh of lawful and unlawful income** triggered by #48 — ribā, the categories of ḥarām earnings, doubtful (*mushtabah*) income. Reference that the lawful must be sought; don't open the full rulings-set. (Ribā appears in a much later ḥadīth of the book — *that's a later chapter*.)
- ❌ **The zuhd debates** triggered by #49 — whether poverty or wealth is superior, the stations of the renunciants, comparisons between Companions. Stay with the clean *love-of-world-is-the-root* point and its careful qualification.
- ❌ **The full fiqh of zakāh vs ṣadaqah** triggered by #50 — niṣāb, the eight categories, calculation. This ḥadīth is about *voluntary* charity's *spirit*, not the obligatory zakāh system.

**Yaad rakho:** Pre-loading aur tangents madad nahin karte. 10 hadithein hain — har ek ke andar dam hai. Apne aap mein. Aur cheezein mat daalo. Class ka focus = *yeh 10 hadithein dil mein bait jaayein.* Bas.

Before the class, make sure:

- [ ] You can recite each of the 10 aḥādīth in Arabic from memory (or near-memory). *(Har hadith ki Arabic yaad ho ya bohot qareeb. #47 aur #50 thori lambi hain — magar baaqi 8 chhoti aur strong honi chahiye.)*
- [ ] You can give the English meaning and the source of each (Ibn Mājah / Tirmidhī / Bukhārī / Muslim / Abū Dāwūd / Aḥmad / Ibn Ḥibbān / Bayhaqī). *(Har hadith ka matlab aur source yaad ho.)*
- [ ] You can name the **three themes** — knowledge, the heart, wealth — and which ḥadīth goes where. *(Teen themes, aur kaunsi hadith kahaan.)*
- [ ] You can explain the **farḍ ʿayn / farḍ kifāyah** distinction for #41 cleanly. *(Farḍ ʿayn aur farḍ kifāyah ka farq saaf bayan kar sako.)*
- [ ] You can explain the **#46 → #49 link** — love blinds (general law) → love of the world is its worst case. *(Mohabbat andha karti hai → dunya ki mohabbat uski sab se buri shakl.)*
- [ ] You can deliver the **#49 qualification** — *love of* the world, not the world itself — in one clean sentence. *(Dunya ki mohabbat, na ke dunya — yeh ek line mein.)*
- [ ] You have the 6 landmine counter-strategies ready, at least the Urdu phrasing. *(Cheh landmines aur har ki Urdu samjhauti.)*
- [ ] You can give a *one-sentence Urdu summary* of each ḥadīth without the book. *(Har hadith ka ek-line Urdu khulasa.)*

If yes to all — *tum tayar ho.*

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Are they still hearing #41 as "everyone must be a scholar"? *(Farḍ ʿayn ka matlab samajh aaya — ya dabaav mein hain?)*
2. Are they inflating #42 into dream-following? *(Khwaab wali hadith ko choti rakha — ya badha rahe hain?)*
3. Are they using #44/#47 as a laziness license? *(Dawaam/kifāyat samjhe — ya "thora hi kaafi hai" sun rahe hain?)*
4. Are they sliding into world-hatred after #49? *(Dunya ki mohabbat vs dunya ka farq pakda?)*
5. Are they judging the genuinely-broke debtor by #45? *(Al-ghaniyy wala point samjhe — taaqat-waala vs muflis?)*
6. Are they over-generalising #48 into "everyone must earn"? *(Zimmedaari wali shart yaad hai?)*
7. Can they state the chapter's arc — *mind → heart → hands* — and the love-of-world thread? *(Chapter ka safar aur asal dhaaga yaad hai?)*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh 7 points tumhe batayenge kahaan revision chahiye. Agar 3+ points par cohort phans rahi hai, agle session se pehle yeh hadithein dohrao — aur khaas taur par #49 ka qualification (dunya vs dunya-ki-mohabbat) phir samjhao, kyunki yahi galat samajhne par poora chapter ulta ho jaata hai.
