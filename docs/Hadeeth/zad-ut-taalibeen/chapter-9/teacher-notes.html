★ Master Overview — نَقْشَةُ الْبَابِ

Ten aḥādīth, three movements — trust given & trials sent, keep the heart steady & true, the unseen at work. One discipline beneath them all: trust the unseen — order your life around what you cannot see.

Teaching arc: the chapter trains the believer in three movements, all under one discipline. First, trust given, trials sent — the one whose counsel is sought carries a trust and must advise sincerely (#81); the truly fortunate is the one spared trials, and the tested-but-patient earns glad tidings too (#82); and even love for your children can secretly breed miserliness and cowardice (#83). Then, keep the heart steady and true — Allah is beautiful and loves beauty as gratitude, not vanity (#84); every burst of eagerness is followed by a slump, so aim for the steady middle (#85); and a purified heart reads truth from falsehood by its own contentment or unease (#86). Finally, the unseen at work — your provision pursues you as surely as your death (#87); Satan moves through you unseen, like blood — so raise your guard (#88); wealth is this Umma's particular test (#89); and the swiftest-answered prayer is the secret one, for an absent brother (#90).

Chapter ka safar: amaanat di gayi, aazmaaish bheji gayi (81, 82, 83) → dil ko mustaqil aur saccha rakho (84, 85, 86) → ghaib ki taaqatein kaam par hain (87, 88, 89, 90). In sab ke neeche ek dhaaga: jo nazar nahin aata us par bharosa karo — ghaib par bharosa.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Recite all 10 aḥādīth in Arabic (every matn is given this chapter), with correct meaning in English + Roman Urdu.
  2. Name the source of each ḥadīth (Tirmidhī, Ibn Māja, Abū Dāwūd, Aḥmad, Muslim, Bukhārī, Abū Nuʿaym–Ḥilya) at the book's exact level.
  3. Recognise that all ten continue the book's "Inna" sayings (إِنَّ…) and explain the chapter's deep thread — trust the unseen.
  4. State #81's principle — being asked for advice creates an amāna; the consultant must give sincere, sound counsel.
  5. Give #82's both halves — the fortunate is spared trials, and the tested-but-patient is given glad tidings — so a trial is not a sign of Allah's displeasure.
  6. Teach #83 carefully — it names the fear that love of children can breed (miserliness, cowardice), not a condemnation of children — and tie the cure to #87 (the child's rizq is decreed).
  7. Explain #84 as beauty being gratitude made visible ("the effects of His blessings"), not a licence for vanity or materialism.
  8. Give #85's practical law — every burst of eagerness is followed by a slump, so choose a sustainable, steady load (moderation), and #86's qualification (the heart-compass works for a purified heart).
  9. Teach #88 carefully — Satan's stealth is a warning to guard (dhikr, taʿawwudh), not a fatalist excuse — and hold #89 (wealth = this Umma's test) together with #76 (wealth is neutral).
  10. Land #90 as the secret prayer's ikhlāṣ — the opposite of riyāʾ (#80) — the chapter's deep thread being trust the unseen.

Ḥadīth 81 · إِنَّ الْمُسْتَشَارَ مُؤْتَمَنٌ · Advice is a trust

Theme 1 — Trust given, trials sent. The moment someone asks your counsel, an amāna lands on you.

إِنَّ الْمُسْتَشَارَ مُؤْتَمَنٌ

inna l-mustashāra muʾtaman

"The consultant is placed in a position of trust."

"Beshak jis se mashwara liya jaaye woh amaanat-daar hai."

Tirmidhī, Ibn Māja
Where this fits: Theme 1 opens — the chapter's first "Inna" saying upgrades an everyday act (giving advice) into a trust. The unseen here: a trust is created though nothing visible changes.

مُسْتَشَار / مُؤْتَمَن · mustashār / muʾtaman — "the consulted one / the entrusted one"

Meaning
The mustashār is the person whose advice is sought; muʾtaman means he is the holder of a trust (amāna). The instant someone turns to you and asks "what should I do?", an invisible trust is placed in your lap. The book: he is obligated to give sincere and sound advice, so that he does not betray the trust placed in him (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 8:791).
Roman Urdu
Mustashār = jis se mashwara maanga jaaye; muʾtaman = jis ke paas amaanat rakhi gayi. Jis lamhe koi poochhe "main kya karoon?", ek nazar na aane wali amaanat tumhare zimme aa girti hai. Kitaab: us par farz hai ke sacha aur durust mashwara de, taake amaanat mein khayaanat na ho (Mirqāt 8:791).
The obligation
You are no longer free to fob them off, give a careless half-answer, or steer them toward what benefits you. You owe them the truth as you honestly see it — the advice you would give yourself.
The takeaway
Mashwara aam baat nahin — amaanat hai. (Chapter 8 ki #71 se jodo: īmān amaanat se zaahir hota hai — yahaan amaanat = poochhe jaane ki.)

📿 Connect to Qurʾān · 3:159

وَشَاوِرْهُمْ فِي الْأَمْرِ

"…and consult them in the matter."

Why this fits: Allah commands even His Prophet ﷺ to consult — making mashwara a built-in part of the believer's life. And if consultation is commanded, the one consulted carries a real responsibility: #81's amāna.

Urdu mein: Allah apne Nabi ﷺ ko bhi mashwara karne ka hukm deta hai — to mashwara momin ki zindagi ka hissa hai; aur jis se mashwara liya jaaye us par asli zimmedaari (#81 ki amaanat) hai.

Ḥadīth 82 · إِنَّ السَّعِيدَ لَمَنْ جُنِّبَ الْفِتَنَ · The fortunate are spared the storm

Theme 1 — Trust given, trials sent. Two safe paths: being spared a trial, or being patient through it.

إِنَّ السَّعِيدَ لَمَنْ جُنِّبَ الْفِتَنَ

inna s-saʿīda la-man junniba l-fitan

"The fortunate one is he who is protected from trials."

"Beshak khush-naseeb woh hai jise fitnon se bacha liya jaaye."

Abū Dāwūd
Where this fits: Theme 1 — true good fortune is redefined: not what you gain, but the unseen storms you are spared. The book's second half rescues the one who is tested.

سَعِيد / جُنِّبَ / فِتَن · saʿīd / junniba / fitan — "fortunate / was kept away / trials"

Meaning
The truly saʿīd (fortunate) is the one kept away from fitan (trials, temptations). Junniba is passive — he was made to avoid — because being spared a trial is a mercy, not your own doing. The book adds the second half: the Messenger ﷺ repeated this thrice, then said, "And the one who is tested but perseveres — glad tidings are for him" (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 9:289).
Roman Urdu
Asli saʿīd (khush-naseeb) woh hai jise fitan (aazmaaishon) se bachaaya jaaye. Junniba majhool hai — use bachaaya gaya — kyunki fitne se bachna rehmat hai. Kitaab doosra hissa jodti hai: Nabi ﷺ ne teen baar dohraaya, phir farmaaya: "jo aazmaaya jaaye magar sabr kare — uske liye khush-khabri" (Mirqāt 9:289).
⚠️ Both halves together
Either way the believer is safe: spared by mercy, or tested and patient. So a trial is not a verdict that Allah is displeased with you — the tested-but-steadfast get glad tidings too.
The takeaway
Asli khush-naseebi = woh fitne jin se bacha liya gaya (jin ka pata bhi nahin chalta). Aur aazmaaye gaye to sabr mein bhi khush-khabri — aazmaaish naaraazgi ki nishaani nahin.

Ḥadīth 83 · إِنَّ الْوَلَدَ مَبْخَلَةٌ مَجْبَنَةٌ · Even love can bend your character

Theme 1 — Trust given, trials sent. The sharpest ḥadīth — teach with care. It targets the fear, not the child.

إِنَّ الْوَلَدَ مَبْخَلَةٌ مَجْبَنَةٌ

inna l-walada mabkhalatun majbanah

"Children are a source of miserliness and cowardice."

"Beshak aulaad bukhl aur buzdili ka sabab (ban sakti) hai."

Ibn Māja, Aḥmad
Where this fits: Theme 1 closes — the unseen fear hidden inside love. Handle Landmine 1 carefully: this is an honest x-ray, not a condemnation of children.

مَبْخَلَة / مَجْبَنَة · mabkhala / majbana — "a cause of stinginess / a cause of cowardice"

Meaning
The book reads this as "two possible negative effects of children on their parents." (1) Miserliness — owing to fear of poverty, or fear of not being able to provide the child a suitable upbringing, a parent can grow stingy, hoarding rather than giving. (2) Cowardice — owing to fear of dying and leaving the children orphaned, a father could be deterred from striving in the path of Allah (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 8:469–470).
Roman Urdu
Kitaab ise "aulaad ke do mumkin manfi asraat" kehti hai. (1) Bukhl — gareebi ke darr se, ya bachche ko sahi parwarish na de paane ke darr se — kanjoosi. (2) Buzdili — marne aur bachchon ko yateem chhodne ke darr se — Allah ki raah se ruk jaana (Mirqāt 8:469–470).
⚠️ Not a condemnation of children
Children are a blessing and a means of reward. This is an honest x-ray of how the intensity of a parent's love, if unguarded, can make him grip wealth too tightly and shrink from risk for the deen. The target is the fear, not the child.
The cure (→ #87)
Ilaaj kam mohabbat nahin — woh kheench dekhna aur mohabbat ko Allah par bharose ki jagah na lene dena. Yaad rakho: bachche ka rizq bhi likha hai (#87) — to darr ka koi jawaaz nahin.

⚠️ Landmine 1 — "so the dīn says children are bad / a burden"

"Yeh hadith aulaad ko bura nahin keh rahi — aulaad to ne'mat hai. Kitaab ise 'do mumkin manfi asraat' kehti hai: aulaad ki mohabbat, agar be-lagaam ho, to (1) bukhl — gareebi ke darr se kanjoosi, aur (2) buzdili — marne aur bachchon ko yateem chhodne ke darr se deen ki raah mein peechhe hatna — paida kar sakti hai. Nishaana darr hai, bachcha nahin. Ilaaj: yaad rakho bachche ka rizq bhi Allah ne likha (#87)."

📿 Connect to Qurʾān · 8:28

وَاعْلَمُوا أَنَّمَا أَمْوَالُكُمْ وَأَوْلَادُكُمْ فِتْنَةٌ

"And know that your wealth and your children are but a trial."

Why this fits: The Qurʾān names children (and wealth) as a fitnah — exactly #83's point: the things we love most can become the arena of our test. Naming the trial is the first step to guarding against it.

Urdu mein: Quran aulaad (aur maal) ko fitnah kehta hai — wahi #83 ki baat: jin se sab se zyada mohabbat hai wahi hamaari aazmaaish ban sakti hain. Aazmaaish ka naam jaan lena uske pehre ka pehla qadam hai.

Ḥadīth 84 · إِنَّ اللهَ جَمِيلٌ يُحِبُّ الْجَمَالَ · Beauty loved as gratitude

Theme 2 — Keep the heart steady & true. The beauty Allah loves is gratitude made visible — not vanity.

إِنَّ اللهَ جَمِيلٌ يُحِبُّ الْجَمَالَ

inna Llāha jamīlun yuḥibbu l-jamāl

"Allāh is beautiful and loves beauty."

"Beshak Allah khoobsoorat hai aur khoobsoorti ko pasand karta hai."

Muslim
Where this fits: Theme 2 opens — turning to the inner life. The book fixes the meaning so it doesn't tip into vanity (Landmine 2): beauty as a quiet thank-you.

جَمِيل / جَمَال · jamīl / jamāl — "beautiful / beauty"

Meaning
The book unpacks "Allah is jamīl" as beauty in His essence, His attributes, and His actions, and "loves beauty" as referring to His creation. It then fixes the meaning with another ḥadīth: "Verily Allah loves to see the effects of His blessings upon His servant" (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 8:829). So the beauty He loves is gratitude made visible — clean clothes, a cared-for appearance, beautiful recitation — as a thank-you that these are His gifts.
Roman Urdu
Kitaab "Allah jamīl hai" ko samjhati hai — Apni zaat, sifaat, aur afʿaal mein — aur "khoobsoorti pasand karta hai" Apni makhlooq mein. Phir ek hadith se maʿna saaf karti hai: "Allah pasand karta hai ke bande par apni ne'mat ke asaraat dekhe" (Mirqāt 8:829). To jo husn Allah ko mehboob hai woh shukr jo nazar aaye — saaf kapde, sanwra hulia, achhi tilaawat.
⚠️ Not vanity
This is the opposite of a licence for materialism or arrogance. Beauty pointed up (thanking the Giver) is worship; beauty pointed across (impressing an audience) slides straight into riyāʾ — last chapter's #80.
The takeaway
Allah ke nazdeek asli khoobsoorti = shukr. Niyyat upar (Dene wale ki taraf) ho to husn ʿibaadat; logon ki taraf ho to riyāʾ (#80).

⚠️ Landmine 2 — "so Islam endorses showing off wealth / dressing to impress"

"Yeh maal ki numaaish ya ghuroor ka parwaana nahin. Kitaab ise doosri hadith se jodti hai: 'Allah pasand karta hai ke bande par apni ne'mat ke asaraat dekhe' — yaʿni khoobsoorti = shukr jo nazar aaye, na ke logon ko impress karne ki numaaish. Husn agar upar (Dene wale ki taraf) ho to ʿibaadat; logon ki taraf ho to riyāʾ (#80)."

Ḥadīth 85 · إِنَّ لِكُلِّ شَيْءٍ شِرَّةً وَلِكُلِّ شِرَّةٍ فَتْرَةً · Plan for the slump

Theme 2 — Keep the heart steady & true. The high-energy start isn't the achievement; outlasting the slump is.

إِنَّ لِكُلِّ شَيْءٍ شِرَّةً وَلِكُلِّ شِرَّةٍ فَتْرَةً

inna li-kulli shayʾin shirratan, wa li-kulli shirratin fatratan

"For everything there is eagerness, and for every eagerness there is a weakening."

"Beshak har cheez ka ek josh hota hai, aur har josh ke baad ek susti aati hai."

Tirmidhī
Where this fits: Theme 2 — a law of human nature applied to worship. Pairs with Chapter 5's #44 (steady deeds, even if few) seen from the other side.

شِرَّة / فَتْرَة · shirra / fatra — "keen eagerness / a slackening"

Meaning
Shirra is a burst of keen eagerness and energy; fatra is the dip that follows. The ḥadīth states a law of nature, and the book applies it to worship: some worshippers set out to perform an abundant amount of deeds, then gradually diminish, and sometimes give up altogether — this is not what the Sharīʿa encourages. What is encouraged is to be moderate, to remain between exaggeration and negligence, and continue unabated (Mazād al-Rāghibīn 33).
Roman Urdu
Shirra = josh, tezi ka ubaal; fatra = uske baad ki susti. Hadith fitrat ka qanoon batati hai; kitaab ise ʿibaadat par lagaati hai: kuch log bohot ʿibaadat shuru karte hain, phir kam karte jaate hain, kabhi chhod dete hain — yeh Sharīʿa pasand nahin karti. Pasandeeda = mutawassit raho, ghulu aur kotahi ke darmiyaan, bila-naagha jaari rakho (Mazād al-Rāghibīn 33).
The practical rule
Knowing the fatra is coming, don't load up more than you can sustain in your burst of zeal. Pick a moderate, steady amount and keep it — so that when the eagerness cools, the habit survives.
The takeaway
Tez shuruaat kaarnaama nahin — susti se aage nikal jaana kaarnaama hai. (Yaad rakho #44: thoda magar paaband amal Allah ko sab se mehboob.)

Where this fits: Theme 2 closes — the heart's own quiet verdict, the unseen compass. Note the book's qualification: reliable in a purified heart.

طُمَأْنِينَة / رِيبَة · ṭumaʾnīna / rība — "settled contentment / unease, doubt"

Meaning
Truthfulness brings ṭumaʾnīna (a settled, calm contentment); falsehood brings rība (unease, a flicker of doubt you can't silence). The book qualifies: this refers to a true believer — one who has purified his heart from the rust of sins and lowly desires — who can then differentiate truth from falsehood by the contentment or unease he feels (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 6:24). The book notes it continues an earlier ḥadīth ("leave what makes you doubt for what does not").
Roman Urdu
Sachaai ṭumaʾnīna (jama hua sukoon) deti hai; jhoot rība (bechaini, na dabnay wala shak) chhodta hai. Kitaab ki qaid: yeh sacche momin ki baat hai — jisne dil gunaahon ki zang se saaf kiya — woh sach aur jhoot mein dili sukoon ya bechaini se farq kar leta hai (Mirqāt 6:24). Yeh ek pichli hadith ("jo shak mein daale use chhod do") ka silsila hai.
⚠️ The qualification matters
This is not a blanket "if it feels fine, it's halal." The compass is reliable in a heart scrubbed clean by taqwā. A heart caked in sin loses the sensitivity — it grows comfortable with falsehood. So the way to sharpen the sensor is to keep cleaning the heart.
The takeaway
Allah ne paak dil mein sach-jhoot ka sensor rakha hai — magar woh saaf dil mein bharosemand hai. Gunaah-zada dil signal kho deta hai. Sensor tez karo = dil saaf rakho.

رِزْق / أَجَل · rizq / ajal — "provision / appointed death"

Meaning
Your rizq (provision) seeks you out just as your ajal (appointed death) does — with the same certainty. You do not chase your death; it comes for you. The ḥadīth says your provision is already coming, just as surely. The book sharpens it: livelihood comes before death, because death only comes once a person has received all the livelihood destined for him (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 9:173). You will not die until your last decreed morsel reaches you.

Kitaab: rizq maut se pehle aata hai — maut tab hi jab saara muqarrar rizq mil jaaye (Mirqāt 9:173). Aakhri likha niwaala milne tak maut nahin.
⚠️ Not idleness — calm, lawful effort
This does not mean sit idle — Islam commands you to work and seek lawful provision (Chapter 5's #48). It removes the panic: strive, but calmly and lawfully, since no grasping brings a dirham beyond your decree.
The takeaway
Hirs nahin, sukoon. Halaal koshish karo — kyunki taqdeer se zyada kuch nahin milega. (Yeh #83 ke gareebi-darr ka jawab hai.)

Ḥadīth 88 · إِنَّ الشَّيْطَانَ يَجْرِي مِنَ الْإِنْسَانِ مَجْرَى الدَّمِ · The enemy inside the walls

Theme 3 — The unseen at work. The heaviest ḥadīth — a call to guard, NOT a fatalist excuse.

إِنَّ الشَّيْطَانَ يَجْرِي مِنَ الْإِنْسَانِ مَجْرَى الدَّمِ

يَجْرِي مَجْرَى الدَّمِ · yajrī majrā d-dam — "runs as blood runs"

Meaning
The book gives two complementary readings. (1) Speed & intimacy: Satan operates so swiftly in deceiving the human and prompting evil thoughts that he is likened to the flow of blood — which a person does not even feel. The whisper arrives so smoothly, in your own "voice," that you don't notice a foreign hand. (2) Strong influence through the evil-inciting self (al-nafs al-ammāra bi 'l-sūʾ) — he works through your own desires. Another reading: just as blood runs until death, Satan too remains with him until then (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 1:245–246).
Roman Urdu
Kitaab do tafseerein deti hai. (1) Raftaar aur qurbat: Shaitaan itni tezi se bhatkaata aur bure khayaal daalta hai ke use khoon ke bahaav se tashbeeh di — jise insaan mehsoos tak nahin karta. (2) Nafs-e-ammaara ke zariye gehra asar — woh tumhari apni khwaahishon se kaam karta hai. Aur: jaise khoon maut tak daudta hai, Shaitaan bhi tab tak saath (Mirqāt 1:245–246).
⚠️ A warning to guard — not fatalism
This is not an excuse ("I can't help it, he made me do it"). It is a call to raise your guard, not surrender responsibility. If the enemy is inside the walls, you stay alert — you do not give up. You feel the whisper arrive, and you answer it.
The antidotes
Ilaaj ghaib-waale hain: dhikr (jo dil Allah ko yaad rakhe darwaza band — #67), taʿawwudh (panaah maango), aur us nafs-e-ammaara ko bhooka rakhna jis par woh sawaar hota hai. Zimmedaari tumhari.

⚠️ Landmine 4 — "so Satan is in my blood — I can't help sinning, he makes me do it"

The heaviest landmine in the chapter. "Khabardaar — yeh bahaana nahin ke 'main majboor hoon, Shaitaan ne karaaya.' Hadith uski tezi, qurbat, aur chhupe-pan ki tambeeh hai — taake tum pehra barhao, na ke haath khade kar do. Dushman agar deewaron ke andar hai to insaan zyada chaukanna hota hai, haar nahin maanta. Waswasa aaye — to use jawab do: Allah ki yaad (dhikr) darwaza band kar deti hai, taʿawwudh maango, aur us nafs-e-ammaara ko bhooka rakho. Zimmedaari tumhari hai."

فِتْنَة / الْمَال · fitna / al-māl — "trial, temptation / wealth"

Meaning
Every Umma (community) has its own characteristic fitnah — the soft spot through which it is most likely to be tested and to fail. For this Umma, the Prophet ﷺ named it precisely: wealth. Not because wealth is evil (Chapter 8's #76 already settled that — wealth is neutral); the point is diagnostic: this is the arena where we, specifically, are most at risk — greed, hoarding, status-by-possession, love of the dunya.
Roman Urdu
Har Umma ki apni khaas fitnah hoti hai — woh kamzor jagah jahaan se woh sab se zyada aazmaayi jaati hai. Is Umma ke liye Nabi ﷺ ne use saaf kar diya: maal. Isliye nahin ke maal bura hai (#76 keh chuki — maal neutral), balke tashkhees ke taur par: yeh woh maidaan hai jahaan khaas hum sab se zyada khatre mein hain.
⚠️ Diagnosis, not condemnation
Naming your test is half of passing it. The unseen here is the gradual, quiet nature of this fitnah — wealth rarely tempts with one dramatic choice; it bends the heart slowly, one small compromise at a time, until the dunya sits where Allah should.
The takeaway
Maal halke haath se pakdo, us mein Allah se daro — yeh tumhari Umma ka khaas imtihaan hai. (Hold with #76 + #87: neutral, aur rizq likha hua — na bhaago na jakdo.)

Maal neutral hai. #89 sirf tashkhees hai: yeh batati hai ke is Umma ki kamzor jagah maal hai — taake hum wahaan pehra dein. Ise 'gareebi zyada deendaar hai' ya 'paisa gunaah hai' mat banao."

⚠️ Common student confusions

#83 — "the dīn says children are bad / a burden"

The opposite — children are a blessing. The book reads this as two possible negative effects of love of children if unguarded: miserliness (fear of poverty) and cowardice (fear of leaving orphans). The target is the fear, not the child.

Aulaad ne'mat hai. Nishaana woh darr hai jo be-lagaam mohabbat paida karti hai — bukhl aur buzdili — bachcha nahin.

#82 — "a tested person is unlucky / Allah is displeased with him"

The book's second half rescues this: "the one who is tested but perseveres — glad tidings are for him." Spared or patient, the believer is safe. A trial is not a verdict of displeasure.

Bachaaya gaya — ya aazmaaye gaye magar sabr kiya — dono khush-naseeb. Aazmaaish naaraazgi ki nishaani nahin.

#84 — "Islam endorses vanity / showing off wealth"

Beauty Allah loves = gratitude made visible ("the effects of His blessings"), not display for people. Aimed up (to the Giver) it is worship; aimed across (to an audience) it slides into riyāʾ (#80).

Husn = shukr jo nazar aaye, numaaish nahin. Upar (Dene wale ki taraf) ʿibaadat; logon ki taraf riyāʾ.

#85 — "it's natural to fizzle out, so no harm in quitting"

Naming the slump (fatra) is a warning to plan for it, not permission to quit. The book: diminishing then abandoning deeds is not what the Sharīʿa encourages. Pick a steady, moderate load and keep it.

Susti fitrat hai — magar chhodne ki ijaazat nahin. Mutawassit, mustaqil amal — tez shuruaat jo khatam ho jaaye, woh nahin.

#88 — "Satan is in my blood, so I can't help sinning"

Not fatalism. His stealth is a call to guard, not an excuse. The enemy inside the walls means stay alert — dhikr, taʿawwudh, starve the nafs ammāra. Responsibility stays yours.

Yeh bahaana nahin — pehra barhaane ki tambeeh hai. Dhikr, taʿawwudh, nafs ko bhooka rakhna. Zimmedaari tumhari.

#89 — "wealth is evil / poverty is holier"

A diagnosis, not a condemnation. #76 already settled it: wealth is neutral, no harm for the God-fearing. #89 only names where this Umma is most at risk — so guard that spot.

Maal bura nahin (#76). #89 sirf tashkhees hai: is Umma ka khaas imtihaan maal hai — wahaan pehra do. Gareebi-parasti nahin.
