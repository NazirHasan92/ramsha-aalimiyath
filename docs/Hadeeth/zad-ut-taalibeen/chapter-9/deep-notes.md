# Part A · Understanding the chapter

These ten aḥādīth (81–90) continue the book's *"Inna" sayings* — the short, pithy *"indeed, X is Y…"* nominal sentences that began at #77. Every single matn this chapter opens with **إِنَّ (inna, "indeed…")**. After the previous chapter trained the believer to *weigh the reality, not the label*, this chapter quietly does something else: it keeps pointing at things you **cannot see** — and tells you that those unseen things are what really run your life.

Look at what these ten are about. The trust hidden *inside* a piece of advice (81). The trials Allah *spares* the fortunate from (82). The fear and stinginess that love of your own children can *secretly* breed in you (83). The beauty Allah loves — in His own essence and in His creation (84). The slackening that *always* follows a burst of eagerness (85). The contentment a truthful heart feels and the unease a lying one cannot shake (86). The provision that is *already* chasing you down (87). The enemy who moves through you *like your own blood* (88). The wealth that is this Umma's particular, quiet test (89). And the prayer that is answered *fastest* precisely because no-one is watching you make it (90). Almost every one of these is about something **invisible** — a decree, a motive, an inner state, a hidden enemy, a secret prayer. The chapter's deep thread is: **trust the unseen — live by what you cannot see, not only by what you can.**

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter 9 ka naqsha — yeh das hadithein "Inna" wali hain (*"beshak X yeh hai…"*) jo #77 se shuru hui theen. Magar in das ke neeche ek dhaaga hai: yeh baar baar un cheezon ki taraf ishaara karti hain jo **nazar nahin aatein** — aur kehti hain ke wahi cheezein asal mein tumhari zindagi chalaati hain. Mashware mein chhupi *amaanat* (81); woh *fitne* jin se khush-naseeb bacha liya jaata hai (82); woh *bukhl aur buzdili* jo apni aulaad ki mohabbat dil mein chupke se daal deti hai (83); woh *jamaal* jo Allah ko mehboob hai (84); woh *susti* jo har josh ke baad aati hai (85); woh *itminaan/shak* jo sacha/jhoota dil mehsoos karta hai (86); woh *rizq* jo tumhe khud dhoondta hai (87); woh *dushman* jo tumhare *khoon ki tarah* tum mein daudta hai (88); woh *maal* jo is ummat ka khaas, khaamosh imtihaan hai (89); aur woh *duʿāʾ* jo sab se jaldi qubool hoti hai kyunki koi dekh nahin raha (90). Dhaaga: **ghaib par bharosa — jo nazar nahin aata usi par bohot kuch ka daar-o-madaar hai.**

Three natural themes — keep this in your head as the spine of the chapter:

1. **Trust given, trials sent** (Aḥādīth 81, 82, 83) — the chapter opens on how the believer stands inside relationships and trials. The one whose counsel is sought carries a *trust* and must advise sincerely (81). The fortunate person is the one *kept away* from trials (82). And then the sharpest turn: even your love for your own children can *secretly* pull you toward miserliness and cowardice (83). The unseen here is the *trust* inside an act, the *decree* behind a trial, and the *fear* hidden in love.
2. **Keep the heart steady and true** (Aḥādīth 84, 85, 86) — three aḥādīth on the inner life. Allah is beautiful and loves beauty (84); every burst of religious eagerness is naturally followed by a slackening, so aim for the *steady middle* (85); and truthfulness leaves the heart *content* while lying leaves it in *doubt* (86). The unseen here is the heart's own compass.
3. **The unseen at work** (Aḥādīth 87, 88, 89, 90) — four aḥādīth on the invisible forces around and inside you. Your provision pursues you as surely as your death does (87); Satan moves through you unseen, like blood (88); wealth is this Umma's particular trial (89); and the swiftest-answered prayer is the *hidden* one, for an absent brother (90). Provision, the enemy, the test, and the weapon — all of them invisible.

Notice the flow: **stand rightly in your trusts and trials → keep the inner heart beautiful, steady, and true → then live in awareness of the unseen forces that actually shape your days.** And the deepest current under all ten is *trust in the unseen*: the believer is trained to order his life around realities he cannot see — Allah's decree of provision and trial, the motive inside his own chest, the enemy in his bloodstream, and the power of a prayer no-one witnesses. Where Chapter 8's hidden thread was *baṣīra* (weigh the reality, not the label), this chapter's hidden thread is *trust the unseen* — the same eye, now turned from *judging* what is in front of you to *relying* on what is beyond your sight.

**Yaad rakho:** Teeno theme ek dhaage se bandhe hain — *ghaib par bharosa.* Pehle apni *amaanaton aur aazmaaishon* mein sahi khade ho (81, 82, 83), phir *andaruni dil ko khoobsoorat, mustaqil, aur saccha* rakho (84, 85, 86), phir un *ghaib ki taaqaton* ke shaur mein jiyo jo asal mein tumhare din chalaati hain (87, 88, 89, 90). Pichle chapter ka dhaaga *asal ko taulo* tha; is chapter ka dhaaga *jo nazar nahin aata us par bharosa karo* — wahi aankh, ab saamne wali cheez ko *taulne* se hat kar nazar se *baahar* wali cheez par *tawakkul* karne par.

Work through each ḥadīth below. For each, ask yourself: *can I explain this hadith to a classmate in one sentence, in Urdu, without reading the book?* (*Kya main yeh hadith ek classmate ko ek line mein Urdu mein samjha sakti hoon, bina kitaab dekhe?*) If yes — move on. If not — re-read.

> **A note on order:** the book gives these in numeric order (81 → 90), and my three themes are *already* in numeric order (81–83 · 84–86 · 87–90), each theme a clean run of consecutive aḥādīth. So this chapter needs **no re-ordering** — the book's sequence and our teaching arc are the same (as in Chapter 8).

> **A note on the Arabic:** like Chapter 8, **all ten matns this chapter are short and clearly printed**, so I give the Arabic for *every one* — read off the rendered page (pp. 52–55), harakaat and all. Two boundaries to watch (both verified off the page): #85 *(li-kulli shayʾin **shirratan**…)* and #89 *(li-kulli ummatin **fitnatan**…)* — the noun after *inna* + its advanced predicate is **manṣūb** (tanwīn fatḥ), and the book prints **no idghām shadda** on the following *wāw* in either, so I reproduce them exactly as printed.

## Theme 1 · Trust given, trials sent

## 81 · إِنَّ الْمُسْتَشَارَ مُؤْتَمَنٌ — "The one whose counsel is sought is entrusted"

**Source:** Tirmidhī, Ibn Māja.

### Advice is a trust, not a casual favour

The chapter opens by quietly upgrading something we do every day. *Al-mustashār* is the person who is *consulted* — asked for advice. *Muʾtaman* means *one who is entrusted, a holder of a trust (amāna)*. So: **the one whose counsel is sought is in a position of trust.** The book explains it exactly: the person being consulted is *obligated to give sincere and sound advice, so that he does not betray the trust placed in him* (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 8:791). The moment someone turns to you and says *"what should I do?"*, an invisible amāna lands in your lap. You are no longer free to fob them off, steer them toward what benefits *you*, or give a careless half-answer. You owe them the truth as you honestly see it — the advice you would give yourself.

This connects straight back to Chapter 8's #71 (faith is proven by amāna). Here the amāna is a *specific* and easily-overlooked one: the trust of being asked. The unseen thing is that a trust has been created — nothing visible changes, but you are now accountable.

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter ek roz-marra ki cheez ko chupke se barha kar khulta hai. *Al-mustashār* = jis se mashwara maanga jaaye. *Muʾtaman* = jis ke paas amaanat rakhi gayi. Yaʿni: **jis se mashwara liya jaaye woh amaanat-daar hai.** Kitaab saaf kehti hai: jo mashwara de raha hai us par farz hai ke *sacha aur durust mashwara de, taake us amaanat mein khayaanat na ho* (Mirqāt 8:791). Jis lamhe koi tumse poochhe *"main kya karoon?"* — ek nazar na aane wali amaanat tumhare zimme aa girti hai. Ab tum use taal nahin sakte, apne faayde ki taraf nahin mod sakte, na laaparwaah aadha-adhoora jawab de sakte ho. Tumhe wahi sach dena hai jo tum khud ke liye chunte. (#71 se jodo — īmān amaanat se zaahir hota hai; yahaan amaanat = *poochhe jaane* ki amaanat.)

## 82 · إِنَّ السَّعِيدَ لَمَنْ جُنِّبَ الْفِتَنَ — "The fortunate one is he who is kept away from trials"

**Source:** Abū Dāwūd.

### True good fortune is being spared the storm — and patience in it

The world measures fortune by what you *gain*: money, status, success. This ḥadīth resets it: **the truly fortunate (saʿīd) one is he who is *kept away* from trials (fitan).** *Junniba* is passive — *he was made to avoid, kept clear of* — because being spared a fitnah is not your own doing; it is a mercy. The book adds a beautiful second half from the same narration: the Messenger of Allah ﷺ *repeated this statement three times* and then said, *"And the one who is tested but perseveres — then glad tidings are for him"* (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 9:289). So the ḥadīth holds two situations together: the one Allah *shields* from trial is fortunate; and the one Allah *does* test, but who *holds firm*, is given glad tidings too. Either way the believer is safe — by being spared, or by being patient.

The unseen here is the trial Allah quietly steers away from you. You rarely notice the fitan you were *protected* from — the bad partnership that fell through, the fight you didn't get drawn into, the temptation that never reached you. Real saʿāda includes all the invisible storms you never had to weather.

**Yaad rakho:** Dunya khush-naseebi ko *jo milta hai* us se naapti hai — paisa, ohda, kaamyaabi. Yeh hadith ise badal deti hai: **asal khush-naseeb (saʿīd) woh hai jise fitnon se *bacha liya gaya*.** *Junniba* majhool hai — *use bachaaya gaya* — kyunki fitne se bach jaana tumhara apna kaam nahin, yeh rehmat hai. Kitaab ek khoobsoorat doosra hissa jodti hai: Nabi ﷺ ne yeh baat *teen baar* dohraai phir farmaaya: *"aur jo aazmaaya jaaye magar sabr kare — to uske liye khush-khabri hai"* (Mirqāt 9:289). To do haalatein saath: jise Allah fitne se *bachaaye* woh khush-naseeb; aur jise *aazmaaye* magar woh *jama raha* uske liye bhi khush-khabri. Dono soorat mein momin mehfooz — ya bach kar, ya sabr kar ke. (Ghaib: woh fitne jo tum se chupke se hata diye gaye — jin ka tumhe pata bhi nahin chalta.)

## 83 · إِنَّ الْوَلَدَ مَبْخَلَةٌ مَجْبَنَةٌ — "Children are a cause of miserliness and cowardice"

**Source:** Ibn Māja, Aḥmad.

### Even your dearest love can quietly bend your character

This is the sharpest ḥadīth of the theme, and the one to teach most carefully. *Al-walad* is the child/children; *mabkhala* is "a cause of stinginess (bukhl)"; *majbana* is "a cause of cowardice (jubn)". The book explains it precisely as **"two possible negative effects of children on their parents"**: (1) **Miserliness** — *owing to fear of poverty, or fear of not being able to provide the child a suitable upbringing*, a parent can become stingy, hoarding rather than giving. (2) **Cowardice** — *owing to fear of dying and leaving the children orphaned*, a father could be deterred from striving and fighting in the path of Allah (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 8:469–470).

Read this exactly as the book frames it — it is **not a condemnation of children** (children are a blessing and a means of reward). It is an honest x-ray of a *hidden* effect: the very intensity of a parent's love can, *if unguarded*, make him grip his wealth too tightly and shrink from risk for the deen. The cure is not to love less — it is to *see* the pull and not let love of the child displace trust in Allah, who is the One providing for that child anyway (which lands you right into #87: your provision, and theirs, is already decreed and pursuing you).

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh theme ki sab se nazuk hadith hai — sab se ehtiyaat se sikhao. *Al-walad* = aulaad; *mabkhala* = *bukhl ka sabab*; *majbana* = *buzdili ka sabab*. Kitaab ise *"aulaad ke maa-baap par do mumkin manfi asraat"* ke taur par samjhati hai: (1) **Bukhl** — *gareebi ke darr se, ya bachche ko sahi parwarish na de paane ke darr se*, insaan kanjoos ban jaata hai. (2) **Buzdili** — *marne aur bachchon ko yateem chhod jaane ke darr se*, baap Allah ki raah mein nikalne/jihad se ruk sakta hai (Mirqāt 8:469–470). **Yeh aulaad ki mazammat nahin** (aulaad ne'mat hai). Yeh ek *chhupe* asar ki saaf tasveer hai: aulaad ki shadeed mohabbat, agar *be-lagaam* ho, insaan ko maal par zyada pakad aur deen ke liye khatre se peechhe hata sakti hai. Ilaaj kam mohabbat nahin — balke us *kheench* ko *dekhna* aur mohabbat ko Allah par bharose ki jagah na lene dena (jo seedha #87 mein le jaata hai: tumhara aur unka rizq pehle se likha hua tumhe dhoondta hai).

## Theme 2 · Keep the heart steady and true

## 84 · إِنَّ اللهَ جَمِيلٌ يُحِبُّ الْجَمَالَ — "Allāh is beautiful and loves beauty"

**Source:** Muslim.

### Beauty is loved — as gratitude, not as vanity

**Allah is *jamīl* (beautiful) and *loves* beauty (jamāl).** The book unpacks "Allah is beautiful" carefully — *in all aspects: in His essence, His attributes, and His actions* — and "loves beauty" as referring to His creation. Then it adds another ḥadīth that fixes the meaning precisely: *"Verily Allah loves to see the effects of His blessings upon His servant"* (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 8:829). That is the key. The "beauty" Allah loves is not the strutting display of wealth or the vanity of the proud — it is the believer letting the *effects of Allah's blessings show*: clean clothes, a cared-for appearance, a beautiful recitation, good order in one's work and home — as a quiet *thank-you* that these are gifts from Him.

So this is not a licence for materialism or arrogance; it is the opposite. True beauty before Allah is *gratitude made visible*. Keep the heart's intention right and the outward beauty becomes worship; let it curdle into showing-off and you have walked straight into #90's territory from last chapter (riyāʾ). Beauty pointed *up* toward the Giver is loved; beauty pointed *across* toward an audience is not.

**Yaad rakho:** **Allah *jamīl* (khoobsoorat) hai aur *jamāl* (khoobsoorti) ko pasand karta hai.** Kitaab "Allah khoobsoorat hai" ko samjhati hai — *Apni zaat, Apni sifaat, aur Apne afʿaal — har lihaaz se* — aur "khoobsoorti ko pasand karta hai" Apni makhlooq mein. Phir ek aur hadith jodti hai jo maʿna saaf kar deti hai: *"beshak Allah pasand karta hai ke woh apne bande par apni ne'mat ke asaraat dekhe"* (Mirqāt 8:829). Yahi asal baat hai. Jo "khoobsoorti" Allah ko mehboob hai woh maal ki numaaish ya takabbur nahin — woh yeh hai ke banda *Allah ki ne'maton ke asaraat zaahir hone de*: saaf kapde, sanwra hulia, khoobsoorat tilaawat, kaam aur ghar mein husn-o-tarteeb — ek khaamosh *shukriya* ke taur par ke yeh sab Usi ki ataa hai. To yeh maddiyat ya ghuroor ka parwaana nahin — iska ulta. Allah ke nazdeek asli khoobsoorti = *shukr jo nazar aaye.* Niyyat upar (Dene wale ki taraf) ho to husn ʿibaadat ban jaata hai; agar woh dikhaawe mein badal jaaye to woh pichle chapter ke riyāʾ (#80) mein chala gaya.

## 85 · إِنَّ لِكُلِّ شَيْءٍ شِرَّةً وَلِكُلِّ شِرَّةٍ فَتْرَةً — "For everything there is eagerness, and for every eagerness a weakening"

**Source:** Tirmidhī.

### Plan for the slump — aim for the steady middle

*Shirra* is a burst of keen eagerness and energy; *fatra* is the slackening, the dip that follows. The ḥadīth states a law of human nature: **everything has its surge of eagerness, and every surge of eagerness is followed by a weakening.** The book applies it straight to worship: *some worshippers set out to perform an abundant amount of deeds, but then gradually diminish in their performance, and sometimes give up altogether. This is not what the Sharīʿa encourages.* What *is* encouraged is that a person be **moderate** in what he takes on — *remain between exaggeration and negligence, and continue unabated* (Mazād al-Rāghibīn 33).

This is one of the most practically useful aḥādīth in the whole book. The high-energy start is not the achievement — *outlasting the slump* is. The believer who knows the fatra is coming doesn't pile on more than he can sustain in his burst of zeal; he picks a moderate, steady load and *keeps* it, so that when the eagerness cools, the habit survives. (This is exactly Chapter 5's #44 — *the most beloved deeds to Allah are the regular ones, even if few* — seen from the other side.)

**Yaad rakho:** *Shirra* = josh, tezi ka ubaal; *fatra* = uske baad aane wali susti, dheelaa-pan. Hadith insaani fitrat ka qanoon batati hai: **har cheez ka ek josh hota hai, aur har josh ke baad ek susti aati hai.** Kitaab ise seedha ʿibaadat par lagaati hai: *kuch ʿibaadat-guzaar bohot saari ʿibaadat shuru kar dete hain, phir dheere dheere kam karte jaate hain, kabhi bilkul chhod dete hain. Yeh woh nahin jo Sharīʿa pasand karti hai.* Jo pasand hai woh yeh ke insaan apne upar **mutawassit (moderate)** bojh le — *ghulu aur kotahi ke darmiyaan, aur musalsal bila-naagha jaari rakhe* (Mazād al-Rāghibīn 33). Sabaq: tez shuruaat kaarnaama nahin — *susti se aage nikal jaana* kaarnaama hai. Jo banda jaanta hai ke fatra aane wala hai woh apne josh mein itna bojh nahin uthaata jo nibha na sake; woh mutawassit, mustaqil load chunta hai aur use *qaayam* rakhta hai. (Yahi Chapter 5 ki #44 hai — *Allah ko sab se mehboob amal woh hai jo paaband ho, chahe thoda ho* — doosri taraf se.)

## 86 · إِنَّ الصِّدْقَ طُمَأْنِينَةٌ وَإِنَّ الْكَذِبَ رِيبَةٌ — "Truthfulness is contentment, and falsehood is doubt"

**Source:** Tirmidhī.

### The heart is a truth-detector — listen to it

**Truthfulness (ṣidq) is *ṭumaʾnīna* (a settled contentment), and falsehood (kadhib) is *rība* (unease, doubt).** The book makes the crucial qualification: *no doubt, this refers to a true believer — one who has purified his heart from the rust and yolk of sins and lowly desires.* Such a believer can *differentiate truth from falsehood by the contentment or unease he feels in his heart* (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 6:24, Mazād al-Rāghibīn 34). The book also notes this ḥadīth is a continuation of an earlier one (Hadith 137 in the book's wider numbering — the famous *"leave what makes you doubt for what does not"*).

So Allah has placed in the *purified* heart a kind of internal sensor: truth brings a calm, settled feeling; falsehood leaves a nagging unease, a flicker of doubt you can't quite silence. The unseen thing here is *your own heart's verdict* — a quiet signal beneath your reasoning. The catch is the qualification: this compass is reliable in a heart *scrubbed clean* by taqwā. A heart caked in sin loses the sensitivity — it grows comfortable with falsehood and uneasy with truth. So the way to *sharpen* the sensor is to keep cleaning the heart.

**Yaad rakho:** **Sachaai (ṣidq) *ṭumaʾnīna* (dili sukoon) hai, aur jhoot (kadhib) *rība* (bechaini, shak) hai.** Kitaab aham qaid lagaati hai: *isका taʿalluq sacche momin se hai — jis ne apna dil gunaahon aur kamzar khwaahishon ki zang se saaf kar liya.* Aisa banda *sach aur jhoot mein apne dil ke sukoon ya bechaini se* farq kar leta hai (Mirqāt 6:24). Kitaab yeh bhi kehti hai ke yeh hadith ek pichli hadith (kitaab ki Hadith 137 — mashhoor *"jo cheez tumhe shak mein daale use chhod do"*) ka silsila hai. To Allah ne *paak* dil mein ek andaruni sensor rakha hai: sach par thanda, jama hua ehsaas; jhoot par ek khalish, shak jo dab nahin-ता. Ghaib ki cheez yahaan = *tumhare apne dil ka faisla.* Magar qaid yaad rahe: yeh qutub-numa us dil mein bharosemand hai jo *taqwa se saaf* ho. Gunaah mein dooba dil hissaasiyat kho deta hai — jhoot se maanoos aur sach se bechain ho jaata hai. To sensor ko *tez* karne ka tareeqa = dil ko saaf rakhte rehna.

## Theme 3 · The unseen at work

## 87 · إِنَّ الرِّزْقَ لَيَطْلُبُ الْعَبْدَ كَمَا يَطْلُبُهُ أَجَلُهُ — "Provision pursues a person as surely as his death does"

**Source:** Abū Nuʿaym (Ḥilya).

### Your provision is chasing you — so chase it calmly, and lawfully

**Provision (rizq) seeks out the servant just as his death (ajal) seeks him out.** The image is striking: you do not chase your *death* — it is coming for you, on its appointed day, with total certainty; you cannot speed it up or escape it. The ḥadīth says your *rizq* is exactly like that — it is *already coming for you*, with the same certainty. The book adds an even sharper note: *in fact, livelihood comes before death, because death only comes once a person has received all the livelihood and provision destined for him* (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 9:173). You will not die until the last morsel decreed for you has reached you.

This does **not** mean sit idle — Islam commands you to *work* and *seek* lawful provision (that was Chapter 5's #48). What it removes is the *panic*. You strive, yes — but calmly, trustingly, and above all *lawfully*, because no amount of grasping will bring you a single dirham beyond your decree, and no honest shortfall of effort will cost you what was written. The unseen here is the decree of provision already in motion toward you. (Hold this with #83: the parent's fear of poverty that breeds miserliness is *answered* here — the child's rizq is decreed too.)

**Yaad rakho:** **Rizq bande ko aise dhoondta hai jaise uski maut (ajal) use dhoondti hai.** Tasveer gehri hai: tum apni *maut* ka peechha nahin karte — woh khud tumhare paas aa rahi hai, apne muqarrara din par, poore yaqeen ke saath; na tum use jaldi laa sakte ho na bach sakte ho. Hadith kehti hai tumhara *rizq* bhi bilkul aisa hi hai — woh *pehle se tumhari taraf aa raha* hai, usi yaqeen ke saath. Kitaab aur tez baat jodti hai: *haqeeqat mein rizq maut se pehle aata hai, kyunki maut tab hi aati hai jab banda apna saara muqarrar rizq paa chuka ho* (Mirqāt 9:173). Tum mar nahin sakte jab tak aakhri likha hua niwaala tum tak na pahonch jaaye. Iska matlab haath par haath rakh kar baithna **nahin** — Islam halaal rizq *kamaane* aur *talaash* karne ka hukm deta hai (Chapter 5 ki #48). Yeh sirf *ghabraahat* nikaalti hai. Tum koshish karo — magar sukoon se, bharose se, aur sab se barh kar *halaal tareeqe se*, kyunki kitni bhi hirs tumhe ek dirham bhi taqdeer se zyada nahin degi. (Ise #83 ke saath rakho: maa-baap ka gareebi ka darr jo bukhl paida karta hai — uska jawab yahaan hai: bachche ka rizq bhi likha hua hai.)

## 88 · إِنَّ الشَّيْطَانَ يَجْرِي مِنَ الْإِنْسَانِ مَجْرَى الدَّمِ — "Satan runs through a human like blood"

**Source:** Bukhārī.

### The enemy is *inside the walls* — so guard the inside

**Satan runs through the human being the way blood runs [through the body].** The book gives two complementary explanations. First, it is about his *speed and intimacy*: Satan *operates so swiftly in deceiving the human and prompting him with evil thoughts that he is likened to the flow of blood through a person's body — which a person does not even feel.* The whisper (waswasa) arrives so smoothly, so much in your own "voice," that you don't notice a foreign hand on it. Second, it points to his *strong influence through the evil-inciting self* (al-nafs al-ammāra bi 'l-sūʾ) — he works through your own lower desires. And another explanation: just as blood keeps running through the body until death, *Satan too will remain with him until then* (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 1:245–246).

The vital point — and the landmine — is that this is **not fatalism.** "Satan is in my bloodstream" is *not* an excuse ("I can't help it, he made me do it"). The ḥadīth is a *warning to raise your guard*, not a surrender of responsibility. If the enemy is *inside the walls*, you don't give up — you stay alert. The classical antidotes are exactly the unseen-facing ones: *dhikr* (the heart that remembers Allah closes the door — Chapter 7's #67), *taʿawwudh* (seeking refuge), and starving the *nafs ammāra* he rides on. You feel the whisper arrive — and you answer it.

**Yaad rakho:** **Shaitaan insaan mein aise daudta hai jaise khoon [jism mein] daudta hai.** Kitaab do mukammil tafseerein deti hai. Pehli, yeh uski *raftaar aur qurbat* ke baare mein hai: Shaitaan *insaan ko bhatkaane aur bure khayaalat daalne mein itni tezi se kaam karta hai ke use khoon ke bahaav se tashbeeh di gayi — jise insaan mehsoos tak nahin karta.* Waswasa itni narmi se, itna tumhari apni "awaaz" mein aata hai ke tumhe koi ghair haath nazar nahin aata. Doosri, yeh uske *nafs-e-ammaara (bure ki taraf uksaane wale nafs)* ke zariye gehre asar ki taraf ishaara hai — woh tumhari apni khwaahishon ke zariye kaam karta hai. Aur ek aur tafseer: jaise khoon maut tak jism mein daudta rehta hai, *Shaitaan bhi tab tak saath rehta hai* (Mirqāt 1:245–246). Aham baat — aur landmine — yeh hai ke yeh **taqdeer-parasti (fatalism) nahin.** "Shaitaan mere khoon mein hai" koi *bahaana nahin* ("main majboor tha, usne karaaya"). Hadith *pehra barhaane ki tambeeh* hai, zimmedaari se daст-bardaari nahin. Agar dushman *deewaron ke andar* hai to tum haar nahin maante — chaukanne rehte ho. Ilaaj wahi ghaib-waale hain: *dhikr* (jo dil Allah ko yaad rakhe woh darwaza band kar deta hai — Chapter 7 ki #67), *taʿawwudh* (panaah maangna), aur us *nafs-e-ammaara* ko bhooka rakhna jis par woh sawaar hota hai.

## 89 · إِنَّ لِكُلِّ أُمَّةٍ فِتْنَةً وَفِتْنَةُ أُمَّتِي الْمَالُ — "Every Umma has a trial; my Umma's trial is wealth"

**Source:** Tirmidhī.

### Know your community's particular test

**For every Umma (nation/community) there is a fitnah (trial), and the fitnah of my Umma is wealth (al-māl).** Every community has its own characteristic temptation — the soft spot through which it is most likely to be tested and to fail. For *this* Umma, the Prophet ﷺ identified it precisely: **wealth.** Not because wealth is evil in itself — Chapter 8's #76 already settled that (*"there is no harm in wealth for one who fears Allah"*; wealth is *neutral*, it takes the colour of the hand that holds it). The point of #89 is *diagnostic*: this is the arena where *we, specifically,* are most at risk — where greed, hoarding, status-by-possession, and love of the dunya will most easily pull us off course.

Naming your test is half of passing it. A community (and a person) that knows its weak point can guard it. The unseen here is the *gradual, quiet* nature of this fitnah — wealth rarely tempts with a single dramatic choice; it bends the heart slowly, one small compromise at a time, until the dunya sits where Allah should. (Hold this with #76 from last chapter and #87 just above: wealth is safe *with taqwā*, your provision is *decreed* — so neither flee wealth nor grasp at it; hold it lightly, fear Allah in it, and remember it is *the* test of your Umma.)

**Yaad rakho:** **Har Umma (qaum) ka ek fitnah hota hai, aur meri Umma ka fitnah maal hai.** Har qaum ki apni khaas aazmaaish hoti hai — woh kamzor jagah jahaan se woh sab se zyada aazmaaye jaane aur phisalne ka khatra rakhti hai. *Is* Umma ke liye Nabi ﷺ ne use saaf pehchaan diya: **maal.** Isliye nahin ke maal khud bura hai — Chapter 8 ki #76 yeh tay kar chuki (*"jo Allah se dare uske liye maal mein koi harj nahin"*; maal *neutral* hai). #89 ka maqsad *tashkhees (diagnosis)* hai: yeh woh maidaan hai jahaan *khaas tor par hum* sab se zyada khatre mein hain — jahaan hirs, jama-khori, maal se ohda, aur dunya ki mohabbat hamein sab se aasaani se raah se hata degi. Apni aazmaaish ka naam jaan lena uske paas hone ka aadha kaam hai. Ghaib ki baat yahaan = is fitne ki *dheemi, khaamosh* fitrat — maal kisi ek bade faisle se kam, dheere dheere dil ko modta hai, ek chhoti rियायत ke baad doosri, jab tak dunya wahaan na baith jaaye jahaan Allah hona chahiye. (Ise #76 aur abhi upar #87 ke saath rakho: maal *taqwa ke saath* mehfooz hai, tumhara rizq *likha* hua hai — to na maal se bhaago na use jakdo; halke haath se pakdo, us mein Allah se daro, aur yaad rakho yeh tumhari Umma *ka* imtihaan hai.)

## 90 · إِنَّ أَسْرَعَ الدُّعَاءِ إِجَابَةً دَعْوَةُ غَائِبٍ لِغَائِبٍ — "The fastest-answered prayer is one made for an absent person"

**Source:** Abū Dāwūd, Tirmidhī.

### The most powerful prayer is the one no-one sees you make

**The fastest of supplications to be answered is the duʿāʾ of an absent person for an absent person** — i.e. one made by somebody, in private, for someone who is not present and does not know about it. The book gives the reason exactly: *such supplications are made with pureness of intention and sincerity, and being far removed from ostentation* (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 5:23). That is the whole secret. When you pray for someone to their face, or where they can see, a sliver of showing-off can creep in. But when you pray for an absent brother — quietly, with no audience, no thanks coming back, nothing in it for you — the duʿāʾ is *pure ikhlāṣ*, and that purity is precisely why it rises fastest.

This is the perfect close to the chapter. After the unseen *enemy* (88) and the unseen *test* (89), here is the unseen *weapon*: a sincere, secret prayer for another. And it doubles back to last chapter's #80 (riyāʾ hollows a deed): the absent prayer is the *opposite* of riyāʾ — worship with the audience deliberately removed — so it carries maximum weight. (The famous wider narration adds: an angel appointed over you says *"āmīn, and for you the same"* — so the secret gift returns to the giver. Teach this only if you're confident of the wording; the book itself gives the *reason*, the ikhlāṣ, which is the point.)

**Yaad rakho:** **Sab se jaldi qubool hone wali duʿāʾ woh hai jo ek ghaair-haazir shakhs doosre ghaair-haazir ke liye kare** — yaʿni jo koi tanhaai mein, us shakhs ke liye maange jo maujood nahin aur jise pata bhi nahin. Kitaab wajah saaf deti hai: *aisi duʿāʾein khaalis niyyat aur ikhlaas se ki jaati hain, aur dikhaawe se bohot door hoti hain* (Mirqāt 5:23). Yahi poora raaz hai. Jab tum kisi ke saamne ya uski nazron ke saamne duʿāʾ karte ho to thoda sa dikhaawa ghus sakta hai. Magar jab tum kisi ghaair-haazir bhai ke liye — chupke se, bina kisi naazir ke, bina shukriya ki ummeed ke, bina apne kisi faayde ke — duʿāʾ karte ho, to woh *khaalis ikhlaas* hai, aur yahi paakeezgi hi wajah hai ke woh sab se tez upar uthti hai. Yeh chapter ka mukammal ikhtitaam hai. Ghaair-haazir *dushman* (88) aur ghaair-haazir *imtihaan* (89) ke baad, yahaan ghaair-haazir *hathiyaar* hai: doosre ke liye saccha, khaamosh duʿāʾ. Aur yeh pichle chapter ki #80 (riyāʾ amal ko khokhla karta hai) ka theek ulta hai: ghaair-haazir ke liye duʿāʾ riyāʾ ka *ulat* hai — ʿibaadat jis se naazir jaan-boojh kar hata diya gaya — isliye uska wazan sab se zyada.

## The big picture — what these 10 aḥādīth are silently building

Stand back. The chapter trains the believer in **three movements**, all under one discipline — *trust in the unseen*:

1. **Trust given, trials sent** — the one whose counsel is sought carries a hidden trust and must advise sincerely (81); the truly fortunate is the one *spared* trials, and the tested-but-patient is given glad tidings too (82); and even love for your children can secretly breed miserliness and cowardice (83).
2. **Keep the heart steady and true** — Allah is beautiful and loves beauty as *gratitude*, not vanity (84); every burst of eagerness is followed by a slump, so aim for the steady middle (85); and a purified heart can read truth from falsehood by its own contentment or unease (86).
3. **The unseen at work** — your provision pursues you as surely as your death (87); Satan moves through you unseen, like blood — so raise your guard, don't surrender (88); wealth is *this* Umma's particular test (89); and the swiftest-answered prayer is the secret one, for an absent brother (90).

The deepest current under all ten is **trust in the unseen — order your life around what you cannot see.** The trust inside a counsel, the decree behind your trials and your provision, the fear hidden in love, the heart's own quiet verdict, the enemy in your bloodstream, the slow tilt of wealth, and the pure power of a hidden prayer — none of these is visible, yet they are what truly shape a believer's days. Chapter 8 taught you to keep the heart's *eyes open* (weigh the reality, not the label); Chapter 9 teaches you to keep the heart's *trust placed rightly* — on Allah, His decree, and the unseen realities He has told you about.

**Yaad rakho:** 10 hadith — magar 3 harkatein, ek tarbiyat: *ghaib par bharosa.* (1) *Amaanat di gayi, aazmaaish bheji gayi* (81, 82, 83). (2) *Dil ko mustaqil aur saccha rakho* (84, 85, 86). (3) *Ghaib ki taaqatein kaam par hain* (87, 88, 89, 90). Sab ke neeche ek dhaaga: **jo nazar nahin aata us par bharosa karo — apni zindagi usi ke gird tarteeb do.** Mashware ki amaanat, fitne aur rizq ke peechhe taqdeer, mohabbat mein chhupa darr, dil ka apna khaamosh faisla, khoon mein daudta dushman, maal ki dheemi kheench, aur ek poshida duʿāʾ ki khaalis taaqat — in mein se koi nazar nahin aata, magar yahi momin ke din asal mein banaate hain. Chapter 8 ne dil ki *aankhein khuli* rakhna sikhaaya; Chapter 9 dil ka *bharosa sahi jagah* rakhna sikhaata hai — Allah, Uski taqdeer, aur un ghaib ki haqeeqaton par jin ki Usne khabar di.

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

Now you understand the chapter. This section is how to *deliver* it to your classmates.

---

## The 5 landmines to watch for

### Landmine 1 · #83 — "so the dīn says children are bad / a burden"

The wording — "children are a cause of miserliness and cowardice" — sounds like a condemnation of children. It is **not**. Children are repeatedly called a blessing and a means of reward. The book frames this as *"two possible negative effects"* — an honest x-ray of how *love of* children, if unguarded, can pull a parent toward hoarding (fear of poverty) and timidity (fear of leaving them orphaned). The target is the *fear*, not the child.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh hadith aulaad ko bura nahin keh rahi — aulaad to ne'mat hai. Kitaab ise 'do mumkin manfi asraat' kehti hai: aulaad ki mohabbat, agar be-lagaam ho, to (1) bukhl — gareebi ke darr se kanjoosi, aur (2) buzdili — marne aur bachchon ko yateem chhodne ke darr se deen ki raah mein peechhe hatna — paida kar sakti hai. Nishaana *darr* hai, bachcha nahin. Ilaaj: yaad rakho ke bachche ka rizq bhi Allah ne likha hai (#87) — to mohabbat ko Allah par bharose ki jagah mat lene do."*

### Landmine 2 · #84 — "so Islam endorses showing off wealth / dressing to impress / vanity"

"Allah loves beauty" is **not** a licence for materialism, extravagance, or arrogance. The book ties it to another ḥadīth: *"Allah loves to see the effects of His blessings upon His servant"* — i.e. beauty as *gratitude made visible*, not display made for people. Beauty pointed *up* (thanking the Giver) is loved; beauty pointed *across* (impressing an audience) slides into riyāʾ (last chapter's #80).

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh maal ki numaaish ya ghuroor ka parwaana nahin. Kitaab ise doosri hadith se jodti hai: 'Allah pasand karta hai ke bande par apni ne'mat ke asaraat dekhe' — yaʿni khoobsoorti = *shukr jo nazar aaye* (saaf kapde, sanwra hulia, achhi tilaawat), na ke logon ko impress karne ki numaaish. Husn agar *upar* (Dene wale ki taraf) ho to ʿibaadat; agar *logon ki taraf* ho to woh riyāʾ (#80) ban jaata hai. Allah khud jameel hai aur shukr-guzaar husn ko pasand karta hai — takabbur ko nahin."*

### Landmine 3 · #85 — "so it's natural to fizzle out — no harm in quitting after a burst"

The ḥadīth names the slump (fatra) as a *law of nature* — but that is a *warning to plan for it*, **not** permission to give up. The book is explicit: gradually diminishing and then abandoning deeds *"is not what the Sharīʿa encourages."* The encouraged path is *moderation* — pick a sustainable load and continue *unabated*, between exaggeration and negligence.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Hadith susti (fatra) ko fitrat ka qanoon kehti hai — magar yeh chhodne ki ijaazat nahin, balke *uske liye tayyaari* ki tambeeh hai. Kitaab saaf kehti hai: dheere dheere kam kar ke chhod dena 'woh nahin jo Sharīʿa pasand karti.' Pasandeeda raasta *mutawassit* hai: itna bojh lo jo nibha sako, aur *musalsal bila-naagha* jaari rakho. Tez shuruaat kaarnaama nahin — susti se aage nikalna kaarnaama hai. (Yaad rakho #44: thoda magar paaband amal Allah ko sab se mehboob.)"*

### Landmine 4 · #88 — "so Satan is in my blood — I can't help sinning, he makes me do it"

This is the heaviest landmine. "Satan runs through me like blood" describes his *speed, closeness, and stealth* — it is a *call to raise your guard*, **not** a removal of your responsibility or a fatalist excuse. The enemy being *inside the walls* means *stay alert*, not *surrender*. The antidotes are dhikr, taʿawwudh, and starving the *nafs ammāra* he rides on.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Khabardaar — yeh bahaana nahin ke 'main majboor hoon, Shaitaan ne karaaya.' Hadith uski tezi, qurbat, aur chhupe-pan ki tambeeh hai — taake tum *pehra barhao*, na ke haath khade kar do. Dushman agar deewaron ke andar hai to insaan zyada chaukanna hota hai, haar nahin maanta. Waswasa aaye — to use *jawab* do: Allah ki yaad (dhikr) darwaza band kar deti hai, taʿawwudh (panaah) maango, aur us nafs-e-ammaara ko bhooka rakho jis par woh sawaar hota hai. Zimmedaari tumhari hai."*

### Landmine 5 · #89 — "so wealth is evil / being poor is holier"

"My Umma's trial is wealth" is a *diagnosis*, **not** a condemnation of wealth itself. Last chapter's #76 already settled it: *wealth is no harm for the God-fearing* — it is neutral. #89 only tells you *where this Umma is most at risk* so you can guard that spot. Don't flip it into "poverty is piety" or "money is sinful."

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh maal ko bura nahin keh rahi — Chapter 8 ki #76 keh chuki: 'jo Allah se dare uske liye maal mein koi harj nahin.' Maal neutral hai. #89 sirf *tashkhees* hai: yeh batati hai ke *is Umma* ki kamzor jagah maal hai — taake hum wahan pehra dein. Ise 'gareebi zyada deendaar hai' ya 'paisa gunaah hai' mat banao. Sabaq: maal halke haath se pakdo, us mein Allah se daro, aur yaad rakho yeh tumhari Umma *ka* khaas imtihaan hai."*

> **Bonus watch-point · #82 — "so a tested person is *not* fortunate / Allah is angry with him"?** No — the book gives the *second half*: *"and the one who is tested but perseveres — glad tidings are for him."* Being spared a trial is good fortune; being tested *and patient* earns glad tidings too. A trial is not a sign of Allah's displeasure. **And #86 — the heart's contentment/unease is a reliable compass only for a *purified* heart** (the book's own qualification) — a sin-caked heart loses the signal; don't tell someone "if it feels fine it must be halal" as a blanket rule.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 9

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum aage dekhenge"* or *"yeh book ke scope se bahar hai"*:

- ❌ **The detailed fiqh of consultation (shūrā), and when a consultant is liable** triggered by #81 — keep it to the *principle*: being asked creates a trust; advise sincerely.
- ❌ **The theology of qadar (decree) — free will vs predestination debates** triggered by #82, #87, #89 — give only the clean point: trials and provision are decreed, so strive calmly and lawfully without panic. Do **not** open the jabr/qadar discussion.
- ❌ **The full fiqh of jihād, or political/military rulings** triggered by #83's "deterred from fighting in the path of Allah" — keep it to: love of children can breed timidity in the deen; the cure is trust in Allah.
- ❌ **The detailed ʿaqīda of Allah's attributes (and how *jamīl* applies to His essence), or rulings on clothing/adornment** triggered by #84 — give the heart of it: beauty Allah loves = gratitude made visible, not vanity.
- ❌ **The detailed ʿilm of waswasa, the nature of jinn, and the science of ruqya** triggered by #88 — give only: Satan is swift and close (a call to guard, not a fatalist excuse); the antidotes are dhikr and taʿawwudh. Don't breed fear or fascination.
- ❌ **Rankings of "which community's fitnah is worst," or economic/political commentary** triggered by #89 — keep it to: wealth is *this* Umma's particular test (neutral in itself, per #76); guard the heart.

**Yaad rakho:** Pre-loading aur tangents madad nahin karte. 10 hadithein hain — har ek apne aap mein kaafi. Aur cheezein mat daalo — khaas taur par #82/#87/#89 par *qadar (taqdeer vs ikhtiyaar)* ki behes, #84 par *sifaat* ki tafseeli behes, aur #88 par *jinn/ruqya* ki tafseelaat mat khol-na. Class ka focus = *yeh 10 hadithein dil mein bait jaayein* aur *ghaib par bharose* ka dhaaga samajh aaye. Bas.
