Chapter 9 — Student Handout

Aḥādīth 81–90 from Part 1 of Zad ut-Taalibeen · trust the unseen

👋 Welcome — what we'll learn today

Today we cover ten short aḥādīth of the Prophet ﷺ that, read together, teach one habit of heart: order your life around what you cannot see. They move through three movements: trust given & trials sent, keep the heart steady & true, and the unseen at work.

Aaj ka sabaq — das choti hadithein jo ek dili aadat sikhaati hain: apni zindagi un cheezon ke gird tarteeb do jo nazar nahin aatein. Teen harkatein: amaanat di gayi, aazmaaish bheji gayi, dil ko mustaqil aur saccha rakho, aur ghaib ki taaqatein kaam par hain.

The 10 fall into three themes (and all continue the book's "Inna" sayings — إِنَّ… "indeed, X is Y…"):

  1. Trust given, trials sent (#81, #82, #83) — being asked for advice is a trust; the fortunate are spared trials (and the tested-but-patient get glad tidings too); and even love of children can secretly breed miserliness and cowardice.
  2. Keep the heart steady & true (#84, #85, #86) — Allah loves beauty as gratitude, not vanity; every burst of eagerness is followed by a slump, so stay steady; and a purified heart reads truth from falsehood.
  3. The unseen at work (#87, #88, #89, #90) — your provision pursues you; Satan moves through you unseen, like blood; wealth is this Umma's test; and the swiftest-answered prayer is the secret one, for an absent brother.

The deep thread beneath all ten: trust the unseen — live by what you cannot see.

Ghaib par bharosa — jo nazar nahin aata usi par bharosa.

Ḥadīth 81 · Advice is a trust

إِنَّ الْمُسْتَشَارَ مُؤْتَمَنٌ

inna l-mustashāra muʾtaman

"The consultant is placed in a position of trust."

"Beshak jis se mashwara liya jaaye woh amaanat-daar hai."

Tirmidhī, Ibn Māja

Being asked for advice places a trust on you. The moment someone turns to you and asks "what should I do?", you owe them sincere, sound counsel — the advice you would give yourself — not a careless half-answer, and never something steered toward your own benefit. To betray that is to betray a trust (amāna).

Ḥadīth 82 · The fortunate are spared the storm

إِنَّ السَّعِيدَ لَمَنْ جُنِّبَ الْفِتَنَ

inna s-saʿīda la-man junniba l-fitan

"The fortunate one is he who is protected from trials."

"Beshak khush-naseeb woh hai jise fitnon se bacha liya jaaye."

Abū Dāwūd

True good fortune is being spared the storm — or being patient in it. We rarely notice the trials we were protected from. And the Prophet ﷺ added (repeating it three times): "the one who is tested but perseveres — glad tidings are for him." So a trial is not a sign of Allah's displeasure — spared or patient, the believer is safe.

Ḥadīth 83 · Even love can bend your character

إِنَّ الْوَلَدَ مَبْخَلَةٌ مَجْبَنَةٌ

inna l-walada mabkhalatun majbanah

"Children are a source of miserliness and cowardice."

"Beshak aulaad bukhl aur buzdili ka sabab (ban sakti) hai."

Ibn Māja, Aḥmad

An honest x-ray, not a condemnation of children. The book names two possible negative effects: love of children can breed (1) miserliness — out of fear of poverty — and (2) cowardice — out of fear of dying and leaving them orphaned, holding a father back from the path of Allah. The target is the fear, not the child. The cure: remember the child's provision is decreed too (Ḥadīth 87).

⚠️ Children are a blessing

This is not "children are bad." It warns that unguarded love can pull you toward hoarding and timidity. Don't love less — just don't let love displace trust in Allah.

Yeh "aulaad buri hai" nahin. Be-lagaam mohabbat bukhl aur buzdili la sakti hai. Kam mat karo — bas mohabbat ko Allah par bharose ki jagah mat lene do.

Ḥadīth 84 · Beauty loved as gratitude

إِنَّ اللهَ جَمِيلٌ يُحِبُّ الْجَمَالَ

inna Llāha jamīlun yuḥibbu l-jamāl

"Allāh is beautiful and loves beauty."

"Beshak Allah khoobsoorat hai aur khoobsoorti ko pasand karta hai."

Muslim

The beauty Allah loves is gratitude made visible. Allah is beautiful in His essence, attributes, and actions, and loves beauty in His creation. Another ḥadīth fixes the meaning: "Allah loves to see the effects of His blessings upon His servant." So clean clothes, a cared-for appearance, beautiful recitation — done as a quiet thank-you — are loved. Beauty aimed up (to the Giver) is worship.

⚠️ Not vanity

This is not a licence for showing off wealth or arrogance. Beauty aimed across — to impress people — slides into ostentation (riyāʾ, Chapter 8 #80).

Yeh numaaish ya ghuroor ka parwaana nahin. Logon ko impress karne wala husn riyāʾ ban jaata hai.

Ḥadīth 85 · Plan for the slump

إِنَّ لِكُلِّ شَيْءٍ شِرَّةً وَلِكُلِّ شِرَّةٍ فَتْرَةً

inna li-kulli shayʾin shirratan, wa li-kulli shirratin fatratan

"For everything there is eagerness, and for every eagerness there is a weakening."

"Beshak har cheez ka ek josh hota hai, aur har josh ke baad ek susti aati hai."

Tirmidhī

The high-energy start isn't the achievement — outlasting the slump is. Every burst of eagerness (shirra) is followed by a slackening (fatra). So don't pile on more than you can sustain in a wave of zeal. The Sharīʿa doesn't praise starting big and then quitting; it praises moderation — a steady, sustainable load, kept up "unabated," between exaggeration and negligence. (Recall Ḥadīth 44: the most beloved deeds are the regular ones, even if few.)

Ḥadīth 86 · The heart is a truth-detector

إِنَّ الصِّدْقَ طُمَأْنِينَةٌ وَإِنَّ الْكَذِبَ رِيبَةٌ

inna ṣ-ṣidqa ṭumaʾnīnatun, wa inna l-kadhiba rībah

"Truthfulness is a source of contentment and falsehood is a source of doubt."

"Beshak sachaai dili sukoon hai, aur jhoot bechaini (shak) hai."

Tirmidhī

Allah has placed an inner sensor in the purified heart. Truth brings a settled calm (ṭumaʾnīna); falsehood leaves a nagging unease (rība) you can't quite silence. The book qualifies this carefully: it works in a believer who has cleaned his heart from the rust of sins. A heart caked in sin loses the signal — so the way to sharpen the sensor is to keep the heart clean. (It is not a blanket "if it feels fine, it must be halal.")

Ḥadīth 87 · Your provision is chasing you

إِنَّ الرِّزْقَ لَيَطْلُبُ الْعَبْدَ كَمَا يَطْلُبُهُ أَجَلُهُ

inna r-rizqa la-yaṭlubu l-ʿabda kamā yaṭlubuhu ajaluh

"Livelihood seeks a person out in the same way that his death does."

"Beshak rizq bande ko aise dhoondta hai jaise uski maut use dhoondti hai."

Abū Nuʿaym (Ḥilya)

Your provision comes for you as certainly as your death does. You don't chase your death — it is coming. The book adds: in fact, livelihood comes before death, because you will not die until you have received every morsel destined for you. This does not mean sit idle — Islam commands lawful effort — it removes the panic: strive calmly and lawfully, since no grasping brings a dirham beyond your decree. (This answers #83's fear of poverty: the child's rizq is decreed too.)

Ḥadīth 88 · The enemy inside the walls

إِنَّ الشَّيْطَانَ يَجْرِي مِنَ الْإِنْسَانِ مَجْرَى الدَّمِ

inna sh-shayṭāna yajrī mina l-insāni majrā d-dam

"Satan runs through a human like blood runs [through the body]."

"Beshak Shaitaan insaan mein aise daudta hai jaise khoon [jism mein] daudta hai."

Bukhārī

Satan is swift, close, and unfelt. His whisper (waswasa) arrives so smoothly — in your own "voice" — that you don't notice a foreign hand on it; and he works through your lower desires (al-nafs al-ammāra). The antidotes are dhikr (remembering Allah closes the door), taʿawwudh (seeking refuge), and starving the desires he rides on.

⚠️ This is not fatalism

"Satan is in my blood" is not an excuse ("I can't help it"). It is a call to raise your guard, not to surrender. The enemy being inside the walls means stay alert — responsibility stays yours.

Yeh bahaana nahin ("main majboor hoon"). Pehra barhaane ki tambeeh hai — haar nahin. Zimmedaari tumhari.

Ḥadīth 89 · Know your community's test

إِنَّ لِكُلِّ أُمَّةٍ فِتْنَةً وَفِتْنَةُ أُمَّتِي الْمَالُ

inna li-kulli ummatin fitnatan, wa fitnatu ummatī l-māl

"For every Umma there is a temptation, and the temptation of my Umma is wealth."

"Beshak har ummat ka ek fitnah hai, aur meri ummat ka fitnah maal hai."

Tirmidhī

Every community has its weak spot; ours is wealth. The Prophet ﷺ named this Umma's particular trial precisely — so we can guard it. Naming the test is half of passing it. Wealth rarely tempts with one big choice; it bends the heart slowly, one small compromise at a time.

⚠️ Wealth is not evil

This is a diagnosis, not a condemnation. Ḥadīth 76 already settled it: there is no harm in wealth for the God-fearing — it is neutral. #89 just names where we are most at risk.

Maal bura nahin (#76). #89 tashkhees hai: is Umma ka khaas imtihaan maal hai. Gareebi-parasti nahin.

Ḥadīth 90 · The secret prayer

إِنَّ أَسْرَعَ الدُّعَاءِ إِجَابَةً دَعْوَةُ غَائِبٍ لِغَائِبٍ

inna asraʿa d-duʿāʾi ijābatan daʿwatu ghāʾibin li-ghāʾib

"The fastest supplication to be accepted is one made by somebody for an absent person."

"Beshak sab se jaldi qubool hone wali duʿāʾ woh hai jo kisi ghaair-haazir ke liye (chupke se) ki jaaye."

Abū Dāwūd, Tirmidhī

The most powerful prayer is the one no-one sees you make. When you pray for an absent brother — in private, with no audience, no thanks coming back, nothing in it for you — the duʿāʾ is pure sincerity (ikhlāṣ), far from any showing-off. That purity is exactly why the book says it rises fastest. It is the perfect opposite of ostentation (riyāʾ, #80) — worship with the audience deliberately removed.

🔑 The chapter in one picture

Ten aḥādīth, three movements, one thread:

  1. Trust given, trials sent (#81, #82, #83) — advice is a trust; the fortunate are spared (and the patient rewarded); love can breed fear & stinginess.
  2. Keep the heart steady & true (#84, #85, #86) — beauty = gratitude, not vanity; plan for the slump, stay steady; the heart reads truth.
  3. The unseen at work (#87, #88, #89, #90) — provision pursues you; the enemy is inside, so guard; wealth is this Umma's test; the secret prayer is strongest.

The thread: trust the unseen — order your life around what you cannot see.

Amaanat/aazmaaish → dil ko mustaqil-o-saccha rakho → ghaib ki taaqatein. Dhaaga: jo nazar nahin aata us par bharosa.

Exercise 5 · Reflection — write a sentence or two each

  1. Recall the last time someone asked your advice (Ḥadīth 81). Did you give them the counsel you'd give yourself, or the easy answer?
  2. Name one trial you were spared that you only noticed later (Ḥadīth 82). How does that change your idea of "good fortune"?
  3. Ḥadīth 87 says provision pursues you. Where in your life is fear of money pushing you toward something — anxiety, stinginess, or a shortcut? How would trust change it?
  4. Ḥadīth 85: name one act of worship you could keep up steadily (small but constant) rather than in big bursts that fade.
  5. Ḥadīth 89 says wealth is this Umma's test. Name one way wealth (yours or others') has recently pulled at your heart.
  6. Ḥadīth 90: pick one absent person and make a sincere, private duʿāʾ for them this week — tell no-one.
