# Part A · Understanding the chapter

These ten aḥādīth (61–70) are still inside the **"Comprehensive Sayings"** run of Part 1 — short, free-standing, pithy. But, as in every chapter we've built, they sit on a quiet spine. Chapter 6 walked the believer through *aim your hunger → honour your ties → guard your heart*, and its hidden thread was **ikhlāṣ** — everything *for Allah*. This chapter opens with a single, sobering word: **you are a shepherd, and you will be questioned** (#61). That one ḥadīth sets the temperature for the whole chapter. The question underneath all ten is: **if everything is going to be asked about — my responsibilities, my place, my time, my words — then how should I live?**

So the spine here is: **answer for your watch → stand together and guard your tongue → keep your heart alive with dhikr.**

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter 7 ka naqsha — *pehle hisaab* (tum jawaab-deh ho: apni zimmedaari, apni jagah, apni maut ka — #61, #62, #64), *phir jamāʿat aur zubaan* (sahi logon ke saath raho, achhi baat karo — #65, #63, #66), *aakhir mein zinda dil* (use dhikr se zinda rakho — #67, #68, #69, #70). Hisaab → jamāʿat aur zubaan → dil ka dhikr. Aur in sab ke neeche ek dhaaga: *jis cheez ka hisaab hoga, usi ko Allah ki yaad se sambhaalo — muḥāsaba se dhikr tak.*

Three natural themes — keep this in your head as the spine of the chapter:

1. **Answer for your watch** (Aḥādīth 61, 62, 64) — every one of you is a *shepherd* over some flock and *will be questioned* about it (#61); the places you choose to stand are weighed — the masjid is most beloved to Allah, the market most disliked (#62); and the watch ends in death, which for a true believer is not terror but a *gift* (#64). This is the believer's *accountability* — and its hopeful end.
2. **Stand together and guard your tongue** (Aḥādīth 65, 63, 66) — Allah's hand (His help and protection) is *with the united body* of believers (#65); choose your company and your speech on a graded ladder — good company over solitude over bad company, good speech over silence over bad speech (#63); and remember that *every word* is recorded against you, *except* enjoining good, forbidding wrong, and the remembrance of Allah (#66). This is the believer's *social and verbal* life.
3. **Keep your heart alive with dhikr** (Aḥādīth 67, 68, 69, 70) — the one who remembers his Lord is *alive*, the one who doesn't is a *dead man walking* (#67); knowledge that is never acted on is a *buried treasure*, useless (#68); the *best* remembrance is *Lā ilāha illa 'Llāh* and the *best* supplication is *Al-ḥamdu li 'Llāh* (#69); and the *first* called to Paradise are those who praised Allah in ease *and* in hardship (#70). This is the believer's *living heart*.

Notice the flow: **accountability (you'll be questioned) → community + speech (stand right, speak well) → a heart kept alive by remembrance.** And the deepest current — the thing that ties #61 (you'll be questioned), #66 (every word counts), #67 (dhikr = life), and #70 (the praisers are called first) into one — is the movement from **muḥāsaba to dhikr**: because all of it is going to be asked about, the believer keeps his heart *awake* with the remembrance of Allah. Where Chapter 6's hidden thread was *ikhlāṣ* (be sincere), this chapter's hidden thread is *muḥāsaba → dhikr* (live awake, remember Him).

**Yaad rakho:** Teeno theme ek dhaage se bandhe hain — *muḥāsaba se dhikr tak*. Sab cheez ka hisaab hoga (#61, #66), to dil ko dhikr se *zinda* rakho (#67), behtareen alfaaz se (#69), har haal mein Allah ki taʿreef karte hue (#70). Pichle chapter ka dhaaga *ikhlāṣ* tha; is chapter ka dhaaga *jaag-te raho aur Allah ko yaad rakho.*

Work through each ḥadīth below. For each, ask yourself: *can I explain this hadith to a classmate in one sentence, in Urdu, without reading the book?* (*Kya main yeh hadith ek classmate ko ek line mein Urdu mein samjha sakti hoon, bina kitaab dekhe?*) If yes — move on. If not — re-read.

> **A note on order:** the book gives these in numeric order (61 → 70). Below — and in all four deliverables — I present them *by theme*, so #64 (death) closes the "watch" group and #63 (solitude/speech) sits with the "community & tongue" group. The number and source travel with each ḥadīth, so nothing is lost; the theme order just makes the chapter *teach* better. Presentation order: **61, 62, 64 · 65, 63, 66 · 67, 68, 69, 70.**

> **A note on the Arabic:** for three of these aḥādīth — **#63, #68, #70** — I have *not* written out the Arabic matn. The book's printed Arabic for them did not extract cleanly, and I am not 100% certain of the exact wording and harakaat from memory. Our rule is firm: *never write a near-correct matn.* So those three are taught from the English meaning + source; if you want the Arabic in class, copy it **exactly from the book**, where it is printed. The other seven (#61, #62, #64, #65, #66, #67, #69) are well-known, verified traditions and their Arabic is given.

## Theme 1 · Answer for your watch

## 61 · أَلَا كُلُّكُمْ رَاعٍ وَكُلُّكُمْ مَسْؤُولٌ عَنْ رَعِيَّتِهِ — "Each of you is a shepherd, and each will be questioned about his flock"

**Full wording:** *Verily each of you is a shepherd, and each of you will be questioned [on the Day of Judgement] regarding his flock.* The ḥadīth continues: *the leader of a people is responsible and will be questioned about his responsibility; a man is responsible for his household and will be questioned about his family; and a woman is responsible for [the sanctity of] her husband's house and will be questioned about that.*
**Source:** Bukhārī, Muslim.

### Authority is a trust, not a trophy

The opening image is a *shepherd* (*rāʿin*) and his *flock* (*raʿiyya*). Every single person — *kullukum*, **all of you** — has some flock: people, affairs, or responsibilities placed under their care. And every single person *will be questioned* about that flock on the Day of Judgement. The book spells out the layers: a ruler over his subjects, a man over his household, a woman over her husband's home. The point is universal — nobody is exempt, from the head of state to the youngest person with even one responsibility. The lesson is a complete reversal of how we usually think about authority: a position over others is not a *privilege to enjoy* but a *trust to answer for* (*amānah*). The bigger your flock, the heavier your questioning.

**Yaad rakho:** Har ek (*kullukum*) ek charwaaha hai, aur har ek se uske *galle* (zimmedaari) ka hisaab hoga. Hukmraan apni riʿaaya ka, mard apne ghar-waalon ka, aurat apne ghar ki hifaazat ka. Koi bhi mustasna nahin. Asal sabaq: kisi par ikhtiyaar koi *inʿaam* nahin jo enjoy karo — yeh ek *amānah* hai jiska jawaab dena hai. Jitna bara galla, utna bara hisaab.

### What "being questioned" should do to you today

Don't read this only as a future scene. It is meant to change *how you carry your responsibilities now*: with diligence and care, not negligence. The book gives concrete examples — a leader must establish justice and protect his people; a man must provide for and oversee his family's welfare; a woman must guard and manage the affairs of her home. The thread for the whole chapter starts here: *because* you'll be questioned, take the watch seriously.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh sirf aage ka manzar nahin — yeh *aaj* tumhara rawayya badalne ke liye hai: ghaflat nahin, ehtimaam. Jis ke bhi tum zimmedaar ho — ghar, kaam, log — unka haq ada karo, kyunki hisaab hoga. Chapter ka dhaaga yahin se shuru: *hisaab hoga, isliye apni nigraani sanjeedgi se nibhaao.*

## 62 · أَحَبُّ الْبِلَادِ إِلَى اللهِ مَسَاجِدُهَا وَأَبْغَضُ الْبِلَادِ إِلَى اللهِ أَسْوَاقُهَا — "The most beloved places to Allah are the masjids; the most disliked are the markets"

**Source:** Muslim.

### Why the masjid is loved and the market disliked

This is not a random ranking of buildings — the book gives the *reason*, and the reason is everything. The **masjid** is the most beloved place to Allah because it is where a person *draws closer to Him* — prayer, recitation, dhikr, sincere gathering. The **market** is the most disliked place because it is where Satan's influences cluster: *avarice, deceit, greed, and heedlessness* (*ghafla*). So the ḥadīth is really about *spiritual atmosphere*: where the remembrance of Allah dominates versus where the forgetfulness of Allah dominates. It is teaching you to *choose where your heart spends its time*.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh imaaraton ki be-wajah daraja-bandi nahin — kitaab *wajah* deti hai. **Masjid** Allah ko sab se mehboob, kyunki wahaan banda Allah ke *qareeb* hota hai (namaz, tilaawat, dhikr). **Bazaar** sab se na-pasand, kyunki wahaan Shaytaan ke asraat jamaa hote hain: laalach, dhoka, hirs, aur *ghaflat*. To asal baat *maahaul* ki hai: jahaan Allah ki yaad chhayi ho, ya jahaan Allah ki bhool chhayi ho. Sabaq: *apne dil ka waqt kahaan guzarta hai, sambhal kar chuno.*

### The landmine — this does NOT condemn trade

Be careful here. The ḥadīth dislikes the market *as a place* because of what tends to dominate there — *not* because earning a lawful living is bad. Trade (*tijāra*) is permitted, even praised in the dīn (recall the honest trader with the prophets, from an earlier chapter). The believer is not told to abandon the market; he is told to *carry the masjid into it* — to keep Allah's remembrance alive even amid buying and selling, so the market's heedlessness never swallows him. The dislike is for the *heedlessness*, not the *commerce*.

**Yaad rakho:** Yahaan ehtiyaat. Hadith bazaar ko *jagah ke taur par* na-pasand karti hai us cheez ki wajah se jo wahaan ghaalib hoti hai — *halal kamai bure hone* ki wajah se nahin. Tijaarat jaayaz hai, balke dīn mein iski taʿreef hai (sacha taajir anbiyā ke saath — pichla chapter). Momin ko bazaar chhodne ko nahin kaha gaya — usse kaha gaya *masjid ko bazaar mein le jaao*: kharo-farokht ke beech bhi Allah ki yaad zinda rakho. Na-pasand *ghaflat* hai, *tijaarat* nahin.

## 64 · تُحْفَةُ الْمُؤْمِنِ الْمَوْتُ — "Death is a gift for a believer"

**Source:** Bayhaqī (*Shuʿab al-Īmān*).

### Reframing the thing we fear most

In four words the Prophet ﷺ overturns the most natural human dread. *Al-mawt* (death) is a *tuḥfa* — a **gift**, a present — *for the believer*. The book's explanation is exact and gentle: for the one who *truly believes*, it is through the *gateway of death* that he finds the *door to eternal contentment and happiness*. To the believer, death is not the end of the good life — it is the *threshold* to it. The world, with all its striving and trials, was the waiting room; death is the door opening onto the meeting with Allah. That is why it closes Theme 1: the "watch" of #61 and the "place" of #62 all run toward this door — and for the believer, the door is a *gift*.

**Yaad rakho:** Chaar lafzon mein Rasool ﷺ insaan ke sab se baray khauf ko ulat dete hain. *Al-mawt* (maut) momin ke liye ek *tuḥfa* — **tohfa**, hadya. Kitaab ka bayan naram aur saaf hai: jo *sach-much* īmān rakhta hai, uske liye *maut ke darwaze* se hi *abadi raahat aur khushi* ka raasta khulta hai. Momin ke liye maut achhi zindagi ka *khaatma* nahin — uski *dehleez* hai. Dunya intezaar-gaah thi; maut Allah se mulaaqaat ka darwaza khol-ti hai. Isi liye Theme 1 yahaan band hota hai: #61 ki nigraani aur #62 ki jagah, sab isi darwaze ki taraf jaati hain — aur momin ke liye yeh darwaza *tohfa* hai.

### The landmine — do NOT turn this into wishing for death

Hold this carefully. "Death is a gift" describes how the believer *views* death — with hope, not dread — it does **not** mean a believer should *seek* or *wish for* death. The Prophet ﷺ explicitly forbade longing for death because of a worldly hardship; he taught us to ask for life as long as life is good for us and death when death is better. So #64 is *reassurance*, not a *summons*: live your watch fully (back to #61), and let death lose its terror — because for the one who believes and prepares, it opens onto something better.

**Yaad rakho:** Ise sambhaal kar pakdo. "Maut tohfa hai" yeh batati hai ke momin maut ko *kaise dekhta hai* — umeed se, khauf se nahin — yeh *nahin* keh rahi ke momin maut *maange* ya *tamanna* kare. Rasool ﷺ ne dunyawi takleef ki wajah se maut ki tamanna se *mana* farmaaya. To #64 *tasalli* hai, *bulaawa* nahin: apni zimmedaari poori nibhaao (#61), aur maut ka khauf jaane do — kyunki jo īmān laaye aur tayyaari kare, uske liye yeh behtar cheez ka darwaza hai.

## Theme 2 · Stand together and guard your tongue

## 65 · يَدُ اللهِ عَلَى الْجَمَاعَةِ — "Allah's hand is with the group"

**Full wording:** *Allah's hand is with the group (jamāʿa).* The narration continues: *whoever becomes apart [from the group] will be set apart in the Hellfire.*
**Source:** Tirmidhī.

> **A note on the Arabic preposition:** the book *prints* the matn with **عَلَى** (*ʿalā*, lit. "upon/over"), while its **English** renders it "Allah's hand is **with** a group." We reproduce the printed Arabic عَلَى exactly and keep the book's English "with" — the same pattern as #59 (where the book printed الظَّمَأُ "thirst" but its English said "hunger"). The transliteration is therefore *yadu-Llāhi **ʿalā** l-jamāʿa*.

### The help of Allah is with the united body

"Allah's hand" — *yadu-Llāh* — is a phrase we handle the way the early Muslims did: we affirm it as it came and *leave its exact reality to Allah*; the scholars say it here *alludes to* Allah's **assistance, guidance, mercy, and protection**. And that help is *with the jamāʿa* — the united body of the believers, gathered on the truth. The lesson is communal: the believer who stays *within* the body of the believers is under Allah's aid and protection; strength, guidance, and mercy come *to the group*. This is why the chapter places it at the head of Theme 2 — after being told you're accountable (#61) and where to stand (#62), you're told *whom to stand with*.

**Yaad rakho:** "Allah ka haath" (*yadu-Llāh*) — hum ise waise hi maante hain jaise salaf ne: jaisa aaya waisa maan liya, uski *haqeeqat Allah par chhod di*; ʿulamā kehte hain yahaan yeh Allah ki *madad, hidaayat, rehmat, aur hifaazat* ki taraf ishaara hai. Aur yeh madad *jamāʿa* ke saath hai — momineen ka muttahid jismaani guroh, haq par jamaa. Sabaq: jo momineen ke jism ke *andar* rahe, woh Allah ki madad aur hifaazat mein hai. Isi liye Theme 2 yahaan se shuru: hisaab (#61) aur jagah (#62) ke baad — *kis ke saath khade ho*.

### The warning — breaking away is dangerous

The continuation is the sharp edge: *whoever becomes apart will be set apart in the Hellfire.* Splitting off — through a *deviant belief, statement, or action* — pulls a person out from under that protection. Teach this as the book frames it: a warning against *deviation* and breaking the unity of the believers on truth, **not** a club to silence every legitimate difference of opinion (the dīn has always had respectful scholarly difference within the jamāʿa). The danger is in *leaving the truth and the body*, not in disagreeing about a detail.

**Yaad rakho:** Aakhri hissa tez kinaara hai: *jo alag ho gaya, woh Jahannam mein alag kar diya jaayega.* Alag hona — *galat ʿaqeedah, baat, ya ʿamal* se — bande ko us hifaazat se kheench leta hai. Ise waise sikhao jaise kitaab kehti hai: yeh *gumraahi* aur momineen ki haq par wahdat torne ke khilaaf tambeeh hai — har jaayaz ikhtilaaf-e-raaye ko dabaane ka hathiyaar *nahin* (dīn mein hamesha jamāʿa ke andar baa-adab ʿilmi ikhtilaaf raha hai). Khatra *haq aur jamāʿa chhodne* mein hai, kisi tafseel par ikhtilaaf mein nahin.

## 63 · Solitude vs company; good speech vs silence

**Full wording (English):** *Solitude is better than being in bad company, and being in good company is better than solitude. Saying what is good is better than silence, and silence is better than saying what is bad.*
**Source:** Mustadrak, Bayhaqī (*Shuʿab al-Īmān*).

> **Arabic matn omitted on purpose.** I am not 100% certain of the exact Arabic wording and harakaat of this narration, and the book's printed Arabic did not extract cleanly. Per our rule, I teach it here from the English + source. To display the Arabic in class, copy it exactly from the book.

### Two ladders for choosing company and speech

This ḥadīth hands you two small ladders, and they pair beautifully with #65. The first is about **company**:

- bad company (worst)
- *solitude* (better than bad company)
- **good company** (best)

The second is about **speech**:

- saying something bad (worst)
- *silence* (better than saying bad)
- **saying something good** (best)

The genius is in the *middle* rung each time. Solitude and silence are not the *ideal* — they are the *safe fallback* when the alternative is harmful. If your only options are bad company or being alone, choose alone. If your only options are harmful speech or saying nothing, say nothing. But the *top* of each ladder is *active good*: good companions, and good words. This is exactly why #65 (be with the jamāʿa) sits right before it — but #63 refines it: *good* company specifically.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh hadith do choti seerhiyaan deti hai, aur #65 ke saath khoob jurti hai. Pehli **sohbat** ki: buri sohbat (sab se buri) < *tanhaai* (buri sohbat se behtar) < **achhi sohbat** (sab se behtar). Doosri **baat** ki: buri baat (sab se buri) < *khaamoshi* (buri baat se behtar) < **achhi baat** (sab se behtar). Kamaal *darmiyaani* darje mein hai: tanhaai aur khaamoshi *aadarsh* nahin — yeh *mehfooz panaah* hain jab doosra raasta nuqsaan-deh ho. Magar har seerhi ka *sab se ooncha* darja *amal wali bhalaai* hai: achhe saathi, achhe alfaaz.

## 66 · كُلُّ كَلَامِ ابْنِ آدَمَ عَلَيْهِ لَا لَهُ — "Every word counts against you, not for you — except…"

**Full wording:** *Every word uttered by a child of Adam will be held against him and not in favour of him, except enjoining good, forbidding wrong, and the remembrance of Allah.*
**Source:** Tirmidhī, Ibn Māja.

### Speech defaults to liability — three exceptions turn it to profit

This is one of the most arresting statements about the tongue in the whole tradition. The Prophet ﷺ sets the *default* setting of human speech to **against you** — *ʿalayhi lā lahu*. Then he names the three categories that flip a word from liability to *asset*:

1. **enjoining good** (*amr bi-l-maʿrūf*)
2. **forbidding wrong** (*nahy ʿani-l-munkar*)
3. **the remembrance of Allah** (*dhikru-Llāh*) — and the book expands this to include glorifying Allah, praising Him, supplicating (duʿāʾ), reciting the Qurʾān, and sending blessings (ṣalawāt) on the Prophet ﷺ.

The book is sobering about the rest: *gossip and idle talk harden the heart and push it further from Allah's mercy.* The person who fills his day with idle chatter is *deprived* of the reward he could have earned had he spent that same breath on good. So the ḥadīth doesn't tell you to go silent (recall #63 — good speech is the *top* rung) — it tells you to make your speech *count*.

**Yaad rakho:** Zubaan ke baare mein sab se jhinjhodne wali baat. Rasool ﷺ insaan ki baat ki *default* setting *tumhare khilaaf* rakhte hain — *ʿalayhi lā lahu*. Phir teen cheezein jo baat ko nuqsaan se *nafa* mein badal deti hain: (1) **amr bi-l-maʿrūf** (neki ka hukm), (2) **nahy ʿani-l-munkar** (burai se rokna), (3) **dhikru-Llāh** (Allah ki yaad — aur kitaab isme tasbeeh, taʿreef, duʿā, tilaawat, aur durood bhi shaamil karti hai). Baaqi ke baare mein kitaab keh-ti hai: *gheebat aur faaltu baatein dil ko sakht karti hain aur Allah ki rehmat se door.* Faaltu baat karne wala us *ajr se mehroom* hota hai jo wahi saans neki par kharch karne se milta. To hadith khaamosh hone ko nahin keh rahi (#63 — achhi baat *sab se ooncha* darja) — yeh keh rahi hai apni baat ko *qeemti* banao.

### The bridge into the next theme

Look where the ḥadīth lands: the third redeeming category is *dhikr* — the remembrance of Allah. That is the exact hinge into Theme 3. The chapter has just told you most speech is a liability *except* remembrance — and now the next four aḥādīth open up *what remembrance does* and *which remembrance is best*. #66 is the doorway between the tongue and the living heart.

**Yaad rakho:** Dekho hadith kahaan utarti hai: teesri nafa-baksh cheez *dhikr* hai — Allah ki yaad. Yahi Theme 3 ka *darwaza* hai. Abhi kaha gaya ke zyada-tar baat nuqsaan hai *siwaaye yaad ke* — aur agli chaar hadithein batati hain *yaad kya karti hai* aur *kaunsi yaad sab se behtar hai*. #66 zubaan aur zinda dil ke beech ka darwaza hai.

## Theme 3 · Keep your heart alive with dhikr

## 67 · مَثَلُ الَّذِي يَذْكُرُ رَبَّهُ وَالَّذِي لَا يَذْكُرُ رَبَّهُ مَثَلُ الْحَيِّ وَالْمَيِّتِ — "The one who remembers his Lord and the one who does not are like the living and the dead"

**Source:** Bukhārī, Muslim.

### Remembrance is the life of the heart

This is the central image of Theme 3, and it is stark. The difference between a person who *remembers his Lord* and one who *does not* is the difference between **a living person and a corpse**. The book is direct: *remembrance of Allah is nourishment for the heart; without it the heart remains spiritually empty.* A heart without dhikr is not merely *under-performing* — in this likeness it is *dead*. The body may walk, eat, talk, trade — but the heart inside it has gone cold. This is why scholars wrote whole books on dhikr: it is not a side-practice, it is the *pulse* of the spiritual life.

**Yaad rakho:** Theme 3 ki markazi tasveer, aur bohot tez. Jo apne Rabb ko *yaad* karta hai aur jo *nahin* karta — in dono mein utna farq hai jitna **zinda insaan aur murde** mein. Kitaab seedhi baat keh-ti hai: *Allah ki yaad dil ki ghiza hai; iske baghair dil rooh-aani taur par khaali reh jaata hai.* Dhikr ke baghair dil sirf *kamzor* nahin — is misaal mein woh *murda* hai. Jism chalta-phirta, khaata, baat karta, tijaarat karta ho — magar andar ka dil thanda ho chuka. Isi liye ʿulamā ne dhikr par poori kitaabein likhin: yeh koi side-practice nahin, yeh rooh-aani zindagi ki *dhadkan* hai.

## 68 · The likeness of unused knowledge is a treasure from which nothing is spent

**Full wording (English):** *The likeness of knowledge from which no benefit is derived is that of a treasure from which no portion is spent in the path of Allah.*
**Source:** Aḥmad, Dārimī.

> **Arabic matn omitted on purpose.** I am not 100% certain of the exact Arabic wording, and the book's printed Arabic did not extract cleanly. Taught here from the English + source; copy the Arabic from the book if needed in class.

### Knowledge, like dhikr, must be *activated* or it is dead weight

This ḥadīth pairs with #67 in a way that is easy to miss but worth showing your class. #67 said a heart *without remembrance* is dead. #68 says *knowledge without benefit* is a buried treasure — *kanz* — hoarded and never spent in Allah's path. Imagine a chest of gold sealed in the ground: its owner is no richer for it; it feeds no one, helps no one, and on the Day of Judgement it becomes a *burden*, not a blessing. Knowledge that you learn but never *act on, teach, or live by* is exactly that chest. The point connects the whole theme: dhikr *activates* the heart (#67); action *activates* knowledge (#68). A thing of value left *unused* is, spiritually, a thing *wasted* — and worse, a thing you'll be questioned about (back to #61).

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh hadith #67 ke saath aise jurti hai jo aasaani se chhoot jaata hai — class ko zaroor dikhao. #67 ne kaha *yaad ke baghair* dil murda. #68 keh-ti hai *be-faayda ʿilm* ek dafan khazaana hai — *kanz* — jamaa kiya magar Allah ke raaste mein kabhi kharch nahin hua. Sone ka sanduuq zameen mein band: maalik us se amīr nahin hua; kisi ka pet nahin bhara, kisi ki madad nahin hui, aur Qayaamat ke din yeh *bojh* ban jaata hai, niʿmat nahin. Jo ʿilm seekha magar *amal, taʿleem, ya zindagi* mein laaya nahin — woh wahi sanduuq hai. Poora theme jurta hai: dhikr dil ko *zinda* karta hai (#67); ʿamal ʿilm ko *zinda* karta hai (#68). Qeemti cheez *be-istemaal* — rooh-aani taur par *zaaya*, aur uska *hisaab* bhi hoga (#61).

---

## 69 · أَفْضَلُ الذِّكْرِ لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا اللهُ وَأَفْضَلُ الدُّعَاءِ الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ — "The best remembrance is *Lā ilāha illa 'Llāh*, and the best supplication is *Al-ḥamdu li 'Llāh*"

**Source:** Tirmidhī, Ibn Māja.

### The best words to fill a living heart with

#67 told you the heart must be kept alive by remembrance; this ḥadīth tells you *which* remembrance is the very best. Two crowns:

1. **The best dhikr: *Lā ilāha illa 'Llāh*** ("there is no deity save Allah"). The book quotes ʿAllāma Ṭībī beautifully: this phrase has the power to *purify the heart from evil*. With *Lā ilāha* a person *renounces all false deities* — every idol of the hand and of the heart; with *illa 'Llāh* he *affirms the oneness of Allah alone*. The remembrance then penetrates the heart, and the believer is *infused with the sweetness of faith*. It is the kalima of tawḥīd itself — the foundation of everything.
2. **The best supplication: *Al-ḥamdu li 'Llāh*** ("all praise belongs to Allah"). Why call praise the best *supplication*? The book's reasoning is elegant: praising Allah for His favours is *indirectly asking Him for more* of His bounty — and it is, at heart, *gratitude* (*shukr*). And Allah Himself ties gratitude to increase: *"If you are grateful, I will surely add more unto you"* (Qurʾān 14:7). So *al-ḥamdu li-Llāh* is at once dhikr, duʿāʾ, and shukr — three acts in three words.

**Yaad rakho:** #67 ne kaha dil ko yaad se zinda rakho; yeh hadith batati hai *kaunsi* yaad sab se behtar. Do taaj: (1) **Behtareen dhikr: *Lā ilāha illa 'Llāh*** — ʿAllāma Ṭībī ke mutaabiq yeh kalima dil ko *burai se paak* karta hai: *Lā ilāha* se banda *har jhoote maʿbood ka inkaar* karta hai (haath ke aur dil ke sab buton ka), *illa 'Llāh* se *akele Allah ki wahdaaniyat ka iqraar*. Yaad dil mein utar-ti hai aur *īmān ki mithaas* bhar jaati hai. (2) **Behtareen duʿā: *Al-ḥamdu li 'Llāh*** — taʿreef ko behtareen *duʿā* kyun kaha? Kyunki Allah ki niʿmaton par taʿreef *gher-seedhe taur par mazeed maangna* hai, aur asal mein yeh *shukr* hai; aur Allah shukr ko izaafe se baandhta hai: *"agar shukr karoge to main zaroor zyada doonga"* (14:7). To *al-ḥamdu li-Llāh* ek saath dhikr, duʿā, aur shukr hai — teen ʿamal teen lafzon mein.

---

## 68→70 the closing crown · 70 · The first called to Paradise are those who praised Allah in ease and hardship

**Full wording (English):** *The first to be called to Paradise on the Day of Judgement will be those who praised Allah during times of prosperity and adversity.*
**Source:** Bayhaqī (*Shuʿab al-Īmān*).

> **Arabic matn omitted on purpose.** I am not 100% certain of the exact Arabic wording, and the book's printed Arabic did not extract cleanly. Taught here from the English + source; copy the Arabic from the book if needed in class.

### The perfect close — praise in *both* weathers

The chapter ends on a reward so beautiful it crowns everything before it. The *first* people *called to Paradise* on the Day of Judgement are *al-ḥammādūn* — those who **praised Allah in prosperity *and* in adversity**. Notice the two weathers. Praising Allah when life is good is easy; praising Him when life is hard is the mark of a heart truly anchored in Him. The one whose *al-ḥamdu li-Llāh* (from #69) keeps flowing *whether the news is good or bad* — that is the believer called first.

See how it ties the whole chapter together: you'll be questioned for everything (#61), most words are a liability except remembrance (#66), the remembering heart is the living one (#67), and the best of that remembrance is praise (#69) — so the person who keeps praising *in all conditions* (#70) is, fittingly, **the first summoned to the Garden**. The chapter that opened with *accountability* (you will be questioned) closes with the *happiest possible answer* to that questioning: called first to Paradise. *Muḥāsaba* flowers into *dhikr*, and dhikr is crowned here.

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter ek aise inʿaam par band hota hai jo sab pichli baaton ka taaj hai. Qayaamat ke din *sab se pehle Jannat ki taraf bulaaye jaane wale* woh hain jo *khushhaali aur tangi — dono* mein Allah ki taʿreef karte rahe (*al-ḥammādūn*). Do mausamon par ghaur karo: achhe waqt mein taʿreef aasaan; mushkil mein taʿreef us dil ki nishaani hai jo sach-much Allah par jama hua. Jiska *al-ḥamdu li-Llāh* (#69) *khabar achhi ho ya buri* bahta rahe — wahi pehle bulaaya jaata hai. Aur dekho poora chapter kaise jurta hai: har cheez ka hisaab (#61), zyada-tar baat nuqsaan siwaaye yaad ke (#66), yaad wala dil zinda (#67), aur us yaad ka behtareen taʿreef (#69) — to jo *har haal mein* taʿreef karta rahe (#70), wahi *sab se pehle Jannat* bulaaya jaata hai. Jo chapter *hisaab* se khula, woh us hisaab ke *sab se khush-naseeb jawaab* par band hota hai: pehle Jannat ki daʿwat. *Muḥāsaba* se *dhikr* phoot-ta hai, aur dhikr ka taaj yahaan hai.

## The big picture — what these 10 aḥādīth are silently building

Stand back. The chapter trains the believer in **three movements**:

1. **Answer for your watch** — each of you is a shepherd who will be questioned (#61); the places you choose are weighed — love the masjid, beware the heedlessness of the market (#62); and the watch ends in death, which for the believer is a *gift*, not a terror (#64).
2. **Stand together and guard your tongue** — Allah's help is with the united body of believers, so don't break away (#65); choose good company over solitude over bad company, and good speech over silence over bad speech (#63); and know that every word is a liability *except* enjoining good, forbidding wrong, and remembrance (#66).
3. **Keep your heart alive with dhikr** — the remembering heart is *alive* and the heedless one *dead* (#67); knowledge unused is a buried treasure (#68); the best remembrance is *Lā ilāha illa 'Llāh* and the best supplication *Al-ḥamdu li 'Llāh* (#69); and the praisers in *every* condition are called *first* to Paradise (#70).

The deepest current under all ten is the movement from **muḥāsaba to dhikr** — *because you'll be questioned, keep your heart awake with the remembrance of Allah*. Chapter 6 handed you the cure for love of the world (a sincere heart); Chapter 7 hands you the way to keep that heart *alive and accountable*: live as one who will be questioned, and never let the remembrance of Allah die in you.

**Yaad rakho:** 10 hadith — magar 3 harkatein, ek tarbiyat. *Hisaab do* (#61, #62, #64) → *jamāʿat aur zubaan sambhaalo* (#65, #63, #66) → *dil ko dhikr se zinda rakho* (#67, #68, #69, #70). Sab ke neeche ek dhaaga: *muḥāsaba se dhikr tak — hisaab hoga, isliye dil ko Allah ki yaad se jaag-ta rakho.* Chapter 6 ne dunya ki mohabbat ka ilaaj diya (saaf dil); Chapter 7 us dil ko *zinda aur jawaab-deh* rakhne ka tareeqa deta hai. Class ko aakhir mein yeh ek-tasveer dikhao.

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

Now you understand the chapter. This section is how to *deliver* it to your classmates.

---

## The 5 landmines to watch for

### Landmine 1 · #61 — "this is only about rulers / leaders"

Classmates may hear "shepherd" and "leader of a people" and assume the ḥadīth is about kings and presidents — *not them*. The whole force of the ḥadīth is the word *kullukum* — **all of you**. Everyone has a flock.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh hadith sirf baadshaahon ke liye nahin. Lafz dekho — 'kullukum', tum sab. Har ek ka koi na koi galla hai: ghar ke log, bachay, kaam, koi zimmedaari — chahe ek hi cheez ho. Aur har ek se uska hisaab hoga. Ikhtiyaar koi inʿaam nahin jo maze lo — yeh ek amānah hai jiska jawaab dena hai. To jo bhi tumhare zimme hai, uska haq aaj se ada karo."*

### Landmine 2 · #62 — "so trade is evil / the market is haram"

The ḥadīth dislikes the market *as a place* because of the heedlessness that dominates there — *not* because lawful earning is sinful. Classmates in business may feel condemned, or may wrongly conclude commerce is blameworthy.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh halal kamai ko bura nahin keh rahi. Tijaarat jaayaz hai, balke dīn mein iski taʿreef hai (yaad karo sacha taajir anbiyā ke saath). Bazaar ko *jagah ke taur par* na-pasand kaha gaya us *ghaflat, laalach, aur dhoke* ki wajah se jo wahaan ghaalib hota hai — masjid mehboob kyunki wahaan Allah ki yaad ghaalib. Sabaq: bazaar chhodo mat — *masjid ko bazaar mein le jaao*: kaarobaar ke beech bhi Allah ki yaad zinda rakho."*

### Landmine 3 · #64 — "death is a gift, so we should want to die"

"Death is a gift for a believer" is about *how the believer views* death — with hope, not dread — not a call to *seek* or *wish for* it. The Prophet ﷺ forbade longing for death over worldly hardship. Smart or struggling classmates may misread this.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"'Maut tohfa hai' ka matlab yeh hai ke momin maut ko *kaise dekhta hai* — umeed se, dehshat se nahin. Yeh *nahin* keh rahi ke maut maango ya tamanna karo. Rasool ﷺ ne dunyawi takleef ki wajah se maut ki tamanna se mana farmaaya. Yeh tasalli hai: apni zindagi aur zimmedaari poori nibhaao, aur maut ka khauf jaane do — kyunki jo īmān laaye aur tayyaari kare, uske liye yeh behtar cheez ka darwaza hai."*

### Landmine 4 · #65 — "Allah's hand with the group" = the majority is always right / silence all disagreement

Two errors hide here: literalising "Allah's hand", and weaponising "jamāʿa" to mean *any crowd* or to crush every difference of opinion. Teach the early-Muslim posture on the attribute, and frame the warning as against *deviation*, not respectful difference.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Do baatein saaf karo. Pehli: 'Allah ka haath' — hum ise waise maante hain jaise salaf ne, haqeeqat Allah par chhod kar; yahaan yeh Allah ki *madad aur hifaazat* ki taraf ishaara hai. Doosri: 'jamāʿa' ka matlab har bheed ya mehez aksariyat nahin — yeh momineen ka *haq par muttahid* jism hai. Tambeeh *gumraahi aur wahdat torne* ke khilaaf hai — har jaayaz ʿilmi ikhtilaaf ko dabaane ka hathiyaar nahin. Dīn mein hamesha baa-adab ikhtilaaf raha hai."*

### Landmine 5 · #66 — "so almost all talking is sin / I should stay silent"

The ḥadīth sets speech's *default* to liability and names three redeeming categories — but it does **not** forbid permissible, beneficial, or kind speech, and #63 just told us *good speech is the top rung* (better than silence). The aim is *deliberate* speech, not a vow of silence.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh hadith har baat ko gunaah nahin keh rahi, na yeh ke chup-chaap baith jaao. Yaad karo #63 — achhi baat khaamoshi se *behtar* hai (sab se ooncha darja). #66 keh rahi hai: zyada-tar *faaltu aur nuqsaan-deh* baat tumhare khilaaf jaati hai, magar teen cheezein use nafa mein badal deti hain — neki ka hukm, burai se rokna, aur Allah ki yaad (jisme tilaawat, durood, duʿā sab shaamil). To zubaan band mat karo — use *qeemti* banao. Har saans ya to khilaaf ya haq mein — usse haq mein lagaao."*

> **Bonus watch-point · #68 — "unused knowledge is a treasure" is praise of hoarding?** No — the *opposite*. A buried treasure is *useless and a burden*. Knowledge is only alive when *acted on, taught, and lived*. Pair it with #67: dhikr activates the heart, action activates knowledge.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 7

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum aage dekhenge"* or *"yeh book ke scope se bahar hai"*:

- ❌ **The full fiqh of governance and the rights/duties of rulers and subjects** triggered by #61 — the conditions of just rule, obedience to authority, etc. Teach the *principle*: everyone has a flock and will be questioned.
- ❌ **The detailed fiqh of markets, trade contracts, and what makes earning ḥalāl/ḥarām** triggered by #62 — keep it to the *spiritual atmosphere* point: love the place of remembrance, beware the place of heedlessness, carry the masjid into the market.
- ❌ **The theology of Allah's attributes** (the *yad*, the methodology of the salaf vs later schools, taʾwīl debates) triggered by #65 — affirm the attribute as the early Muslims did, say it alludes to His help, and *leave its reality to Allah*. Do not open the kalām debate.
- ❌ **The full ʿaqīda of who counts as "the saved group" and the rulings on takfīr / sects** triggered by #65's warning — teach the simple point: stay with the believers on truth, beware breaking away through deviation; this is *not* a tool for declaring people out of Islam.
- ❌ **The detailed ādāb and fiqh of the tongue** (every category of forbidden speech, the science of gossip and slander) triggered by #66 — keep it to the clean principle: make your speech count; the three redeeming categories.
- ❌ **The science and rulings of dhikr** (audible vs silent, gatherings of dhikr, fixed counts, Sufi practices) triggered by #67 and #69 — teach the *heart* of it: remembrance is the life of the heart; the best words are the kalima and al-ḥamd. Keep it simple and universal.

**Yaad rakho:** Pre-loading aur tangents madad nahin karte. 10 hadithein hain — har ek apne aap mein kaafi hai. Aur cheezein mat daalo — khaas taur par #65 par ʿaqeedah/kalām ki behes mat khol-na. Class ka focus = *yeh 10 hadithein dil mein bait jaayein* — khaas taur par #61 ka hisaab aur #67–70 ka zinda dil. Bas.

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Did they catch that #61 includes *everyone* — that they too have a flock to answer for? *(Kullukum — har ek ka galla?)*
2. Are they reading #62 as "trade is evil", or as a warning about heedlessness vs remembrance? *(Tijaarat buri — ya ghaflat vs yaad?)*
3. Did they mishear #64 as "we should want to die"? *(Maut ki tamanna — ya umeed se dekhna?)*
4. Do they hold #65's two cautions — the attribute, and "jamāʿa" as the body on truth (not crushing all difference)? *(Do ehtiyaat yaad?)*
5. Are they reading #66 as "all talk is sin / stay silent", or as making speech count? *(Sab baat gunaah — ya qeemti banao?)*
6. Did they grasp #67–#68 together — heart kept alive by dhikr, knowledge kept alive by action? *(Dil dhikr se, ʿilm ʿamal se zinda?)*
7. Can they say *why* al-ḥamd is the "best duʿā" (#69) — praise = gratitude = a request for more (14:7)? *(Taʿreef = shukr = mazeed ki duʿā?)*
8. Can they state the arc — *hisaab → jamāʿat/zubaan → dhikr* — and the **muḥāsaba → dhikr** thread, ending on #70? *(Chapter ka safar aur asal dhaaga yaad hai?)*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh 8 points tumhe batayenge kahaan revision chahiye. Agar 3+ points par cohort phans rahi hai, agle session se pehle yeh hadithein dohrao — khaas taur par #65 (do ehtiyaat) aur #66 (zubaan band nahin, qeemti banao), kyunki inhi do ko ghalat samajhne par poora chapter ka rukh bigad jaata hai.
