Chapter 7 — Student Handout

Aḥādīth 61–70 from Part 1 of Zad ut-Taalibeen · answer for your watch, stand together, keep your heart alive

👋 Welcome — what we'll learn today

Today we cover ten short aḥādīth of the Prophet ﷺ that, read together, answer one question: if everything I do is going to be asked about, how should I live? They move through three movements: answer for your watch (responsibility, place, death), stand together and guard your tongue (community and speech), and keep your heart alive (the remembrance of Allah).

Aaj ka sabaq — das choti hadithein jo ek sawaal ka jawaab deti hain: agar har cheez ka hisaab hoga, to main kaise jiyoon? Teen harkatein: hisaab (zimmedaari, jagah, maut), jamāʿat aur zubaan (sahi logon ke saath, achhi baat), dil ka dhikr (use Allah ki yaad se zinda rakho).

The 10 fall into three themes:

  1. Answer for your watch (#61, #62, #64) — each of you is a shepherd who will be questioned; the masjid is most beloved to Allah and the market most disliked; and death is a gift for the believer.
  2. Stand together & guard your tongue (#65, #63, #66) — Allah's help is with the united body of believers; choose good company over solitude over bad, and good speech over silence over bad; and every word counts against you except enjoining good, forbidding wrong, and remembrance.
  3. Keep your heart alive (#67, #68, #69, #70) — the remembering heart is alive and the heedless one dead; unused knowledge is a buried treasure; the best remembrance is Lā ilāha illa 'Llāh and the best supplication Al-ḥamdu li 'Llāh; and the praisers in ease and hardship are called first to Paradise.

The deep thread beneath all ten: muḥāsaba → dhikr — because you'll be questioned, keep your heart alive with the remembrance of Allah.

(We take the aḥādīth by theme, so #64 closes the "watch" group and #63 sits with the "community & tongue" group. The number and source travel with each one. For #63, #68, #70 the Arabic is read from the book itself.)

Ḥadīth 61 · Each of you is a shepherd

أَلَا كُلُّكُمْ رَاعٍ وَكُلُّكُمْ مَسْؤُولٌ عَنْ رَعِيَّتِهِ

kullukum rāʿin wa kullukum masʾūlun ʿan raʿiyyatih

"Each of you is a shepherd, and each of you will be questioned [on the Day of Judgement] regarding his flock." (The leader over his people, the man over his household, the woman over her husband's home — each responsible, each questioned.)

"Tum mein se har ek charwaaha hai, aur har ek se uske galle ke baare mein poocha jaayega."

Bukhārī, Muslim

Authority is a trust, not a trophy. Everyone — kullukum, all of you — has some flock: people, affairs, responsibilities placed in your care. And everyone will be questioned about it. A position over others is not a prize to enjoy; it is a trust (amānah) to answer for. The bigger your flock, the heavier the questioning.

⚠️ Not just for rulers

The key word is kullukum — everyone. A parent, an elder sibling, anyone with even one responsibility is a shepherd who will be questioned.

"Kullukum" — har ek. Sirf hukmraan nahin; ghar, bachay, kaam — jo bhi tumhare zimme hai.

Ḥadīth 62 · The masjids are most beloved; the markets most disliked

أَحَبُّ الْبِلَادِ إِلَى اللهِ مَسَاجِدُهَا وَأَبْغَضُ الْبِلَادِ إِلَى اللهِ أَسْوَاقُهَا

aḥabbu l-bilādi ilā Llāhi masājiduhā, wa abghaḍu l-bilādi ilā Llāhi aswāquhā

"The most beloved of places in the sight of Allah are the masjids, and the most disliked of places in the sight of Allah are the markets."

"Allah ke nazdeek sab se mehboob jagahein masjidein hain, aur sab se na-pasand bazaar hain."

Muslim

It's about atmosphere. The masjid is beloved because it is where a person draws near to Allah. The market is disliked because it is where Satan's influences cluster — greed, deceit, and heedlessness. The ḥadīth teaches you to be mindful of where your heart spends its time.

⚠️ This does NOT condemn trade

Lawful trade is permitted and even praised. The dislike is for the heedlessness of the market, not commerce itself. The believer's job is to carry the masjid into the market — keep Allah's remembrance alive even while buying and selling.

Tijaarat jaayaz hai. Na-pasand ghaflat hai, kaarobaar nahin. Bazaar mein bhi Allah ko yaad rakho.

Ḥadīth 64 · Death is a gift for the believer

تُحْفَةُ الْمُؤْمِنِ الْمَوْتُ

tuḥfatu l-muʾmini l-mawt

"Death is a gift for a believer."

"Maut momin ke liye ek tohfa hai."

Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān)

The thing we fear, reframed. For the one who truly believes, the gateway of death opens onto the door of eternal contentment. The world was the waiting room; death opens onto meeting Allah. So the believer faces death with hope, not dread.

⚠️ Don't wish for death

This is about how the believer views death — not a call to seek it. The Prophet ﷺ forbade longing for death over worldly hardship. It is reassurance, not a summons: live your watch fully, and let death lose its terror.

Yeh batati hai momin maut ko kaise dekhe — umeed se. Maut maango mat; Rasool ﷺ ne is se mana farmaaya.

Ḥadīth 65 · Allah's hand is with the group

يَدُ اللهِ عَلَى الْجَمَاعَةِ

yadu Llāhi ʿalā l-jamāʿa

"Allah's hand is with the group (jamāʿa)." (It continues: "and whoever becomes apart will be set apart in the Hellfire.")

"Allah ka haath jamāʿa ke saath hai." (Aage: "jo alag ho gaya, woh Jahannam mein alag kar diya jaayega.")

Tirmidhī

Note on the Arabic: the book prints the preposition عَلَى (ʿalā, "upon/over") and translates it into English as "with." We keep the printed Arabic عَلَى and the book's English "with." (Kitaab ki Arabic ʿalā, English "with" — dono asal.)

Stay with the body. "Allah's hand" — which we affirm as the early Muslims did, leaving its reality to Allah — alludes to His help, guidance, mercy, and protection. That help is with the jamāʿa: the believers united on the truth. Stay within the body, and you are under Allah's aid.

⚠️ Two cautions

(1) "Jamāʿa" means the body united on truth — not any crowd or mere majority. (2) The warning is against deviation and breaking unity — it is not a tool to silence every respectful difference of opinion (the dīn has always had scholarly difference within the jamāʿa).

Jamāʿa = haq par muttahid guroh, mehez aksariyat nahin. Tambeeh gumraahi ke khilaaf hai, har ikhtilaaf-e-raaye dabaane ke liye nahin.

Ḥadīth 63 · Solitude & company; speech & silence

"Solitude is better than being in bad company, and being in good company is better than solitude. Saying what is good is better than silence, and silence is better than saying what is bad."

"Tanhaai buri sohbat se behtar hai, aur achhi sohbat tanhaai se behtar. Achhi baat khaamoshi se behtar, aur khaamoshi buri baat se behtar."

Mustadrak, Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān)

Two ladders. Company: bad company < solitude < good company. Speech: saying bad < silence < saying good. The middle rung — solitude, silence — is the safe fallback when the only alternative is harmful. But the top of each ladder is active good: good companions, good words. (The Arabic is read from the book.)

Ḥadīth 66 · Every word counts against you — except three

كُلُّ كَلَامِ ابْنِ آدَمَ عَلَيْهِ لَا لَهُ، إِلَّا أَمْرٌ بِمَعْرُوفٍ أَوْ نَهْيٌ عَنْ مُنْكَرٍ أَوْ ذِكْرُ اللهِ

kullu kalāmi bni ādama ʿalayhi lā lah, illā amrun bi-maʿrūfin aw nahyun ʿan munkarin aw dhikru Llāh

"Every word uttered by a child of Adam will be held against him and not in favour of him, except enjoining good, forbidding wrong, and the remembrance of Allah."

"Insaan ki har baat uske khilaaf jaati hai — siwaaye neki ka hukm, burai se rokne, aur Allah ki yaad ke."

Tirmidhī, Ibn Māja

Make your words count. Speech defaults to a liability. Three categories flip it to an asset: enjoining good, forbidding wrong, and remembrance (which includes praising Allah, duʿāʾ, reciting Qurʾān, and ṣalawāt). Idle talk and gossip harden the heart; the chatterer is deprived of the reward that same breath could have earned.

⚠️ Not a vow of silence

Recall #63 — good speech is the top rung, better than silence. #66 isn't telling you to stop talking; it's telling you to make your speech count.

Zubaan band mat karo — qeemti banao. Achhi baat khaamoshi se behtar (#63).

Ḥadīth 67 · The remembering heart is alive; the heedless one is dead

مَثَلُ الَّذِي يَذْكُرُ رَبَّهُ وَالَّذِي لَا يَذْكُرُ رَبَّهُ مَثَلُ الْحَيِّ وَالْمَيِّتِ

mathalu lladhī yadhkuru rabbahu wa-lladhī lā yadhkuru rabbahu mathalu l-ḥayyi wa-l-mayyit

"The likeness of one who remembers his Lord and one who does not is the likeness of the living and the dead."

"Jo apne Rabb ko yaad karta hai aur jo nahin — in dono ki misaal zinda aur murde jaisi hai."

Bukhārī, Muslim

Dhikr is the pulse of the heart. Remembrance of Allah is nourishment for the heart; without it the heart is spiritually empty — in this likeness, dead. The body may walk, eat, talk, and trade while the heart inside has gone cold. This is why dhikr is not a side-practice but the life of the spiritual self.

Ḥadīth 68 · Unused knowledge is a buried treasure

"The likeness of knowledge from which no benefit is derived is that of a treasure from which no portion is spent in the path of Allah."

"Jis ʿilm se faayda nahin uthaaya jaata, woh us khazaane jaisa hai jisme se Allah ke raaste mein kuch kharch nahin hota."

Aḥmad, Dārimī

Knowledge must be activated. A treasure sealed in the ground makes no one richer; it feeds no one and becomes a burden on the Day of Judgement. Knowledge you never act on, teach, or live by is exactly that. Pairs with #67: dhikr activates the heart, action activates knowledge. (The Arabic is read from the book.)

Ḥadīth 69 · The best dhikr and the best supplication

أَفْضَلُ الذِّكْرِ لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا اللهُ وَأَفْضَلُ الدُّعَاءِ الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ

afḍalu dh-dhikri lā ilāha illa Llāh, wa afḍalu d-duʿāʾi l-ḥamdu li-Llāh

"The most virtuous remembrance is Lā ilāha illa 'Llāh, and the most virtuous supplication is Al-ḥamdu li 'Llāh."

"Sab se afzal dhikr Lā ilāha illa 'Llāh, aur sab se afzal duʿā Al-ḥamdu li 'Llāh."

Tirmidhī, Ibn Māja

Two crowns. The best dhikr is the kalima of tawḥīd: with Lā ilāha you renounce all false gods, with illa 'Llāh you affirm Allah alone — it purifies the heart. The best supplication is Al-ḥamdu li 'Llāh, because praising Allah for His favours is gratitude — and gratitude draws more bounty: "If you are grateful, I will surely increase you" (Qurʾān 14:7). Praise is dhikr, duʿāʾ, and shukr in three words.

Ḥadīth 70 · The first called to Paradise

"The first to be called to Paradise on the Day of Judgement will be those who praised Allah during times of prosperity and adversity."

"Qayaamat ke din sab se pehle Jannat woh bulaaye jaayenge jo khushhaali aur tangi — dono — mein Allah ki taʿreef karte rahe."

Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān)

Praise in both weathers. Praising Allah when life is good is easy; praising Him when life is hard is the mark of a heart truly anchored in Him. The one whose al-ḥamdu li-Llāh keeps flowing — good news or bad — is called first to the Garden. This is the chapter's crown: the one who lived accountably (#61) and kept his heart alive with praise (#69) gets the happiest possible answer to his questioning. (The Arabic is read from the book.)

🔑 The chapter in one picture

Ten aḥādīth, three movements, one thread:

  1. Answer for your watch (#61, #62, #64) — you'll be questioned; choose the place of remembrance; death is a gift.
  2. Stand together & guard your tongue (#65, #63, #66) — stay with the believers; choose good company & good speech; make your words count.
  3. Keep your heart alive (#67, #68, #69, #70) — dhikr is the heart's life; act on your knowledge; the best words are the kalima and al-ḥamd; the praisers are called first.

The thread: muḥāsaba → dhikr — because everything is asked about, keep your heart awake with the remembrance of Allah.

Hisaab → jamāʿat aur zubaan → dil ka dhikr. Dhaaga: hisaab hoga, isliye dil ko Allah ki yaad se zinda rakho.
