Teaching arc: the chapter trains the believer in three movements. First, answer for your watch — every one of you is a shepherd who will be questioned (#61); the places you choose are weighed — love the masjid, beware the heedlessness of the market (#62); and the watch ends in death, which for the believer is a gift, not a terror (#64). Then, stand together and guard your tongue — Allah's help is with the united body of believers (#65); choose good company over solitude over bad, and good speech over silence over bad (#63); and every word is a liability except enjoining good, forbidding wrong, and remembrance (#66). Finally, keep your heart alive — the remembering heart is alive (#67), unused knowledge is a buried treasure (#68), the best remembrance is the kalima and the best supplication is al-ḥamd (#69), and the praisers in every condition are called first to Paradise (#70).

Chapter ka safar: hisaab (tum jawaab-deh ho) → jamāʿat aur zubaan (sahi logon ke saath, achhi baat) → dil ka dhikr (use Allah ki yaad se zinda rakho). In sab ke neeche ek dhaaga: muḥāsaba se dhikr tak — hisaab hoga, isliye dil ko jaag-ta rakho.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Recite each of the 10 aḥādīth in Arabic where given (the matn of #63, #68, #70 is read from the book), with correct meaning in English + Roman Urdu.
  2. Name the source of each ḥadīth (Bukhārī, Muslim, Tirmidhī, Ibn Māja, Bayhaqī, Aḥmad, Dārimī, Mustadrak).
  3. Explain #61's universal reach — kullukum, everyone is a shepherd with a flock to answer for, not just rulers — and that authority is a trust, not a trophy.
  4. State why the masjid is most beloved and the market most disliked (#62) — remembrance vs heedlessness — without condemning lawful trade.
  5. Frame #64 (death is a gift) as how the believer views death, not a call to wish for it.
  6. Deliver #65's two cautions — affirm "Allah's hand" as the salaf did (His help/protection, reality left to Allah) and read "jamāʿa" as the body on truth, not mere majority.
  7. Lay out #63's two ladders (bad company < solitude < good company; bad speech < silence < good speech).
  8. State #66's principle — speech defaults to liability except enjoining good, forbidding wrong, and remembrance — without turning it into a vow of silence.
  9. Explain #67 (remembrance = the life of the heart) and #68 (unused knowledge = a buried treasure) as a pair on activation.
  10. Give #69's two crowns (best dhikr = Lā ilāha illa 'Llāh; best duʿāʾ = Al-ḥamdu li 'Llāh, because praise is gratitude that draws increase, 14:7) and #70's reward (praisers in ease and hardship are called first) — the chapter's deep thread being muḥāsaba → dhikr.

Ḥadīth 61 · أَلَا كُلُّكُمْ رَاعٍ وَكُلُّكُمْ مَسْؤُولٌ عَنْ رَعِيَّتِهِ · Each of you is a shepherd

Theme 1 — Answer for your watch. Authority is a trust, not a trophy.

أَلَا كُلُّكُمْ رَاعٍ وَكُلُّكُمْ مَسْؤُولٌ عَنْ رَعِيَّتِهِ

kullukum rāʿin wa kullukum masʾūlun ʿan raʿiyyatih

"Each of you is a shepherd, and each of you will be questioned [on the Day of Judgement] regarding his flock." (It continues: the leader over his people, the man over his household, the woman over her husband's home — each responsible, each questioned.)

"Tum mein se har ek charwaaha hai, aur har ek se uske galle ke baare mein poocha jaayega."

Bukhārī, Muslim
Where this fits: Theme 1, node 1 — opens the chapter and sets its temperature: you will be questioned. Everything that follows answers "so how should I live?"

رَاعٍ / رَعِيَّة · rāʿin / raʿiyya — "shepherd / flock"

Meaning
A rāʿin is a shepherd; his raʿiyya is the flock in his care. Every person has some flock — people, affairs, responsibilities — and will be questioned about it. The image turns authority on its head: a position over others is a trust to answer for, not a prize to enjoy.
Roman Urdu
Rāʿin charwaaha; raʿiyya uska galla. Har ek ka koi galla hai — log, maamlaat, zimmedaariyaan — aur har ek se uska hisaab hoga. Ikhtiyaar koi inʿaam nahin — yeh ek amānah hai.
⚠️ The key word
Kullukum — all of you. This is not a ḥadīth about kings and presidents alone. The head of a home, a parent, an elder sibling, anyone with even one responsibility — all are shepherds, all questioned.
What it changes now
Sirf aage ka manzar nahin — yeh aaj ka rawayya badle: ghaflat nahin, ehtimaam. Jis ke bhi zimmedaar ho, uska haq ada karo — kyunki hisaab hoga.

⚠️ Landmine 1 — "this is only about rulers"

Classmates may file this under "kings and bosses" and feel off the hook. "Lafz dekho — 'kullukum', tum sab. Har ek ka koi na koi galla hai: ghar, bachay, kaam, koi ek zimmedaari bhi. Har ek se hisaab hoga. Ikhtiyaar inʿaam nahin — amānah hai. Jo bhi tumhare zimme hai, uska haq aaj se ada karo."

وَقِفُوهُمْ إِنَّهُم مَّسْئُولُونَ

"And stop them; indeed, they are to be questioned."

Why this fits: The verse names the exact scene the ḥadīth points to — on the Day of Judgement people are halted to be questioned. #61 tells you the subject of that questioning: your flock.

Ḥadīth 62 · أَحَبُّ الْبِلَادِ إِلَى اللهِ مَسَاجِدُهَا · The masjids are the most beloved places; the markets the most disliked

Theme 1 — Answer for your watch. Where your heart spends its time is weighed.

أَحَبُّ الْبِلَادِ إِلَى اللهِ مَسَاجِدُهَا وَأَبْغَضُ الْبِلَادِ إِلَى اللهِ أَسْوَاقُهَا

aḥabbu l-bilādi ilā Llāhi masājiduhā, wa abghaḍu l-bilādi ilā Llāhi aswāquhā

"The most beloved of places in the sight of Allah are the masjids, and the most disliked of places in the sight of Allah are the markets."

"Allah ke nazdeek sab se mehboob jagahein masjidein hain, aur sab se na-pasand jagahein bazaar hain."

Muslim
Where this fits: Theme 1 — after "you'll be questioned" (#61), the places you choose to stand are themselves weighed. The reason is everything.

مَسَاجِد / أَسْوَاق · masājid / aswāq — "masjids / markets"

Meaning
Not a random ranking of buildings — the book gives the reason. The masjid is beloved because it is where a person draws closer to Allah. The market is disliked because it is where Satan's influences cluster — avarice, deceit, greed, heedlessness. It is about spiritual atmosphere: where remembrance dominates vs where forgetfulness does.
Roman Urdu
Imaaraton ki be-wajah daraja-bandi nahin — kitaab wajah deti hai. Masjid mehboob, kyunki wahaan banda Allah ke qareeb hota hai. Bazaar na-pasand, kyunki wahaan laalach, dhoka, hirs, aur ghaflat jamaa. Baat maahaul ki hai: yaad ghaalib ya bhool ghaalib.
⚠️ NOT against trade
The dislike is for the heedlessness, not the commerce. Lawful trade is permitted and praised (recall the honest trader with the prophets). The believer isn't told to abandon the market — he is told to carry the masjid into it: keep Allah's remembrance alive while buying and selling.
The takeaway
Apne dil ka waqt sambhal kar chuno. Bazaar mein bhi Allah ko yaad rakho — taake bazaar ki ghaflat tumhe nigal na le.

⚠️ Landmine 2 — "so trade is evil / the market is ḥarām"

Business-owning classmates may feel condemned. "Yeh halal kamai ko bura nahin keh rahi — tijaarat jaayaz hai, balke dīn mein iski taʿreef hai. Bazaar ko jagah ke taur par na-pasand kaha gaya us ghaflat aur laalach ki wajah se jo wahaan ghaalib hai. Sabaq: bazaar chhodo mat — masjid ko bazaar mein le jaao: kaarobaar ke beech bhi Allah ki yaad zinda rakho."

📿 Connect to Qurʾān · 24:36

فِي بُيُوتٍ أَذِنَ اللَّهُ أَن تُرْفَعَ وَيُذْكَرَ فِيهَا اسْمُهُ

"In houses [of worship] which Allah has ordered to be raised and that His name be mentioned therein."

Why this fits: The Qurʾān honours the masjids precisely as the places where Allah's name is remembered — the very reason #62 makes them the most beloved of places.

Urdu mein: Quran masjidon ki ʿizzat isi liye karta hai ke wahaan Allah ka naam liya jaata hai — yahi #62 ki wajah hai.

Ḥadīth 64 · تُحْفَةُ الْمُؤْمِنِ الْمَوْتُ · Death is a gift for the believer

Theme 1 — Answer for your watch. The watch ends at a door — and for the believer, the door is a gift.

تُحْفَةُ الْمُؤْمِنِ الْمَوْتُ

tuḥfatu l-muʾmini l-mawt

"Death is a gift for a believer."

"Maut momin ke liye ek tohfa hai."

Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān)
Where this fits: Theme 1 closes — the "watch" (#61) lived in the right "places" (#62) runs toward this door. For the believer, the door is a gift.

تُحْفَة · tuḥfa — "a gift, a present"

Meaning
In four words the Prophet ﷺ overturns our deepest dread. For the one who truly believes, it is through the gateway of death that he reaches the door to eternal contentment and happiness. Death is not the end of the good life — it is the threshold to it.
Roman Urdu
Chaar lafzon mein Rasool ﷺ insaan ka sab se bara khauf ulat dete hain. Jo sach-much īmān rakhta hai, uske liye maut ke darwaze se hi abadi raahat ka raasta khulta hai. Maut achhi zindagi ka khaatma nahin — uski dehleez hai.
⚠️ NOT wishing for death
This describes how the believer views death — with hope, not dread. It does not mean he should seek or long for it; the Prophet ﷺ forbade wishing for death because of worldly hardship. It is reassurance, not a summons.
The takeaway
Apni zindagi aur zimmedaari poori nibhaao (#61), aur maut ka khauf jaane do — kyunki tayyaari karne wale momin ke liye yeh behtar cheez ka darwaza hai.

⚠️ Landmine 3 — "death is a gift, so we should want to die"

Struggling classmates may misread this. "'Maut tohfa hai' batati hai ke momin maut ko kaise dekhta hai — umeed se, dehshat se nahin. Yeh maut maangne ya tamanna karne ka kehna nahin. Rasool ﷺ ne dunyawi takleef par maut ki tamanna se mana farmaaya. Yeh tasalli hai — zindagi nibhaao, aur maut ka darr jaane do."

Ḥadīth 65 · يَدُ اللهِ عَلَى الْجَمَاعَةِ · Allah's hand is with the group

Theme 2 — Stand together. Allah's help is with the united body of believers.

يَدُ اللهِ عَلَى الْجَمَاعَةِ

yadu Llāhi ʿalā l-jamāʿa

"Allah's hand is with the group (jamāʿa)." (It continues: "and whoever becomes apart [from the group] will be set apart in the Hellfire.")

"Allah ka haath jamāʿa ke saath hai." (Aage: "jo alag ho gaya, woh Jahannam mein alag kar diya jaayega.")

Tirmidhī

📖 Matn note — printed عَلَى, translated "with"

The book's printed Arabic reads يَدُ اللهِ عَلَى الْجَمَاعَةِ (yadu-Llāhi ʿalā l-jamāʿa, lit. "upon/over the group"), while its English renders it "Allah's hand is with a group." We reproduce the printed Arabic عَلَى exactly and keep the book's English "with" — the same pattern as Chapter 6 #59 (printed الظَّمَأُ "thirst" / English "hunger"). Both are authentic and the lesson is identical. (Kitaab ki Arabic ʿalā hai, English "with" — dono asal, baat aik.)

Where this fits: Theme 2 opens — after being told you're accountable (#61) and where to stand (#62), now whom to stand with.

يَدُ اللهِ / الْجَمَاعَة · yadu-Llāh / al-jamāʿa — "Allah's hand / the group"

Meaning
We affirm "Allah's hand" as the early Muslims did — as it came, leaving its exact reality to Allah; the scholars say it here alludes to His assistance, guidance, mercy, and protection. And that help is with the jamāʿa — the united body of believers gathered on the truth.
Roman Urdu
"Allah ka haath" — hum ise salaf ki tarah maante hain: jaisa aaya waisa, haqeeqat Allah par chhod kar; yahaan yeh Allah ki madad, hidaayat, rehmat, hifaazat ki taraf ishaara. Aur yeh madad jamāʿa ke saath — momineen ka haq par muttahid jism.
⚠️ The warning
"Whoever becomes apart will be set apart in the Hellfire." Splitting off — through a deviant belief, statement, or action — pulls a person out of that protection. The danger is in leaving the truth and the body, not in respectful difference of opinion within it.
⚠️ What "jamāʿa" is NOT
Har bheed ya mehez aksariyat nahin — yeh momineen ka haq par muttahid jism hai. Aur tambeeh gumraahi ke khilaaf hai, har jaayaz ʿilmi ikhtilaaf ko dabaane ka hathiyaar nahin.

⚠️ Landmine 4 — literalising "Allah's hand" / weaponising "jamāʿa"

Two errors at once. "Pehli: 'Allah ka haath' — hum ise waise maante hain jaise salaf ne, haqeeqat Allah par chhod kar; yahaan yeh Allah ki madad aur hifaazat ki taraf ishaara. Doosri: 'jamāʿa' ka matlab har bheed ya aksariyat nahin — yeh momineen ka haq par muttahid jism hai. Tambeeh gumraahi aur wahdat torne ke khilaaf hai — har ikhtilaaf-e-raaye dabaane ka hathiyaar nahin." (Don't open the kalām debate on the attributes — see "What NOT to teach".)

📿 Connect to Qurʾān · 3:103

وَاعْتَصِمُوا بِحَبْلِ اللَّهِ جَمِيعًا وَلَا تَفَرَّقُوا

"And hold firmly to the rope of Allah all together, and do not become divided."

Why this fits: The Qurʾān commands the believers to hold together and forbids division — exactly the unity #65 says Allah's help accompanies, and the splitting it warns against.

Urdu mein: Quran hukm deta hai sab mil kar Allah ki rassi thaamo aur tafarqa na daalo — yahi #65 ki wahdat aur uski tambeeh.

Ḥadīth 63 · Solitude & good company; speech & silence

Theme 2 — Guard your tongue. Two graded ladders for company and speech.

"Solitude is better than being in bad company, and being in good company is better than solitude. Saying what is good is better than silence, and silence is better than saying what is bad."

"Tanhaai buri sohbat se behtar hai, aur achhi sohbat tanhaai se behtar. Achhi baat kehna khaamoshi se behtar, aur khaamoshi buri baat kehne se behtar."

Mustadrak, Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān)

⚠️ Arabic matn omitted on purpose

The exact Arabic wording/harakaat of this narration is not reproduced here — our rule is firm: never write a near-correct matn. Teach it from the English meaning + source above. To display the Arabic in class, copy it exactly from the book, where it is printed.

Where this fits: Theme 2 — refines #65. Be with the body — and specifically with good company; and let your speech climb the same kind of ladder.

The genius of the middle rung

Meaning
Two ladders. Company: bad company < solitude < good company. Speech: saying bad < silence < saying good. The middle rung each time — solitude, silence — is the safe fallback when the alternative is harmful, not the ideal. The top of each ladder is active good: good companions, good words.
Roman Urdu
Do seerhiyaan. Sohbat: buri sohbat < tanhaai < achhi sohbat. Baat: buri baat < khaamoshi < achhi baat. Darmiyaani darja (tanhaai, khaamoshi) mehfooz panaah hai jab doosra raasta nuqsaan-deh ho — aadarsh nahin. Sab se ooncha darja amal wali bhalaai: achhe saathi, achhe alfaaz.
Link to #65 & #66
#65 said be with the body; #63 refines it — be with good company. And the speech ladder leads straight into #66: good speech is the top rung — so make your words count.

Ḥadīth 66 · كُلُّ كَلَامِ ابْنِ آدَمَ عَلَيْهِ لَا لَهُ · Every word counts against you — except three

Theme 2 — Guard your tongue. The doorway from the tongue to the living heart.

كُلُّ كَلَامِ ابْنِ آدَمَ عَلَيْهِ لَا لَهُ، إِلَّا أَمْرٌ بِمَعْرُوفٍ أَوْ نَهْيٌ عَنْ مُنْكَرٍ أَوْ ذِكْرُ اللهِ

kullu kalāmi bni ādama ʿalayhi lā lah, illā amrun bi-maʿrūfin aw nahyun ʿan munkarin aw dhikru Llāh

"Every word uttered by a child of Adam will be held against him and not in favour of him, except enjoining good, forbidding wrong, and the remembrance of Allah."

"Insaan ki har baat uske khilaaf jaati hai, uske haq mein nahin — siwaaye neki ka hukm dene, burai se rokne, aur Allah ki yaad ke."

Tirmidhī, Ibn Māja
Where this fits: Theme 2's hinge — speech's default is liability, and the third redeeming category (dhikr) is the doorway into Theme 3.

عَلَيْهِ لَا لَهُ · ʿalayhi lā lah — "against him, not for him"

Meaning
The Prophet ﷺ sets the default of human speech to against you. Three categories flip a word to asset: amr bi-l-maʿrūf (enjoining good), nahy ʿani-l-munkar (forbidding wrong), and dhikru-Llāh (remembrance — which the book expands to glorifying Allah, praising Him, duʿāʾ, reciting the Qurʾān, and ṣalawāt on the Prophet ﷺ).
Roman Urdu
Rasool ﷺ baat ki default setting tumhare khilaaf rakhte hain. Teen cheezein use nafa mein badalti hain: amr bi-l-maʿrūf, nahy ʿani-l-munkar, aur dhikru-Llāh (jisme tasbeeh, taʿreef, duʿā, tilaawat, aur durood shaamil).
The cost of idle talk
The book is sobering: gossip and idle talk harden the heart and push it from Allah's mercy. The chatterer is deprived of the reward that same breath could have earned in good. So speech is not neutral — it is constantly tilting for or against you.
⚠️ Not a vow of silence
Yeh keh rahi hai baat ko qeemti banao, chup mat ho jaao — yaad rakho #63: achhi baat khaamoshi se behtar hai. Har saans ya khilaaf ya haq mein — usse haq mein lagaao.

⚠️ Landmine 5 — "so almost all talking is sin / I should stay silent"

The ḥadīth names a default and three exceptions — it does not forbid kind, beneficial, or permissible speech. "Yeh har baat ko gunaah nahin keh rahi, na chup baith jaane ko. Yaad karo #63 — achhi baat khaamoshi se behtar. #66 keh rahi hai: zyada-tar faaltu/nuqsaan-deh baat khilaaf jaati hai, magar teen cheezein use nafa mein badal deti hain. Zubaan band mat karo — qeemti banao."

📿 Connect to Qurʾān · 50:18

مَّا يَلْفِظُ مِن قَوْلٍ إِلَّا لَدَيْهِ رَقِيبٌ عَتِيدٌ

"He utters not a word except that with him is an observer, ready [to record]."

Why this fits: Every word is recorded by a watchful angel — which is exactly why #66 weighs each word for or against you. The verse is the reason the ḥadīth's accounting is so exact.

Urdu mein: Har lafz ek nigraan farishta likh leta hai — isi liye #66 har baat ko taraazu mein taul-ti hai. Aayat hadith ke hisaab ki wajah hai.

Ḥadīth 67 · مَثَلُ الَّذِي يَذْكُرُ رَبَّهُ ... كَالْحَيِّ وَالْمَيِّتِ · The remembering heart is alive; the heedless one is dead

Theme 3 — Keep your heart alive. Remembrance is the pulse of the heart.

مَثَلُ الَّذِي يَذْكُرُ رَبَّهُ وَالَّذِي لَا يَذْكُرُ رَبَّهُ مَثَلُ الْحَيِّ وَالْمَيِّتِ

mathalu lladhī yadhkuru rabbahu wa-lladhī lā yadhkuru rabbahu mathalu l-ḥayyi wa-l-mayyit

"The likeness of one who remembers his Lord and one who does not is the likeness of the living and the dead."

"Jo apne Rabb ko yaad karta hai aur jo nahin karta — in dono ki misaal zinda aur murde jaisi hai."

Bukhārī, Muslim
Where this fits: Theme 3 opens — picking up #66's redeeming category (dhikr) and showing what it does: it keeps the heart alive.

الْحَيّ / الْمَيِّت · al-ḥayy / al-mayyit — "the living / the dead"

Meaning
The difference between a person who remembers his Lord and one who does not is the difference between a living person and a corpse. The book is direct: remembrance is nourishment for the heart; without it the heart remains spiritually empty. A heart without dhikr is not under-performing — in this likeness it is dead.
Roman Urdu
Jo Rabb ko yaad karta hai aur jo nahin — farq utna jitna zinda insaan aur murde mein. Kitaab: yaad dil ki ghiza hai; iske baghair dil rooh-aani taur par khaali. Dhikr ke baghair dil kamzor nahin — is misaal mein murda hai.
Why it matters this much
This is why scholars wrote whole books on dhikr: it is not a side-practice, it is the pulse of the spiritual life. The body may walk, eat, talk, and trade while the heart inside has gone cold.

Ḥadīth 68 · Unused knowledge is a buried treasure

Theme 3 — Keep your heart alive. Knowledge, like dhikr, must be activated or it is dead weight.

"The likeness of knowledge from which no benefit is derived is that of a treasure from which no portion is spent in the path of Allah."

"Jis ʿilm se koi faayda nahin uthaaya jaata, uski misaal us khazaane jaisi hai jisme se Allah ke raaste mein kuch kharch nahin kiya jaata."

Aḥmad, Dārimī

كَنْز · kanz — "a hoarded treasure"

Meaning
Knowledge that is never acted on, taught, or lived by is like a chest of gold sealed in the ground. Its owner is no richer for it; it feeds no one, helps no one — and on the Day of Judgement it becomes a burden, not a blessing.
Roman Urdu
Jo ʿilm amal, taʿleem, ya zindagi mein na aaye, woh zameen mein band sone ke sanduuq jaisa hai. Maalik amīr nahin hua; kisi ki madad nahin hui — aur Qayaamat ke din yeh bojh ban jaata hai, niʿmat nahin.
The pairing with #67
Dhikr activates the heart (#67); action activates knowledge (#68). A thing of value left unused is, spiritually, a thing wasted — and worse, a thing you'll be questioned about (back to #61).

Ḥadīth 69 · أَفْضَلُ الذِّكْرِ لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا اللهُ · The best dhikr and the best supplication

Theme 3 — Keep your heart alive. The best words to fill a living heart with.

أَفْضَلُ الذِّكْرِ لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا اللهُ وَأَفْضَلُ الدُّعَاءِ الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ

afḍalu dh-dhikri lā ilāha illa Llāh, wa afḍalu d-duʿāʾi l-ḥamdu li-Llāh

"The most virtuous remembrance is Lā ilāha illa 'Llāh (there is no deity save Allah), and the most virtuous supplication is Al-ḥamdu li 'Llāh (all praise belongs to Allah)."

"Sab se afzal dhikr Lā ilāha illa 'Llāh hai, aur sab se afzal duʿā Al-ḥamdu li 'Llāh hai."

Tirmidhī, Ibn Māja
Where this fits: Theme 3 — #67 said keep the heart alive by remembrance; #69 names which remembrance is best.

لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا اللهُ · the best dhikr

Meaning
ʿAllāma Ṭībī: this phrase purifies the heart from evil. With Lā ilāha a person renounces all false deities (of the hand and of the heart); with illa 'Llāh he affirms the oneness of Allah alone. The remembrance penetrates the heart and the believer is infused with the sweetness of faith. It is the kalima of tawḥīd — the foundation of everything.
Roman Urdu
ʿAllāma Ṭībī: yeh kalima dil ko burai se paak karta hai. Lā ilāha se har jhoote maʿbood ka inkaar; illa 'Llāh se akele Allah ki wahdaaniyat ka iqraar. Yaad dil mein utar-ti hai aur īmān ki mithaas bhar jaati hai.

الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ · the best duʿāʾ

Meaning
Why call praise the best supplication? Praising Allah for His favours is indirectly asking for more of His bounty — and it is, at heart, gratitude (shukr). Allah ties gratitude to increase: "If you are grateful, I will surely add more unto you" (14:7). So al-ḥamdu li-Llāh is dhikr, duʿāʾ, and shukr at once — three acts in three words.
Roman Urdu
Taʿreef ko behtareen duʿā kyun kaha? Kyunki niʿmaton par taʿreef gher-seedhe taur par mazeed maangna hai, aur asal mein shukr. Allah shukr ko izaafe se baandhta hai (14:7). To al-ḥamdu li-Llāh ek saath dhikr, duʿā, aur shukr hai.

📿 Connect to Qurʾān · 14:7

لَئِن شَكَرْتُمْ لَأَزِيدَنَّكُمْ

"If you are grateful, I will surely increase you [in favour]."

Why this fits: This is the verse the book itself cites to explain why al-ḥamd is the best supplication — praise is gratitude, and gratitude is met with increase.

Urdu mein: Yahi woh aayat hai jo kitaab khud naqal karti hai — taʿreef shukr hai, aur shukr ka badla izaafa.

Ḥadīth 70 · The first called to Paradise — those who praised Allah in ease and hardship

"The first to be called to Paradise on the Day of Judgement will be those who praised Allah during times of prosperity and adversity."

Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān)

The praisers in both weathers

Meaning
The first people called to Paradise are those who praised Allah in prosperity and adversity. Praising Allah when life is good is easy; praising Him when life is hard is the mark of a heart truly anchored in Him. The one whose al-ḥamdu li-Llāh (#69) keeps flowing whether the news is good or bad is called first.
Roman Urdu
Sab se pehle Jannat bulaaye jaane wale woh hain jo khushhaali aur tangi dono mein Allah ki taʿreef karte rahe. Achhe waqt mein taʿreef aasaan; mushkil mein taʿreef us dil ki nishaani jo sach-much Allah par jama hai.

Muḥāsaba (accountability) flowers into dhikr — and dhikr is crowned here.

⚠️ Common student confusions

#61 — "this is only about rulers"

Kullukum — everyone has a flock and will be questioned, not just kings and bosses. Authority is a trust (amānah), not a trophy.

"Kullukum" — har ek ka galla hai, sirf hukmraan nahin. Ikhtiyaar amānah hai, inʿaam nahin.

#62 — "trade is evil / the market is ḥarām"

The dislike is for the heedlessness, greed, and deceit that dominate the market, not for lawful trade (which the dīn praises). Carry the masjid into the market.

Na-pasand ghaflat aur laalach hai, halal tijaarat nahin. Bazaar mein bhi Allah ko yaad rakho.

#64 — "death is a gift, so wish for it"

It describes how the believer views death — with hope, not dread. Wishing for death over worldly hardship is forbidden; this is reassurance, not a summons.

Yeh batati hai momin maut ko kaise dekhe — umeed se. Maut maango mat; Rasool ﷺ ne mana farmaaya.

#65 — "the majority is always right" / literal "hand"

"Jamāʿa" = the body of believers on truth, not mere numbers or any crowd. Affirm "Allah's hand" as the salaf did — His help and protection, its reality left to Allah. The warning is against deviation, not respectful difference.

Jamāʿa = haq par muttahid guroh, mehez aksariyat nahin. "Allah ka haath" salaf ki tarah maano; tambeeh gumraahi ke khilaaf hai.

#66 — "all talk is sin / stay silent"

Make speech count — good speech is #63's top rung, better than silence. The default is liability for idle/harmful talk; three categories (enjoining good, forbidding wrong, remembrance) redeem it.

Zubaan band mat karo — qeemti banao. Achhi baat khaamoshi se behtar; teen cheezein use nafa mein badalti hain.

#68 — "unused knowledge is praised hoarding"

The opposite — a buried treasure is useless and becomes a burden. Knowledge lives only when acted on, taught, and lived; it must be spent, not hoarded.

Iska ulta — dafan khazaana be-faayda aur bojh hai. ʿIlm tab zinda jab amal, taʿleem, aur zindagi mein aaye.
