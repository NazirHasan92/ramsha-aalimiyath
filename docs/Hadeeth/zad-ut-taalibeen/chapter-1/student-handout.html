Chapter 1 — Student Handout

My first ten aḥādīth · Aḥādīth 1–10 from Part 1 of Zad ut-Taalibeen

👋 Welcome — what we'll learn today

Today we begin Zad ut-Taalibeen (Provisions for the Seekers) — a small book of short, powerful sayings of the Prophet ﷺ. Each saying is one line — but each line is meant to be carried in the heart for a lifetime.

In this chapter, we cover 10 aḥādīth. They fall into three themes:

  1. What faith really is (#1, #2, #6) — religion, worship, modesty.
  2. The believer's character (#3, #4, #5, #8, #9) — love, calmness, trust, nobility, justice.
  3. The believer & this world (#7, #10) — temptations and the right perspective on dunyā.

Each ḥadīth section gives you: the Arabic, the meaning in English and Roman Urdu, the source, and a brief explanation. Then there are reflection questions and exercises at the end.

Ḥadīth 1 · Religion is naṣīḥa

الدِّيْنُ النَّصِيْحَةُ

al-dīnu al-naṣīḥa

"Religion is naṣīḥa — sincere, pure-hearted goodwill."

"Deen naṣīḥa hai — saaf-dili se bhalaai chahna."

Muslim · Tamīm al-Dārī raḍiyallāhu ʿanhu

What it means: The word naṣīḥa (نَصِيْحَة) is much bigger than the Urdu word naseehat ("advice"). The root meaning is "pure, unmixed" — like pure honey. So naṣīḥa = a pure inner disposition of wanting good for someone, without any hidden motive. It shows up in word, action, support — whatever is needed.

The Prophet ﷺ then said this naṣīḥa is directed at five things:

  1. Toward Allah — faith in Him, obeying Him, being grateful.
  2. Toward His Book — honour the Qurʾān, recite it correctly, practise it.
  3. Toward His Messenger ﷺ — follow the Sunnah, honour him and his family.
  4. Toward the Muslim leaders / scholars — assist them in good, correct them politely.
  5. Toward the common people — guide them, support them, deal with them well.

💡 Key insight

The whole of religion is summed up in this one word. If your inner disposition toward Allah, His Book, His Messenger ﷺ, leaders, and ordinary people is pure goodwill — you are practising religion. If it is not — even if the outward acts look fine, the essence is missing.

Ḥadīth 2 · Duʿāʾ is the marrow of worship

الدُّعَاءُ مُخُّ الْعِبَادَةِ

al-duʿāʾu mukhkhu l-ʿibādah

"Supplication (duʿāʾ) is the marrow / essence of worship."

"Dua, ibadat ki gudda (asal jauhar) hai."

Bukhārī

What it means: The word mukhkh (مُخّ) literally means the marrow of a bone — the innermost, most nourishing, most concentrated part. So the Prophet ﷺ is saying: duʿāʾ is the inner core of all worship — not just one kind of worship among many.

Why? Because every act of worship — ṣalāh, ṣawm, ḥajj, dhikr — contains the same inner posture:

  • I cannot do this myself.
  • No one else can do this for me.
  • Only Allah can.

That posture IS duʿāʾ. So when you stand in ṣalāh and recite iyyāka naʿbudu wa-iyyāka nastaʿīn ("You alone we worship, You alone we ask for help") — that "You alone we ask" is duʿāʾ, sitting inside your prayer.

💡 Key insight

Don't separate "ibadat" (formal worship) from "dua" (asking). They are the same act — duʿāʾ is the marrow that runs through every ʿibādah. Sūrat al-Fātiḥah is a long duʿāʾ. Sujūd is the body language of duʿāʾ.

Ḥadīth 3 · A person is with whom they love

الْمَرْءُ مَعَ مَنْ أَحَبَّ

al-marʾu maʿa man aḥabb

"A person will be with whom they love."

"Insan unhi ke saath hoga jin se woh dil se mohabbat karta hai."

Bukhārī, Muslim

The story behind it: A Companion came to the Prophet ﷺ and said: "O Messenger of Allah, what about someone who loves a group of righteous people but cannot reach their station?" The Prophet ﷺ replied with this ḥadīth.

What it means — two sides:

  • The comfort: If you love the righteous — even if your own actions are not at their level — you will be raised with them on the Day of Judgement. Allah says in Sūrat al-Nisāʾ (4:69) that those who obey Allah and the Messenger will be with the Prophets, the truthful, the martyrs, and the righteous.
  • The warning: If you love sinners — admire them, follow them, watch them, secretly approve of their actions — you will be raised with them, even if you didn't do their sins yourself.

⚠️ A sobering thought

Look at who you follow on social media, whose lifestyle entertains you, whose words you defend. Your true loves determine your eternal company — not just your outward actions. This is not casual liking — it's deep heart-attachment.

Ḥadīth 4 · Calmness from Allah, haste from Satan

التَّأَنِّيْ مِنَ اللهِ وَالْعَجَلَةُ مِنَ الشَّيْطَانِ

al-taʾannī min Allāh, wa-l-ʿajalatu min al-shayṭān

"Calmness and patient deliberation is from Allah; haste is from Satan."

"Tahmal aur ṣabr Allah ki taraf se hai; jaldbazi Shaitan ki taraf se hai."

Tirmidhī

Important — the scope rule: This ḥadīth refers to everyday affairs, NOT to acts of worship. There are three different speeds in the believer's life:

SituationTempoWhy
Daily affairs (decisions, replies, opinions)Slow · ṣabrHaste skips your ʿaql (intellect). Without ʿaql, only impulse drives.
Toward acts of worshipHastenQurʾān 3:133: "Hasten to forgiveness from your Lord"
Inside acts of worshipCalm · ṭamaʾnīnahOnce you're in the worship, slow down for concentration

💡 Practical example

Make wuḍūʾ at home and walk to the masjid early (haste toward worship). Once in ṣalāh, slow your recitation, your rukūʿ, your sujūd (calmness in worship). When dealing with someone who has annoyed you — pause, breathe, think, then respond (slowness in daily affairs).

Ḥadīth 5 · Gatherings are with trust

الْمَجَالِسُ بِالأَمَانَةِ

al-majālisu bi-l-amānah

"Gatherings are to be kept in confidence."

"Mehfilon ki baat amanat hoti hai — usay chhupaaya jaaye."

Abū Dāwūd

The rule: What is said in a meeting is amānah — a trust deposited with you. By default, what is said there stays there. You don't need anyone to ask for confidentiality — it is the default.

The three exceptions: The rest of the narration mentions three situations where confidentiality MUST be broken:

  1. Plans to murder (protect jān · life)
  2. Plans to violate a woman (protect izzat · honour)
  3. Plans to wrongfully seize wealth (protect māl · property)

⚠️ Important

This ḥadīth protects against gossip, not against crime. If someone in a "private" gathering is planning to hurt someone, exploit someone, or take what's not theirs — confidentiality does NOT apply. Reporting that is a duty, not a betrayal.

Ḥadīth 6 · Ḥayāʾ is a branch of faith

الْحَيَاءُ شُعْبَةٌ مِنَ الإِيْمَانِ

al-ḥayāʾu shuʿbatun min al-īmān

"Ḥayāʾ (God-conscious modesty / restraint) is a branch of faith."

"Ḥayāʾ iman ki ek shaakh hai."

Bukhārī, Muslim

The two kinds of ḥayāʾ:

  • Natural shyness / sharm — everyone has this. Not specifically Islamic. Useful, but not what this ḥadīth means.
  • Īmānī ḥayāʾ — restraint from sin born of awareness that Allah is watching. This is what the ḥadīth refers to.

What does īmānī ḥayāʾ protect? A separate ḥadīth (narrated by ʿAbdullāh ibn Masʿūd raḍiyallāhu ʿanhu) explains:

ProtectWhat this means
SAR · headWhat you let into your mind — eyes (what you see), ears (what you hear), mouth (what you say)
PEṬ · stomachLawful earnings (what you eat / where it comes from), lawful relations (private parts kept lawful)
QALB · heartRemember death, hold this world lightly, prepare for what comes after

💡 Reflection

Out of 70+ branches of faith, the Prophet ﷺ specially named ḥayāʾ. Why? Because it's the restraint branch. All other branches push you toward something — only ḥayāʾ holds you back from sin. Without it, the rest cannot survive.

Ḥadīth 7 · Alcohol is the gathering of sin

الْخَمْرُ جِمَاعُ الإِثْمِ

al-khamru jimāʿu l-ithm

"Khamr (alcohol / intoxicants) is the gathering-place of all sin."

"Sharab tamam gunaahon ka majma hai."

Razīn

What it means: The word jimāʿ (جِمَاع) means "the gathering, the assembly". So alcohol isn't one sin among many — it's the gateway through which other sins enter. Once a person drinks, the rational mind (ʿaql) goes offline. ʿAql is what guards a person from sin. With the guard asleep, every other sin walks in.

⚠️ A famous story (mentioned in the book)

A man was asked to prostrate to an idol — he refused. He was asked to murder — he refused. He was asked to commit adultery — he refused. Then he was offered alcohol — he accepted. After drinking, he committed all three of the sins he had previously refused. One yes turned into many yeses, because the guardian (ʿaql) had been silenced.

Broader application: The principle is — anything that overcomes your ʿaql (rational mind) opens the gate. Recreational drugs are categorised as khamr in fiqh. The same principle extends to:

  • Anger that takes you over (the Prophet ﷺ said: "the strong one is he who controls himself when angry")
  • Lust that takes you over
  • Anything that suspends your judgement and lets impulse drive

Ḥadīth 8 · The believer is uncynical & noble

الْمُؤْمِنُ غِرٌّ كَرِيْمٌ وَالْفَاجِرُ خِبٌّ لَئِيْمٌ

al-muʾminu ghirrun karīm, wa-l-fājiru khibbun laʾīm

"The believer is uncynical and noble; the transgressor is deceitful and ignoble."

"Momin saaf-dil aur shareef hota hai; faasiq chālāk aur kameena hota hai."

Abū Dāwūd, Tirmidhī

Four key words:

WordMeaningRoman Urdu
غِرّ · ghirruncynical, trusting — doesn't expect deceptionsaaf-dil, dhoka khaa lene wala
كَرِيْم · karīmnoble, generous of charactershareef, fayyaaḍ
خِبّ · khibbcunning, deceitful, calculatingchālāk, makkar
لَئِيْم · laʾīmbase, mean, petty (opposite of karīm)kameena, ochha

Why is being ghirr (deceivable) praised? The believer's mind is optimised for the Akhirah, not for the dunyā. They don't plan deception, so they don't anticipate it from others. Losing a small amount to a dishonest shopkeeper is a non-event in the Akhirah ledger — so why pre-empt with cynicism?

The pairing matters: ghirr + karīm come together. khibb + laʾīm come together. You cannot be cunning AND noble at the same time. Being calculating makes a person mean-spirited. Two packages — choose one.

💡 Reflection

This ḥadīth doesn't say "be foolish". The believer has ʿaql — they're not stupid. They just don't weaponise their intelligence to outmanoeuvre others in worldly transactions. Their nobility is more valuable to them than the occasional loss.

Ḥadīth 9 · Oppression becomes literal darkness

الظُّلْمُ ظُلُمَاتٌ يَوْمَ الْقِيَامَةِ

al-ẓulmu ẓulumātun yawm al-qiyāmah

"Oppression / wrongdoing will be darknesses on the Day of Judgement."

"Zulm Qiyamat ke din andheray ban jaayega."

Bukhārī

Notice the wordplay: The words ẓulm (الظُّلْم) and ẓulumāt (ظُلُمَات) share the same root. The root literally means "placing a thing where it doesn't belong". Physical = light replaced by darkness. Moral = rights replaced by wrong. So the ḥadīth says: doing wrong (placing rights wrongly) produces literal darkness (light placed wrongly) on the Day.

Ẓulm has three directions — most people only think of the third:

DirectionWhat it looks like
Against yourselfEvery sin = ẓulm against yourself. Shirk (associating partners with Allah) is the worst — Qurʾān 31:13 says "Indeed shirk is the greatest ẓulm".
Against AllahRefusing His rights, denying His attributes, withholding worship.
Against othersWrongful taking of others' honour, life, or property.

⚠️ Don't limit ẓulm to "I haven't hurt anyone"

Most people think only of the third direction. The first — ẓulm against yourself — is the most overlooked. Every sin you commit is a wrong placed against your own soul. You are loading your eternal account with consequences. That is ẓulm.

Opposite of darkness — the believer's light: Qurʾān 57:12 says of the believing men and women on that Day:

يَسْعَىٰ نُوْرُهُم بَيْنَ أَيْدِيْهِمْ وَبِأَيْمَانِهِمْ

"their light proceeding before them and on their right"

Believer = light. Oppressor = darknesses. Exact opposites — on the same Day.

Ḥadīth 10 · The world — prison vs paradise

الدُّنْيَا سِجْنُ الْمُؤْمِنِ وَجَنَّةُ الْكَافِرِ

al-dunyā sijnu l-muʾmini wa jannatu l-kāfir

"The world is a prison for the believer and a paradise for the disbeliever."

"Dunya momin ke liye qaid khana hai aur kafir ke liye jannat."

Muslim, Tirmidhī

Important — this is comparative, not absolute. Three common misreadings, all wrong:

  1. "So believers must be miserable in this world." — Wrong. The Prophet ﷺ enjoyed lawful pleasures: good food, perfume, the company of his family.
  2. "So we should never enjoy anything." — Wrong. Halāl enjoyments are not just permitted — they are encouraged, and we thank Allah for them.
  3. "So all disbelievers are happy." — Wrong. Many disbelievers are deeply miserable. The "paradise" claim isn't about psychology — it's about what comes after.

The correct reading — relative to what comes next:

  • For the believer, this world is a "prison" only compared to the immense reward prepared for them in the next world. Whatever they have here is small in that comparison. They also accept the constraints of Allah's law — and live in waiting (for prayer, for Ramaḍān, for the Akhirah).
  • For the disbeliever, this world is a "paradise" only compared to what awaits them in the next. This is all they will ever get. No prison can be worse than that.

A saying from Fuḍayl ibn ʿIyāḍ (d. 187 AH):

"Whoever forgoes the pleasures and desires of the world is in a prison. As for the one who does not abandon them — what kind of prison is he in?"

Meaning: the "prison" sensation comes only when the believer is actually restraining themselves within Allah's limits. If a person indulges everything, they don't feel the prison — but they also forfeit the reward.

💡 A diagnostic question for yourself

Check your heart: Are you ready to leave this world for what's after, or do you cling to it in fear? If your heart is tightly attached to this dunyā with no longing for the Akhirah — that's the diagnostic this ḥadīth offers. Not a guilt trip — just a question to sit with.
