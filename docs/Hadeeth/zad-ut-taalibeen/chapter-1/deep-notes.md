# Part A · Understanding the chapter

These 10 aḥādīth are the opening of Part 1 of Zad ut-Taalibeen — the "Comprehensive Sayings" section. The author has arranged them by Arabic *grammatical form* (all 10 begin with a noun — nominal sentences). But you, as teacher, are not teaching grammar — you are teaching what these short, pithy sayings of the Messenger ﷺ are *actually doing* in a person's life.

**Yaad rakho:** Zad ut-Taalibeen book Arabic grammar bhi sikhati hai par tum yeh grammar nahin sikha rahi — tum meaning aur amal sikha rahi ho. Sab dus hadith chhote chhote hain — magar inka asar bohot bara hai jab samjh aa jaaye.

Three natural themes emerge across the 10 — keep this in your head as the spine of the chapter:

1. **What faith actually is** (Aḥādīth 1, 2, 6) — religion = sincere counsel; worship's core = duʿāʾ; faith has branches, of which ḥayāʾ is one.
2. **The character of a believer** (Aḥādīth 3, 4, 5, 8, 9) — who you love shapes who you become; calmness vs haste; confidentiality of gatherings; nobility vs cunning; ẓulm shows up as darkness.
3. **The believer's relationship with this world** (Aḥādīth 7, 10) — alcohol as the gathering of sins; the world as prison vs paradise.

The chapter takes the learner from *defining* faith → to *embodying* it as character → to *positioning* oneself against the world's temptations. That arc is the journey of a *ṭālib* (seeker) — which is why the book is called *Zad al-Ṭālibīn* (provisions for the seekers).

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter ka safar — *kya cheez deen hai* → *deendaar ka kirdaar kya hai* → *dunya se kya rishta hai*. Yeh teen marhalay hain ek seeker (talib) ke. Class ko yeh arc dikhao — sirf 10 alag hadith nahin, ek poora safar hai.

Work through each ḥadīth below. For each, ask yourself: *can I explain this hadith to a classmate in one sentence, in Urdu, without reading the book?* (*Kya main yeh hadith ek classmate ko ek line mein Urdu mein samjha sakti hoon, bina kitaab dekhe?*) If yes — move on. If not — re-read.

## 1 · الدِّينُ النَّصِيحَةُ — "Religion is *naṣīḥa*"

**Source:** Muslim. Narrated by Tamīm al-Dārī (raḍiyallāhu ʿanhu).

The full ḥadīth (per Imam Muslim): the Prophet ﷺ said *"al-dīnu al-naṣīḥa"* three times. The Companions asked, *"Toward whom, O Messenger of Allah?"* He replied: *"For Allah, His Book, His Messenger, the leaders of the Muslims, and their common folk."*

### What *naṣīḥa* really means

The Arabic word *al-naṣīḥa* (النَّصِيحَة) is one of the very few words that classical scholars admit has *no exact synonym* — even in Arabic itself. Translators reach for "sincerity", "sincere counsel", "benevolence", "good advice" — each captures one facet.

Roman Urdu speakers hear *"naseehat"* (نصیحت) and immediately think "advice / wagging finger". This is a **dangerous narrowing**. The Arabic *naṣīḥa* is broader and deeper. The root *n-ṣ-ḥ* (ن-ص-ح) literally means: *to be free of contamination*, *to be pure* (used for honey: *ʿasal nāṣiḥ* = pure, unadulterated honey). From there: a *purity* of intention toward the wellbeing of another — without ulterior motive.

So *naṣīḥa* = an inner disposition of pure, unmixed wishing-well, which then expresses itself in word, deed, counsel, action — whatever the situation needs.

**Yaad rakho:** Naṣīḥa ≠ sirf naseehat (advice). Naṣīḥa = dil ka woh saaf jazba jo doosre ki bhalaai chahta hai — bina kisi milaawat ke. Yeh disposition hai, sirf "advice dena" nahin. Imam Muslim ne is ek hadith ko *rubʿ al-dīn* (deen ka chautha hissa) kaha hai — sirf is ek lafz "naṣīḥa" ki gehrai ki wajah se.

### The five objects of *naṣīḥa*

The Prophet ﷺ specified five — and the Companions had to ask. He didn't volunteer this; they had to draw it out. The five form a hierarchy of relationships:

1. **Toward Allah** — faith in His essence, obedience to His commands, gratitude for His blessings, fulfilling His rights.
2. **Toward His Book** — accepting it as Allah's word, honouring it, reciting it correctly, practising its injunctions.
3. **Toward His Messenger ﷺ** — affirming his message, following his Sunnah, honouring him and his family.
4. **Toward the leaders of the Muslims** — assist them in virtue, obey them in good, correct them politely. Same applies to scholars.
5. **Toward the masses** — guide them aright, prevent them from wrong, support them in need, deal with them in the best manner.

**Yaad rakho:** Naṣīḥa "Allah ki taraf" se shuru ho ke "aam logon" tak aati hai — har rishte mein deen yeh saaf-dili maangti hai. Sirf "doosron ko naseehat" nahin — Allah aur Quran ke saath bhi "naṣīḥa" hai. Yeh mushkil concept hai magar zaroori.

### Why "religion *is* naṣīḥa" — not "includes"

The Arabic structure is striking: *al-dīnu* (the religion) [is] *al-naṣīḥa* (the naṣīḥa). Two definite nouns equated with no copula — the strongest form of identification in Arabic. The Prophet ﷺ didn't say "naṣīḥa is *part of* religion" — he equated them. This is the same rhetorical structure as *al-ḥajj ʿArafah* ("Hajj IS [the standing of] ʿArafah").

So *all* of religion can be cashed out in terms of *naṣīḥa* in some direction. If a person isn't acting from *naṣīḥa* — toward Allah, His Book, His Messenger, the leaders, the masses — they aren't practising religion in its essence.

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith mein "Deen mein NAṢĪḤA hai" nahin kaha — "Deen NAṢĪḤA HAI" kaha. Yeh equation hai, sirf shaamil hona nahin. Agar dil mein bhalaai ki saaf neeyat nahin, to baqi sab deen ka khol hai, jaan nahin.

## 2 · الدُّعَاءُ مُخُّ الْعِبَادَةِ — "Duʿāʾ is the *mukhkh* of worship"

**Source:** Bukhārī.

### The metaphor of *mukhkh*

*Mukhkh* (مُخّ) literally = **marrow** — the innermost part of a bone, the most nourishing, the most concentrated. Sometimes also translated as "brain", "essence", "core". Not just *a* component — the *innermost essence*.

So the Prophet ﷺ is saying: take all the acts of worship — ṣalāh, ṣawm, zakāh, ḥajj, dhikr, recitation — and ask "what is the *core* substance running through all of them?" The answer: duʿāʾ. The act of turning to Allah in need.

**Yaad rakho:** *Mukhkh* matlab gudda — haddi ke andar ka. Sirf "kisra" nahin, "asal jauhar". Dua ibadaat ki gudda hai — sab ibadaat ke andar yahi running hai: Allah ki taraf rujooʿ.

### Why duʿāʾ is the essence

The book gives two reasons. They are deep and worth understanding:

**Reason 1 — Duʿāʾ is the direct fulfilment of a Qurʾānic command.** Allah says: *"And your Lord says: Call on Me, I will answer you"* (Qurʾān 40:60). Worship is fundamentally about *obedience to Allah's command*. The command to call on Him is itself a primary command. So duʿāʾ is worship *in its purest, most direct form* — you are doing exactly what He commanded.

**Reason 2 — Duʿāʾ embodies the inner reality of worship.** Worship = recognising that *only Allah* can fulfil one's needs. When a person calls on Allah, they are silently asserting:
- *I cannot do this myself.*
- *No one else can do this for me.*
- *Only Allah can.*

That is the heart of *tawḥīd al-ulūhiyyah* (oneness in worship). Every other act of worship contains this same posture — but duʿāʾ makes it *explicit*. You are *literally* turning away from all causes and turning to Allah alone.

**Yaad rakho:** Dua karte waqt insaan teen baatein kehta hai dil mein, bina lafz ke — *main yeh nahin kar sakta · koi aur nahin kar sakta · sirf Allah kar sakta hai*. Yahi tawheed ki bunyaad hai. Isi liye dua "ibadat ki gudda" hai — har ibadat ke andar yahi posture chhupi hoti hai.

### The Urdu-speaker's separation problem

In Urdu, "dua" and "ibadat" are often treated as *two different things* — namaz is "ibadat", and after namaz we make "dua". The ḥadīth dissolves this separation. **Duʿāʾ is not a sequel to worship — it is the marrow of worship itself.**

When you stand in ṣalāh and recite *iyyāka naʿbudu wa-iyyāka nastaʿīn* — that *iyyāka nastaʿīn* ("You alone we ask for help") IS duʿāʾ. The fātiḥa is a duʿāʾ. The rukūʿ and sujūd are postures of duʿāʾ. You don't make duʿāʾ *after* worshipping — you are *constantly* making duʿāʾ when you worship.

**Yaad rakho:** Urdu speakers ki habit hai — "pehle namaz, phir dua". Yeh hadith yeh barrier todti hai. Namaz hi dua hai. Surah Fatihah ek lambi dua hai. Sajda position hi dua ka body language hai. Yeh classmates ko explicit batao — woh ne kabhi soche nahin honge yeh.

## 3 · الْمَرْءُ مَعَ مَنْ أَحَبَّ — "A person is with whom they love"

**Source:** Bukhārī, Muslim.

### The story behind the ḥadīth

A man came to the Prophet ﷺ and asked: *"O Messenger of Allah, what about someone who loves a group of people but is unable to join them?"* — meaning: I love righteous people but I'm not at their level in knowledge or action. The Prophet ﷺ answered: *"A person is with whom they love."*

The phrase *"unable to join"* refers to someone who *aspires* to be like the righteous but hasn't reached their station. The reassurance: aspiration counts. On the Day of Judgement, this person will be raised with those they loved.

This is **the Qurʾānic principle of association by love** — directly echoing the verse: *"Whosoever obeys Allah and the Messenger — they will be with those upon whom Allah has bestowed favour: of the Prophets, the truthful, the martyrs, and the righteous. The best of company are they."* (Qurʾān 4:69)

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith ka asbāb (sabab-e-wurud) yaad rakho — ek aspiring sahabi ne pucha tha. Jawab dilasa bhi tha aur warning bhi. Tum jin ko *dil se* chahti ho — woh tumhari manzil hain Akhirah mein.

### The double edge — aspiration AND warning

This ḥadīth is profoundly **two-sided**. Most people quote only the comforting half: *"don't worry, if you love the pious, you'll be with them"*. But Imam Mullā ʿAlī al-Qārī and others extend the principle:

> *"This ḥadīth could also be applied to those who aspire to be with evil people and sinners and secretly admire their actions, even though they may not have perpetrated those evil actions themselves. They will be gathered with the sinners on the Day of Judgement."* (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 8:740)

So the rule is: **your eternal companions are determined by who you truly love, not just what you outwardly do.** A person who secretly admires fāsiqs, who is entertained by their lifestyles, who consumes their stories — even if they don't commit those sins themselves — is recording their loyalty.

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith ka doosra rukh aksar bhul jaate hain. Sirf "neki walon se mohabbat" ki khushkhabri nahin — *"jo bhi tum sachhe dil se chahti ho — wahi tumhari company hogi"*. Drama, music, films, social media — un mein jo characters tum *admire* karti ho, tum unhi ke saath uthayi jaaogi. Yeh sobering hai. Classmates ko gently yeh batao.

### What "love" means here

Not casual liking. Not "Oh I follow them on Instagram". The Arabic *aḥabba* (to love) here means deep heart-attachment, the kind that orients your life choices. Imam al-Khaṭṭābī clarifies: this is the love that *shapes your actions* — you imitate them, you defend them, you spend time on them, you orient yourself toward them. Surface-level "likes" don't fall under this; deep affinity does.

**Yaad rakho:** "Mohabbat" yahaan sirf pasand nahin — *dil ki gehrai se chahna, unke jaise banne ki kashish*. Casual "I like that person" nahin — *"main unke jaisi banna chahti hoon"* wali feeling. Yahi taur ka pyaar shapes destiny.

## 4 · الْأَنَاةُ مِنَ اللهِ وَالْعَجَلَةُ مِنَ الشَّيْطَانِ — "Calmness is from Allah, haste is from Satan"

**Source:** Tirmidhī.

### The critical scope condition

This ḥadīth is one of the most *misquoted* aḥādīth in casual settings. People take it as a blanket rule — "always be slow, never rush". This is **wrong**, and it's important you teach the proper scope.

The classical commentators (Mullā ʿAlī al-Qārī in *Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ* 8:786) explicitly clarify: **this ḥadīth refers to ordinary daily activities, not to acts of worship.** For acts of worship, the *opposite* is true — *"hasten toward forgiveness from your Lord"* (Qurʾān 3:133), *"compete with one another in good"* (Qurʾān 5:48). *(Note: the book makes this "hasten toward good" point in its commentary but does **not** print these verse numbers on this page — 3:133 and 5:48 are linked by us, not book-sourced.)*

The two rules together:
- **Daily affairs** (transactions, decisions, reactions, opinions, judgements): calmness, deliberation, patience. Acting in haste = spoiling the action.
- **Acts of worship** (prayer, charity, knowledge, repentance): hasten *toward* them, then perform them with calmness, concentration, devotion.

The example the book gives is exact: *"it is disliked to run to join the prayer, but it is commendable to perform wuḍūʾ at home and set out early."* You hurry to *get* to the worship; you don't hurry *in* the worship.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh hadith ka scope SAMJHO — *rozmarra ke kaam mein ṣabr · ibādat ki taraf jaldi · ibādat ke andar ṭamāʾnīnah*. Three different speeds. Class ko ek line mein clarify karo, warna woh isay galat samjhenge.

### Why *ʿajala* (haste) is from Satan

*ʿAjala* (الْعَجَلَة) in classical Arabic isn't speed itself — it's *premature action without proper deliberation*. The Qurʾān describes humans as *khuliqa al-insānu min ʿajal* (the human was created from haste — 21:37). It's a defining human weakness — we want results before we've earned them, judgements before we've understood, retorts before we've listened.

Satan exploits this. He pushes you to:
- Respond before thinking → bad decisions
- Speak before listening → broken relationships
- Judge before understanding → injustice
- Act on impulse → regret
- Reach conclusions before evidence → harm

When you slow down, you give your *ʿaql* (intellect) time to engage. When you rush, the *nafs* takes over. Hence the spiritual logic: haste = Satan's doorway; calmness = Allah's protection.

**Yaad rakho:** Shaitan *ʿajala* (jaldbazi) se kaam karta hai kyunki jaldi mein insan apni *ʿaql* (samajh) ka istimal nahin karta — sirf *nafs* (impulse) chalata hai. Ṣabr-e-aql ko jagaata hai, ʿajala-e-aql ko sulaata hai. Yeh hi rooḥani science hai.

## 5 · الْمَجَالِسُ بِالأَمَانَةِ — "Gatherings are with trust"

**Source:** Abū Dāwūd.

### The grammar carries the meaning

The Arabic construction *al-majālisu bi-l-amāna* — literally, "the gatherings [are] by/with trust". The preposition *bi-* (بـ) here indicates *accompaniment* or *condition*: gatherings are *bound* by trust, *conditioned* on confidentiality.

So the default rule of any meeting is: **what is said here, stays here.** This is not extra-pious behaviour — it is the *baseline* of Muslim social ethics.

**Yaad rakho:** Default rule hai — *jo majlis mein hua, woh majlis mein raha*. Iske liye specifically promise lena nahin parta — har baat-cheet by default amānah hoti hai. Yeh Islam ki bunyādī social ethic hai.

### The three life-protecting exceptions

The continuation of the narration (which the book references) gives **three exceptions** — three situations where confidentiality is not only broken but *must* be broken:

1. **Plans to murder** — protect the targeted life.
2. **Plans to rape / dishonour a woman** — protect her safety and ʿiḍḍ.
3. **Plans to wrongfully seize someone's wealth** — protect property rights.

Notice the pattern: **all three are about preventing a future harm against someone's protected rights** (life, honour, wealth — three of the five *maqāṣid al-sharīʿah*, the higher objectives of the Sacred Law). When confidentiality would protect a crime against another's protected right, confidentiality *yields*.

**Yaad rakho:** Teen exceptions hain — jaan, izzat, maal. Jab in teen mein se kisi ke khilaaf saazish hai, to majlis ki amānah todni *farḍ* hai. Yeh "ghulu" nahin, yeh sharīʿah ka usool hai. Maqāṣid (jaan, izzat, maal) confidentiality se zyada azeem hain.

### What this is NOT

This ḥadīth is *not* a license to:
- Cover up a teacher's abuse "because the meeting was confidential"
- Hide a relative's domestic violence "because it's a family matter"
- Suppress reporting financial fraud "because the conversation was private"

These all fall under the three exceptions. The ḥadīth protects *gossip*, not *crime*.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh hadith *gheebat* aur *fitnah* se rokti hai — *jurm chhupaane* se nahin. Agar koi kisi ki jaan, izzat, ya maal ke khilaaf hai, to "private baat thi" wali bahaana laagu nahin. Yeh balance bohot important hai — aaj kal classmates is mein confuse hoti hain.

## 6 · الْحَيَاءُ شُعْبَةٌ مِّنَ الإِيمَانِ — "*Ḥayāʾ* is a branch of faith"

**Source:** Bukhārī, Muslim.

### The "branches" tradition

Other aḥādīth (also in Bukhārī and Muslim) state that *al-īmānu biḍʿ wa sabʿūna shuʿbatan* — "faith has 70-something branches" (the exact number varies by narration: 73, 77). The Prophet ﷺ then specified a few — and *ḥayāʾ* is the only one explicitly named in this short ḥadīth.

That's significant. Out of 70+ branches, he picks *ḥayāʾ* for this single-line distillation. **Why?**

Because *ḥayāʾ* is uniquely **the restraining branch**. Every other branch of faith pushes you *toward* something (toward prayer, toward charity, toward justice). *Ḥayāʾ* alone *restrains* you *from* sin. It's the inhibitor. Without that inhibitor, no other branch can survive — because the lower self will pull you out of all of them.

**Yaad rakho:** Iman ke 70+ branches mein se "ḥayāʾ" hi *break* hai — gunaah se rokne wala. Baqi branches "accelerator" hain — naik amal ki taraf le jaate hain. Break ke baghair gaadi crash ho jaati hai. Isi liye Prophet ﷺ ne is ek ko khaas karke specify kiya.

### Two types of *ḥayāʾ* — only one is from faith

This is the subtle distinction you need to internalise yourself before teaching. The book quotes the famous saying:

> *"Your Lord should not find you in places He has forbidden."*

This is *ḥayāʾ* from Allah — the *ḥayāʾ* of faith. It's a *learned, cultivated* quality.

There is another *ḥayāʾ* — natural shyness, embarrassment, social timidity (Urdu: *sharm, jhijhak*). Everyone has some of this. It's not specifically Islamic. A non-Muslim can have it.

The ḥadīth refers to the **first** kind only — the *ḥayāʾ* born of faith, of awareness that Allah is watching. Not the second.

**Yaad rakho:** Do *ḥayāʾ* hain — *naturaal sharm* (sab insaano mein hai) aur *iman wali ḥayāʾ* (Allah ke saamne hone ka ehsaas). Hadith mein doosri wali maqsood hai — pehli nahin. Yeh distinction zaroori hai warna classmates "main toh sharmili hoon" keh ke samajh lengi.

### The deeper ḥadīth from ʿAbdullāh ibn Masʿūd

The book mentions this fuller account (from Ahmad), and it's important to know yourself:

The Prophet ﷺ said one day: *"Be shy of Allah Most High as much as is His due."* The Companions said, *"All praise to Allah, we are shy of Him."* He replied: *"That is not the point. Whoever is shy of Allah as His due should:*

- *Protect his head and what it comprises (mind, mouth, ears)*
- *Protect his stomach and what is adjoining it (preserve from unlawful wealth, protect private parts from the unlawful)*
- *Remember death and what comes after it*
- *Whoever desires the Hereafter should abandon the adornments of this world.*

*Whoever fulfils these has been shy of Allah as His due."*

This is the **operational definition** of *ḥayāʾ* of faith. It's not a feeling — it's a discipline over what enters the head, the stomach, and the heart.

**Yaad rakho:** *Ḥayāʾ* sirf feeling nahin — yeh teen jagah ki hifaazat hai: *sar* (kya dekha, suna, kaha, socha), *pet* (kya khaaya, kahaan se kamaaya, kahaan kharch hua), *qalb* (Akhirah ki yaad, dunya se bechari taʿalluq). Yeh discipline hai, sirf sharm nahin.

## 7 · الْخَمْرُ جُمَّاعُ الإِثْمِ — "*Khamr* is the gathering of sin"

**Source:** Razīn.

### The word *jummāʿ*

*Jummāʿ* (جُمَّاع) — literally "the gathering, the convening, the bringing together". Not "one sin among many" — but the *gathering* of sins.

The Arabic conveys: *khamr* (alcohol / intoxicants) is the place where sins *meet, assemble, and feed each other*. It's the gateway through which other sins enter. The Prophet ﷺ is saying: if you take this one thing, all the other sins are now within reach.

**Yaad rakho:** *Jummāʿ* matlab "majma" (gathering). Khamr akela gunaah nahin — yeh wo dawaza hai jahaan se baqi gunaah ghar mein daakhil hote hain. Ek se chhoot do, to baqi automatically aa jaate hain. Yeh hi root logic hai is hadith ki.

### The supporting narration

The book quotes a narration from Sunan al-Bayhaqī (from ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿUmar raḍiyallāhu ʿanhumā):

> *"Alcohol is the mother of all obscenities and the gravest of sins. Whoever consumes alcohol abandons prayer and commits adultery with his mother and with his paternal and maternal aunts."*

The graphic specifics are deliberate — the narration is naming what intoxication produces when the rational mind is suspended. The point: when *ʿaql* (intellect) is offline, the *nafs* runs unopposed.

And the story the book includes is sharp: a man was once invited to **prostrate to an idol** — he refused. Asked to **commit murder** — he refused. Asked to **commit adultery** — he refused. Then offered **alcohol** — he accepted... and then committed all the other sins.

That story is the perfect illustration of *jummāʿ* — the gathering. One yes leads to many yeses, because the gatekeeper (reason, conscience) is gone.

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith ka story important hai — ek banda jo shirk, qatl, zina sab ko mana karta tha, sirf sharab pi li, aur phir woh sab kar gaya. Sharab kyun? Kyunki sharab *ʿaql* ko band kar deti hai. Aur ʿaql gunah ka guard hai. Guard so jaaye to chor ghar mein.

### The broader application

Adult learners may say "I don't drink, this doesn't apply to me." Push back gently. The principle of the ḥadīth is: **anything that overcomes the rational faculty is a gateway sin.** That includes:

- Recreational drugs (the fiqh ruling extends *khamr*'s legal category to any intoxicant)
- Anger when it overwhelms reason (the Prophet ﷺ said: *"the strong one is not the wrestler — the strong one is he who controls himself when angry"*)
- Lust when it overwhelms reason
- Even excessive social media binges that suspend judgement and lead to wasteful consumption

The principle = *anything that takes your ʿaql offline opens the gate*. Apply it.

**Yaad rakho:** "Main sharab nahin peeti" kehna easy hai. Magar principle apply karo — *jo bhi cheez ʿaql ko band karti hai, woh gunaah ka dawaza kholti hai*. Drugs, ghussa, social media binge, lust. ʿAql Allah ki amanat hai — usay sambhalo.

## 8 · الْمُؤْمِنُ غِرٌّ كَرِيمٌ وَالْفَاجِرُ خَبٌّ لَئِيمٌ — "The believer is *ghirr karīm*; the *fājir* is *khabb laʾīm*"

**Source:** Abū Dāwūd, Tirmidhī.

### The four words

This is one of the most lexically rich short aḥādīth — four key adjectives, in two paired contrasts:

- **غِرّ** (*ghirr*) — uncynical, trusting, easily deceived because they don't expect deception. Root *gh-r-r* = the deceived/unaware. Used for someone whose default posture is *good faith*.
- **كَرِيم** (*karīm*) — noble, generous, of high character. Not financially rich — *spiritually* generous.
- **خَبّ** (*khabb*) — cunning, deceitful, calculating. Someone who anticipates and plans deception, often as their default.
- **لَئِيم** (*laʾīm*) — base, mean, low. Stingy in spirit, ungenerous, petty. The opposite of *karīm*.

So the believer is **uncynical + noble**; the transgressor is **cunning + base**. Notice the *pairing* — Allah connects these two qualities each time. They cluster together: nobility brings simplicity; baseness brings cunning.

**Yaad rakho:** Char lafz hain — *ghirr* (saaf-dil, dhoka khaane wala) + *karīm* (sharif) = momin. *Khabb* (chālāk) + *laʾīm* (kameena) = faasiq. Yeh quality "pairs" hain — alag nahin. Sharif log saaf-dil hote hain; kameena log chālāk hote hain.

### Why being *ghirr* (deceivable) is praised

This is the surprising part — and the part you must teach carefully.

We normally consider being *uncynical* as a weakness. "Don't be naive!" we say to children. "Trust no one!" The world tells us street-smartness is a virtue.

The ḥadīth flips this. The believer is **deliberately, even constitutionally, uncynical** in worldly matters — because:

1. The believer's mind is not optimising for *worldly* gain. They don't expect deception in transactions because they themselves don't *plan* deception. Their inner world doesn't have a "deceive-others" subroutine, so it doesn't have a corresponding "anticipate-deception" subroutine either.
2. Being *karīm* (noble) means giving people the benefit of the doubt — even at one's own occasional cost.
3. The believer's portfolio is the *Hereafter*. Losing 200 rupees to a dishonest shopkeeper is a non-event in the Akhirah ledger. So why pre-empt with cynicism?

The *fājir* (transgressor) is the inverse. Their mind is **optimised for worldly gain**, so it's constantly modelling other people's deception, planning around it, deploying their own. The cunning is functional — but it makes them *laʾīm* (mean-spirited). You can't be calculating and noble at the same time.

**Yaad rakho:** Momin kyun "dhoka khaane wala" hai? Kyunki uska mind hisaab Akhirah ka rakhta hai, dunya ka nahin. Dunya ke 100 rupay ka dhoka uske liye nukᶊan nahin — woh "uchaa" hai uske badle Akhirah ki kaamyaabi pe. Faasiq dunya ke liye chālāk hai — aur isi chālāki ki keemat uska "laʾīm" (kameena) ban jaana hai. Dono nahin mil sakte: shareef aur chālāk.

### The Urdu cultural caution

Urdu-speaking culture has a strong "*hoshiyaar*" tradition — "be smart, don't get fooled". This ḥadīth doesn't say "be foolish". It says: in worldly transactions, accept that occasional loss is the price of remaining *karīm*. The believer is *not stupid* — they have *ʿaql*. They just don't *weaponise* it.

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith yeh nahin keh rahi *"bewakoof bano"*. Yeh keh rahi hai — *dunya ke kaamon mein chālāki ko mat optimize karo*. Thoda nukᶊan accept karo agar uska badla hai ke tum kameena banne se bach jaao. Aql barqaraar — chālāki na ho.

## 9 · الظُّلْمُ ظُلُمَاتٌ يَوْمَ الْقِيَامَةِ — "*Ẓulm* is *ẓulumāt* on the Day of Judgement"

**Source:** Bukhārī.

### The wordplay

Arabic wordplay at its sharpest. **Ẓulm** (الظُّلْم) and **ẓulumāt** (ظُلُمَات) share the root *ẓ-l-m* (ظ-ل-م). The literal meaning of the root: *to place a thing in a place that is not its own*.

- Physical *ẓulm* = displacing things (light replaced by darkness — hence *ẓulumāt* = "darknesses").
- Moral *ẓulm* = displacing rights (putting your action where the right of another belongs).

So the Prophet ﷺ is saying — using a single root in two grammatical forms — that the **act** of misplacing rights (*ẓulm*) becomes a literal physical **state** of displaced light (*ẓulumāt*) on the Day. The internal logic is exact: you produce darkness, and on the Day you walk in it.

In contrast: *the believer's deeds become light* — see Qurʾān 57:12 (*"on the Day when you will see the believers, the men and women, with their light proceeding before them and on their right"*). *(57:12 is linked by us to illuminate the contrast; the book does not cite it on this page.)*

**Yaad rakho:** Arabic ka khoobsurat khel — *ẓulm* (zulm) aur *ẓulumāt* (andheray) — ek hi jad se. Insaan zulm karta hai — andheray paida karta hai. Qiyamat ke din wahi andheray uski raah par hote hain. Buraai ka phal "metaphor" nahin — *literal* roohani roshni ka ghaayb ho jaana hai.

### The three directions of *ẓulm*

The book gives a precise categorisation (from *Jāmiʿ al-ʿUlūm wa-l-Ḥikam* of Ibn Rajab al-Ḥanbalī, p. 334) — three directions:

1. **Ẓulm against oneself** — the worst of which is *shirk* (associating partners with Allah). Then the major sins. Then the minor sins. *"Indeed shirk is the greatest ẓulm"* (Qurʾān 31:13 — the verse is added by us; the book names shirk as the worst ẓulm but does not cite this āyah here).
2. **Ẓulm against Allah** — denying His rights, refusing His worship.
3. **Ẓulm against others** — wrongful taking of honour, life, or property.

Most people, when they hear "ẓulm", think only of #3 — *"I haven't oppressed anyone"*. The ḥadīth's reach is much wider. **Every sin you commit is ẓulm against yourself** (because you're putting your own deeds where they don't belong — burdening yourself with consequences). Every act of shirk or refusal of Allah is ẓulm against Him. Every wrong taken from a brother or sister is ẓulm against them.

**Yaad rakho:** *Ẓulm* sirf "kisi ko maara" wali baat nahin. Teen taraf hai — *apne aap par* (har gunaah), *Allah par* (shirk, ʿibadat se inkaar), aur *doosron par* (haq toḍna). Pehli wali sabse zyada bhul jaate hain. Har gunaah apne aap par ẓulm hai — kyunki apne nafs ko Akhirah ka nukᶊan tum pohncha rahe ho.

### Why "darknesses" plural

Notice: *ẓulumāt* is the **plural** of *ẓulma* (darkness). Why plural?

Two readings:
1. **Layered darkness** — many darknesses, accumulated, stacked. Each act of *ẓulm* adds another layer. On the Day, the oppressor walks in a *compound* darkness.
2. **Symbolic of hardships** — the book notes that *ẓulumāt* can also symbolise the various hardships and torments of the Day. Multiple kinds, not one.

Either way: it's plural for emphasis and intensity.

**Yaad rakho:** *Ẓulumāt* singular nahin — *plural* hai. Har ẓulm ek layer add karta hai. Qiyamat ke din yeh layers stacked hain — ek nahin, kayee andhere. Aur unmein bhatakna parta hai.

## 10 · الدُّنْيَا سِجْنُ الْمُؤْمِنِ وَجَنَّةُ الْكَافِرِ — "The world is the prison of the believer and the paradise of the disbeliever"

**Source:** Muslim, Tirmidhī.

### The most-misunderstood ḥadīth of the chapter

Of all ten, this one is the most likely to be misread by classmates. Three common misreadings to disarm:

1. *"So a believer must be miserable in this world."* — **Wrong.** The Prophet ﷺ enjoyed lawful pleasures: he loved good food, perfume, his family.
2. *"So we should never enjoy anything."* — **Wrong.** Halāl enjoyments are not just permitted, they are encouraged. The believer thanks Allah for them.
3. *"So disbelievers must all be happy."* — **Wrong.** Many disbelievers are deeply unhappy in this world. The "paradise" claim is not psychological.

The ḥadīth is **comparative, not absolute**.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh hadith mein "comparison" hai, "absolute statement" nahin. *"Momin ke liye dunya jail hai"* matlab nahin *"momin ko dukhi rahna hai"*. Tum khush bhi raho, halāl lazzat bhi lo. Yeh sirf compare kar rahi hai aage wale baal-bachay ka kya hone wala hai.

### The proper reading — comparative

Imam al-Nawawī's commentary on Sahih Muslim (and Mullā ʿAlī al-Qārī echoes it in *Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ* 9:7) lays it out:

**For the believer**, the world is a "prison" because:
- They are constrained by Allah's law (no zina, no riba, no khamr, etc.)
- They cannot pursue every desire
- They are perpetually waiting — for ṣalāh, for Ramaḍān, for Akhirah
- Most importantly: **whatever they have here is *negligible* compared to what's prepared for them in the next.** Even a king's palace is a "prison cell" compared to Jannah.

**For the disbeliever**, the world is a "paradise" because:
- They live as they please (no halāl/harām constraint)
- They have no fear of accountability
- **Most importantly: whatever they have here is *all they will ever get* — and so it is, comparatively, their "paradise".** It's not paradise in any absolute sense — but it is, for them, the maximum.

The Salaf-saying the book quotes (Fuḍayl ibn ʿIyāḍ, d. 187 AH) seals it:
> *"Whoever forgoes the pleasures and desires of the world is in a prison. As for the one who does not abandon them — what type of prison is he in?!"*

The point: even for the believer, the "prison" sensation arises from *deliberate restraint* in this world for the sake of the next. If a believer indulges everything, they don't feel the "prison" — but they also don't have the next-world reward.

**Yaad rakho:** *Comparison* hai — momin ki yeh dunya Akhirah ki bay-azim niyamat ke saamne *qaid khana hai*. Kafir ki yeh dunya Akhirah ki azab ke saamne *jannat hai*. Yeh psychological statement nahin — *eternal* perspective hai. Aur "qaid" wali feeling tab aati hai jab momin sharʿi haddon mein rehta hai — agar har cheez allow kar do, to "qaid" ki feeling bhi nahin hogi, lekin reward bhi nahin.

### The deeper teaching — *desire for return*

The book makes a subtle point that's worth catching: the believer's *desire is to leave this world and meet Allah*; the disbeliever's *desire is to live in this world forever*. So:

- **Believer**: longs to leave → world = prison they're waiting to be released from.
- **Disbeliever**: longs to stay → world = paradise they're terrified to leave.

This is the orientation difference. If you find yourself in this world terrified of dying, never wanting it to end, with no longing for Akhirah — diagnose. *Where is my heart actually attached?*

**Yaad rakho:** Momin ki dil mein hota hai — *"Allah se mulaqat ka shoq"*. Kafir ki dil mein hota hai — *"hamesha yahin rehna"*. Yeh dil ki direction hai — agar tumhara dil dunya se chipka hua hai aur Akhirah ka shoq nahin, to apni iman ka jaaiza lo. Yeh hadith ka *diagnostic* use hai.

## The big picture — what these 10 aḥādīth are silently building

Stand back. The chapter has taken you and your classmates through a **complete portrait of a believer**:

1. **Faith's *content*** — Religion = *naṣīḥa* (1), Worship's marrow = duʿāʾ (2), Faith's restraint = ḥayāʾ (6).
2. **The believer's *character*** — they reflect who they love (3), they slow down (4), they keep confidences (5), they are uncynical and noble (8), they avoid all ẓulm (9).
3. **The believer's *worldview*** — they recognise alcohol/intoxicants as the gathering of sins (7), they hold this world lightly because their portfolio is Akhirah (10).

These aren't 10 isolated wisdoms. They are 10 facets of one diamond — a portrait of the *ṭālib* (seeker).

**Yaad rakho:** 10 hadith — par 1 picture banti hai. *Talib ki tasveer*. Class ko aakhir mein yeh sketch dikhao — *"yeh saari hadithein milkar ek tasveer banati hain"*. Isolation mein dekhne se yeh moti hain — milakar haar hain.

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

Now you understand the chapter. This section is how to *deliver* it to your classmates.

---

## The 7 landmines to watch for

### Landmine 1 · "Naṣīḥa" being heard as "naseehat" (advice)

The Urdu word *naseehat* means "advice / lecture". Classmates will hear ḥadīth 1 and think it's about *giving advice*. They'll miss the inner-disposition meaning. They'll also miss the four other directions of *naṣīḥa* (toward Allah, His Book, His Messenger, leaders).

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Dekho — 'naṣīḥa' aur 'naseehat' do alag cheez hain. Naseehat sirf 'advice' hai. Naṣīḥa matlab dil ki saaf-dili — kisi ke fayde ki sachhi tamanna, bina kisi milaawat ke. Aur yeh 'naṣīḥa' Allah ke liye bhi hoti hai, Qur'an ke liye bhi, Rasool ﷺ ke liye bhi — sirf insaano ke liye nahin. Ek lafz ne saara deen samet liya."*

### Landmine 2 · Separating duʿāʾ from ʿibādah

Classmates have been taught — implicitly — that "ibadat" = formal worship (ṣalāh, ṣawm), and "dua" = something you do after. Ḥadīth 2 says they are the same. Some classmates may resist this collapse.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Hum keh dete hain 'pehle namaz, phir dua'. Magar Allah ke Rasool ﷺ farmate hain ke 'dua ibadat ki gudda hai'. Surah Fatihah jo har namaz mein parhte hain — woh poori ek lambi dua hai. Iyyāka naʿbudu wa-iyyāka nastaʿīn — yeh dua hai. Yani namaz ka content hi dua hai. Dua koi alag cheez nahin — ibadat ka asal jism hai."*

### Landmine 3 · The double edge of "love" (Ḥadīth 3)

Classmates will hear the comforting half ("if I love the pious I'll be with them") and miss the warning half ("if you love sinners, you'll be with them"). Don't let them off the hook — but deliver it gently.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Hadith do taraf wali hai. Pehli taraf — naik logon se mohabbat karoge to unke saath uthaayi jaaogi, chahe tum unke darjay tak na pohnchi ho. Yeh khushkhabri hai. Doosri taraf — agar tum kisi ko dil se chahti ho jo gunaah karta hai, uske social media follow karti ho, uske kaam admire karti ho — to yeh bhi recorded hai. Tum unke saath ho mulaaqat ke din. Yeh sobering hai — magar warning bhi rehmat hai, taake hum apna dil saaf rakhen."*

### Landmine 4 · Misapplying "calmness from Allah" (Ḥadīth 4)

Classmates will use this ḥadīth to justify dawdling, being lazy, missing prayers on time, etc. — "Calmness is from Allah!". You must clarify the scope from the start.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh hadith har jagah laagu nahin hoti. Sirf *rozmarra kaam* ke liye hai — kharidari, faisle, baat-cheet, ghussa. Inhe halki phulki karo. Magar *ibadat* mein? Bilkul opposite hai. Sariʿu ilā maghfira (Allah ki maghfirat ki taraf daudo) — yeh Quran kehti hai. Namaz par jaldi, neki par jaldi, sadaqah par jaldi. Sirf *andar* ibadat ke tamannā (calmness) honi chahiye. Teen alag speeds hain — yaad rakho."*

### Landmine 5 · Treating gathering-confidentiality as cover for crime (Ḥadīth 5)

In some cultures, "what happens in the family stays in the family" gets stretched to cover abuse, fraud, or violence. Classmates may unconsciously bring that frame. Distinguish *gossip protection* from *crime protection*.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Hadith fitnah aur gheebat se rokti hai — magar jurm chhupaane ke liye nahin. Agar majlis mein kisi ne kaha hai ke 'main qatl karunga' ya 'main yeh maal hadap luungaa' ya 'main is ladki ke khilaaf saazish kar raha hoon' — yeh confidential nahin. Yeh teen cheezein expose karna farḍ ban jaata hai — jaan, izzat, maal. Ghar ki baat ghar mein — magar sirf jab woh baat *ghar ke andar ka mamla* ho, doosre ke haq ka nahin."*

### Landmine 6 · "Why is being deceivable praised?" (Ḥadīth 8)

Smart classmates will push back: "But isn't being smart and discerning good?" Take the question seriously. Don't dismiss it.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Hadith yeh nahin keh rahi ke bewakoof bano. Yeh keh rahi hai ke dunya ke khareed-farokht aur deals mein chālāki ko apna identity mat banao. Momin ki *ʿaql* zinda hai — magar uska target Akhirah hai. Faasiq apna pooraa mind dunya ke nuksaan-fayde optimize karne mein lagaata hai — isi liye woh chālāk hai, magar isi liye woh kameena bhi hai. Dono qualities saath aati hain. Tum decide karo — saaf-dili aur sharaafat (momin wala combo), ya chālāki aur kameena pan (faasiq wala combo). Beech ka raasta nahin hai."*

### Landmine 7 · "World as prison" leading to misery (Ḥadīth 10)

Some classmates will internalise this as "I must be sad in this world" or "I can't enjoy halāl pleasures". Important to disarm.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh hadith comparison hai, absolute nahin. Allah ke Rasool ﷺ khud halāl khushiyon se khush hote thay — achi khushboo, achi khaane, biwi aur bachon ke saath waqt. 'Qaid khana' matlab hai ke jo niyamat is dunya mein hai woh Akhirah ki niyamat ke saamne kuch bhi nahin — qaid jaisi mahsoos hoti hai sirf comparison mein. Kafir ke liye dunya 'jannat' isi liye hai kyunki yahi sab kuch hai uska — Akhirah mein kuch nahin milne wala. Tum khush bhi raho, halāl lazzat bhi lo, Allah ka shukr bhi karo — magar dil ka attachment dunya pe nahin, Akhirah pe rakho."*

## Suggested 60-minute class plan

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0–5 min | Greeting, recitation of Surah Fatiḥah, intention. Introduce Zad ut-Taalibeen — what the book is, who wrote it, why we study it. |
| 5–10 min | The chapter's three themes (faith / character / world) — write the arc on the board. Tell them: 10 ḥadīths, one portrait. |
| 10–25 min | **Aḥādīth 1, 2, 6** (faith's content). Read each, recite the Arabic together, explain the depth, take Q&A. Spend extra time on "naṣīḥa = not just naseehat". |
| 25–45 min | **Aḥādīth 3, 4, 5, 8, 9** (the believer's character). Same pattern. Use real-life examples. Drill Landmines 3, 4, 5, 6 specifically. |
| 45–55 min | **Aḥādīth 7, 10** (the world). Walk through the *jummāʿ al-ithm* logic of ḥadīth 7. Then unpack ḥadīth 10 as comparative-not-absolute. |
| 55–60 min | Recap — the chapter's arc, one-line takeaway per ḥadīth. Distribute student handout. Assign reflection homework. Close with duʿāʾ. |

**Yaad rakho:** Pacing important hai. 10 hadithein hain — har ek pe 4-5 minute average. Kuch zyada lambi hongi (jaise #1 naṣīḥa, #10 dunya), kuch chhoti (#5 majlis, #6 ḥayāʾ). Time prison ban gayi to themes pe focus karo — agle hafte revisit kar dena.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 1

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum aage dekhenge"* or *"yeh book ke scope se bahar hai"*:

- ❌ **Detailed fiqh rulings** triggered by individual aḥādīth — e.g. don't go into the full fiqh of music when ḥadīth 13 (Ch 2) comes up. We're doing the *spirit* of these aḥādīth, not a fiqh course.
- ❌ **Political contemporary applications** — classmates may want to apply ḥadīth 9 (ẓulm) to modern political situations. Stay general and personal — the chapter is about *your* ẓulm, not "their" ẓulm.
- ❌ **The longer narrations from Part 3** of the book — keep them for later if she covers Part 3 in another series.
- ❌ **Sufi/spiritual hierarchies** — even though some commentaries reference *maqāmāt* and *aḥwāl*, don't bring this in. Stay with the plain text.
- ❌ **Adding more aḥādīth that "support"** — don't pile on. Each of these 10 stands on its own.
- ❌ **Iʿrāb / Arabic grammar of the matn** — yes, the book organises by grammatical form, but you're not teaching grammar in this class. (If a classmate notices "they all start with a noun" — congratulate them, mention it briefly, move on.)

**Yaad rakho:** Pre-loading aur tangents madad nahin karte. 10 hadithein hain — har ek ke andar dam hai. Apne aap mein. Aur cheezein mat daalo. Class ka focus = *yeh 10 hadithein dil mein bait jaayein*. Bas.

## Self-check before teaching

Before the class, make sure:

- [ ] You can recite each of the 10 aḥādīth in Arabic from memory (or near-memory). *(Har hadith ki Arabic yaad ho ya bohot qareeb yaad ho.)*
- [ ] You can give the English meaning of each, and the source attribution (Bukhārī / Muslim / Tirmidhī / etc.) *(Har hadith ka matlab aur source — Bukhārī ya Muslim ya jo bhi — yaad ho.)*
- [ ] You can name the **three themes** of the chapter — and which ḥadīth goes where. *(Teen themes — faith, character, world — aur kaunsi hadith kahaan jaati hai.)*
- [ ] For each ḥadīth, you can give a *one-sentence Urdu summary* without reading the book. *(Har hadith ka ek-line Urdu khulasa.)*
- [ ] You have prepared the 7 landmine counter-strategies — at least the Urdu phrasing for each. *(Saat landmines aur har ki Urdu samjhauti tayar.)*
- [ ] You can answer "why is being uncynical praised?" (Landmine 6) — this is the hardest question of the chapter. *(Yeh sawaal sabse mushkil hai — iska jawab tayar rakho.)*

If yes to all six — *tum tayar ho.*

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Are they treating *naṣīḥa* as just "naseehat"? *(Naṣīḥa ko sirf "advice" samajh rahe hain?)*
2. Are they still separating duʿāʾ from ʿibādah? *(Dua aur ibadat ko alag treat kar rahe hain?)*
3. Are they extending the "calmness" ḥadīth to acts of worship? *(Ibadat mein bhi sustī ka justification de rahe hain?)*
4. Are they avoiding the second half of ḥadīth 3 (love = also a warning)? *(Love wali hadith ka second half samajh aaya?)*
5. Are they reducing ẓulm to only *ẓulm against others*? *(Ẓulm ka sirf "doosre par zulm" wala matlab le rahe hain?)*
6. Are they reading "world as prison" as a call to misery? *(Dunya = qaid wali hadith ko "udaas raho" samajh rahe hain?)*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh 6 points tumhe batayenge kahaan revision chahiye. Agar 3+ points pe cohort phans rahi hai, agle class mein woh hadithein dohrao — phir Chapter 2 par jaao. Foundation strong honi chahiye — yeh 10 hadithein iman ki bunyaad hain.
