★ Master Overview — نَقْشَةُ الْبَابِ

Ten aḥādīth, four movements — provision (keep it clean), the heart (Allah's real scale), the open face, the closing fork. One discipline beneath them all: the real measure — Allah weighs the heart, the deed, the truth — not the surface. The capstone of the hundred.

Teaching arc: the final chapter re-prices a life in four movements, all under one scale. First, provision — keep it clean — sin quietly costs you barakah or reward (#91), yet your decreed rizq still arrives in full (#92), so the move is to give: charity puts out Allah's anger and wards off an evil death (#93). Then, Allah weighs the heart — no-one is superior by race or colour, only by taqwā (#94); and Allah looks not at your forms and wealth but at your hearts and deeds (#95). Then carry it to people — that inner soundness shows on the face: meeting your brother cheerfully is a good deed (#96), and greeting first puts you closest to Allah (#97). Finally, what ruins, what saves — interest ends in ruin (#98), anger rots faith's sweetness (#99), and the capstone of the hundred: truth is the road to Paradise, falsehood the road to the Fire (#100).

Chapter ka safar: rizq saaf rakho (91, 92, 93) → Allah dil taulta hai (94, 95) → use logon tak pahonchao — khula chehra, pehla salaam (96, 97) → kya giraata hai, kya bachaata hai (98, 99, 100). In sab ke neeche ek dhaaga: asal taraazu — Allah upar-upar nahin, dil-amal-sach taulta hai. Aur yeh sau ka ikhtitaam hai.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Recite all 10 aḥādīth in Arabic (every matn is given this chapter), with correct meaning in English + Roman Urdu.
  2. Name the source of each ḥadīth (Ibn Māja, Mustadrak, Tirmidhī, Ibn Ḥibbān, Aḥmad, Muslim, Abū Dāwūd, Bayhaqī–Shuʿab al-Īmān) at the book's exact level.
  3. Recognise that all ten continue the book's "Inna" sayings (إِنَّ…) and explain the chapter's deep thread — the real measure.
  4. Hold #91 with #92 — sin costs barakah/reward, but the decreed rizq still arrives in full, so #91 is not a wealth-meter for judging people.
  5. Land #93 as sincere generosity drawing Allah's mercy and warding off an evil death — not a transactional coin.
  6. Teach #94 on both sides — it demolishes racism (only taqwā raises), and taqwā paraded is spiritual pride.
  7. Give #95 cleanly — Allah weighs hearts and deeds, not forms and wealth (heart and deed, paired).
  8. Land #96/#97 — a cheerful face is a real good deed, and greeting first puts you closest to Allah — the inner soundness made outward.
  9. Correct #98 (penury = spiritual ruin/lost barakah, not a naive bankruptcy claim) and #99 (anger ruins faith's completeness/light, not "every flash = loss of īmān"; righteous anger exists).
  10. Land #100 as the capstone of the hundred — truth → Paradise, falsehood → Fire — a habituated direction framed as hope, and close the session as a milestone.

Ḥadīth 91 · إِنَّ الرَّجُلَ لَيُحْرَمُ الرِّزْقَ بِالذَّنْبِ يُصِيْبُهُ · Sin has a hidden cost

Theme 1 — Provision, keep it clean. Read "deprived of provision" the way the book reads it — barakah/reward, not a wealth-meter.

إِنَّ الرَّجُلَ لَيُحْرَمُ الرِّزْقَ بِالذَّنْبِ يُصِيْبُهُ

inna r-rajula la-yuḥramu r-rizqa bi-dh-dhanbi yuṣībuh

"A person is deprived of livelihood by the sins he commits."

"Beshak insaan ek gunaah ki wajah se rizq se mehroom ho jaata hai."

Ibn Māja, Mustadrak
Where this fits: Theme 1 opens — the chapter's first "Inna" saying weighs the hidden cost of sin. The deep thread starts: the real loss isn't always visible.

رِزْق / ذَنْب · rizq / dhanb — "provision / sin"

Meaning
A man is deprived of rizq (provision) because of a dhanb (sin). The book gives "livelihood" two meanings: "either the reward of the next world, which a person is deprived of by the sins he commits, or the livelihood of this world (wealth, health, and general well-being), which a Muslim is deprived of so that his sins can be removed and his status in the Hereafter elevated" (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 8:662). So the loss is an ākhira reward or a withdrawn barakah — and even that can be a mercy that wipes the sin.
Roman Urdu
Insaan ek gunaah (dhanb) ki wajah se rizq se mehroom hota hai. Kitaab "rizq" ke do maʿne deti hai: ya aakhirat ka ajr jis se gunaah mehroom kar deta hai, ya duniya ka rizq (maal, sehat, aafiyat) jo isliye le liya jaata hai taake gunaah mit jaayein aur aakhirat mein darja buland ho (Mirqāt 8:662). To nuqsaan aakhirat ka ajr ya uthi hui barakat hai — aur woh bhi rehmat ho sakti hai.
⚠️ Not a wealth-meter
This is not a licence to call a poor person "sinful" or a rich person "righteous." The barakah reading rules that out — and #92 (next) guarantees every soul receives its decreed provision in full.
The takeaway
Gunaah kabhi muft nahin — chupke se kuch cheen leta hai, aksar woh jo nazar nahin aata. Magar kisi ke maal/gareebi se uske gunaah ka faisla mat karo (#92 ise band karti hai).

Ḥadīth 92 · إِنَّ نَفْسًا لَّنْ تَمُوْتَ حَتَّى تَسْتَكْمِلَ رِزْقَهَا · Your provision is guaranteed in full

Theme 1 — Provision, keep it clean. The guarantee that blocks #91 from becoming "the poor are sinners".

إِنَّ نَفْسًا لَّنْ تَمُوْتَ حَتَّى تَسْتَكْمِلَ رِزْقَهَا

inna nafsan lan tamūta ḥattā tastakmila rizqahā

"A soul will never die until it fully receives all of its livelihood."

"Beshak koi jaan tab tak nahin marti jab tak apna poora rizq paa na le."

Ibn Māja
Where this fits: Theme 1 — the book's own commentary is one line: "See ḥadīth 87 and 91." That cross-reference is the teaching: hold this with #87 (provision pursues you) and #91 (sin's hidden cost).

تَسْتَكْمِلَ رِزْقَهَا · tastakmila rizqahā — "completes its provision"

Meaning
No soul will die until it has fully received (tastakmila) every portion of rizq decreed for it. The book's whole commentary is the cross-reference "See ḥadīth 87 and 91" — so read all three together: provision pursues you (#87), arrives in full before death (#92), and #91's "deprivation" can only be a barakah/reward adjustment, never the decreed floor.
Roman Urdu
Koi jaan tab tak nahin marti jab tak apna poora muqarrar rizq paa (tastakmila) na le. Kitaab ki sharah yahaan ek line hai — "hadith 87 aur 91 dekho" — to teeno saath parho: rizq tumhe dhoondta hai (#87), maut se pehle poora aata hai (#92), aur #91 ki "mehroomi" sirf barakat/ajr ka farq hai, muqarrar farsh ka nahin.
Two freedoms at once
He won't sin chasing provision (it's already coming), and he won't despair or judge by wealth (the loss in #91 is barakah/reward, not the decreed floor). Strive — calmly, lawfully.
The takeaway
Muqarrar rizq zamānat-shuda aur poora hai. Na rizq ke peechhe gunaah, na maal se faisla/mayoosi. Sukoon se halaal koshish.

📿 Connect to Qurʾān · 51:22

وَفِي السَّمَاءِ رِزْقُكُمْ وَمَا تُوعَدُونَ

"And in the heaven is your provision and whatever you are promised."

Why this fits: Provision is not down to your scramble on earth — it is decreed "in the heaven," guaranteed and secured. That is exactly why #92 can say no soul dies before completing its rizq.

Urdu mein: Rizq tumhari zameeni daud ka mohtaaj nahin — woh "aasman mein" muqarrar, zamānat-shuda hai. Isi liye #92 keh sakti hai ke koi jaan apna rizq poora hue baghair nahin marti.

Ḥadīth 93 · إِنَّ الصَّدَقَةَ لَتُطْفِئُ غَضَبَ الرَّبِّ وَتَدْفَعُ مِيْتَةَ السُّوْءِ · The open hand draws mercy back

Theme 1 — Provision, keep it clean. If sin withdraws barakah (#91), charity is the hand that draws mercy back — sincerely, not as a transaction.

إِنَّ الصَّدَقَةَ لَتُطْفِئُ غَضَبَ الرَّبِّ وَتَدْفَعُ مِيْتَةَ السُّوْءِ

inna ṣ-ṣadaqata la-tuṭfiʾu ghaḍaba r-rabbi wa-tadfaʿu mītata s-sūʾ

"Voluntary charity extinguishes the anger of the Lord and prevents an unpleasant death."

"Beshak sadaqa Rabb ke ghusse ko bujha deta hai aur buri maut ko taal deta hai."

Tirmidhī, Ibn Ḥibbān
Where this fits: Theme 1 closes — the cure beside the disease. Sin (#91) pushes mercy away; ṣadaqa (giving from the rizq guaranteed in #92) draws it back. Handle Landmine 5: sincere, not transactional.

صَدَقَة / تُطْفِئُ / مِيْتَة السُّوْء · ṣadaqa / tuṭfiʾu / mītat al-sūʾ — "charity / extinguishes / an evil death"

Meaning
Voluntary ṣadaqa tuṭfiʾu (extinguishes — like water on fire) the anger of the Lord, and tadfaʿu (pushes away) a mītat al-sūʾ, an evil or wretched death. Set against #91: if sin quietly withdraws barakah and invites displeasure, charity — giving from the very rizq Allah guaranteed in #92 — does the opposite.
Roman Urdu
Sadaqa Rabb ke ghusse ko bujha (tuṭfiʾu — aag par paani) deta hai, aur ek buri maut (mītat al-sūʾ) ko taal (tadfaʿu) deta hai. #91 ke samne: agar gunaah barakat utha kar naaraazgi laata hai, to sadaqa — usi rizq mein se dena jiski #92 mein zamānat hai — uska ulta karta hai.
⚠️ Sincere, not transactional
Keep this from sliding into a superstitious toll — "drop a coin, dodge a bad death." It is the spiritual posture of generosity, by Allah's grace, that turns away wrath. Charity given grudgingly or to "buy off" fate is not the ṣadaqa being praised.
The takeaway
Dil se, khaalis niyyat se do — yeh sauda nahin, sakhaawat ka roohani rawayya hai. Haath ke peechhe ka dil hi woh cheez hai jise Allah taulta hai (#95).

Ḥadīth 94 · إِنَّكَ لَسْتَ بِخَيْرٍ مِّنْ أَحْمَرَ وَلَا أَسْوَدَ إِلَّا أَنْ تَفْضُلَهُ بِتَقْوَى · The only superiority is taqwā

Theme 2 — Allah weighs the heart. The chapter's pivot, and a charter of equality. Teach both traps (Landmine 2).

إِنَّكَ لَسْتَ بِخَيْرٍ مِّنْ أَحْمَرَ وَلَا أَسْوَدَ إِلَّا أَنْ تَفْضُلَهُ بِتَقْوَى

innaka lasta bi-khayrin min aḥmara wa-lā aswada illā an tafḍulahu bi-taqwā

"You are not superior to a white- [lit., red], or black-skinned person, unless you excel him in piety."

"Beshak tum kisi surkh (yaʿni gore) ya kaale shakhs se behtar nahin — siwaaye iske ke taqwa mein us se aage ho."

Aḥmad
Where this fits: Theme 2 opens — and is the chapter's heart. Every line of race, colour, lineage is wiped off the scale; the one axis left is taqwā. Spend real time here.

تَقْوَى · taqwā — "God-consciousness, piety"

Meaning
You are no better than a "red"- (the book notes: i.e. white-) or black-skinned person except that you excel him in taqwā. Every human line — race, colour, lineage, nation — is off the scale; the only axis of superiority is God-consciousness. The book ranks taqwā in three grades: abstaining from shirk; abstaining from sins and heedlessness; and constant presence (ḥuḍūr) with Allah, absent from all besides Him (Mirqāt 7:54).
Roman Urdu
Tum kisi "surkh"- (kitaab: yaʿni gore-) ya kaale shakhs se behtar nahin siwaaye iske ke taqwa mein aage ho. Har insaani lakeer — nasl, rang, khaandaan, qaum — taraazu se baahar; buland karne wali واحد cheez taqwa hai. Kitaab taqwa ke teen darje deti hai: shirk se bachna; gunaahon aur ghaflat se bachna; aur Allah ke saath musalsal huzoor, sab ghair se ghaayab (Mirqāt 7:54).
⚠️ Two traps
(1) Don't let it pass as a polite line — it demolishes racism, spoken to a man who thought his colour raised him. (2) The reverse danger: if taqwā raises you, the ego claims it — "then I'm superior, the pious one." But taqwā you parade is gone the moment you look down on the "less religious."
The takeaway
Nasl-parasti ki imaarat giraana — aur saath hi roohani ghuroor se bachna. Asli taqwa jhukaata hai, akadta nahin.

📿 Connect to Qurʾān · 49:13 (cited by the book itself)

إِنَّ أَكْرَمَكُمْ عِندَ اللَّهِ أَتْقَاكُمْ

"Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous of you."

Why this fits: The book quotes this verse in its own commentary on #94. It is the Qurʾānic root of the ḥadīth: Allah made you into nations and tribes only that you may know one another — the single measure of nobility is taqwā, never colour or tribe.

Urdu mein: Kitaab khud yeh aayat #94 ki sharah mein naqal karti hai. Allah ne qaumein aur qabeele sirf pehchaan ke liye banaaye — izzat ka واحد paimana taqwa hai, rang ya qabeela nahin.

Ḥadīth 95 · إِنَّ اللهَ لَا يَنْظُرُ إِلَى صُوَرِكُمْ وَأَمْوَالِكُمْ، وَلٰكِنْ يَنْظُرُ إِلَى قُلُوْبِكُمْ وَأَعْمَالِكُمْ · He photographs the heart

Theme 2 — Allah weighs the heart. Where #94's "only taqwā" is actually measured: the heart and the deed.

إِنَّ اللهَ لَا يَنْظُرُ إِلَى صُوَرِكُمْ وَأَمْوَالِكُمْ، وَلٰكِنْ يَنْظُرُ إِلَى قُلُوْبِكُمْ وَأَعْمَالِكُمْ

Muslim

رِبَا / عَاقِبَة / قُلّ · ribā / ʿāqiba / qull — "interest / outcome / penury"

Meaning
Interest, however much it increases (wa-in kathura), its ʿāqiba (end-state) comes to qull (littleness, want, ruin). The book sharpens it: wealth from interest "will not only be devoid of divine value or blessing, but it will lead the earner to disgrace and spiritual poverty" — and spiritual poverty is worse than financial, because it can lead to kufr (the book points on to ḥadīth 110).

⚠️ Spiritual, not a naive prediction
Don't flatten this into "everyone who takes interest goes bankrupt" — some die rich. The ruin is the barakah-stripped, value-hollowed, faith-endangering end. The number can rise while the ʿāqiba falls.
The takeaway
Upar-upar barhta number mat parho — rooh ka anjaam parho. Yeh #91 ki "cheeni hui barakat" poori awaaz mein.

Meaning
Anger ruins faith just as al-ṣabir — the bitter aloe plant — ruins honey. (Read the harakah off the page: ṣabir, the bitter plant, not ṣabr, "patience" — the whole image is a bitter substance fouling sweet honey.) ⚠️ Not every anger
This is not "every flash of anger costs your īmān," nor a condemnation of all anger — there is a righteous anger for Allah's sake. The target is uncontrolled, ego-driven rage that hijacks a person.

⚠️ Common student confusions

#91 — "so the poor are sinful and the rich are righteous"

The book blocks this: "provision" here is the reward of the next world or a withdrawn worldly barakah (which can be a mercy that wipes the sin). And #92 guarantees every soul its decreed rizq in full. It is not a wealth-meter for judging people.

Maal/gareebi se gunaah ka faisla nahin. "Rizq" = aakhirat ka ajr ya uthi hui barakat (jo rehmat bhi ho sakti hai). #92: muqarrar rizq poora milta hai.

#93 — "so charity is a coin I pay to dodge a bad death"

Not a transaction. It is the posture of sincere generosity, by Allah's grace, that draws mercy back. Charity given grudgingly or to "purchase" a guarantee is not the ṣadaqa praised — the heart behind the hand is what Allah weighs (#95).

Sauda nahin. Khaalis sakhaawat ka rawayya rehmat kheenchta hai. Behdili ka ya "guarantee" khareedne wala sadaqa woh nahin. Dil hi taula jaata hai (#95).

#94 — "fine, equality — but then I'm the superior one, the pious"

Two traps. The line demolishes racism (only taqwā raises) — don't pass it as polite. And taqwā you parade is gone the moment you look down on the "less religious." Real taqwā humbles, never struts.

Nasl-parasti giraana — aur roohani ghuroor se bachna. Jis taqwa ki numaaish karo woh taqwa nahin. Asli taqwa jhukaata hai.

#98 — "so every interest-earner goes bankrupt"

Not a naive economic prediction — some die rich. The "penury" is spiritual: barakah-stripped, value-hollowed, faith-endangering (it can lead to kufr). The number can rise while the ʿāqiba (end-state) falls. #91 at full volume.

Saada maʿaashi peshangoi nahin. "Tabaahi" roohani hai — barakat-cheen, imaan-khatre wali. Number barhe, ʿāqiba gire. #91 poori awaaz mein.

#99 — "so any time I feel angry, my faith is gone"

"Ruins" = ruins faith's completeness, light, and beauty (it usually spoils, not deletes) — though unrestrained anger can drive faith-breaking words/acts. And not all anger is condemned: righteous anger for Allah's sake exists. The target is ego-driven rage.

"Saada deta hai" = imaan ki takmeel/noor ko — mitaata nahin (haan be-qaaboo ghussa imaan-tod baat tak le ja sakta hai). Har ghussa bura nahin — Allah ke liye neik ghussa hai. Nishaana nafs-ka ghussa.

#100 — "so one lie damns me forever?"

No — a habituated direction, not a single slip. Truth bends a life toward Paradise; falsehood toward the Fire — until one is written a ṣiddīq or a kadhdhāb. Truthfulness is the road back too: return to ṣidq by being truthful now. Frame it as hope.

Ek jhoot hamesha ke liye barbaad nahin karta — yeh zindagi ka rukh hai. Sach wapsi ka bhi raasta hai. Umeed ke taur par sikhaao.

★ The hundred complete — a milestone تَمَّتِ الْمِائَةُ

Ḥadīth 100 is the last of the first hundred "Comprehensive Sayings" — the target this resource set out to teach. Close the session as a milestone.

Step back from the ten and see the whole journey. Part 1 of Zad ut-Taalibeen moved, across a hundred short sayings, through faith → character → the world. And the three closing chapters form their own arc — three ways of looking past the surface to the reality:

  • Chapter 8 — weigh the reality, not the label (baṣīra): judge things by their truth, not their name.
  • Chapter 9 — trust the unseen: order your life around the decree, the heart, the hidden enemy, the secret prayer.
  • Chapter 10 — the real measure: live by the scale Allah Himself uses — the heart, the deed, the truth — and choose the right road at the final fork.

The hundred ends not with information but with a fork (#100) and a single habit — ṣidq, truthfulness — that walks the believer through the right gate. When you close this chapter, mark it: "Aaj sau hadithein mukammal huin — aur sab ka khulasa aakhri hadith mein hai: do raaste, ek aadat — sach."

Yeh sau ka ikhtitaam hai. Part 1 ka safar imaan → akhlaaq → duniya tha; aakhri teen chapter ek arc: asal taulo (Ch 8) → ghaib par bharosa (Ch 9) → asal taraazu (Ch 10). Sau ka anjaam ek do-raahe par, aur ek aadat — ṣidq — jo sahi darwaze se le jaati hai. الْحَمْدُ لِلّٰهِ.
