# Part A · Understanding the chapter

These ten aḥādīth (91–100) continue the book's *"Inna" sayings* — the short *"indeed, X is Y…"* nominal sentences that began at #77. Every matn this chapter opens with **إِنَّ (inna, "indeed…")**. But coming as they do at the *end* of the hundred, they do one last thing on purpose: they **re-price everything**. They quietly take the things people use to measure a life — money, race, looks, status, what you accrue — and show you they are the *wrong* scale. Then they hand you the right one: clean provision, a sound heart, an open face, and the truth.

Look at what these ten weigh. The livelihood a sin can quietly cost you (91). The livelihood no soul can die before completing (92). The charity that puts out the Lord's anger and turns away an evil death (93). The piety that is the *only* thing that makes one person better than another — not the colour of his skin (94). The hearts and deeds that are the *only* thing Allah actually looks at — not your face or your bank balance (95). The smile that counts as a good deed (96). The first salām that puts you *closest to Allah* (97). The interest that looks like increase but ends in ruin (98). The anger that rots faith the way a bitter plant rots honey (99). And the truth that walks a person all the way to Paradise — while the lie walks him to the Fire (100). Every single one is about **what truly raises you, and what truly ruins you.** The chapter's deep thread is: **the real measure — Allah does not weigh you by the surface; He weighs the heart, the deed, the truth.**

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter 10 ka naqsha — yeh das hadithein "Inna" wali hain (*"beshak X yeh hai…"*) jo #77 se shuru hui theen. Magar yeh sau ke *aakhir* mein aati hain, isliye ek aakhri kaam karti hain: har cheez ko **dobara taulti** hain. Jin paimaano se log zindagi naapte hain — paisa, nasl, soorat, ohda, jo jama karo — yeh kehti hain ke yeh *ghalat* taraazu hai. Phir sahi taraazu thama deti hain: halaal rizq, saaf dil, khula chehra, aur sach. Gunaah jo rizq cheen le (91); woh rizq jo poora hue baghair koi marta nahin (92); woh sadaqa jo Rabb ka ghussa bujha de (93); woh taqwa jo *واحد* cheez hai jo ek ko doosre se behtar banaati hai — rang nahin (94); woh dil aur amal jo *واحد* cheez hai jise Allah dekhta hai — chehra ya maal nahin (95); woh muskurahat jo neki hai (96); woh pehla salaam jo Allah ke *sab se qareeb* kar de (97); woh sood jo barhta dikhe magar tabaahi par khatam ho (98); woh ghussa jo imaan ko aise saada de jaise kadwa paudha shahad ko (99); aur woh sach jo bande ko Jannat tak le jaaye — aur jhoot Jahannam tak (100). Dhaaga: **asal taraazu — Allah tumhe upar-upar se nahin taulta; woh dil, amal, aur sach taulta hai.**

Four natural themes — keep this in your head as the spine of the chapter:

1. **Provision is decreed — keep it clean** (Aḥādīth 91, 92, 93) — the chapter opens on the *economy* of rizq. Sin can cost you provision/barakah (91); yet no soul dies until its decreed provision is *complete* (92); so the move is not to grab but to *give* — charity puts out Allah's anger and wards off an evil death (93). Rizq is settled: don't sin for it, don't panic for it, give from it.
2. **Allah weighs the heart, not the surface** (Aḥādīth 94, 95) — the two aḥādīth at the centre, and the chapter's pivot. No-one is superior by race or colour — *only* by taqwā (94); and Allah does not look at your forms or your wealth, but at your hearts and your deeds (95). The real scale.
3. **Carry it to people — an open face, the first salām** (Aḥādīth 96, 97) — faith made social. Meeting your brother with a cheerful face is itself a good deed (96), and the one who greets first is the *closest of people to Allah* (97). The inner soundness of Theme 2 shows on the face and in the greeting.
4. **What ruins, what saves — the closing summons** (Aḥādīth 98, 99, 100) — the final fork. Interest ends in ruin (98); anger ruins faith (99); and the capstone of the whole hundred: truth leads to Paradise, falsehood to the Fire (100). End on the choice the entire book has been preparing you to make.

Notice the flow: **settle your relationship with provision → re-set what actually makes you valuable → let that soundness reach people → then stand at the final fork between truth and falsehood.** And the deepest current under all ten is *the real measure*: where Chapter 8 trained you to *weigh the reality, not the label* (baṣīra) and Chapter 9 trained you to *trust the unseen*, Chapter 10 hands you the **scale Allah Himself uses** — and asks you to live by it.

**Yaad rakho:** Chaaro theme ek dhaage se bandhe hain — *asal taraazu.* Pehle rizq se apna rishta theek karo (91, 92, 93), phir woh cheez dobara set karo jo tumhe asal mein qeemti banaati hai (94, 95), phir woh saaf-dili logon tak pahonchne do — khula chehra, pehla salaam (96, 97), phir sach aur jhoot ke aakhri do-raahe par khade ho (98, 99, 100). Chapter 8 ne *asal taulna* sikhaaya, Chapter 9 ne *ghaib par bharosa*; Chapter 10 woh **taraazu** thama deta hai jo Allah khud istemaal karta hai — aur kehta hai: isi par jiyo.

Work through each ḥadīth below. For each, ask yourself: *can I explain this hadith to a classmate in one sentence, in Urdu, without reading the book?* (*Kya main yeh hadith ek classmate ko ek line mein Urdu mein samjha sakti hoon, bina kitaab dekhe?*) If yes — move on. If not — re-read.

> **A note on order:** the book gives these in numeric order (91 → 100), and my four themes are *already* in numeric order (91–93 · 94–95 · 96–97 · 98–100), each a clean run of consecutive aḥādīth. So this chapter needs **no re-ordering** — the book's sequence and our teaching arc are the same (as in Chapters 8 and 9).

> **A note on the Arabic:** like the last two chapters, **all ten matns this chapter are short and clearly printed**, so I give the Arabic for *every one* — read off the rendered page (pp. 55–57), harakaat and all. Three spots to watch (all verified off the page): (a) #92 *nafsan* + *lan* prints the **idghām shadda** on the *lām* (*nafsan lan* → the page marks it: نَفْسًا لَّنْ); (b) #94 *bi-khayrin* + *min* likewise prints the **idghām shadda** on the *mīm* (بِخَيْرٍ مِّنْ); and (c) #99 the bitter plant is **الصَّبِر (aṣ-ṣabir — aloe, kasra on the bāʾ)**, *not* الصَّبْر (ṣabr, "patience") — read the kasra off the page, it is the whole image.

## Theme 1 · Provision is decreed — keep it clean

## 91 · إِنَّ الرَّجُلَ لَيُحْرَمُ الرِّزْقَ بِالذَّنْبِ يُصِيْبُهُ — "A person is deprived of provision by the sin he commits"

**Source:** Ibn Māja, Mustadrak.

### Sin has a hidden cost — but read "provision" the way the book reads it

**A man is deprived of *rizq* (provision) because of a *dhanb* (sin) he commits.** Before this gets misread, hear the book's own careful gloss — it gives "livelihood" *two* meanings: it *"either means the reward of the next world, which a person is deprived of by the sins he commits, or the livelihood of this world (wealth, health, and general well-being), which a Muslim is deprived of so that his sins can be removed and his status in the Hereafter elevated"* (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 8:662). So the "deprivation" is not a crude "sin → you go broke" rule. It is either a loss of *ākhira* reward, or a *barakah*/well-being that is withdrawn — and even that withdrawal can be a *mercy* that wipes the sin and raises the rank. The point that lands: sin is never free; it quietly costs you something, often something you can't see.

The landmine is obvious and I flag it now: this is **not** a licence to look at a poor person and say "he must be sinful," or at a rich person and say "he must be righteous." The book's *barakah* reading rules that out — and the very next ḥadīth (92) blocks it completely.

**Yaad rakho:** **Insaan ek *gunaah (dhanb)* ki wajah se *rizq* se mehroom hota hai.** Ghalat samajhne se pehle kitaab ki apni saaf taʿreef suno — "rizq" ke *do* maʿne diye: ya to *aakhirat ka ajr* jis se gunaah mehroom kar deta hai, ya *duniya ka rizq (maal, sehat, khair-o-aafiyat)* jo musalmaan se isliye le liya jaata hai *taake uske gunaah mit jaayein aur aakhirat mein darja buland ho* (Mirqāt 8:662). To "mehroomi" koi *"gunaah → kangaal"* ka seedha qanoon nahin. Ya aakhirat ke ajr ka nuqsaan, ya barakat/aafiyat ka uthna — aur woh uthna bhi *rehmat* ho sakta hai jo gunaah dho de aur darja barha de. Sabaq: gunaah kabhi muft nahin — chupke se kuch na kuch cheen leta hai, aksar woh jo nazar nahin aata. **Landmine:** yeh kisi gareeb ko "gunehgaar" ya kisi ameer ko "neek" kehne ka parwaana **nahin** (#92 ise bilkul band kar deti hai).

## 92 · إِنَّ نَفْسًا لَّنْ تَمُوْتَ حَتَّى تَسْتَكْمِلَ رِزْقَهَا — "No soul will die until it completes its provision"

**Source:** Ibn Māja.

### Your provision is guaranteed in full — so #91 can't mean "the poor are sinners"

**No soul will die until it has *fully received* (tastakmila) all of its rizq.** The book's commentary here is a single line — *"See ḥadīth 87 and 91"* — and that cross-reference is the teaching. Hold this with #87 from last chapter (*provision pursues you as surely as your death*) and with #91 just above. Together they form a complete picture: every soul will receive *every last portion* decreed for it before it dies — the decreed amount is *guaranteed and complete*. So #91's "deprivation" can never mean a believer is starved of his decreed provision; it means a *barakah* or an *ākhira*-reward is adjusted. The guaranteed floor stays.

This is why these two sit together. #91 says *sin costs you something*; #92 immediately guarantees *your decreed rizq still arrives in full*. The believer is freed from two errors at once: he won't sin chasing provision (it's already coming, #92), and he won't despair or judge by wealth (the loss in #91 is barakah/reward, not the decreed floor).

**Yaad rakho:** **Koi jaan tab tak nahin marti jab tak apna poora rizq *paa* (tastakmila) na le.** Kitaab ki sharah yahaan ek hi line hai — *"hadith 87 aur 91 dekho"* — aur yahi hawaala asal sabaq hai. Ise pichle chapter ki #87 (*rizq tumhe maut ki tarah dhoondta hai*) aur abhi upar #91 ke saath rakho. Mil kar mukammal tasveer banti hai: har jaan apna *aakhri hissa* tak le kar marti hai — muqarrar miqdaar *zamānat-shuda aur poori* hai. Isliye #91 ki "mehroomi" ka matlab kabhi yeh nahin ke momin apne *muqarrar* rizq se bhooka rahe; matlab barakat ya aakhirat-ka-ajr ghata. Zamānat wali sطح qaayam rehti hai. Isi liye yeh dono saath hain: #91 kehti hai *gunaah ki qeemat hai*; #92 foran zamānat deti hai *tera muqarrar rizq phir bhi poora aayega.* Momin do ghaltiyon se ek saath aazaad: na rizq ke peechhe gunaah karega (woh aa raha hai, #92), na maal se naap kar mayoosi/faisla karega (#91 ka nuqsaan barakat/ajr hai, muqarrar farsh nahin).

## 93 · إِنَّ الصَّدَقَةَ لَتُطْفِئُ غَضَبَ الرَّبِّ وَتَدْفَعُ مِيْتَةَ السُّوْءِ — "Charity extinguishes the Lord's anger and wards off an evil death"

**Source:** Tirmidhī, Ibn Ḥibbān.

### If sin withdraws barakah, charity is the open hand that draws mercy back

**Voluntary charity (ṣadaqa) *extinguishes* (tuṭfiʾu) the anger of the Lord and *wards off* (tadfaʿu) an evil death (mītat al-sūʾ).** Set against #91, this is the cure placed beside the disease. If a sin can quietly withdraw barakah and invite Allah's displeasure, then *ṣadaqa* — giving from the very provision Allah guaranteed you in #92 — does the opposite: it *puts out* the fire of His anger like water on a flame (that is the force of *tuṭfiʾu*, "extinguishes"), and it *pushes away* a *mītat al-sūʾ*, an evil or wretched death. The open hand draws mercy back where the closing hand of sin pushed it away.

The teaching landmine here (flagged for Part B): keep this from sliding into a *transactional*, almost superstitious view — "drop a coin, dodge a bad death" like a toll you pay. It is the *spiritual posture* of generosity, by Allah's grace, that turns away wrath; charity given grudgingly or to buy off fate is not the ṣadaqa being praised. The heart behind the hand is what Allah weighs (which is exactly where Theme 2 is about to go).

**Yaad rakho:** **Sadaqa Rabb ke *ghusse ko bujha* (tuṭfiʾu) deta hai aur *buri maut (mītat al-sūʾ) ko taal* (tadfaʿu) deta hai.** #91 ke samne yeh marz ke paas rakha hua ilaaj hai. Agar gunaah chupke se barakat utha kar Allah ki naaraazgi laata hai, to *sadaqa* — usi rizq mein se dena jiski Allah ne #92 mein zamānat di — uska ulta karta hai: ghusse ki aag ko paani ki tarah *bujha* deta hai (yahi *tuṭfiʾu* ki taaqat hai), aur ek *buri maut* ko *dhakel* deta hai. Khula haath rehmat ko wapas kheenchta hai jahaan gunaah ke band haath ne use dhakela tha. **Landmine (Part B ke liye):** ise *len-den* / tawahhum-parasti na bana do — "sikka daalo, buri maut se bach jaao" jaise koi toll. Yeh sakhaawat ka *roohani rawayya* hai jo Allah ke fazl se ghussa taalta hai; behdili se ya taqdeer khareedne ke liye diya gaya sadaqa woh nahin jiski taʿreef hai. Haath ke peechhe ka dil hi woh cheez hai jise Allah taulta hai (aur Theme 2 wahi ja rahi hai).

## Theme 2 · Allah weighs the heart, not the surface

## 94 · إِنَّكَ لَسْتَ بِخَيْرٍ مِّنْ أَحْمَرَ وَلَا أَسْوَدَ إِلَّا أَنْ تَفْضُلَهُ بِتَقْوَى — "You are no better than a red or black man — except by taqwā"

**Source:** Aḥmad.

### Islam's charter of equality: the only superiority is piety

**You are not better than a "red"- [the book notes: i.e. white-] skinned or a black-skinned person, *except* that you excel him in *taqwā*.** This is the chapter's pivot and one of the great equality statements of the dīn. Every line people draw between themselves — race, colour, lineage, nationality — is wiped off the scale. The book grounds it exactly where the Qurʾān grounds it, quoting the verse in its own commentary: *"O mankind! We created you from a single [pair of] male and female, and made you into nations and tribes, that you may know each other. Verily the most honoured of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous of you"* (49:13). The *only* axis of superiority left standing is **taqwā** — God-consciousness — and the book even ranks it in three grades (abstaining from shirk; abstaining from sins and heedlessness; and constant presence with Allah, *ḥuḍūr*, absent from all besides Him — Mirqāt 7:54).

Two landmines (both for Part B). First, don't let this pass as a polite throwaway — it is a *demolition* of racism, spoken to a man who thought his colour made him better. Second, watch the *reverse* danger: if taqwā is the one thing that raises you, the ego will instantly try to claim it — "then *I* am superior, I'm the pious one." But taqwā that you *parade* is not taqwā; the moment you look down on the "less religious" you have lost the very thing you claimed. Real taqwā humbles; it never struts.

**Yaad rakho:** **Tum kisi "surkh"- (kitaab: yaʿni gore-) ya kaale shakhs se behtar nahin — *siwaaye* iske ke taqwa mein us se aage ho.** Yeh chapter ka markaz aur deen ke azeem barabari ke bayanaat mein se hai. Har lakeer jo log apne aur doosron ke darmiyaan kheenchte hain — nasl, rang, khaandaan, qaumiyat — taraazu se mita di gayi. Kitaab ise wahin se jodti hai jahaan Quran jodta hai, apni sharah mein aayat naqal kar ke: *"Aye logon! Humne tumhe ek (jode) mard-o-aurat se paida kiya, aur qaumein aur qabeele banaaye taake ek doosre ko pehchaano. Beshak Allah ke nazdeek tum mein sab se zyada izzat waala woh hai jo sab se zyada parhezgaar hai"* (49:13). Buland karne wali *واحد* cheez = **taqwa.** **Do landmines (Part B):** (1) ise halki si baat na samjho — yeh nasl-parasti ki *imaarat giraana* hai, us shakhs se kaha gaya jo apna rang behtar samajhta tha. (2) *Ulta* khatra: agar taqwa hi buland karta hai to nafs foran daawa karega — "to *main* behtar hoon, main deendaar." Magar jis taqwa ki *numaaish* karo woh taqwa nahin; jis lamhe tum "kam-deendaar" ko haqeer samjho, woh cheez hi kho di jis ka daawa kiya. Asli taqwa jhukaata hai, akadta nahin.

## 95 · إِنَّ اللهَ لَا يَنْظُرُ إِلَى صُوَرِكُمْ وَأَمْوَالِكُمْ، وَلٰكِنْ يَنْظُرُ إِلَى قُلُوْبِكُمْ وَأَعْمَالِكُمْ — "Allah looks not at your forms and wealth, but at your hearts and deeds"

**Source:** Muslim.

### The camera angle of Allah: He photographs the heart

**Allah does not look at your *ṣuwar* (forms, outward appearances) and your *amwāl* (wealth), but He looks at your *qulūb* (hearts) and your *aʿmāl* (deeds).** If #94 told you the *one axis* that raises a person (taqwā), #95 tells you *where Allah's gaze actually falls* to measure it. He does not photograph the face or the bank balance; He photographs the *heart* and what it produces in *action*. The book unpacks both halves: "looks at your hearts" = at the conviction, sincerity, truthfulness, or ostentation harboured within; "looks at your actions" = at their validity and soundness, or their corruption — and He rewards each accordingly (Mirqāt 9:175).

Notice the pairing the ḥadīth makes: *heart **and** deed*, not heart alone. A sound heart must show up in sound action; an inner claim with no outward deed, or a polished deed with a rotten intention, both fail the test. This is the natural completion of Theme 1 — there we learned not to measure ourselves by *wealth* (#91/#92); here Allah Himself confirms wealth is simply *not on His scale*. The surface is invisible to the One whose opinion is the only one that counts.

**Yaad rakho:** **Allah tumhari *sooraton (ṣuwar)* aur *maal (amwāl)* ko nahin dekhta, balke tumhare *dilon (qulūb)* aur *aʿmaal* ko dekhta hai.** Agar #94 ne woh *واحد* cheez batayi jo bande ko buland karti hai (taqwa), to #95 batati hai ke Allah ki nazar use *naapne kahaan girti* hai. Woh chehra ya bank-balance ki tasveer nahin leta; woh *dil* aur uske *amal* ki tasveer leta hai. Kitaab dono hisse kholti hai: "dil dekhta hai" = us yaqeen, ikhlaas, sachaai, ya dikhaawe ko jo andar hai; "amal dekhta hai" = uski durustagi ya kharaabi ko — aur har ek ka usi hisaab se badla deta hai (Mirqāt 9:175). Hadith ka jod dekho: *dil **aur** amal*, sirf dil nahin. Saaf dil ko saaf amal mein zaahir hona chahiye; andaruni daawa bila-amal, ya chamakta amal sade niyyat ke saath — dono fail. Yeh Theme 1 ki takmeel hai — wahaan seekha apne aap ko *maal* se na naapo; yahaan Allah khud tasdeeq karta hai ke maal Uski taraazu par hai hi nahin.

## Theme 3 · Carry it to people — an open face, the first salām

## 96 · إِنَّ مِنَ الْمَعْرُوْفِ أَنْ تَلْقَى أَخَاكَ بِوَجْهٍ طَلْقٍ — "It is a kindness to meet your brother with a cheerful face"

**Source:** Muslim, Tirmidhī.

### The cheapest good deed, and a real one

**It is part of *al-maʿrūf* (goodness, kindness) that you meet your brother with a *wajh ṭalq* — a cheerful, open, smiling face.** After the inner scale of Theme 2, the chapter turns the heart's soundness *outward*, and starts with the smallest possible act: how your face looks when you meet a fellow believer. The ḥadīth dignifies it — a warm face is counted *maʿrūf*, the same broad word the Qurʾān uses for all recognised good. It costs nothing, requires no wealth (which #95 just told us Allah doesn't weigh anyway), and yet it is a genuine ṣadaqa of the face: it gladdens a brother's heart, removes coldness, and builds the affection (*ulfa*) the believers are meant to have.

The quiet link back: a *sound heart* (#95) naturally produces a *kind face* (#96). You cannot reliably fake warmth — a scowling, contemptuous face usually reports a heart that holds the other person cheap. So the smile is not mere etiquette; it is the heart's soundness becoming visible to people. Don't overcomplicate it: the lesson is exactly as small and exactly as serious as it sounds.

**Yaad rakho:** **Neki (al-maʿrūf) mein se yeh bhi hai ke tum apne bhai se *khile chehre (wajh ṭalq)* — muskuraate, khule chehre — se milo.** Theme 2 ke andaruni taraazu ke baad, chapter dil ki saaf-dili ko *baahar* modta hai, aur sab se chhote amal se shuru karta hai: jab kisi momin se milo to tumhara chehra kaisa lagta hai. Hadith ise izzat deti hai — garmjosh chehra *maʿrūf* gina gaya, wahi kushaada lafz jo Quran har pehchaani neki ke liye istemaal karta hai. Kuch kharch nahin, koi maal nahin chahiye (jo #95 keh chuki Allah taulta hi nahin), phir bhi yeh chehre ka asli sadaqa hai: bhai ka dil khush karta hai, sardmehri hataata hai, aur woh *ulfa* banaata hai jo momineen mein honi chahiye. Khaamosh jod: *saaf dil* (#95) qudrati taur par *meherbaan chehra* (#96) paida karta hai. Garmjoshi ka dhong mushkil hai — tursha, haqaarat-bhara chehra aksar us dil ki khabar deta hai jo doosre ko haqeer samajhta hai. To muskurahat sirf aadaab nahin; yeh dil ki saaf-dili ka logon ke saamne zaahir hona hai.

## 97 · إِنَّ أَوْلَى النَّاسِ بِاللهِ مَنْ بَدَأَهُمْ بِالسَّلَام — "The closest of people to Allah is the one who greets first"

**Source:** Abū Dāwūd.

### Beat them to the peace

**The *closest of people to Allah* (awlā n-nāsi bi-Llāh) is the one who *begins* (badaʾahum) the salām.** The book's note is precise: "closest to Allah" means in *receiving His mercy and forgiveness*; and "begins the salām" means the one who *says the salām to others first*. Theme 3 climbs from the cheerful face (#96) to the spoken greeting — and crowns the one who does not wait to be greeted but *initiates*. Why is the initiator nearer to Allah? Because beginning the salām takes a small death of the ego — you don't stand on your dignity waiting to be honoured; you give honour and peace first, to high and low alike. That humility, that eagerness to spread *salām* (peace), is what draws a person nearest to the Most Merciful.

Tie the theme together: a sound heart (#95) → a warm face (#96) → and a tongue quick to greet first (#97). All three are the *outward radiation* of the inner soundness Theme 2 located. And note how light the demands are — a smile, a "as-salāmu ʿalaykum" said first — yet the reward is to be *the closest of people to Allah.* The dīn prices sincerity, not spectacle.

**Yaad rakho:** **Logon mein Allah ke *sab se qareeb (awlā n-nāsi bi-Llāh)* woh hai jo salaam mein *pehl* (badaʾahum) kare.** Kitaab ki wazaahat saaf hai: "Allah ke qareeb" yaʿni *Uski rehmat aur maghfirat paane mein*; aur "salaam mein pehl" yaʿni jo doosron ko *pehle* salaam kare. Theme 3 khile chehre (#96) se bol kar salaam tak charhti hai — aur usko taaj pehnaati hai jo salaam ka intezaar nahin karta balke *khud shuru* karta hai. Pehl karne wala Allah ke qareeb kyun? Kyunki salaam mein pehl karne mein nafs ki chhoti si maut hai — tum apni "izzat" par khade ho kar intezaar nahin karte ke koi tumhe izzat de; tum pehle izzat aur aman dete ho, chhote-bade sab ko. Yahi tawazuʿ, yahi *salaam (aman)* phailaane ki tezi, bande ko sab se Raheem ke qareeb kheenchti hai. Theme ka jod: saaf dil (#95) → garm chehra (#96) → aur zabaan jo pehle salaam kare (#97). Teeno andaruni saaf-dili ki *baahar phailti roshni* hain. Aur taqaaze kitne halke — ek muskurahat, ek pehle kaha "as-salāmu ʿalaykum" — magar inʿaam yeh ke *logon mein Allah ke sab se qareeb.*

## Theme 4 · What ruins, what saves — the closing summons

## 98 · إِنَّ الرِّبَا وَإِنْ كَثُرَ فَإِنَّ عَاقِبَتَهُ تَصِيْرُ إِلَى قُلٍّ — "Interest, however much it grows, ends in scarcity"

**Source:** Ibn Māja, Aḥmad, Mustadrak.

### What looks like increase is, at the end, ruin

**Interest (ribā), however much it *increases* (wa-in kathura), its *outcome* (ʿāqibatahu) comes *to scarcity / penury* (qull).** Theme 4 opens the final fork with the great economic illusion: ribā *looks* like multiplication — the number on the statement grows — but the ḥadīth says read the *ʿāqiba*, the end-state, and you find *qull*: littleness, want, ruin. The book sharpens what kind of ruin it means: wealth from interest "will not only be devoid of divine value or blessing, but it will lead the earner to disgrace and *spiritual* poverty" — and spiritual poverty is *worse* than financial poverty, because it can lead a person to *kufr* (Allah forbid), while many who live meagre lives never approach kufr because their souls are healthy and tasting the sweetness of faith (the book points on to ḥadīth 110).

The landmine (for Part B): do **not** flatten this into a naive economic prediction — "everyone who takes interest goes bankrupt." Some interest-earners die rich. The ruin the ḥadīth names is the *barakah-stripped, value-hollowed, faith-endangering* end — the same "deprivation" register as #91 (lost barakah), now at full volume. The number can rise while the *ʿāqiba* falls. That is exactly the chapter's deep thread one more time: don't read the surface (the growing figure); read the real measure (the end-state of the soul).

**Yaad rakho:** **Sood (ribā), chahe kitna bhi *barhe* (wa-in kathura), uska *anjaam* (ʿāqiba) *qillat/tabaahi (qull)* par aata hai.** Theme 4 aakhri do-raahe ko sab se bade maʿaashi dhoke se kholti hai: ribā *barhta dikhta* hai — statement ka number barhta hai — magar hadith kehti hai *ʿāqiba* (anjaam) parho, wahaan *qull* milega: kami, mohtaaji, tabaahi. Kitaab batati hai kaisi tabaahi: sood ka maal "na sirf Allah ki qadar-o-barkat se khaali hoga, balke kamaane wale ko ruswai aur *roohani* gurbat tak le jaayega" — aur roohani gurbat maali gurbat se *badtar* hai, kyunki woh insaan ko *kufr* tak le ja sakti hai (Allah bachaaye), jabke bohot se tang-haali waale kabhi kufr ke qareeb nahin jaate kyunki unke dil tandurust hain aur imaan ki mithaas chakh rahe hain (kitaab hadith 110 ki taraf ishaara karti hai). **Landmine (Part B):** ise saada maʿaashi peshangoi mat banao — "har sood-khor diwaaliya ho jaata hai." Kuch sood-khor ameer marte hain. Jo tabaahi hadith bata rahi hai woh *barakat-cheen, qeemat-khokli, imaan-khatre* wali aakhir hai — wahi "mehroomi" jo #91 mein thi, ab poori awaaz mein. Number barh sakta hai jabke *ʿāqiba* girti hai. Yahi chapter ka dhaaga phir se: upar-upar (barhta number) mat parho; asal taraazu (rooh ka anjaam) parho.

---

## 99 · إِنَّ الْغَضَبَ لَيُفْسِدُ الْإِيْمَانَ كَمَا يُفْسِدُ الصَّبِرُ الْعَسَلَ — "Anger ruins faith as the aloe ruins honey"

**Source:** Bayhaqī, Shuʿab al-Īmān.

### The corrosive that eats faith's sweetness

**Anger (al-ghaḍab) *ruins* (yufsidu) faith just as *al-ṣabir* — the bitter aloe plant — *ruins* honey.** (Read the harakah off the page: it is *ṣabir*, the bitter plant, **not** *ṣabr*, "patience" — the whole image depends on it: a bitter substance dropped into sweet honey, fouling it.) The book gives the exact register of "ruins": *"its completeness, light, and beauty"* — i.e. uncontrolled anger doesn't usually *delete* faith outright, it *spoils* it: drains the sweetness, dims the light, wrecks the perfection. And then the book adds the heavy clause: *"sometimes it could actually lead to the destruction of one's faith"* (Mirqāt 8:843) — when anger drives a person to speech or action that breaks faith.

The landmine (for Part B): this is **not** "every flash of anger costs you your īmān," and it is **not** a condemnation of *all* anger — the dīn knows a righteous anger *for Allah's sake* (the Prophet ﷺ became angry when Allah's limits were violated). The target is *al-ghaḍab* as the *uncontrolled, ego-driven* rage that hijacks a person — the kind that drops bitterness into the honey of a believing heart. Teaching it as "feeling angry = loss of faith" will only breed anxiety; teach it as *guard the sweetness of your faith from the corrosive of unrestrained anger.*

**Yaad rakho:** **Ghussa (al-ghaḍab) imaan ko aise *saada* deta hai (yufsidu) jaise *al-ṣabir* — kadwa aloe (ailwa) ka paudha — shahad ko saada deta hai.** (Harakah page se parho: yeh *ṣabir* hai, kadwa paudha, *ṣabr* "sabar" **nahin** — poori tasveer isi par hai: meethe shahad mein kadwi cheez gir kar use kharaab kar de.) Kitaab "saada deta hai" ka theek darja batati hai: *"uski takmeel, noor, aur khoobsoorti"* — yaʿni be-qaaboo ghussa aam taur par imaan ko *mitaata* nahin, *kharaab* karta hai: mithaas nichod leta hai, noor dhuandhla karta hai, kamaal todta hai. Phir kitaab bhaari jumla jodti hai: *"kabhi yeh imaan ki tabaahi tak bhi le ja sakta hai"* (Mirqāt 8:843) — jab ghussa insaan ko aisi baat/amal par majboor kare jo imaan tod de. **Landmine (Part B):** yeh "har ghusse ki lehar imaan le leti hai" **nahin**, aur yeh *har* ghusse ki mazammat **nahin** — deen *Allah ke liye* neik ghusse ko jaanta hai (Nabi ﷺ ko Allah ki hadood tootne par ghussa aata tha). Nishaana *al-ghaḍab* yaʿni woh *be-qaaboo, nafs-ka* ghussa hai jo insaan ko hijack kar le. "Ghussa aana = imaan ka jaana" sikhaaoge to sirf bechaini phailegi; sikhaao: *be-lagaam ghusse ki kadwaahat se apne imaan ki mithaas ki hifaazat karo.*

## 100 · إِنَّ الصِّدْقَ بِرٌّ وَإِنَّ الْبِرَّ يَهْدِي إِلَى الْجَنَّةِ، وَإِنَّ الْكَذِبَ فُجُوْرٌ وَإِنَّ الْفُجُوْرَ يَهْدِي إِلَى النَّارِ — "Truth is piety, and piety leads to Paradise; falsehood is iniquity, and iniquity leads to the Fire"

**Source:** Muslim, Abū Dāwūd.

### The capstone of the hundred: two roads, one choice

**Truth (ṣidq) is *birr* (piety, righteousness), and birr *leads* (yahdī) to Paradise; and falsehood (kadhib) is *fujūr* (iniquity, wickedness), and fujūr leads to the Fire.** This is the final ḥadīth of the hundred, and it is built like a gate with two roads through it. Notice the *chain* in each half — truth is not merely *rewarded* with Paradise; truth *is* birr, and birr *guides you onward* to Paradise. Falsehood *is* fujūr, and fujūr *guides you onward* to the Fire. Each is a *road with momentum*: one truth makes the next easier and bends the whole life toward goodness and the Garden; one lie loosens the next and bends the whole life toward corruption and the Fire. A person keeps walking until — in the famous fuller wording — he is *written down* with Allah as a *ṣiddīq* (utterly truthful) or a *kadhdhāb* (an inveterate liar).

This is the perfect close to the whole series. Everything the hundred taught — clean provision, a sound heart, taqwā over colour, a kind face, the spread of salām, the flight from ribā and rage — all of it comes down to the *direction* you face: toward *ṣidq* or *kadhib*, *birr* or *fujūr*, the Garden or the Fire. The book has spent a hundred sayings furnishing the believer's inner and outer life; it ends by pointing at the *destination* and the single habit — *truthfulness* — that walks you there. (Teaching landmine: it is about the *habituated direction* of a life, not "one lie damns you forever"; truthfulness is the road back as much as the road on.)

**Yaad rakho:** **Sach (ṣidq) *birr* (neki) hai, aur birr Jannat ki taraf *le jaata* (yahdī) hai; aur jhoot (kadhib) *fujūr* (badi) hai, aur fujūr Aag ki taraf le jaata hai.** Yeh sau ki aakhri hadith hai, aur yeh do raston wala darwaza hai. Har hisse mein *zanjeer* dekho — sach ko sirf Jannat ka *badla* nahin milta; sach *hai* birr, aur birr tumhe Jannat ki taraf *aage le jaata* hai. Jhoot *hai* fujūr, aur fujūr Aag ki taraf le jaata hai. Har ek *raftaar waala raasta* hai: ek sach agle ko aasaan karta hai aur poori zindagi ko bhalaai aur Jannat ki taraf modta hai; ek jhoot agle ko dheela karta hai aur poori zindagi ko kharaabi aur Aag ki taraf. Insaan chalta rehta hai yahaan tak ke — mashhoor mukammal alfaaz mein — Allah ke yahaan *ṣiddīq* (intihaai saccha) ya *kadhdhāb* (pakka jhootha) *likh diya* jaata hai. Yeh poore silsile ka mukammal ikhtitaam hai. Jo kuch sau hadithon ne sikhaaya — halaal rizq, saaf dil, rang par taqwa, meherbaan chehra, salaam ka phailna, ribā aur ghusse se bhaagna — sab us *rukh* par aa jaata hai jis taraf tum mooh karte ho: *ṣidq* ya *kadhib*, *birr* ya *fujūr*, Jannat ya Aag. (**Landmine:** yeh zindagi ke *aadat-shuda rukh* ki baat hai, "ek jhoot hamesha ke liye barbaad kar deta hai" nahin; sachaai jitna aage ka raasta hai utna hi wapsi ka bhi.)

---

## The big picture — what these 10 aḥādīth (and the whole hundred) are silently building

Stand back. This final chapter trains the believer in **four movements**, all under one discipline — *the real measure*:

1. **Provision is decreed — keep it clean** — sin quietly costs you barakah/reward (91), yet your decreed provision still arrives in full (92), so give from it: charity puts out Allah's anger and wards off an evil death (93).
2. **Allah weighs the heart, not the surface** — no-one is superior by race or colour, only by taqwā (94); and Allah looks not at your forms and wealth but at your hearts and deeds (95).
3. **Carry it to people — an open face, the first salām** — a sound heart shows on the face: meeting your brother cheerfully is a good deed (96), and greeting first puts you closest to Allah (97).
4. **What ruins, what saves — the closing summons** — interest ends in ruin (98), anger rots faith's sweetness (99), and the capstone: truth is the road to Paradise, falsehood the road to the Fire (100).

And step back further still — this is the **end of the hundred.** The whole of Part 1 has moved through *faith → character → the world*, and these three closing chapters form their own arc: Chapter 8 taught you to **weigh the reality, not the label** (baṣīra); Chapter 9 taught you to **trust the unseen**; and Chapter 10 hands you the **scale Allah Himself uses** — and ends by pointing down the two roads that scale opens onto. The deepest current under all hundred is this: *do not live by the surface — by what you own, how you look, what people see — live by the heart, the deed, the truth, because that is what Allah weighs and that is where the two roads begin.* The hundred ends not with information but with a **fork**, and a single habit — *ṣidq*, truthfulness — that walks the believer through the right gate.

**Yaad rakho:** 10 hadith — magar 4 harkatein, ek tarbiyat: *asal taraazu.* (1) *Rizq likha hai — use saaf rakho* (91, 92, 93). (2) *Allah dil taulta hai, upar-upar nahin* (94, 95). (3) *Use logon tak pahonchao — khula chehra, pehla salaam* (96, 97). (4) *Kya giraata hai, kya bachaata hai — aakhri pukaar* (98, 99, 100). Aur aur peechhe hato — yeh *sau ka ikhtitaam* hai. Poora Part 1 *imaan → akhlaaq → duniya* se guzra, aur yeh aakhri teen chapter apna ek arc banate hain: Chapter 8 ne *asal taulna* (baṣīra), Chapter 9 ne *ghaib par bharosa*, aur Chapter 10 woh **taraazu** thama deta hai jo Allah khud istemaal karta hai — aur un do raston par khatm hota hai jo woh taraazu kholti hai. Sab sau ke neeche dhaaga: *upar-upar se mat jiyo — jo tumhare paas hai, kaise dikhte ho, log kya dekhte hain — dil, amal, aur sach se jiyo, kyunki yahi Allah taulta hai aur yahin se do raaste shuru hote hain.* Sau ka ikhtitaam maʿloomaat par nahin — ek *do-raahe* par, aur ek aadat — *ṣidq, sachaai* — jo momin ko sahi darwaze se le jaati hai.

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

Now you understand the chapter. This section is how to *deliver* it to your classmates — and, because this is the **final chapter of the hundred**, how to land it as a milestone.

---

## The 5 landmines to watch for

### Landmine 1 · #91 — "so poverty proves sin, and wealth proves righteousness"

The wording — "a man is deprived of provision by his sin" — invites a cruel inversion: *look at the poor and judge them sinful, look at the rich and call them righteous.* The book blocks this directly. "Provision" here is read as *either* the reward of the next world *or* a withdrawn worldly barakah — and that withdrawal can be a *mercy* that wipes the sin and raises the rank. And the very next ḥadīth (92) guarantees every soul receives its decreed provision *in full*. So this is **not** a wealth-meter for judging people.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh hadith gareeb ko 'gunehgaar' aur ameer ko 'neek' kehne ka parwaana nahin. Kitaab khud 'rizq' ke do maʿne deti hai: ya aakhirat ka ajr, ya duniya ki barakat jo le li jaati hai — aur woh lena bhi rehmat ho sakti hai jo gunaah dho de aur darja barha de. Aur agli hadith (#92) zamānat deti hai ke har jaan apna poora muqarrar rizq paati hai. To kisi ke maal ya gareebi se uske gunaah ka faisla mat karo — sabaq apne liye hai: gunaah kabhi muft nahin, chupke se barakat cheen leta hai."*

### Landmine 2 · #94 — "fine, equality — but then *I'm* the superior one, I'm the pious"

Two traps in one ḥadīth. First, don't let "you're no better than a red or black man" pass as a *polite* line — it is a *demolition* of racism, spoken to a man who thought his colour raised him. Make the cohort feel its force. Second, the *reverse* danger: if taqwā is the one thing that raises you, the ego instantly claims it — "then *I* am better, I'm the religious one." But taqwā you *parade* is gone the moment you look down on the "less religious." Real taqwā humbles.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Pehli baat: yeh halki si baat nahin — yeh nasl-parasti ki imaarat giraana hai. Rang, khaandaan, qaumiyat — sab taraazu se mit gaye; buland karne wali واحد cheez taqwa hai (Quran 49:13: 'sab se baizzat woh jo sab se parhezgaar'). Doosri baat — khabardaar: agar taqwa hi buland karta hai to nafs foran kahega 'to main behtar hoon.' Magar jis taqwa ki numaaish karo woh taqwa nahin; jis lamhe tum kisi 'kam-deendaar' ko haqeer samjho, woh cheez kho di jiska daawa kiya. Asli taqwa jhukaata hai, akadta nahin."*

### Landmine 3 · #98 — "so every person who takes interest goes bankrupt" (a naive economic claim)

"Interest ends in penury" is easy to hear as a *worldly prediction* — and then someone objects, "but plenty of interest-earners die rich." Don't get cornered there. The book's "penury" is *spiritual*: wealth stripped of barakah and divine value, leading to disgrace and even endangering faith (it can lead to *kufr*) — and spiritual poverty is worse than financial. The *number* on the statement can rise while the *ʿāqiba* (the end-state of the soul) falls. It is #91's "lost barakah" at full volume.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh saada maʿaashi peshangoi nahin ke 'har sood-khor diwaaliya ho jaata hai' — kuch ameer bhi marte hain. Jo 'qillat/tabaahi' hadith bata rahi hai woh roohani hai: maal barakat aur Allah ki qadar se khaali, jo ruswai aur imaan ke khatre (kufr tak) le jaa sakta hai — aur roohani gurbat maali gurbat se badtar hai. Number barh sakta hai jabke anjaam (ʿāqiba) girti hai. Yahi #91 ki 'cheeni hui barakat' poori awaaz mein. Sabaq: upar-upar barhta number mat dekho, rooh ka anjaam dekho."*

### Landmine 4 · #99 — "so any time I feel angry, my faith is gone"

The image is strong — anger rots faith like the bitter aloe rots honey — and an anxious student will hear "feeling angry = losing īmān." Correct it two ways. First, "ruins" = ruins faith's *completeness, light, and beauty* (the book's words); it usually *spoils*, not *deletes* — though unrestrained anger *can* drive someone to faith-breaking words/acts. Second, not *all* anger is condemned — there is a righteous anger *for Allah's sake*. The target is the *uncontrolled, ego-driven* rage that hijacks you.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh 'ghussa aana = imaan ka jaana' nahin. Kitaab kehti hai ghussa imaan ki 'takmeel, noor, aur khoobsoorti' ko saada deta hai — aam taur par mitaata nahin, kharaab karta hai (haan, be-qaaboo ghussa kabhi imaan-tod baat/amal tak le ja sakta hai). Aur har ghusse ki mazammat nahin — Allah ke liye neik ghussa to Nabi ﷺ ko bhi aata tha jab Allah ki hadood tooti. Nishaana woh be-lagaam, nafs-ka ghussa hai jo tumhe hijack kar le. Sabaq: be-qaaboo ghusse ki kadwaahat se apne imaan ki mithaas bachaao — har jazbe se mat daro."*

### Landmine 5 · #93 — "so charity is a coin you pay to dodge a bad death" (transactional/superstitious)

"Charity wards off an evil death and puts out the Lord's anger" can curdle into a *transaction* — drop a coin like a toll, buy off fate. Keep it spiritual. It is the *posture of generosity*, by Allah's grace, that draws mercy back; ṣadaqa given grudgingly, or to "purchase" a guarantee, is not the charity being praised. The heart behind the hand is what Allah weighs (#95) — so the *sincerity* of the giving is the point, not a magic mechanism.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh sikka-daalo-buri-maut-se-bach-jaao wala toll nahin. Sadaqa Allah ke fazl se rehmat wapas kheenchta hai — magar yeh sakhaawat ka roohani rawayya hai, na ke taqdeer khareedne ka sauda. Behdili se ya 'guarantee' khareedne ke liye diya gaya sadaqa woh nahin jiski taʿreef hai. Haath ke peechhe ka dil hi woh cheez hai jise Allah taulta hai (#95). Sabaq: dil se, khaalis niyyat se do — mechanism nahin, ikhlaas."*

> **Bonus watch-point · #100 (the capstone) — "so one lie damns me forever?"** No — the ḥadīth describes a *habituated direction*, not a single slip. Truth *is* birr and *guides onward* to Paradise (each truth bends the life toward the Garden); falsehood *is* fujūr and guides toward the Fire — until a person is *written* a ṣiddīq or a kadhdhāb. Truthfulness is the road *back* as much as the road *on*: one returns to ṣidq by being truthful now. Frame #100 as **hope and direction**, not a verdict.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 10

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum aage / kisi aur jagah dekhenge"* or *"yeh book ke scope se bahar hai"*:

- ❌ **The detailed fiqh of qadar (decree) — free will vs predestination, and how sin "affects" decreed rizq** triggered by #91/#92 — give only the clean point: sin costs barakah/reward, your decreed provision still arrives in full; strive calmly and lawfully. Do **not** open the jabr/qadar debate.
- ❌ **The detailed fiqh of zakāt vs ṣadaqa, nisāb, and which charity "guarantees" what** triggered by #93 — keep it to: sincere giving draws Allah's mercy and wards off evil; it is a spiritual posture, not a purchase.
- ❌ **Race theory, politics, or historical grievance debates** triggered by #94 — keep it to the dīn's clean principle: the only superiority is taqwā; humility, not pride.
- ❌ **The detailed ʿaqīda of Allah "looking" / divine attributes (and *how* He "looks at hearts")** triggered by #95 — give the heart of it: Allah weighs sincerity and deed, not appearance and wealth. Don't open the ṣifāt discussion.
- ❌ **The full fiqh of ribā — banking, mortgages, contemporary financial rulings** triggered by #98 — keep it to: interest looks like increase but ends in (spiritual) ruin; flee it. Send detailed cases to a muftī. (The book itself points on to ḥadīth 110.)
- ❌ **The fiqh of when anger is sinful, ṭalāq-in-anger, oaths-in-anger** triggered by #99 — give only: guard faith's sweetness from uncontrolled anger; righteous anger for Allah exists. Don't open the rulings.

**Yaad rakho:** Pre-loading aur tangents madad nahin karte. 10 hadithein hain — har ek apne aap mein kaafi. Khaas taur par #91/#92 par *qadar* ki behes, #95 par *sifaat* ki tafseeli behes, aur #98 par *banking/mortgage* ke tafseeli masaail mat kholo (muftī ki taraf bhej do). Class ka focus = *yeh 10 hadithein dil mein bait jaayein*, *asal taraazu* ka dhaaga samajh aaye, aur **sau ka ikhtitaam** ehtaraam se ho.

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Did they hear #91 as "the poor are sinful / the rich are righteous," or as *sin costs barakah/reward — and the decreed rizq still arrives in full* (#92)? *(Maal se faisla — ya barakat ka nuqsaan?)*
2. Did they grasp #92's guarantee — every soul completes its decreed provision — as the block on judging people by wealth? *(Poora rizq zamānat-shuda?)*
3. Did #93 land as *sincere generosity drawing mercy*, or as a *transactional coin* to dodge fate? *(Ikhlaas — ya sauda?)*
4. **Did #94 land on both sides** — racism demolished (only taqwā raises) *and* no room for spiritual pride? *(Barabari — aur ghuroor nahin?)*
5. Are they reading #95 rightly — Allah weighs *hearts and deeds*, not *forms and wealth*? *(Dil-o-amal — ya soorat-o-maal?)*
6. Did they take #96/#97 seriously — a cheerful face and the first salām as real good deeds, the heart's soundness made visible? *(Khula chehra, pehla salaam — neki?)*
7. Did they hear #98 as a naive "interest-earners go bankrupt," or as *spiritual* ruin / lost barakah (#91 at full volume)? *(Roohani tabaahi — ya saada maʿaashi daawa?)*
8. **Did #99 land correctly** — guard faith's sweetness from *uncontrolled* anger, not "every flash = loss of īmān"; righteous anger exists? *(Be-qaaboo ghussa — ya har jazba?)*
9. Can they state #100 as the capstone — truth → Paradise, falsehood → Fire, a *direction* and a *hope*, not "one lie damns you"? *(Do raaste, ek aadat — umeed?)*
10. Can they state the arc and the deep thread — **asal taraazu** — and feel that the **hundred is complete**? *(Chapter ka dhaaga — aur sau mukammal?)*

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh points tumhe batayenge kahaan revision chahiye. Agar cohort #91 (gareebi=gunaah), #94 (barabari/ghuroor), #98 (sood ki saada peshangoi), ya #99 (har ghussa=imaan ka jaana) par phans rahi hai — agle session se pehle yeh zaroor dohrao, kyunki inhi ko ghalat samajhne par poora chapter ka rukh bigad jaata hai. Aur kyunki yeh **aakhri chapter** hai — ise milestone ki tarah band karo: sau hadithein mukammal, sab ka khulasa #100 mein — *do raaste, ek aadat: sach.*
