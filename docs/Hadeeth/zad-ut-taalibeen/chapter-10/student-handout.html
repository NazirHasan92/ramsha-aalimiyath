Chapter 10 — Student Handout

Aḥādīth 91–100 from Part 1 of Zad ut-Taalibeen · the real measure · the hundred complete

👋 Welcome — what we'll learn today

Today we cover ten short aḥādīth of the Prophet ﷺ — and they are the last ten of the hundred. Read together, they re-price a life: they take the things people measure by (money, race, looks, status) and show them to be the wrong scale — then hand you the right one: clean provision, a sound heart, an open face, and the truth.

Aaj ka sabaq — das choti hadithein, aur yeh sau ki aakhri das hain. Mil kar yeh zindagi ko dobara taulti hain: jin paimaano se log naapte hain (paisa, nasl, soorat, ohda) unhein ghalat taraazu batati hain — aur sahi taraazu thama deti hain: halaal rizq, saaf dil, khula chehra, aur sach.

The 10 fall into four themes (and all continue the book's "Inna" sayings — إِنَّ… "indeed, X is Y…"):

  1. Provision — keep it clean (#91, #92, #93) — sin quietly costs you barakah, yet your decreed rizq still arrives in full, so the move is to give: charity draws Allah's mercy back.
  2. Allah weighs the heart (#94, #95) — no-one is superior by race or colour, only by taqwā; and Allah looks at your hearts and deeds, not your forms and wealth.
  3. Carry it to people (#96, #97) — a cheerful face is a real good deed, and greeting first puts you closest to Allah.
  4. What ruins, what saves (#98, #99, #100) — interest ends in ruin; anger rots faith's sweetness; and the capstone — truth leads to Paradise, falsehood to the Fire.

The deep thread beneath all ten: the real measure — Allah weighs the heart, the deed, the truth, not the surface.

Asal taraazu — Allah upar-upar nahin, dil-amal-sach taulta hai.

Ḥadīth 91 · Sin has a hidden cost

إِنَّ الرَّجُلَ لَيُحْرَمُ الرِّزْقَ بِالذَّنْبِ يُصِيْبُهُ

inna r-rajula la-yuḥramu r-rizqa bi-dh-dhanbi yuṣībuh

"A person is deprived of livelihood by the sins he commits."

"Beshak insaan ek gunaah ki wajah se rizq se mehroom ho jaata hai."

Ibn Māja, Mustadrak

Sin is never free — it quietly costs you something. The book reads "livelihood" two ways: the reward of the next world, or a worldly barakah/well-being withdrawn — and even that withdrawal can be a mercy that wipes the sin and raises your rank. So the loss is often something you can't see.

⚠️ Not a way to judge people

This is not "the poor are sinful, the rich are righteous." The next ḥadīth (92) guarantees every soul its decreed provision in full. The lesson is for you: don't sin chasing rizq.

Maal/gareebi se kisi ke gunaah ka faisla nahin. #92: muqarrar rizq poora milta hai. Sabaq apne liye: rizq ke peechhe gunaah mat karo.

Ḥadīth 92 · Your provision is guaranteed in full

إِنَّ نَفْسًا لَّنْ تَمُوْتَ حَتَّى تَسْتَكْمِلَ رِزْقَهَا

inna nafsan lan tamūta ḥattā tastakmila rizqahā

"A soul will never die until it fully receives all of its livelihood."

"Beshak koi jaan tab tak nahin marti jab tak apna poora rizq paa na le."

Ibn Māja

Your decreed provision arrives in full — every last morsel — before you die. The book's whole commentary is one line: "See ḥadīth 87 and 91." Hold the three together: provision pursues you (87), arrives complete (92), and so #91's "deprivation" can only mean a barakah or a reward, never your guaranteed floor. This frees you twice: don't sin to chase rizq, and don't judge or despair by wealth.

Ḥadīth 93 · The open hand draws mercy back

إِنَّ الصَّدَقَةَ لَتُطْفِئُ غَضَبَ الرَّبِّ وَتَدْفَعُ مِيْتَةَ السُّوْءِ

inna ṣ-ṣadaqata la-tuṭfiʾu ghaḍaba r-rabbi wa-tadfaʿu mītata s-sūʾ

"Voluntary charity extinguishes the anger of the Lord and prevents an unpleasant death."

"Beshak sadaqa Rabb ke ghusse ko bujha deta hai aur buri maut ko taal deta hai."

Tirmidhī, Ibn Ḥibbān

If sin withdraws barakah, charity draws mercy back. Giving from the very rizq Allah guaranteed you (92) does the opposite of sin: it extinguishes the Lord's anger like water on a flame, and wards off an evil death.

⚠️ Sincere, not a transaction

This isn't "drop a coin, dodge a bad death" like a toll. It is the posture of sincere generosity, by Allah's grace, that turns away wrath. The heart behind the hand is what Allah weighs (#95).

Sauda nahin. Khaalis dil se dena rehmat kheenchta hai. Dil hi taula jaata hai (#95).

Ḥadīth 94 · The only superiority is taqwā

إِنَّكَ لَسْتَ بِخَيْرٍ مِّنْ أَحْمَرَ وَلَا أَسْوَدَ إِلَّا أَنْ تَفْضُلَهُ بِتَقْوَى

innaka lasta bi-khayrin min aḥmara wa-lā aswada illā an tafḍulahu bi-taqwā

"You are not superior to a white- [lit., red], or black-skinned person, unless you excel him in piety."

"Beshak tum kisi surkh (yaʿni gore) ya kaale shakhs se behtar nahin — siwaaye iske ke taqwa mein aage ho."

Aḥmad

Islam's charter of equality. Every line people draw — race, colour, lineage, nation — is wiped off the scale. The only axis of superiority left is taqwā (God-consciousness). The Qurʾān grounds it, as the book itself quotes: "the most honoured of you to Allah is the most righteous of you" (49:13).

⚠️ …and watch the reverse

If taqwā is the one thing that raises you, the ego will claim it: "then I'm superior." But taqwā you parade is gone the moment you look down on the "less religious." Real taqwā humbles, never struts.

Jis taqwa ki numaaish karo woh taqwa nahin. Asli taqwa jhukaata hai, akadta nahin.

Ḥadīth 95 · He looks at the heart

إِنَّ اللهَ لَا يَنْظُرُ إِلَى صُوَرِكُمْ وَأَمْوَالِكُمْ، وَلٰكِنْ يَنْظُرُ إِلَى قُلُوْبِكُمْ وَأَعْمَالِكُمْ

inna Llāha lā yanẓuru ilā ṣuwarikum wa-amwālikum, wa-lākin yanẓuru ilā qulūbikum wa-aʿmālikum

"Allāh does not look at your appearance or wealth but looks at your heart and actions."

"Beshak Allah tumhari sooraton aur maal ko nahin dekhta, balke tumhare dilon aur aʿmaal ko dekhta hai."

Muslim

Allah photographs the heart, not the face or the bank balance. If #94 named the axis (taqwā), #95 shows where Allah's gaze actually falls to measure it: your heart (its sincerity or ostentation) and your deeds (their soundness or corruption) — never your form or wealth. Note the pairing: heart and deed, not heart alone. A sound heart must show up in sound action.

Ḥadīth 96 · The cheapest good deed, and a real one

إِنَّ مِنَ الْمَعْرُوْفِ أَنْ تَلْقَى أَخَاكَ بِوَجْهٍ طَلْقٍ

inna mina l-maʿrūfi an talqā akhāka bi-wajhin ṭalq

"It is a good deed that you meet your [Muslim] brother with a smiling face."

"Beshak neki mein se yeh bhi hai ke apne bhai se khile (muskuraate) chehre se milo."

Muslim, Tirmidhī

A warm face is counted a good deed (maʿrūf). It costs nothing and needs no wealth — which #95 just said Allah doesn't weigh anyway — yet it is a genuine ṣadaqa of the face: it gladdens a brother's heart and builds affection (ulfa). And it can't reliably be faked — a sound heart (#95) naturally produces a kind face. So the smile is the heart's soundness becoming visible to people. Don't overcomplicate it.

Ḥadīth 97 · Beat them to the peace

إِنَّ أَوْلَى النَّاسِ بِاللهِ مَنْ بَدَأَهُمْ بِالسَّلَام

inna awlā n-nāsi bi-Llāhi man badaʾahum bi-s-salām

"The closest to Allāh of people is the one who begins the salām."

"Beshak logon mein Allah ke sab se qareeb woh hai jo salaam mein pehl kare."

Abū Dāwūd

The one who greets first is closest to Allah's mercy and forgiveness. Going first takes a small death of the ego — you don't stand on your dignity waiting to be honoured; you give honour and peace first, to high and low alike. That humility, that eagerness to spread salām (peace), draws a person nearest to the Most Merciful. The theme, complete: a sound heart (#95) → a warm face (#96) → a tongue quick to greet first (#97).

Ḥadīth 98 · What looks like increase ends in ruin

إِنَّ الرِّبَا وَإِنْ كَثُرَ فَإِنَّ عَاقِبَتَهُ تَصِيْرُ إِلَى قُلٍّ

inna r-ribā wa-in kathura fa-inna ʿāqibatahu taṣīru ilā qull

"Interest, however much it might accrue, leads in the end to penury."

"Beshak sood, chahe kitna bhi barhe, uska anjaam qillat (tabaahi) par aata hai."

Ibn Māja, Aḥmad, Mustadrak

Interest looks like multiplication — but read the end-state. The number on the statement grows, yet its ʿāqiba (outcome) comes to qull: ruin. The book makes plain what ruin: wealth stripped of blessing and divine value, leading to disgrace and spiritual poverty — which is worse than financial poverty, because it can endanger faith itself.

⚠️ It's spiritual, not a naive prediction

This isn't "every interest-earner goes bankrupt" — some die rich. The ruin is barakah-stripped, value-hollowed, faith-endangering. The number can rise while the end-state falls.

"Har sood-khor diwaaliya" nahin. Tabaahi roohani hai — barakat-cheen, imaan-khatre wali. Number barhe, ʿāqiba gire.

Ḥadīth 99 · The corrosive that eats faith's sweetness

إِنَّ الْغَضَبَ لَيُفْسِدُ الْإِيْمَانَ كَمَا يُفْسِدُ الصَّبِرُ الْعَسَلَ

inna l-ghaḍaba la-yufsidu l-īmāna kamā yufsidu ṣ-ṣabiru l-ʿasal

"Anger ruins faith just as the aloe plant ruins honey."

"Beshak ghussa imaan ko aise saada deta hai jaise kadwa aloe shahad ko saada deta hai."

Bayhaqī, Shuʿab al-Īmān

Anger drops bitterness into the honey of a believing heart. (The plant is al-ṣabir, the bitter aloe — not ṣabr, "patience.") The book explains "ruins" as ruining faith's completeness, light, and beauty: uncontrolled anger usually spoils faith, not deletes it — though sometimes it can drive a person to faith-breaking speech or action.

⚠️ Not every flash of anger

This is not "feeling angry = losing īmān," and not a condemnation of all anger — there is a righteous anger for Allah's sake. The target is the uncontrolled, ego-driven rage that hijacks you.

"Ghussa aana = imaan ka jaana" nahin. Allah ke liye neik ghussa bhi hai. Nishaana be-qaaboo, nafs-ka ghussa.

Ḥadīth 100 · The capstone — two roads, one choice

إِنَّ الصِّدْقَ بِرٌّ وَإِنَّ الْبِرَّ يَهْدِي إِلَى الْجَنَّةِ، وَإِنَّ الْكَذِبَ فُجُوْرٌ وَإِنَّ الْفُجُوْرَ يَهْدِي إِلَى النَّارِ

inna ṣ-ṣidqa birr, wa-inna l-birra yahdī ilā l-jannah, wa-inna l-kadhiba fujūr, wa-inna l-fujūra yahdī ilā n-nār

"Truth is a piety, and piety leads to Paradise; falsehood is an iniquity, and iniquity leads to Hellfire."

"Beshak sach neki hai, aur neki Jannat ki taraf le jaati hai; aur jhoot badi hai, aur badi Aag ki taraf le jaati hai."

Muslim, Abū Dāwūd

The last ḥadīth of the hundred — a gate with two roads. Each half is a chain: truth is piety, and piety guides onward to Paradise; falsehood is iniquity, and iniquity guides onward to the Fire. Each is a road with momentum — one truth makes the next easier and bends a whole life toward the Garden; one lie loosens the next and bends a life toward the Fire — until a person is written, with Allah, a ṣiddīq (utterly truthful) or a kadhdhāb (an inveterate liar).

📿 The hundred in one verse · 9:119

يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا اتَّقُوا اللَّهَ وَكُونُوا مَعَ الصَّادِقِينَ

"O you who believe, fear Allah and be with the truthful."

Taqwā (the chapter's #94 axis) paired with ṣidq — and the command to be with the truthful. It is not "one lie damns you forever" — truthfulness is the road back too. Choose the road of ṣidq, and keep its company.

Taqwa aur sach ek saath — aur hukm: sacchon ke saath raho. Ek jhoot hamesha ke liye barbaad nahin karta; sach wapsi ka bhi raasta hai.

🔑 The chapter — and the hundred — in one picture

Ten aḥādīth, four movements, one thread:

  1. Provision — keep it clean (#91, #92, #93) — sin costs barakah; decreed rizq still arrives in full; charity draws mercy back.
  2. Allah weighs the heart (#94, #95) — only taqwā raises (not race); Allah looks at hearts & deeds, not forms & wealth.
  3. Carry it to people (#96, #97) — a cheerful face is a good deed; greeting first puts you closest to Allah.
  4. What ruins, what saves (#98, #99, #100) — interest ends in ruin; anger rots faith; truth → Paradise, falsehood → Fire.

The thread: the real measure — Allah weighs the heart, the deed, the truth. And with #100 the hundred is complete — its whole summary is the last ḥadīth: two roads, one habit — truth.

Rizq saaf rakho → Allah dil taulta hai → logon tak pahonchao → kya giraata/bachaata hai. Dhaaga: asal taraazu. Aur sau mukammal — khulasa #100 mein: do raaste, ek aadat — sach.

🏠 Homework — and a milestone

🎉 You have reached Ḥadīth 100

These ten complete the first hundred "Comprehensive Sayings" of Zad ut-Taalibeen — the whole journey through faith, character, and the world. Take a moment to thank Allah for it, and let the last ḥadīth be the one you carry: truth is the road to Paradise.

Yeh das sau hadithon ko mukammal karti hain — imaan, akhlaaq, aur duniya ka poora safar. Allah ka shukr ada karo, aur aakhri hadith dil mein utaaro: sach Jannat ka raasta hai.

  1. Memorise the Arabic matn of at least 4 of these aḥādīth — the short ones are a good start (94, 95, 96, 97).

  6. The ṣidq commitment (from Ḥadīth 100): choose one situation this week where the easy path is a small lie — and take the harder truth instead.
