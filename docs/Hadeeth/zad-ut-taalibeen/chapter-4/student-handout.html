Chapter 4 — Student Handout

Aḥādīth 31–40 from Part 1 of Zad ut-Taalibeen · the closing of Part 1

👋 Welcome — what we'll learn today

Today we cover the last 10 aḥādīth of Part 1 of Zad ut-Taalibeen — the chapter that closes the "Comprehensive Sayings" section. These 10 short sayings of the Prophet ﷺ have a strong thematic unity: they tell us who a believer actually is, how believers treat each other, and what a believer is like on the road.

Aaj ka sabaq — Part 1 ki aakhri 10 hadithein. Yeh hadith milkar batati hain ke momin kaun hai, momin doosre momin ke saath kaisa hai, aur momin safar mein kaisa hai.

The 10 fall into three themes:

  1. Who IS a believer? (#31, #32, #33, #34) — four nested definitions: Muslim, muʾmin, mujāhid, muhājir.
  2. Believers among believers (#35, #36, #37) — justice, mirroring, single body.
  3. The believer on the road (#38, #39, #40) — travel as discipline, return as worship, leadership as service.

Each ḥadīth section gives you: the Arabic, the meaning in English and Roman Urdu, the source, and a brief explanation. Then there are reflection questions and exercises at the end.

Ḥadīth 31 · The Muslim is one from whom others are safe

الْمُسْلِمُ مَنْ سَلِمَ الْمُسْلِمُونَ مِن لِّسَانِهِ وَيَدِهِ

al-muslimu man salima l-muslimūna min lisānihi wa yadih

"The Muslim is the one from whose tongue and hand other believers are safe."

"Musalman woh hai jiski zaban aur haath se baqi musalman mehfooz hain."

Bukhārī, Muslim

What it means: Notice how the Prophet ﷺ defines a Muslim. He does not say "the Muslim is one who prays, fasts, gives zakāh" — even though those are obligatory. He defines a Muslim by what other people experience of him. The test is outward and observable.

Why "tongue" and "hand"? These are the two main exits of harm to others:

  • Tongue (lisān) — gheebat, slander, insult, lies, mocking, broken promises. The tongue commits the most sins because it works fastest.
  • Hand (yad) — striking, stealing, withholding rights, harmful posts. (Today's "hand" includes the smartphone and the keyboard.)

⚠️ Watch your tongue

Most of us check the hand and assume we're clear: "I haven't hit anyone." But the tongue sneaks through with daily harms — taunts, sarcasm, gossip, jokes that wound. Apne zaban ka roz hisaab lo: kya aaj koi mom mein mehfooz nahin raha mere zaban se?

Ḥadīth 32 · The believer is one whom people trust with their lives and wealth

وَالْمُؤْمِنُ مَنْ أَمِنَهُ النَّاسُ عَلَى دِمَائِهِمْ وَأَمْوَالِهِمْ

wa-l-muʾminu man aminahu l-nāsu ʿalā dimāʾihim wa amwālihim

"And the believer is the one whom people entrust with their lives and their wealth."

"Aur muʾmin woh hai jis pe log apni jaan aur maal ki amanat soup dein."

Tirmidhī

The upgrade from #31 to #32: Notice two upgrades. The audience changes from "the Muslims" to al-nās (all people). And the stake changes from "tongue and hand" to life and wealth. We move from "don't be a threat" (minimum) to "be actively trusted" (above minimum).

What's at stakeTongue and handLife and wealth
PostureDon't harmBe a refuge

💡 The Makkan example

Even before prophethood, the polytheists of Makkah called the Prophet ﷺ al-Amīn ("the Trustworthy"). They didn't share his beliefs — but they deposited their valuables with him. That's the standard muʾmin means.

Rasool ﷺ ko "Al-Amīn" laqab Quraish ke kafir ne diya tha — woh aap ka aqida share nahin karte thay, magar aapki amanat-daari par yaqeen rakhte thay. Yahi muʾmin ki definition hai.

Ḥadīth 33 · The mujāhid is one who strives against his own nafs

وَالْمُجَاهِدُ مَنْ جَاهَدَ نَفْسَهُ فِي طَاعَةِ الله

wa-l-mujāhidu man jāhada nafsahu fī ṭāʿati Llāh

"And the mujāhid is the one who strives against his own self in obedience to Allah."

"Aur mujāhid woh hai jo apne nafs se Allah ki itāʿat mein mehnat karta hai."

Aḥmad, Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān)

Re-grounding the word jihād: The Arabic root j-h-d means exertion against resistance. The battlefield is one application of this root — not the only or primary meaning. The Prophet ﷺ re-grounds the term: the true mujāhid strives against his own nafs, in obedience to Allah.

Urdu mein: "Jihād" ka asal matlab — mehnat-against-resistance. Battlefield wala matlab ek application hai, asal matlab nahin. Asal mujāhid woh hai jo apne nafs se Allah ki itāʿat mein mehnat karta hai — yeh roz lagta hai.

Concrete examples of nafs-jihād:

  • Waking for Fajr when nafs wants sleep.
  • Holding the tongue when nafs wants to gossip.
  • Lowering the gaze when nafs wants to look.
  • Paying zakāh when nafs clings to money.
  • Forgiving when nafs wants revenge.

⚠️ Don't dismiss battlefield jihād

The Prophet ﷺ is expanding the definition, not abolishing the battlefield meaning. The book itself says: "the lesser jihad — fighting in the path of Allah — is included in this ḥadīth." Both are valid. The point is that the most constant jihād is the one against the nafs.

Ḥadīth 34 · The muhājir is one who abandons sins

وَالْمُهَاجِرُ مَنْ هَجَرَ الْخَطَايَا وَالذُّنُوبَ

wa-l-muhājiru man hajara l-khaṭāyā wa-l-dhunūb

"And the muhājir is the one who abandons mistakes and sins."

"Aur muhājir woh hai jo khataaon aur gunaahon ko chhor de."

Aḥmad, Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān) · (a parallel in Bukhārī: "...who abandons what Allah has forbidden")

Re-grounding hijrah: After the Conquest of Makkah, the Prophet ﷺ said: "There is no hijrah after the Conquest" — meaning, the obligation to physically emigrate from Makkah to Madīnah had ended. But the spirit of hijrah — leaving behind for Allah — is permanent. The true emigrant is the one who abandons sins. This hijrah is open to every Muslim, in every era, until the Day of Judgement.

Why moral hijrah is harder than physical:

  1. Physical hijrah ends — you arrive, you settle. Moral hijrah is constant.
  2. Physical hijrah was one act. Moral hijrah is every act of every day.
  3. Physical hijrah had visible borders. Moral hijrah has no exit point.

💡 The four-step staircase

You've now met all four definitions. Notice the staircase:

  1. #31 Muslim — don't harm others (tongue, hand). Minimum.
  2. #32 Muʾmin — others trust him with life and wealth. Above minimum.
  3. #33 Mujāhid — strives against his nafs in obedience. Inner struggle.
  4. #34 Muhājir — abandons sins continually. Inner movement.

Each rung includes the previous. Where are you on this staircase right now?

Char level — har upar wali pichli ko shamil karti hai. Apne aap se poochho — abhi kis level pe hoon?

Ḥadīth 35 · Evidence is on the accuser, oath is on the one accused

الْبَيِّنَةُ عَلَى الْمُدَّعِيْ وَالْيَمِينُ عَلَى الْمُدَّعَى عَلَيْهِ

al-bayyinatu ʿalā l-muddaʿī wa-l-yamīnu ʿalā l-muddaʿā ʿalayh

"The burden of proof is on the accuser, and the oath is on the one accused."

"Saboot ilzaam lagaane wale ke zimme, aur qasam jis par ilzaam hai (mulzim) ke zimme."

Bayhaqī · (Also Tirmidhī, Dāraqutnī. Imam Nawawī's Forty Ḥadīth #33)

The principle: If you make a claim against someone, you must bring evidence. If you can't, the accused only needs to swear an oath of denial, and the case stands in their favour. This is one of the foundational principles of Islamic legal procedure — and the source of the modern "innocent until proven guilty".

Why? The default state of every person is innocence. The one claiming a departure from default is the one introducing the claim — so the burden of proof falls on them.

Urdu mein: Default position = bay-gunah. "Tum gunahgaar ho" kehne wala woh hai jo normal se hatkar dawa kar raha hai. Saboot uska kaam hai. 14 sau saal pehle Islam ne yeh principle qaayam kiya — aaj western legal systems isi ko "presumption of innocence" kehte hain.

⚠️ This is not just a court rule

The principle applies everywhere:

  • Don't accuse a spouse, sibling, or child without evidence.
  • Don't pass on a workplace claim about a colleague unless evidence exists.
  • Don't forward WhatsApp claims about public figures without verification.
  • When suspicion of someone rises in your heart — ask: "What's my evidence?"

No evidence = the claim or suspicion is to be dismissed, not nurtured. This is the foundation of ḥusn al-ẓann (good opinion).

Ḥadīth 36 · A believer is a mirror for a believer

الْمُؤْمِنُ مِرْآةُ الْمُؤْمِنِ وَالْمُؤْمِنُ أَخُو الْمُؤْمِنِ، يَكُفُّ عَلَيْهِ ضَيْعَتَهُ وَيَحُوطُهُ مِنْ وَرَائِهِ

al-muʾminu mirʾātu l-muʾmini wa-l-muʾminu akhū l-muʾmini, yakuffu ʿalayhi ḍayʿatahu wa yaḥūṭuhu min warāʾih

"A believer is a mirror for a believer, and a believer is the brother of a believer; he safeguards him from destruction and protects him in his absence."

"Momin doosre momin ka aaina hai, aur momin doosre momin ka bhai hai; usay nuksaan se bachata hai aur uski peeth peechay uska defence karta hai."

Abū Dāwūd, Tirmidhī, al-Bukhārī's al-Adab al-Mufrad

The mirror metaphor — three properties:

  1. Privately — a mirror shows you only to yourself. So a believer corrects another privately, not by broadcasting their flaws to others.
  2. Truthfully — a mirror doesn't flatter or lie. So a believer corrects honestly, not with empty politeness.
  3. Without judgement — a mirror doesn't mock or shame. So a believer corrects gently, in love.

The "brother" half — protection behind the back: When your brother/sister in faith is not present, and someone speaks badly of them — you defend them. When their reputation is being damaged, you intervene. When their interests are at stake, you represent them faithfully.

💡 The reversal — worldly vs believer

Worldly pattern: flattery in front, gossip behind.

Believer pattern: truth in front, defence behind.

Dunyawi tarz: saamne taareef, peeth peechay buraai. Momin ka tarz: saamne sach, peeth peechay defence. Yeh do tarzon ka farq hai — aur yahi farq "normal" community aur "muʾmin community" mein hota hai.

Ḥadīth 37 · Believers are like a single person

الْمُؤْمِنُونَ كَرَجُلٍ وَّاحِدٍ إِنِ اشْتَكَى عَيْنُهُ اشْتَكَى كُلُّهُ وَإِنِ اشْتَكَى رَأْسُهُ اشْتَكَى كُلُّهُ

al-muʾminūna ka-rajulin wāḥidin, ini shtakā ʿaynuhu shtakā kulluh, wa-ini shtakā raʾsuhu shtakā kulluh

"The believers are like a single person; if his eye is in pain, his whole body is in pain, and if his head is in pain, his whole body is in pain."

"Momin ek hi shakhs (ek jism) ki tarah hain: agar uski aankh mein dard ho to poora jism dard mein hota hai, aur agar uske sar mein dard ho to poora jism dard mein hota hai."

Muslim

The escalation from #36 to #37: Ḥadīth 36 spoke of two believers as mirror and brother. Ḥadīth 37 takes it further — believers are not even two individuals: they are one body. Different limbs of the same organism.

Urdu mein: Hadith #36 = do alag log (aaina, bhai). Hadith #37 = ek hi badan. Yeh upgrade hai. Tumhare musalman bhai ki takleef = tumhari takleef. "Mera masla, tumhara masla" — yeh separation hi yeh hadith todti hai.

The medical accuracy: When you have a headache, your whole body is affected — appetite, sleep, energy all drop. When the eye hurts, productivity collapses. The body operates as one system. The Prophet ﷺ is saying the community of believers should have a spiritual nervous system that connects them the same way.

💡 A diagnostic, not a guilt trip

Use this ḥadīth as a check on your own heart:

  • When refugees suffer in Gaza, Sudan, Myanmar — do you feel it, or scroll past?
  • When a neighbour is struggling with rent — do you know?
  • When a sister hasn't been in masjid for three weeks — do you notice?

If you don't feel the pain — the spiritual nerve has weakened. Re-connect.

Yeh hadith diagnostic hai — guilt nahin. Apne dil ko check karo. Agar pain mehsoos nahin ho rahi, to "jasad ka nerve" kahaan disconnect hua hai, dekho.

Ḥadīth 38 · Travel is a piece of punishment

السَّفَرُ قِطْعَةٌ مِّنَ الْعَذَابِ يَمْنَعُ أَحَدَكُمْ نَوْمَهُ وَطَعَامَهُ وَشَرَابَهُ فَإِذَا قَضَى أَحَدُكُمْ نَهْمَتَهُ مِنْ وَجْهِهِ فَلْيُعَجِّلْ إِلَى أَهْلِهِ

al-safaru qiṭʿatun mina l-ʿadhābi yamnaʿu aḥadakum nawmahu wa ṭaʿāmahu wa sharābah, fa-idhā qaḍā aḥadukum nahmatahu min wajhihi fal-yuʿajjil ilā ahlih

"Travelling involves a degree of punishment; it deprives one of his sleep, food, and drink. Therefore, when one has accomplished his purpose, he should hurry back to his family."

"Safar azaab ka tukda hai — yeh tum se tumhari neend, khana aur paani cheen leta hai. Toh jab kaam pura ho jaaye, ghar walon ke paas jaldi lautho."

Bukhārī, Muslim

The opening surprise: We normally think of travel as a positive — adventure, vacation. The Prophet ﷺ frames it as a piece of punishment. Not moral punishment — but physical and emotional deprivation: disrupted sleep, food, drink, family bonds, prayer routines, work rhythms.

Urdu mein: Aaj ki dunya safar ko "vacation" kehti hai — magar Rasool ﷺ ne usay azaab ka tukda kaha. Neend, khaana, paani, ghar walay sab disturb hote hain. Yeh moral punishment nahin — jismani aur jazbati takleef hai.

The operational rule: When your purpose is done, hasten home. Three reasons:

  1. Your family has rights — your absence is a deprivation for them too.
  2. Extended travel = missed Jumʿah and congregational prayers.
  3. Extended travel erodes your spiritual rhythms.

⚠️ This is not "travel is haraam"

The Prophet ﷺ himself travelled — for Hajj, for trade, for battles. The point is: don't over-romanticise travel, and return when business is done. Halāl travel for a purpose is fully permitted.

💡 The deeper teaching

Classical commentators extend the metaphor: the believer's whole life in this world is a journey. If physical travel is "a piece of punishment", so is this dunyā compared to Akhirah. Don't stretch the trip artificially. Finish your purpose, return home — both here and now, and ultimately to Allah.

Ḥadīth 39 · The return from battle is like the battle itself (in reward)

قَفْلَةٌ كَغَزْوَةٍ

qaflatun ka-ghazwah

"The return from battle is like the battle itself [in reward]."

"Jang se wapsi (qaflah) bhi ek ghazwah (jang) jaisi hai (ajr mein)."

Abū Dāwūd

The startling generosity: We normally think — the active fighter is rewarded; the returning one is just heading home. The Prophet ﷺ flips this. The return is not "free time" — it is still part of the worship.

Why? Imam Mullā ʿAlī al-Qārī explains: the warrior is returning to rest, regain strength, and see to the needs of his family — with the intention of returning to battle if needed. Two anchors keep the reward flowing:

  1. Intention to return — the heart hasn't exited the cause.
  2. Family rights — going home is itself an obligation (per Ḥadīth 38 above).

The principle generalised: Intention sustains worship across pauses. Apply broadly:

  • A ḥāfizah on a break to care for parents — if intending to return, the gap is rewarded.
  • A student stepping away from study for a family crisis — time is not lost.
  • A daʿwah worker slowing down for a season — preserved if intention is preserved.

💡 Relief from guilt

If you feel guilty over pauses in your worship — Qurʾān memorisation interrupted, halqa attendance broken, active worship paused — this ḥadīth is raḥmah (mercy). If your intention is intact, the pause is itself rewarded. Allah counts the intention chain, not just the active moments.

Agar tumhe apne worship ke "pauses" pe pachhtawa hai — yeh hadith tumhare liye raḥmah hai. Niyyat saath hai? Toh gap mein bhi ajr hai. Allah ka hisaab niyyat ki zanjeer dekhta hai — sirf active moments nahin.

Ḥadīth 40 · The leader of a group is their servant

سَيِّدُ الْقَوْمِ فِي السَّفَرِ خَادِمُهُمْ

sayyidu l-qawmi fī l-safari khādimuhum

"The leader of a group on a journey is their servant."

"Safar mein qaum ka sardar woh hai jo unki khidmat karta hai."

Ibn Māja, Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-Īmān)

This is the closing ḥadīth of Part 1 of Zad ut-Taalibeen — the very last of the 40 "Comprehensive Sayings". And it closes on a beautiful inversion: leadership = service.

Two readings (both true):

  1. If you are leading → you must serve. Leadership is responsibility, not privilege.
  2. If you are serving → you qualify to lead. Service is the path, not status.

The Prophet ﷺ's example: At the digging of the trench (Khandaq), he carried earth alongside the Companions. On the journey to Tabūk, when a camel slipped, he helped push it. He took the last portion when food was distributed. He was the leader because he was the servant.

Urdu mein: Rasool ﷺ har safar mein khidmat mein shamil thay — Khandaq mein mitti uthayi, Tabuk mein oont ko dhakka diya, khaane mein aakhri hissa liya. Aap ki sardari isi liye thi — woh sab se bara khadim thay. Khidmat = sardari ka haq.

Apply it: If you lead a household, a class, a project, a halqa — your job is to serve those under you, not be served by them. The honour is the work. There is no separate "izzat" you can extract from the position. The service is the honour.

💡 The chapter's closing arc

Notice where Chapter 4 began and where it ended:

  • #31 — "A Muslim is one from whom others are safe." (Don't harm.)
  • #40 — "A leader is one who serves those under him." (Actively serve.)

The arc is from minimum protection to active offering. That is the whole chapter — and the whole of Part 1 — in one line.

Chapter ka safar: #31 mein "doosron ko nuksaan na do" se shuru, #40 mein "doosron ki khidmat karo" pe khatam. Minimum → maximum. Yeh poori Part 1 ka safar hai.

Exercise 4 · True or False

  5. A believer who criticises another believer publicly on social media is acting as a "mirror" per Ḥadīth 36. —
