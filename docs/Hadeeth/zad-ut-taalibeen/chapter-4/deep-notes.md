# Part A · Understanding the chapter

These 10 aḥādīth close out the *Comprehensive Sayings* section of Part 1 of Zad ut-Taalibeen — and unlike the previous three chapters, **this one has the strongest thematic unity of all four.** Almost every ḥadīth in the chapter is doing one of three things: (1) *defining* who a believer actually is, (2) *describing how believers relate to each other*, or (3) *closing out with travel and leadership* — what a believer is like on the road, away from home.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh chapter ki khaas baat — yeh sab hadith milkar ek tasveer banati hain: *momin kaun hai · momin doosre momin ke saath kaisa hai · momin safar mein kaisa hai*. Pichle teen chapters mein themes thay magar yeh chapter sab se zyada *joined-up* hai. Class ko yeh ek-paigham wali narrative dikhao.

Three natural themes — keep this in your head as the spine of the chapter:

1. **Who IS a believer?** (Aḥādīth 31, 32, 33, 34) — four redefinitions: Muslim, believer, mujāhid, muhājir. Each one re-grounds an outward category in an inner reality. The Prophet ﷺ takes *labels* people use casually and forces us to ask, *"are you really one of these?"*
2. **Believers among believers** (Aḥādīth 35, 36, 37) — how the community of believers actually functions. Justice (#35), mutual mirroring (#36), shared body (#37). From the inner definitions of Theme 1, we move outward into how those believers form a *single fabric*.
3. **The believer on the road** (Aḥādīth 38, 39, 40) — closing out the chapter (and Part 1's "Comprehensive Sayings") with the question of travel. Travel as discipline (#38), returning as worship (#39), leading on the road (#40).

The chapter takes the learner from *who you are* → *how you treat your brothers and sisters* → *who you are when you are out of your comfort zone (on safar)*. Notice: travel is a test of everything Theme 1 and Theme 2 just built. When you are tired, far from home, dependent on others — *that's* when "muslim = safe tongue and hand" gets tested. So the closing arc is not unrelated to what came before; it is the *test conditions*.

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter ka safar — *kaun hai momin* → *momin doosre momin ke saath kaisa hai* → *momin safar mein, ghar se door, kaisa hai*. Safar wali hadithein test hain — pehli do themes ka. Jab thakaan ho, ghar door ho, doosron pe inhisaar ho — tab pata chalta hai zaban aur haath mehfooz hain ya nahin.

Work through each ḥadīth below. For each, ask yourself: *can I explain this hadith to a classmate in one sentence, in Urdu, without reading the book?* (*Kya main yeh hadith ek classmate ko ek line mein Urdu mein samjha sakti hoon, bina kitaab dekhe?*) If yes — move on. If not — re-read.

## 31 · الْمُسْلِمُ مَنْ سَلِمَ الْمُسْلِمُوْنَ مِنْ لِسَانِهِ وَيَدِهِ — "The Muslim is the one *from whom* other believers are safe"

**Source:** Bukhārī, Muslim. Narrated by ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿAmr ibn al-ʿĀṣ (raḍiyallāhu ʿanhumā).

### The structural move

Notice the grammar. The Prophet ﷺ does not say *"the Muslim is one who prays, fasts, gives zakāh"* — even though those are absolutely required. He defines a Muslim by **what other people experience of him**. The test of "Muslim" is *outward*, observable, and *measured by other people*.

The Arabic *salima ... min* (سَلِمَ ... مِنْ) literally = "is safe / sound from". Believers are *safe* from this person's tongue and hand. So a Muslim is a *safe person* to be near.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh hadith Muslim ki *outward* tasveer deti hai — tum jis level pe namaz parhti ho, woh tumhare aur Allah ke darmiyan hai. Magar tumhari *zaban aur haath se doosre log mehfooz hain ya nahin* — yeh sab dekh sakte hain. Yahi yardstick hai jo Prophet ﷺ ne diya.

### Tongue and hand — the two main exits of sin

Of all the body parts the Prophet ﷺ could have named (eye, ear, foot), he picked *tongue and hand*. Why?

- **Tongue** (*lisān*) — the chief exit of *spoken* harm: backbiting, slander, insult, lies, mocking, false oaths, breaking promises. The tongue is small but commits the most sins because it works fastest.
- **Hand** (*yad*) — the chief exit of *physical* harm: striking, stealing, withholding rights, writing false statements, gesturing in mockery, posting harmful content (yes, the hand reaches into "the hand" of today — phones, keyboards, social media).

Together: speech-harm and action-harm. Almost every interpersonal sin exits through one of these two. The Prophet ﷺ named the two doorways. *Guard these two — and most of your relationships with others become safe.*

**Yaad rakho:** *Zaban* aur *haath* — yahi do darwaze hain jahaan se doosron ko nuksaan pohnchta hai. Zaban se: gheebat, jhoot, gaali, mazaaq. Haath se: maarna, chori, haq dabaana, social media pe galat post karna (haath aaj smartphone bhi hai). Yeh do mehfooz, to bohot kuch mehfooz.

### What about non-Muslims?

The ḥadīth says *al-muslimūn* — "the Muslims/believers". Classmates may notice this and ask: "So can a Muslim harm non-Muslims?" Absolutely not. Other aḥādīth establish the inviolability of *every* protected human life. This ḥadīth is *zooming in* on intra-community ethics — the *minimum baseline* of being claimed by the label *Muslim* is that the *Muslim community itself* is safe from you. If you can't even achieve that, the label is hollow. Other texts then extend the principle outward.

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith ne sirf "muslimoon" likha — magar matlab yeh nahin ke ghair-muslim se zulm jaaiz hai. Yeh hadith *minimum* bayan kar rahi hai — *agar musalman bhi tumse mehfooz nahin, to "Muslim" label tum pe lagta hi nahin*. Baqi nass se non-muslims ka haq bhi establish hota hai. Yeh point pre-empt karo, classmate poochhe.

### The continuation in some narrations

In some narrations the Prophet ﷺ extends this with: *"and the muhājir is one who abandons what Allah has forbidden."* That second clause becomes Ḥadīth 34 of our chapter. So this opening ḥadīth is structurally part of a *series of four definitions* — Muslim, mu'min, mujāhid, muhājir — each redefining a public label by an inner reality.

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith #31, 32, 33, 34 — yeh char *definitions* hain. Char alag categories ko Rasool ﷺ ne *andar se* re-define kiya. Class ko yeh series structure dikhao — sab alag nahin, ek silsila hai.

## 32 · وَالْمُؤْمِنُ مَنْ أَمِنَهُ النَّاسُ عَلَى دِمَائِهِمْ وَأَمْوَالِهِمْ — "And the believer is the one whom people *entrust* with their lives and wealth"

**Source:** Tirmidhī, an-Nasāʾī, Ibn Mājah. Narrated by Abū Hurayrah and other Companions.

### The upgrade from #31 to #32

Notice the move from Ḥadīth 31 to Ḥadīth 32 — and notice the new word: *al-nās* ("the people") instead of *al-muslimūn* ("the Muslims"). The bar has lifted on two counts:

| | Ḥadīth 31 (Muslim) | Ḥadīth 32 (Believer) |
|---|---|---|
| **Threshold** | Other Muslims are **safe** from him | **All people** *trust* him with |
| **What's at stake** | Tongue and hand | **Life and wealth** |
| **Posture** | Don't harm | **Be actively trusted** |

The believer (*muʾmin*) is one level *above* the Muslim definition. It's no longer "I don't harm you" — it's "you trust me with your *life* and *property*". That is a *higher* social standing. Not just *not a threat* — *actively a refuge*.

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith #31 minimum hai — *"main tumhe nuksaan nahin pohnchata"*. Hadith #32 upgrade hai — *"tum mujhe apni jaan aur maal soup sakte ho"*. Pehla bar: *threat na ho*. Doosra bar: *refuge ho*. Iman ki upper level yahi hai.

### Why "people" — not "Muslims"

Significantly, this ḥadīth uses *al-nās* — "the people" (all people, Muslim or not). The believer's *trustworthiness* extends to humanity at large. A non-Muslim should be able to deposit money with him without fear. A non-Muslim should be able to leave a child in his care. *That* is *īmān* — outward, observable, trusted by everyone.

This is the *Makkan-period* portrait of the Prophet ﷺ — even before prophethood, the polytheists of Quraysh called him *al-Amīn* ("the Trustworthy"). They didn't share his beliefs — but they deposited their valuables with him. That's the standard.

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith #32 mein "al-nās" likha hai — sirf musalman nahin, *sab log*. Yeh *amānah* ki khoobi hai jo non-muslim ko bhi nazar aati hai. Rasool ﷺ ko aap "Al-Amīn" kya nubuwwat ke baad kaha gaya? Nahin — *pehle*. Quraish ke kafir ne hi yeh laqab diya. Class ko yeh batao — yeh ek *visible* virtue hai.

### "Life and wealth" — the two heaviest deposits

Why these two specifically? Because *life* and *wealth* are two of the five *maqāṣid al-sharīʿah* — the highest objectives of the Sacred Law (the other three being *dīn*, *ʿaql*, *nasl*). When you can be trusted with *life* and *wealth*, you've crossed the highest social trust threshold there is.

In practice: do people leave their children with you? Their elderly parents? Their money during travel? Their secrets that could ruin them? *That* is the diagnostic of *al-muʾmin* in this ḥadīth.

**Yaad rakho:** *Jaan aur maal* — yeh sabse bhaari amānat hain. Insaan apni jaan aur maal kisi waise admi ko nahin saupta jiska kirdaar shak mein ho. Apne aap se poochho — kya log tum pe yeh do cheezein chhod sakte hain? Yahi *muʾmin* ka asal yardstick hai.

## 33 · وَالْمُجَاهِدُ مَنْ جَاهَدَ نَفْسَهُ فِيْ طَاعَةِ اللهِ — "And the *mujāhid* is one who *strives* against his own nafs in obedience to Allah"

**Source:** Aḥmad, Bayhaqī (*Shuʿab al-Īmān*). Part of a longer compound narration including #31, #32, and #34.

### The conceptual unlocking

This is the ḥadīth that breaks open the word *jihād* for an audience that has been culturally exposed to the term only in its battlefield sense.

The Arabic verb *jāhada* (root *j-h-d* جهد) means *to exert effort* against resistance. Anywhere. Against *anything*. The *battlefield* sense is *one application* — and a legitimate one, in its proper time and conditions — but it is **not** the root meaning. The root meaning is *exertion against resistance*.

The Prophet ﷺ here re-grounds the term: the *true* mujāhid is the one striving against the *nafs* (the lower self) in *obedience to Allah*. Because that struggle is *constant*. Battlefield jihād is *occasional* and externally triggered. The struggle against one's nafs is *daily*, *internal*, and *never paused*.

**Yaad rakho:** *Jihād* ka asal matlab — *mehnat-against-resistance*. Battlefield wala matlab ek *application* hai, asal matlab nahin. Asal mujāhid woh hai jo apne nafs se Allah ki itāʿat mein mehnat karta hai. Yeh roz ka jihad hai — sirf jang ke maidan ka nahin.

### The "greater jihād" tradition

The book itself frames this: striving against the nafs is *al-jihād al-akbar* — the greater jihād. This terminology has a history. There is a narration (whose authenticity scholars debate) that on returning from a battle, the Prophet ﷺ said: *"We have returned from the lesser jihād to the greater jihād."* When asked what is the greater, he replied: *"The struggle of a servant against his desires."*

Whether or not that specific narration is rigorously authentic, **its meaning is established by sound texts** — including the ḥadīth we're studying right now. The *content* of the principle is sound: striving against the nafs is the more constant, more pervasive struggle. The battlefield struggle is included in the broader definition, not excluded.

**Yaad rakho:** "Jihād-e-akbar" wali rivayat ki sanad pe scholars ki bahas hai magar uska *mafhoom* yeh hadith se sahih hota hai. Mujāhid sirf woh nahin jo battlefield mein hai — woh bhi hai jo apne nafs ke khilaaf roz mehnat karta hai. Donon shamil hain — magar nafs ka jihad sab ka common hai.

### What the nafs-jihād looks like

Concrete examples — name these for classmates:

- Getting up for Fajr when nafs wants to sleep → *jihād*.
- Holding tongue back from gossip when nafs wants to gossip → *jihād*.
- Lowering gaze when nafs wants to look → *jihād*.
- Paying zakāh when nafs is attached to money → *jihād*.
- Forgiving someone who wronged you when nafs wants revenge → *jihād*.

Each is a struggle against resistance, *in obedience to Allah*. Each makes you a *mujāhid* in the original Prophetic sense.

**Yaad rakho:** Mujāhid ki misalein — Fajr ke liye uthna jab neend zyada laggi hai · gheebat na karna jab dil chahta hai · nigaah jhukana jab dekhne ko ji chahe · zakāh dena jab pesa azeez hai · maaf karna jab nafs intiqaam chahta hai. Har ek struggle = mujāhid ka kaam. Yeh roz ki cheezein hain.

### The pre-emption — don't dismiss battlefield jihād

Classmates may take this ḥadīth as "so battlefield jihad doesn't matter / is unimportant." Wrong. The Prophet ﷺ is *redefining* the term to include the daily struggle — not *excluding* the battlefield. The book explicitly says: "*the lesser jihad—that is, fighting in the path of Allah—is included in this ḥadīth.*" Both are valid. The point is that the *real* test of a mujāhid is *consistent obedience under nafs's resistance*, which the battlefield warrior also undergoes, magnified.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh hadith battlefield jihād ko mansookh nahin kar rahi — sirf usay *redefine* kar rahi hai ki *asal* test nafs ke khilaaf mehnat hai. Battlefield wala bhi shamil hai, sirf woh akeli definition nahin. Class ke kisi mauqe pe yeh pre-empt karo.

## 34 · وَالْمُهَاجِرُ مَنْ هَجَرَ الْخَطَايَا وَالذُّنُوْبَ — "And the *muhājir* is one who *abandons* mistakes and sins"

**Source:** Aḥmad, Bayhaqī. (A widely-attested version is also in Bukhārī with slightly different wording: *al-muhājiru man hajara mā nahā Llāhu ʿanhu* — "the emigrant is one who abandons what Allah has forbidden".)

### The historical move

After the Conquest of Makkah (8 AH), the Prophet ﷺ said: *"There is no hijrah after the Conquest"* — meaning, the obligation to physically emigrate from Makkah to Madīnah no longer applies (because Makkah is now Muslim territory). But the *spirit* of hijrah — the willingness to *leave behind for the sake of Allah* — is permanent.

So the Prophet ﷺ *redefines* the term. Just as he re-grounded *mujāhid* in Ḥadīth 33, here he re-grounds *muhājir*. The true emigrant is one who *leaves behind* sins and mistakes. *That* hijrah is open to every Muslim, in every era, until the Day of Judgement.

**Yaad rakho:** Conquest ke baad Makkah se Madinah wali hijrah khatam ho gayi. Magar *hijrah ki rooh* — Allah ke liye chhorne ki tayari — woh qayamat tak khuli hai. Aaj ka *muhājir* woh hai jo gunaahon se hijrah karta hai. Yeh hijrah har musalman ke liye possible hai.

### Why "sins" is "harder than geography"

The book gives a sharp logic: *"This is because it is more difficult to completely abstain from sins than to emigrate from one place to another, or because the sole purpose of emigrating is to facilitate the worship of Allah."*

Read that twice. **Physical hijrah is *easier* than *moral* hijrah.** Why? Because:

1. **Physical hijrah ends.** You arrive at Madīnah, you settle, you're done. Moral hijrah from your sins is *constant* — the nafs will keep proposing the sin tomorrow, the day after, next year.
2. **Physical hijrah is *one act*.** Moral hijrah is *every act of every day*.
3. **Physical hijrah has visible borders.** Moral hijrah has no visible exit point — you can't tell *when* you've fully crossed.

And the second reason: the *purpose* of physical hijrah was to *enable worship*. So if you can already worship Allah where you are — by *leaving sins* — you've fulfilled the *substance* of hijrah. The geography was a means; abandoning sin is the *end*.

**Yaad rakho:** Geography wali hijrah aasaan thi — pohnchi, kaam khatam. Gunah wali hijrah har waqt chalti hai — roz tumhara nafs gunah propose karta hai, roz tumhe inkaar karna parta hai. Aur asal hijrah ka maqsad hi ibadat ko aasan banana tha — agar tum gunah chhod ke ibadat kar rahi ho, tumne hijrah ka *jauhar* paa liya, sirf geography nahin.

### Connecting #31–#34 as a series

Step back and see the four-ḥadīth structure:

- **#31** — *Muslim* = doesn't harm others (tongue, hand) — the **minimum**.
- **#32** — *Muʾmin* = others trust him with life/wealth — **above minimum**.
- **#33** — *Mujāhid* = strives against his own nafs in obedience — **inner struggle**.
- **#34** — *Muhājir* = abandons sins — **inner movement**.

Each rung is a *higher* test. The labels are nested: a *muʾmin* is a *muslim* who has cleared a higher bar. A *mujāhid* / *muhājir* is at yet another level — internalised, daily, willed.

**Yaad rakho:** *Char hadith — char level.* Muslim (zaban-haath se mehfooz) → Muʾmin (jaan-maal ki amanat) → Mujāhid (nafs ke khilaaf jang) → Muhājir (gunah chhornay wala). Yeh nested levels hain — har upar wali pichli ko shamil karti hai. Class ko yeh "levels" ki tasveer board pe likhwa do.

## 35 · الْبَيِّنَةُ عَلَى الْمُدَّعِيْ وَالْيَمِيْنُ عَلَى مَنْ أَنْكَرَ — "The *evidence* is on the claimant; the *oath* is on the one who denies"

**Source:** Bayhaqī. (Also reported with similar wording in Tirmidhī, Dāraqutnī, and the well-known forty-ḥadīth collection of Imam al-Nawawī as Ḥadīth 33.)

### One of the four pillars of Islamic procedural law

This single short ḥadīth establishes a foundational rule of *fiqh al-qaḍāʾ* (legal procedure). Imam al-Nawawī classifies it as one of the *foundational ḥadīths* — Imām Bulandshehrī, citing Mullā ʿAlī al-Qārī, calls it "one very important principle of Islamic law".

The rule has two sides:

1. **The accuser** (*muddaʿī* — the one *making the claim*) must bring **evidence** (*bayyinah* — proof, witnesses, documents).
2. **The denier** (*man ankar* — the one *being accused*) is required only to **swear an oath** that the claim is false.

If the accuser can produce no evidence, the case is decided in favour of the accused on the strength of his oath alone.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh hadith Islamic legal system ka ek foundational usool deti hai. Ilzaam lagaane wala — *gawaah aur saboot* laaye. Inkaar karne wala — *qasam* khaaye. Saboot na ho, qasam laazim. Yeh principle ʿumar ke khilafat se aaj tak laagu raha hai.

### Why "evidence on the claimant" — not the other way around?

The default state of every person is *innocence*. The accused doesn't need to *prove* he didn't do something — because *not doing* something is the default state of every human at any moment. You and I, right now, are *not* murdering anyone — we don't need to *prove* that.

So the burden naturally falls on the one *claiming a departure from the default*. If you say "this person stole from me", you are claiming the *non-default* state — that he, who normally would not steal, *did* steal. *You* are the one introducing a deviation. *You* prove it.

This is *the same logic* as modern systems of "innocent until proven guilty" — which Islamic law established 14 centuries ago, with this exact principle.

**Yaad rakho:** Default = bay-gunah. Agar koi kehta hai "tum gunahgaar ho" — woh dahin pe hai jo *normal* se hatkar dawa kar raha hai. Isi liye saboot uska kaam hai, na ke mulzim ka. Yeh aqlmandi ka bhi usool hai aur insaniyat ka bhi. Islam ne 14 sau saal pehle yeh sikhaaya.

### The oath's gravity

The *oath* (*yamīn*) given by the denier is not casual. To swear by Allah falsely is *a major sin* and exposes the swearer to terrible consequences — both in this world (per other aḥādīth) and in the Hereafter. The system *trusts* that a person willing to swear by Allah is generally telling the truth, because a person with even basic faith fears that oath.

But the system also has a back-stop: if the denier later confesses, or new evidence emerges, the case can be reopened. The *oath* is not the absolute end — it is the *legal* end given what was knowable at the time.

**Yaad rakho:** Qasam ka bohot wazan hai — jhooti qasam Allah ke naam pe lena bohot bara gunah hai. System ne yeh maan liya ke jo banda Allah ki qasam khaane ke liye tayar hai woh aam taur pe sach bol raha hoga (kyunki imaan wala jhooti qasam se darta hai). Aur agar baad mein saboot mil jaaye — case dobara khul sakta hai. Qasam *legal* end hai, *haqeeqi* end nahin.

### Why teach this ḥadīth in a non-legal context?

Classmates are not judges. So why this ḥadīth? Because *the principle applies everywhere in life*:

- In family disputes — don't accuse your spouse / sibling / child without evidence.
- In workplace gossip — don't repeat a claim about a colleague unless the person has *proof*.
- In WhatsApp groups — when someone forwards a claim about a public figure, ask: *where's the evidence?*
- In your own self-reflection — when you suspect bad of someone, ask: *what's my evidence?*

The ḥadīth installs the *habit of asking for evidence* and the *habit of giving the benefit of the doubt*. That alone, applied across a community, transforms social relations.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh hadith sirf judge sahab ke liye nahin — *roz marra* ki zindagi ke liye hai. Family mein, dost mein, workplace mein, WhatsApp mein — *kisi pe ilzaam lagaane se pehle saboot poochho*. Aur jab tumhare paas saboot na ho — chup raho ya husn-e-zann (benefit of doubt) rakho. Yeh hadith husn-e-zann ki bunyaad hai.

## 36 · الْمُؤْمِنُ مِرْآةُ الْمُؤْمِنِ — "A believer is a *mirror* for a believer"

**Source:** Abū Dāwūd, Tirmidhī, al-Bukhārī's *al-Adab al-Mufrad*. Narrated by Abū Hurayrah (raḍiyallāhu ʿanhu).

### The fuller wording

The complete ḥadīth has *two* paired clauses:

> *"A believer is a mirror for a believer, and a believer is the brother of a believer; he safeguards him from destruction and protects him in his absence."*

Two images, each loaded:

1. **Mirror** (*mirʾāh*) — a believer shows another believer their flaws *honestly* but also *only to that person*.
2. **Brother** (*akh*) — protective, watchful, present even when the other is absent.

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith ke do hisse hain: *aaina (mirror)* aur *bhai (brother)*. Aaina = saaf bata dena — magar sirf saamne, peeth peechay nahin. Bhai = hifaazat karna — saath saath bhi aur peeth peechay bhi.

### The mirror metaphor — unpacked

What does a mirror do? It shows you *yourself* — *as you are*. It doesn't flatter you; it doesn't mock you. If your hair is messy, the mirror shows it. If your collar is crooked, it shows it. *Only to you.* The mirror doesn't broadcast your flaws to anyone else.

So a believer's role in another believer's life: **show them their flaws privately, accurately, and without judgement.** Not to humiliate, not to gossip, not to feel superior — just *honestly*, the way a mirror does.

This is *the proper form of naṣīḥa* we learned in Chapter 1 — recall that *naṣīḥa* means pure inner goodwill, expressed as needed. Here is the mechanism: the believer is a *mirror* of *naṣīḥa* for another believer.

**Yaad rakho:** Aaina ki khoobi: saaf bataata hai, sirf tumhe bataata hai, judgement ke baghair bataata hai. Yahi naṣīḥa ki amali shakal hai (Chapter 1 ki #1 wali). Doosre ki kami batao — magar privately aur bina nakhra. Aaina banna seekho.

### The brother metaphor — what "protects him in his absence" means

The phrase "*safeguards him from destruction and protects him in his absence*" has a specific commentary tradition. Imām Mullā ʿAlī al-Qārī explains: *"protects him in his absence"* means **preserving his respect, honour, and rights when he is not present**.

In practical terms: when your brother / sister in faith is not in the room, and someone starts speaking badly of them, *you defend them*. When their reputation is being damaged in their absence, *you intervene*. When their interests are at stake in a meeting they couldn't attend, *you represent them faithfully*.

This is the *opposite* of the gossip culture. In gossip, people speak freely about absent others; in believers' culture, the absent are *protected* by the present.

**Yaad rakho:** "Min warāʾihi" matlab — uski peeth peechay. Jab tumhara bhai/behan kamre mein nahin, aur log uske khilaaf bol rahe hain — tum uska defence karo. Uski izzat, uska haq, uske mufaad — peeth peechay bhi tumhari zimmedaari hain. Yeh "brother" wali baat ka asal matlab hai.

### The combination: mirror + protector

The combination is exact. The mirror is *honest* — even harsh — but *in your face* (saamne). The protector is *defensive* — but *behind your back* (peeth peechay). **Together: honest in front, protective behind.**

This is the *exact opposite* of the worldly pattern: the worldly pattern is *flattery in front, gossip behind*. The believer reverses it: *truth in front, defence behind*.

**Yaad rakho:** Dunyawi tarz: *saamne taareef, peeth peechay buraai*. Momin ka tarz: *saamne sach, peeth peechay defence*. Yeh do tarzon ka farq hai — aur yahi farq ek normal community aur ek "mu'min community" mein hota hai.

## 37 · مَثَلُ الْمُؤْمِنِيْنَ كَمَثَلِ الْجَسَدِ الْوَاحِدِ — "Believers are like a *single body*"

**Source:** Muslim. (The famous fuller wording: *"The example of the believers in their mutual love, mercy, and affection is like that of a single body — when one limb suffers, the rest of the body responds with sleeplessness and fever."* Narrated by Nuʿmān ibn Bashīr — Bukhārī and Muslim.) The version in Zad ut-Taalibeen is the more compact form referencing the eye and the head.

### From mirror (#36) to body (#37)

Notice the escalation. Ḥadīth 36 said believers are *mirrors* and *brothers* — two individuals in relationship. Ḥadīth 37 takes it further: believers are not even two individuals — they are **one body**. One *jasad*. Different limbs of the same person.

So the *separation* between believers — the sense that "your problem is your problem, my problem is mine" — *that very separation* is broken by this ḥadīth. The Prophet ﷺ is saying: your brother / sister in faith hurting somewhere = *you* hurt somewhere. Because you're the same body.

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith #36 — *do alag log* (mirror, brother). Hadith #37 — *ek hi badan*. Yeh upgrade hai. Tumhara musalman bhai ki takleef = tumhari takleef. Tumhari nahin, *humari*. "Mera masla, tumhara masla" — yeh separation hi yeh hadith todti hai.

### The medical accuracy of the metaphor

Read the metaphor closely: *"if his eye is in pain his whole body pains, if his head is in pain his whole body pains."* This is *medically* exact.

When you have a headache, your whole body feels off — appetite drops, sleep is disturbed, energy crashes. When you have eye-strain, your whole productivity collapses. The body operates as *one system* — pain in one limb is *signalled* to every other limb through the nervous system. The whole body knows.

**The community of believers is supposed to operate the same way.** When one part is hurting — financially, emotionally, spiritually — *every* part should know and respond. Not because of social pressure, but because of *jasad* — being one body.

**Yaad rakho:** Body ki misal *medically* sahih hai. Sar dard mein poora body off-track ho jaata hai — neend, bhook, energy sab gir jaati hai. Yahi ḥaal ummah ka hona chahiye — kahin koi taklif mein hai, baqi sab usay mehsoos karein. Yeh "jasad" hone ka matlab hai. Sirf "concern karna" nahin — *ek hi nervous system ka hissa* hona.

### The diagnostic for our own community

Apply this ḥadīth as a *diagnostic*:

- When refugees are killed in Gaza, in Sudan, in Myanmar — do *we* feel pain, or do we scroll past?
- When a neighbour is struggling with rent — do *we* know?
- When a sister in the masjid hasn't come in three weeks — do *we* notice?

If we *don't* feel the pain of these limbs, the diagnostic is: *we may not be in the same body*. Or — more precisely — *our spiritual nervous system is disconnected*.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh hadith *diagnostic* hai bhi. Apne aap se poochho — kya tum Gaza ki taklif mehsoos karti ho? Kya tumhe pata hai ke padosan kis pareshani mein hai? Kya tumne notice kiya ke woh behan teen hafte se masjid mein nahin? Agar nahin — to body ka nerve system kahaan disconnect hua, dekho.

### The Qurʾānic parallel

The Qurʾān supports this with the verse:

> *"The believing men and women are *awliyāʾ* of one another — they enjoin good and forbid evil…"* (Qurʾān 9:71)

*Awliyāʾ* (singular *walī*) = protectors, allies, intimate associates. The verse establishes the *legal* fact of believers' mutual *walāyah* (allegiance/protection); this ḥadīth gives the *organic* metaphor — they're one body.

**Yaad rakho:** Quran 9:71 mein Allah ne kaha hai *"muʾminoon aur muʾminaat ek doosre ke walī (protector / ally) hain"*. Yahi baat yeh hadith *jism (body)* ki misal se kehti hai. Quran ne *legal* taur pe bandha, hadith ne *organic* tasveer di. Donon ek hi haqeeqat ke do pehlu hain.

## 38 · السَّفَرُ قِطْعَةٌ مِنَ الْعَذَابِ — "Travel is a *piece* of punishment"

**Source:** Bukhārī, Muslim. Narrated by Abū Hurayrah (raḍiyallāhu ʿanhu).

The full wording: *"Travel is a piece of punishment — it deprives one of his food, his drink, and his sleep. So when one has accomplished his purpose, let him hasten back to his family."*

### The opening surprise

The opening line is *surprising*. We normally think of travel as adventure, tourism, vacation — a *positive*. The Prophet ﷺ frames it as *qiṭʿah min al-ʿadhāb* — *a piece of punishment*. Not a small phrase.

Why? Because — at the time of the ḥadīth — and still substantially today — travel involves *deprivation*:

- Disrupted sleep (different bed, different schedule, jet-lag).
- Disrupted food (you eat what's available, not what you want).
- Disrupted drink (water quality changes, hydration drops).
- Disrupted family bonds (you're away from those who love you).
- Disrupted routines (your prayers, your work, your studies all shift).

Each disruption is a *small punishment* — not a moral punishment, but a *physical and emotional* deprivation. The ḥadīth simply *names* what we tend to romanticise.

**Yaad rakho:** Safar ko aaj ki dunya "vacation" kehti hai — magar Rasool ﷺ ne usay *azaab ka tukda* kaha. Kyun? Kyunki safar mein neend, khaana, paani, ghar walay — sab disturb hote hain. Yeh moral punishment nahin — *jismani aur jazbati* takleef hai. Hadith sirf is haqeeqat ko bay-naqaab karti hai jo hum aksar bhul jaate hain.

### Why "hasten back" — the operational instruction

The second half of the ḥadīth gives the *practical* guidance: *"when one has accomplished his purpose, let him hasten back to his family."*

This is not a casual phrase. The Prophet ﷺ is instructing: *don't stretch the trip*. If your business / work / errand is complete, *go home*. Why?

- **Your family has rights** (Ḥadīth 38 implicitly establishes this). Wife, children, parents — your absence is a deprivation *for them too*.
- **Friday and congregational prayers** — Imām Khaṭṭābī (commentator) explicitly notes: residency is encouraged so that you don't keep missing the congregational prayers (which are not obligatory in congregation for a traveller).
- **Your own balance** — extended travel erodes your spiritual rhythms.

So *travel-as-needed*, *return-as-soon-as-business-is-done*. This is the Prophetic discipline of travel.

**Yaad rakho:** Doosri half mein amali hukm hai — *"jab kaam khatam ho, ghar lautho jaldi"*. Yeh sirf nasihat nahin — yeh *zimmedaari* hai. Tumhara ghar wala bhi tumhara intizaar kar rahe hain — woh bhi takleef mein hain. Jumʿah aur jamaʿat bhi miss ho rahi hai. Kaam hua — ghar wapas.

### The deeper teaching — life itself

Many of the classical commentators (Ibn al-ʿArabī, others) extend the metaphor: *the believer's whole life in this world is a journey*. We are *all* travellers in this world, with the *real* home being the Akhirah.

If the Prophet ﷺ said *physical travel is a piece of punishment* and *we should hasten back* — what does that suggest about *this world*? It suggests that the believer *also* should not over-romanticise this dunyā, should *finish their purpose here*, and not stretch the trip artificially. The connection to Ḥadīth 10 of Chapter 1 ("the world is a prison of the believer") is direct.

**Yaad rakho:** Aalim kehte hain: *yeh dunya ka safar bhi qiṭʿah min al-ʿadhāb hai*. Hum sab musafir hain — asal ghar Akhirah hai. Toh dunya mein bhi "kaam khatam, ghar wapas" wali zehniyat rakho. Yeh Chapter 1 ki *dunyā sijn al-muʾmin* wali hadith se directly jura hai. Saari kitab ke aakhir wali hadith — har do parts ko joorti hai.

## 39 · الرَّاجِعُ مِنَ الْغَزْوِ كَالْغَازِيْ — "The one returning from battle is *like* the one going to battle" *(in reward)*

**Source:** Abū Dāwūd. (The English wording: "The return from battle is like the battle itself [in reward].")

### The startling generosity

This ḥadīth is a small but striking statement of *prophetic generosity* in the *Akhirah accounting*. The warrior who went out to fight — and is now returning home — receives reward *equivalent to* the one still going out to battle.

Why is this surprising? Because we normally think: "the *active* fighter is rewarded; the *returning* one is just heading home." The ḥadīth flips this. The *return* is not "free time" — it is *part of the same act of worship*.

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith mein ek choti magar bohot khoobsurat baat hai — *jang se wapas aane wala bhi jang jaane wale jaisa ajr paata hai*. Hum sochte hain "lautna toh rest hai" — magar Rasool ﷺ ne lautne ko bhi *worship ka hissa* qaraar diya hai.

### The commentary — why the return is rewarded

Imām Mullā ʿAlī al-Qārī (*Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ* 7:402) explains: the warrior is returning *to rest, regain strength, and see to the needs of his family — with the intention of returning to battle if needed.*

Two things make the return rewarded:

1. **The *intention* to return** if needed — the heart has not exited the cause. The pause is *operational*, not *abandonment*.
2. **The *rights of family*** — going home is *itself* an obligation (Ḥadīth 38 just established this). Fulfilling family rights is worship.

So: the return is rewarded because *intention* keeps the worship alive, and *family rights* keep the worship continuing in a different form.

**Yaad rakho:** Wapasi ka ajr kyun? Do baatein hain — (1) *niyyat* qaayam hai (zaroorat parey to wapas chala jaaye), aur (2) *ghar walay ke haq* khud ibadat hain. Jang ka safar ek shakal mein ibadat thi, ghar lautna doosri shakal mein ibadat hai. Niyyat saath hai to ajr saath hai.

### Generalising the principle — the *intention chain*

The deeper teaching: **intention sustains worship across pauses**. As long as your *intention* remains aligned, the *gap* is not a gap — it is *part of the trajectory*.

Apply broadly:

- The hāfizah taking a break to look after her parents — if she intends to return to recitation, the gap is rewarded.
- The student stepping away from a degree to handle a family crisis — if his intention is to return when he can, the time is not lost.
- The active daʿwah worker who has to slow down for a season — the season is *not* a loss if intention is preserved.

This is *prophetic generosity*. Allah counts the *intention chain*, not just the *active moments*.

**Yaad rakho:** Gehri baat — *niyyat se ibadat continuous rehti hai, gap mein bhi*. Agar tumhari niyyat hai ke "main wapas aaungi" — to woh gap "lost time" nahin, ibadat ka hissa hai. Allah ka hisaab *niyyat ki zanjeer* dekhta hai — sirf active moments nahin. Yeh bohot raahat wali baat hai.

## 40 · سَيِّدُ الْقَوْمِ فِي السَّفَرِ خَادِمُهُمْ — "The leader of a group on a journey is their servant"

**Source:** Ibn Mājah, Bayhaqī (*Shuʿab al-Īmān*). (The general form *"sayyidu l-qawmi khādimuhum"* — "the leader of a people is their servant" — is widely attested across collections.)

### The closing of the chapter — servant-leadership

Of all 40 aḥādīth in Part 1 of the *Comprehensive Sayings*, this is the *last* one. (Aḥādīth 41 onward begin a new section.) And it closes with **a definition of true leadership** — not as power, but as *service*.

The Prophet ﷺ frames the leader of a travelling group as *khādim* — *servant*. Not the one served *by* the group — but the one serving the group. This is the opposite of the worldly leadership pattern, where the leader expects the comforts and the followers serve him.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh chapter ki — aur Part 1 "Jamʿ" ki — *akhri hadith* hai. Aur yeh khatm karti hai *leadership* ki definition par — *"sardar woh hai jo khidmat karta hai"*. Dunyawi leadership: log sardar ki khidmat karein. Islami leadership: sardar log ki khidmat kare. Yeh ulta hai.

### Two readings of the ḥadīth

Imām Mullā ʿAlī al-Qārī (*Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ* 7:465) gives two complementary readings:

1. **Descriptive of duty** — *the person who has been elected leader is responsible for the rest of them and should see to their interests.* Leadership = responsibility, not privilege.

2. **Descriptive of merit** — *the one from among them who voluntarily attends to their needs and serves them deserves to be their leader, even if he may not be their equal in status in other ways.* Leadership = *earned* through service.

Both are operative. The first says: "if you are leading, become a servant." The second says: "if you are serving, you are already qualifying to lead."

**Yaad rakho:** Hadith ke do matlab hain: (1) *Agar tum sardar bana diye gaye ho — tumhari zimmedaari khidmat hai.* (2) *Agar tum khidmat karte ho — tumhe sardari ka haq hai, chahe baqi maamlaat mein tum se zyada qabil log mojood hon.* Khidmat = sardari ka haq. Yeh formula yaad rakho.

### The Prophet ﷺ's example

On every major journey of his life, the Prophet ﷺ shared in the work. At the digging of the trench (Khandaq), he carried earth alongside the Companions. On the journey to Tabūk, when the camel slipped, he helped push it. When food was distributed, he took the last portion. *He was the leader because he was the servant.*

For your classmates, the personal application: **if you are leading a household, a class, a project, a halqa — your job is to serve the others, not to be served.** The honour of leading *is* the work of serving. There is no separate "honour" you can extract from the position. The service *is* the honour.

**Yaad rakho:** Rasool ﷺ har safar mein khidmat mein shamil hote thay — Khandaq mein mitti uthayi, Tabuk mein oont ko dhakka diya, khaane mein aakhri hissa liya. Sardari unka isi liye thi — woh sab se bara *khadim* thay. Tum agar ghar, class, halqa, ya project chala rahi ho — tumhari "sardari" wahi hai jo tum khidmat karti ho. Aur koi alag "izzat" position se nahin milti — khidmat hi izzat hai.

### Closing the chapter on a deliberate note

Notice how the chapter *ends*. Started with *#31: a Muslim is one from whose tongue and hand others are safe* — and ended with *#40: a leader is one who serves those under him*. The arc is from *do no harm* → *actively serve*. From *minimum protection* → *active offering*.

That is the whole chapter, compressed. *Be safe to others. Be trusted by all. Strive against your nafs. Abandon your sins. Demand evidence before accusation. Mirror without humiliating. Be one body. Travel only as needed. Sustain intention. Lead as a servant.*

The believer at the end of Chapter 4 is not the same person they were at the beginning of Chapter 1. They have walked from defining faith → embodying it as character → positioning against the world → and now: *embodied as a member of a fabric that knows itself by service.*

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter ka aakhir gor karo — #31 mein "doosron ko nuksaan na do" se shuru, #40 mein "doosron ki khidmat karo" pe khatam. *Minimum (don't harm)* se *maximum (actively serve)*. Yeh sirf chapter ka safar nahin — *poori 40 hadithon ka* safar hai. Ramsha, jab classmates ko yeh hadith batao — yeh closing arc dikhana mat bhulo. Yahi pyaari baat hai.

## The big picture — what these 10 aḥādīth are silently building

Stand back. The chapter has taken you and your classmates through **the architecture of a believer-in-community**:

1. **Definitions** — Muslim (#31), believer (#32), mujāhid (#33), muhājir (#34). Four labels, four inner tests.
2. **Relationships** — accuser & accused (#35), mirror & brother (#36), single body (#37). Justice, honesty, organic unity.
3. **The road** — travel as discipline (#38), return as worship (#39), leadership as service (#40).

These are not 10 isolated wisdoms. They are 10 facets of *"who the believer is when seen by other believers, by all people, by Allah — at home and on the road."*

**Yaad rakho:** 10 hadith — par 1 picture banti hai. *Momin ki tasveer — alag alag rishton mein.* Class ko aakhir mein yeh sketch dikhao — *"yeh saari hadithein milkar batati hain ke momin kis kis se kya hota hai — apne se, apnon se, sab logon se, aur safar mein"*. Isolation mein dekhne se yeh moti hain — milakar haar hain.

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

Now you understand the chapter. This section is how to *deliver* it to your classmates.

---

## The 6 landmines to watch for

### Landmine 1 · #31 Heard as "I haven't *physically* harmed anyone, so I'm fine"

Classmates will hear *"tongue and hand"* and check the *hand* — *"I haven't hit anyone"* — and assume they're clear. The *tongue* gets a quick pass — *"I haven't sworn at anyone"* — but the everyday harms (gheebat, taunting, sarcasm, undermining, hurtful jokes) sneak through. Push them on the tongue.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Hadith ne *zaban aur haath* dono ka zikr kiya. Haath ka hisaab toh aksar log lete hain — 'maine toh kisi ko maara nahin'. Magar zaban ka hisaab kaun leta hai? Gheebat ki? Taanay maaray? Maaza diya kisi ki kamzori pe? Group mein kisi ki nuqsaani ki? Yahi zaban ka muharra hai. Aur zaban se aksar zyada nuksaan hota hai haath se. Apne zaban ka hisaab lo — har shaam, har raat. Kya aaj koi mom mein mehfooz nahin raha mere zaban se?"*

### Landmine 2 · #32 #33 #34 — Heard as "These are all the same thing"

The four definitions (Muslim, mu'min, mujāhid, muhājir) sound similar in English — and *worse*, classmates often hear them as alternative names for "good person." But each is a *distinct level*. If you blur them, you flatten the structure.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh char lafz alag alag hain — woh ek doosre ko replace nahin karte, balke build karte hain. *Muslim* matlab tum se doosre mehfooz hain — yeh **minimum** hai. *Muʾmin* matlab log tum pe apni jaan-maal soup sakte hain — yeh **upgrade** hai. *Mujāhid* matlab tum apne nafs se larrti ho — yeh **andar** ki baat hai. *Muhājir* matlab tum gunaahon ko chhorrti ho — yeh **mustaqil amal** hai. Char level hain — ek doosre se zyada ucha — ek doosre par bante hain. Class ko yeh staircase board pe likhwa do."*

### Landmine 3 · #33 — "Greater jihād" denying battlefield jihād

Some classmates will pick up the "*greater jihād is the nafs*" framing and conclude that battlefield jihād is unimportant or even abolished. Wrong. The Prophet ﷺ *included* battlefield jihād in this expanded definition — he didn't exclude it.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Hadith yeh nahin keh rahi ke maidan ka jihād khatam ya choti baat hai. Yeh keh rahi hai ke jihād ka *asal matlab* nafs ke khilaaf mehnat hai — aur woh maidan wala bhi shamil hai. Mujāhid duniya ke aakhir tak duniya mein hota rahega — kabhi maidan mein, kabhi nafs ke maidan mein. Donon haqiqi hain. Yeh hadith sirf priorities clear kar rahi hai — sab se aam jihād nafs ka hai, kyunki woh roz lagaa hua hai."*

### Landmine 4 · #35 — "Burden of proof" reduced to "court rule"

Classmates may think this is a *legal* ḥadīth — relevant only for judges and qazis. Wrong. The principle of "demand evidence before accusing" applies *everywhere* — in family, friends, gossip, WhatsApp forwards, self-suspicion.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh hadith sirf judge sahab ke liye nahin — *har waqt*, *har rishtay* mein laagu hoti hai. Apne shauhar pe shak hai? Saboot hai? Nahin? Toh chup raho. Padosan ke baare mein koi baat suni? Saboot hai? Nahin? Chup raho. WhatsApp forward aaya — kisi public figure pe ilzaam? Saboot hai? Nahin? Mat aagay phenkho. Yeh hadith roz ke akhlaq ki bunyaad hai. Bina saboot ilzaam — gunaah hai. Husn-e-zann — yahi default hai."*

### Landmine 5 · #36 — "Mirror" used as license for harsh criticism

Some classmates may take *"believer = mirror"* and conclude: "I'm being a good Muslim by pointing out everyone's flaws." Wrong. The mirror metaphor has *two* parts they forget: (a) the mirror shows *only the person*, not anyone else; and (b) the mirror shows *truthfully*, not in a humiliating or judgemental tone.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Aaina ke do qaaide yaad rakho. (1) Aaina sirf *us insan ko* dikhata hai — *doosron ko* uski kami nahin chhapaata. Yani jis ko kami batani hai, *saamne* batao, *peeth peechay* nahin. (2) Aaina *taunt nahin karta* — woh sirf dikhata hai. Tum bhi gentle ho, sirf bayan karo, judgement na karo. Agar tum kisi ko 'mirror' bante ho lekin *tone* taanaa wali hai, *audience* doosre log hain — tum aaina nahin, *megaphone* ho. Yeh galat hai."*

### Landmine 6 · #38 — "Travel is punishment" interpreted as "tourism is haram"

Classmates may take ḥadīth 38 and conclude that travel is forbidden or sinful. Wrong. The Prophet ﷺ himself travelled — for Hajj, for trade (before prophethood), for battles. The point is *not* "don't travel" — it is "don't *over-romanticise* travel, and *return* when business is done."

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh hadith yeh nahin keh rahi ke safar haraam hai ya bura hai. Rasool ﷺ khud safar karte thay — Hajj ke liye, tijaarat ke liye, jang ke liye. Hadith yeh keh rahi hai ke safar mein takleef hoti hai — neend, khaana, ghar walay sab affect hote hain — toh isay over-romanticise mat karo, aur jab kaam khatam ho, ghar wapas lautho jaldi. Yeh balance hai. Halaal safar bilkul jaaiz hai — magar ghar ka haq, jamaʿat ka haq, aur apni rooḥ ka haq bhi hai."*

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 4

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum aage dekhenge"* or *"yeh book ke scope se bahar hai"*:

- ❌ **Full fiqh of evidence and oaths** triggered by #35 — judges, witnesses, *qasāmah*, oaths of *liʿān*. We're doing the *principle*, not a procedural law course.
- ❌ **Full theology of jihad** triggered by #33 — siyar, conditions of war, kufr/iman binaries. Stay with the *nafs-jihad* framing for this class.
- ❌ **Contemporary geopolitics** triggered by #37 (one-body). Classmates may want to discuss Gaza / Sudan etc. — *gently* allow brief mention, but don't let it dominate. The *ḥadīth* is about how *you* feel and respond, not a policy debate.
- ❌ **Detailed travel-fiqh rulings** triggered by #38 — qaṣr, jamʿ, fasting in travel. Reference briefly that travel has its own concessions, but don't open the full ruling-set.
- ❌ **Hierarchies of leadership / fiqh of *imāmah*** triggered by #40 — political theory, conditions of leadership. Stay with the *servant-leader* principle for this class.
- ❌ **The Bukhārī version of #34** ("the muhājir is one who abandons *what Allah has forbidden*") — though it's beautifully related, we're using the Aḥmad/Bayhaqī version that the book cites. *Same spirit, slight variation* — don't confuse classmates by comparing.

**Yaad rakho:** Pre-loading aur tangents madad nahin karte. 10 hadithein hain — har ek ke andar dam hai. Apne aap mein. Aur cheezein mat daalo. Class ka focus = *yeh 10 hadithein dil mein bait jaayein*. Bas.

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Are they still hearing #31 as "no physical harm only"? *(Zaban ka hisaab le rahe hain ya sirf haath ka?)*
2. Are they blurring the four definitions into "same thing"? *(Muslim / muʾmin / mujāhid / muhājir — alag levels samajh aaye?)*
3. Are they dismissing battlefield jihād after #33? *(Maidan ka jihad bhi shamil hai — yeh samjh aaya?)*
4. Are they reducing #35 to "only relevant in court"? *(Roz ke akhlaaq mein laagu kar rahe hain?)*
5. Are they using #36 as a license to publicly criticise others? *(Aaina ki do shartein yaad hain — saamne, gentle?)*
6. Are they over-applying #38 to mean "travel is haraam"? *(Halaal safar ko bilkul jaaiz mana rahe hain?)*
7. Are they hearing #40 as a flat statement, missing the closing arc? *(Khidmat = sardari ka formula yaad hai?)*
