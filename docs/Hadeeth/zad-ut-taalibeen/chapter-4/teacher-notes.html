Teaching arc: the chapter opens with four nested definitions of who a believer actually is (Theme 1), moves into the fabric of believers' relationships with each other (Theme 2), and closes with the believer on the road — travel, return, leadership (Theme 3). Watch the arc from #31 ("do no harm") to #40 ("actively serve") — from minimum to maximum. That arc is the closing message of Part 1 itself.

Chapter ka safar: kaun hai momin → momin doosre momin ke saath kaisa → momin safar mein kaisa. Aur khaas baat — #31 mein "doosron ko nuksaan na do" se shuru, #40 mein "doosron ki khidmat karo" pe khatam. Minimum → maximum.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

  1. Recite each of the 10 aḥādīth in Arabic, with correct meaning in English + Roman Urdu.
  2. Name the source of each ḥadīth (Bukhārī, Muslim, Tirmidhī, Abū Dāwūd, Aḥmad, Bayhaqī, Ibn Mājah).
  3. Identify the four nested definitions of a believer (#31–#34) — Muslim, muʾmin, mujāhid, muhājir — and how each builds on the previous.
  4. Explain that the test of "Muslim" is what other people experience of you (tongue + hand) — not what you do in private worship.
  5. Distinguish "Muslim" (others safe from) from "muʾmin" (others trust with) — and recognise al-nās means all people, not just Muslims.
  6. Explain the principle of the greater jihād against the nafs, without dismissing the legitimacy of battlefield jihād.
  7. State the Islamic legal principle: burden of proof on the accuser; oath on the denier, and apply it to daily life, not just courtrooms.
  8. Articulate both halves of the mirror/brother ḥadīth — honest in front, protective behind — and contrast it with the worldly pattern.
  9. Apply the "single body" metaphor as a diagnostic for one's own sensitivity to the umma's pain.
  10. Read "travel is a piece of punishment" as a Prophetic discipline, not a prohibition on travel.
  11. Explain the "intention chain" — how the returning warrior earns the same reward as the going warrior.
  12. State the closing principle: leadership = service. Apply it to a household, a class, a project.

Ḥadīth 31 · الْمُسْلِمُ مَنْ سَلِمَ الْمُسْلِمُونَ مِنْ لِسَانِهِ وَيَدِهِ · The Muslim is one from whom believers are safe

Theme 1 — Who IS a believer? Level 1: the minimum.

الْمُسْلِمُ مَنْ سَلِمَ الْمُسْلِمُونَ مِنْ لِسَانِهِ وَيَدِهِ

"The Muslim is the one from whose tongue and hand other believers are safe."

"Musalman woh hai jiski zaban aur haath se baqi musalman mehfooz hain."

Bukhārī, Muslim
Where this fits: Theme 1, node 1 — the minimum threshold for the label "Muslim". Other people's safety is the yardstick.

سَلِمَ ... مِنْ · salima ... min — "is safe / sound from"

Meaning
The Arabic structure is "are safe/sound from" — the believers are at peace when this person is near them. The test is outward and relational — measured by others' experience of you.
Roman Urdu
"Salima ... min" matlab "... se mehfooz hain". Test outward hai — namaz tum aur Allah ke beech hai, magar tumhari zaban-haath ka asar sab dekh sakte hain. Yeh hadith iss visible yardstick par hai.
The two exits
Of all body parts the Prophet ﷺ could have named, he picked tongue + hand. These are the two main doorways for harm to others — almost every interpersonal sin exits through one of these.
Example
A man prays five times, fasts Ramadan — but in conversation he constantly belittles, taunts, makes hurtful jokes. Per this ḥadīth, he has failed the minimum of "Muslim". The label isn't earned.

⚠️ Landmine 1 — "I haven't hit anyone, so I'm fine"

Classmates will check the hand first and assume they're clear. Push them on the tongue: "Zaban ka hisaab kaun leta hai? Gheebat ki? Taanay maaray? Mazaaq mein kisi ko zaleel kiya? Group mein kisi ki kamzori share ki? Zaban se aksar zyada nuksaan hota hai haath se. Apne zaban ka roz hisaab lo."

💡 Teaching tip — but what about non-Muslims?

Classmates may notice al-muslimūn ("the Muslims") and ask: "Can a Muslim harm non-Muslims?" Pre-empt: "Yeh hadith minimum bata rahi hai. Agar tumse musalman bhi mehfooz nahin, to label hi nahin lagta. Doosre nass se ghair-muslim ka haq alag se establish hota hai — yeh isay khatam nahin karti."

أَمِنَهُ النَّاسُ · aminahu l-nās — "the people trusted him"

Meaning
Same root as īmān (faith) and amānah (trust) — a-m-n. The believer (muʾmin) is the one whom al-nās (all people, Muslim or not) trust — specifically with their dimāʾ (lives/blood) and amwāl (wealth).
Roman Urdu
"Amana" aur "iman" ek hi jar se. Muʾmin woh hai jis pe sab log (sirf musalman nahin) — apni jaan aur maal ki amanat soup sakte hain. Yeh trust ki *highest* shakal hai.
The Makkan model
Even before prophethood, the polytheists of Quraysh called the Prophet ﷺ al-Amīn ("the Trustworthy"). They did not share his beliefs — but they deposited their valuables with him. That is the standard this ḥadīth holds up.
Roman Urdu
Rasool ﷺ ko "Al-Amīn" laqab nubuwwat ke pehle Quraish ke kafir ne diya tha — woh aap ka aqida share nahin karte thay magar aapki amanat-daari par yaqeen rakhte thay. Yahi yardstick hai.

Why "life and wealth" specifically

The maqāṣid
Dimāʾ (life) and amwāl (wealth) are two of the five maqāṣid al-sharīʿah — the highest objectives of Islamic Law (the others: dīn, ʿaql, nasl). To be trusted with these two = the highest social trust threshold.
Diagnostic
Ask yourself: do people leave their children with you? Their elderly parents? Their money during travel? Their secrets that could destroy them? That is the diagnostic of al-muʾmin.
Roman Urdu
Jaan aur maal sab se bhaari amanat hain. Apne aap se poochho — log apne bachhe, apni budhi maa, apna pesa, apne raaz — yeh chaar cheezein tum pe chhod sakte hain? Yeh muʾmin ka asal test hai.

💡 Teaching tip — write the staircase on the board

This is the first of a four-step staircase. Draw it now and keep it visible for #33 and #34 too: #31 Muslim (minimum: don't harm) → #32 Muʾmin (above min: be trusted) → #33 Mujāhid (inner: strive against nafs) → #34 Muhājir (daily: abandon sins). Each rung includes the previous.

Ḥadīth 33 · وَالْمُجَاهِدُ مَنْ جَاهَدَ نَفْسَهُ فِي طَاعَةِ الله · The mujāhid is one who strives against his nafs in obedience to Allah

Theme 1 — Who IS a believer? Level 3: inner struggle.

وَالْمُجَاهِدُ مَنْ جَاهَدَ نَفْسَهُ فِي طَاعَةِ الله

جَاهَدَ · jāhada — "to strive against resistance"

Meaning
From the root j-h-d = exertion against resistance. Battlefield is one application of this root; it is not the only or even primary meaning. The verb means "to push hard against something that pushes back".
Roman Urdu
"Jihād" ka asal matlab — mehnat against resistance. Battlefield wala matlab ek application hai, asal matlab nahin. Asal mujāhid woh hai jo apne nafs se Allah ki itāʿat mein mehnat karta hai — yeh roz lagta hai.
Concrete examples
Fajr when sleep is heavy. Tongue held when gossip is tempting. Eyes lowered when curiosity is loud. Zakāh paid when nafs clings to money. Forgiveness offered when nafs wants revenge. Each = jihād.

The "greater jihād" tradition

Background
There is a narration (whose chain scholars debate) that the Prophet ﷺ returned from a battle and said: "We have returned from the lesser jihād to the greater jihād" — meaning the struggle against the nafs. Whether or not that specific narration is rigorously authentic, its meaning is fully established by sound ḥadīths like this one (#33).
Roman Urdu
"Jihād-e-akbar" wali rivayat ki sanad pe ikhtilaaf hai magar uska mafhoom sahih hadith se confirm hota hai (jaise yahi #33). Mujāhid sirf maidan wala nahin — nafs ke khilaaf roz mehnat karne wala bhi mujāhid hai.
Both included
The book explicitly notes: "the lesser jihād — fighting in the path of Allah — is included in this ḥadīth." Battlefield jihād is not abolished; it is contained within the broader definition.

⚠️ Landmine 3 — battlefield jihād dismissed

Don't let classmates conclude that battlefield jihād is unimportant. Push back: "Hadith yeh nahin keh rahi ke maidan ka jihād khatam ho gaya. Yeh keh rahi hai ke jihād ka asal matlab nafs ke khilaaf mehnat hai — aur woh maidan wala bhi shamil hai. Donon haqiqi hain. Sirf priorities clear ho rahi hain — sab se aam jihād nafs ka hai, kyunki woh roz lagaa hua hai."

Ḥadīth 34 · وَالْمُهَاجِرُ مَنْ هَجَرَ الْخَطَايَا وَالذُّنُوبَ · The muhājir is one who abandons mistakes & sins

Theme 1 — Who IS a believer? Level 4: daily, willed.

وَالْمُهَاجِرُ مَنْ هَجَرَ الْخَطَايَا وَالذُّنُوبَ

wa-l-muhājiru man hajara l-khaṭāyā wa-l-dhunūb

"And the muhājir (emigrant) is the one who abandons mistakes and sins."

"Aur muhājir woh hai jo khataaon aur gunaahon ko chhor de."

Why moral hijrah is harder than geography

Reason 1
Physical hijrah ends — you arrive, you settle. Moral hijrah is constant — your nafs proposes the sin again tomorrow, next week, next year.
Reason 2
Physical hijrah was one act. Moral hijrah is every act of every day.
Reason 3
Physical hijrah had visible borders. Moral hijrah has no exit point — you can't tell when you've fully crossed.
Roman Urdu
Geography wali hijrah aasaan thi — pohnchi, kaam khatam. Gunah wali hijrah har waqt chalti hai. Roz tumhara nafs gunah propose karta hai, roz tumhe inkaar karna parta hai. Aur visible "border" bhi nahin — kabhi pata nahin chalta kahaan tak pohnchi.

The four-step staircase — recap

#31 Muslim
Don't harm others (tongue, hand). Minimum.
#32 Muʾmin
Others trust him with life and wealth. Above minimum.
#33 Mujāhid
Strives against his nafs in obedience. Inner struggle.
#34 Muhājir
Abandons sins continually. Inner movement.
Roman Urdu
Char level, ek doosre par bante hain. Muslim (zaban-haath se mehfooz) → Muʾmin (jaan-maal ki amanat) → Mujāhid (nafs ke khilaaf jang) → Muhājir (gunah chhornay wala). Class ko yeh staircase board pe likhwa do.

⚠️ Landmine 2 — blurring the four definitions

Classmates will hear all four as "synonyms for good Muslim". Don't let them. Each is a distinct level. "Yeh char alag baatein hain. Pehli outward (doosron ki mehfooz), doosri trust ki upper level (jaan-maal ki amanat), teesri inner (nafs ka jihād), chauthi mustaqil amal (gunaah chhorna). Mat blur karo."

💡 Teaching tip — bring the Bukhārī parallel

Briefly mention: there's a famous parallel in Bukhārī: al-muhājiru man hajara mā nahā Llāhu ʿanhu ("the emigrant is one who abandons what Allah has forbidden"). Same spirit, slight variation. Don't dwell — but show classmates that this principle has multiple authentic chains.

Ḥadīth 35 · الْبَيِّنَةُ عَلَى الْمُدَّعِيْ وَالْيَمِينُ عَلَى الْمُدَّعَى عَلَيْهِ · Evidence on the accuser, oath on the one accused

Theme 2 — Believers among believers. One of the foundational principles of Islamic legal procedure.

الْبَيِّنَةُ عَلَى الْمُدَّعِيْ وَالْيَمِينُ عَلَى الْمُدَّعَى عَلَيْهِ

al-bayyinatu ʿalā l-muddaʿī wa-l-yamīnu ʿalā l-muddaʿā ʿalayh

"The burden of proof is on the accuser, and the oath is on the one accused."

"Saboot ilzaam lagaane wale ke zimme, aur qasam jis par ilzaam hai (mulzim) ke zimme."

Bayhaqī · (Also Tirmidhī, Dāraqutnī. Imam Nawawī cites this as Ḥadīth 33 in his Forty)
Where this fits: Theme 2, node 1 — installs the principle of demanding evidence before accepting an accusation. Applies in court and in life.

Why the burden falls on the accuser

Logic
The default state of every human at any moment is not committing the sin in question. Not doing something is the default. So the burden of proof falls on the one claiming a deviation from the default — the accuser.
Roman Urdu
Default position = bay-gunah. "Tum gunahgaar ho" kehne wala woh hai jo normal se hatkar dawa kar raha hai. Saboot uska kaam hai, na ke mulzim ka. Yeh aqlmandi ka usool bhi hai aur insaaniyat ka bhi.
Modern parallel
This is the same logic as "innocent until proven guilty" — which Islamic Law established 14 centuries ago, in this exact form.

The everyday application — far beyond court

Family
Don't accuse spouse / sibling / child without evidence. Apply ḥusn al-ẓann (good opinion).
Workplace / WhatsApp
Don't pass on a claim about a colleague — or forward a claim about a public figure — unless evidence exists.
Self-reflection
When suspicion of someone arises in your heart, ask: "What's my evidence?" No evidence = the suspicion is to be dismissed, not nurtured.
Roman Urdu
Yeh hadith roz marra mein laagu hai — family mein, dost mein, WhatsApp groups mein, apne dil ke shak mein. Bina saboot ilzaam mat lagao. Bina saboot baat mat aage badhao. Bina saboot dil mein shak mat paalo. Husn-e-zann default hai.

⚠️ Landmine 4 — "this is only for judges"

Classmates may shelve this ḥadīth as a "court rule". Don't let them. "Yeh hadith har waqt, har rishtay mein laagu hai. Shauhar pe shak hai, saboot hai? Padosan pe shak hai, saboot hai? WhatsApp pe public figure ka ilzaam aaya, saboot hai? Saboot nahin = chup raho, ya husn-e-zann rakho. Yeh roz ke akhlaaq ki bunyaad hai."

Ḥadīth 36 · الْمُؤْمِنُ مِرْآةُ الْمُؤْمِنِ · A believer is a mirror for a believer

Theme 2 — Believers among believers. Honest in front, protective behind.

الْمُؤْمِنُ مِرْآةُ الْمُؤْمِنِ وَالْمُؤْمِنُ أَخُو الْمُؤْمِنِ، يَكُفُّ عَلَيْهِ ضَيْعَتَهُ وَيَحُوْطُهُ مِنْ وَرَائِهِ

"A believer is a mirror for a believer, and a believer is the brother of a believer; he safeguards him from destruction and protects him in his absence."

The mirror metaphor — three properties

Property 1
A mirror shows you only to you — it doesn't broadcast your flaws to others. So a believer corrects another privately, not publicly.
Property 2
A mirror shows you truthfully — it doesn't flatter, doesn't lie. So a believer corrects honestly, not with empty politeness.
Property 3
A mirror is without judgement — it doesn't mock or shame. So a believer corrects gently, in love, not in superiority.
Roman Urdu
Aaina ki teen khoobian: (1) sirf tumhe dikhata hai, doosron ko nahin → privately batao. (2) Sach dikhata hai, jhoot nahin → honest raho. (3) Taunt nahin karta → narmi se kaho. Yeh teenon ek saath. Sirf "sach kaha" kafi nahin — saamne kaha? gentle kaha? Donon honi chahiye.

"Protects him in his absence" — Mullā ʿAlī al-Qārī

Commentary
Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 8:720: "preserving his respect, honour, and rights in front of others in his absence."
Concrete
When your brother/sister in faith is not in the room and someone speaks badly of them, you defend them. When their reputation is being damaged, you intervene. When their interests are at stake in a meeting they couldn't attend, you represent them faithfully.
Roman Urdu
"Min warāʾihi" matlab uski peeth peechay. Jab woh kamre mein nahin aur log uski buraai karein — tum uska defence karo. Uski izzat, uska haq, uske mufaad peeth peechay tumhari zimmedaari hain.

The reversal — worldly vs believer

Worldly pattern
Flattery in front, gossip behind.
Believer pattern
Truth in front, defence behind.
Roman Urdu
Dunyawi tarz: saamne taareef, peeth peechay buraai. Momin ka tarz: saamne sach, peeth peechay defence. Yeh do tarzon ka asal farq hai — ek "normal" community aur ek "muʾmin community" ka farq.

⚠️ Landmine 5 — "I'm being a good mirror by criticising publicly"

Some classmates will use this ḥadīth as license. Disarm: "Aaina sirf us shakhs ko dikhata hai — doosron ko uski kami nahin chhapaata. Agar tum public mein criticise kar rahi ho, tum aaina nahin, megaphone ho. Aaina ki teen shartein hain — saamne, sach, gentle. Teenon ki zaroorat hai."

Ḥadīth 37 · الْمُؤْمِنُونَ كَرَجُلٍ وَّاحِدٍ · Believers are like a single person

Theme 2 — Believers among believers. Not two individuals — one body.

الْمُؤْمِنُونَ كَرَجُلٍ وَّاحِدٍ إِنِ اشْتَكَى عَيْنُهُ اشْتَكَى كُلُّهُ وَإِنِ اشْتَكَى رَأْسُهُ اشْتَكَى كُلُّهُ

al-muʾminūna ka-rajulin wāḥidin, ini shtakā ʿaynuhu shtakā kulluh, wa-ini shtakā raʾsuhu shtakā kulluh

"The believers are like a single person; if his eye is in pain, his whole body is in pain, and if his head is in pain, his whole body is in pain."

"Momin ek hi shakhs (ek jism) ki tarah hain: agar uski aankh mein dard ho to poora jism dard mein hota hai, aur agar uske sar mein dard ho to poora jism dard mein hota hai."

Muslim
Where this fits: Theme 2, node 3 — from mirror (#36, two individuals) to body (#37, one organism). The deepest version of believers' unity.

The escalation from #36 to #37

#36
Two believers = mirror & brother. Two distinct individuals in close relationship.
#37
Believers = one body. Not even two. Limbs of the same organism. The separation between believers is dissolved.
Roman Urdu
Hadith #36 = do alag log (aaina, bhai). Hadith #37 = ek hi badan. Yeh upgrade hai. Tumhara musalman bhai ki takleef = tumhari takleef. "Mera masla, tumhara masla" — yeh separation hi yeh hadith todti hai.

The medical accuracy of the metaphor

Observation
Pain in one limb travels through the nervous system to the whole body — appetite drops, sleep is disturbed, fever, fatigue. The whole organism knows. This is not poetic — it is biological fact.
Application
The believer community is supposed to have a spiritual nervous system with the same connectivity. Pain anywhere registers everywhere. If it doesn't — the nervous system is disconnected.
Roman Urdu
Body wali misal scientifically sahih hai — sar dard mein poora badan off ho jaata hai. Hadith yeh keh rahi hai ke ummah ka "spiritual nerve system" bhi waisa hi hona chahiye — ek hissay ki takleef sab tak pohnchay.

Diagnostic for your own heart

Ask
When refugees are killed (Gaza, Sudan, Myanmar) — do you feel it, or scroll past? When a neighbour struggles with rent — do you know? When a sister hasn't been in masjid for three weeks — do you notice?
Diagnosis
If you don't feel the pain of these limbs — your nervous system may be disconnected. The ḥadīth gives this as a personal diagnostic, not a guilt trip.
Roman Urdu
Apne aap se poochho — kya tum Gaza ki taklif mehsoos karti ho? Kya tumhe pata hai padosan kis pareshani mein hai? Kya tumne notice kiya woh behan teen hafte se masjid mein nahin? Agar nahin — to "jasad" ka nerve kahaan disconnect hua, dekho.

📿 Connect to Qurʾān · 9:71

وَالْمُؤْمِنُوْنَ وَالْمُؤْمِنَاتُ بَعْضُهُمْ أَوْلِيَاءُ بَعْضٍ يَأْمُرُوْنَ بِالْمَعْرُوْفِ وَيَنْهَوْنَ عَنِ الْمُنْكَرِ

"The believing men and the believing women — they are awliyāʾ (protectors, allies) of one another — they enjoin good and forbid wrong..."

Why this fits: The Qurʾān establishes the legal walāyah (mutual protection) of believers; this ḥadīth gives the organic metaphor — they are one body. Legal fact + organic image = the same reality.

Ḥadīth 38 · السَّفَرُ قِطْعَةٌ مِنَ الْعَذَابِ · Travel is a piece of punishment

Theme 3 — The believer on the road. Travel as deprivation; return as duty.

السَّفَرُ قِطْعَةٌ مِّنَ الْعَذَابِ يَمْنَعُ أَحَدَكُمْ نَوْمَهُ وَطَعَامَهُ وَشَرَابَهُ فَإِذَا قَضَى أَحَدُكُمْ نَهْمَتَهُ مِنْ وَجْهِهِ فَلْيُعَجِّلْ إِلَى أَهْلِهِ

al-safaru qiṭʿatun mina l-ʿadhābi yamnaʿu aḥadakum nawmahu wa ṭaʿāmahu wa sharābah, fa-idhā qaḍā aḥadukum nahmatahu min wajhihi fal-yuʿajjil ilā ahlih

"Travelling involves a degree of punishment; it deprives one of his sleep, food, and drink. Therefore, when one has accomplished his purpose, he should hurry back to his family."

"Safar azaab ka tukda hai — yeh tum se tumhari neend, khana aur paani cheen leta hai. Toh jab apna kaam pura ho jaaye, ghar walon ke paas jaldi lautho."

Bukhārī, Muslim
Where this fits: Theme 3, node 1 — opens the closing arc of the chapter. Travel demystified; return prioritised.

قِطْعَة · qiṭʿah — "a piece / a portion"

Meaning
Not "punishment like" — the Prophet ﷺ said travel is a portion of punishment itself. Not moral punishment — physical and emotional deprivation. The ḥadīth names what we tend to romanticise.
Roman Urdu
Aaj ki dunya safar ko "vacation" kehti hai — magar Rasool ﷺ ne usay azaab ka tukda kaha. Kyun? Neend, khaana, paani, ghar walay — sab disturb hote hain. Yeh moral punishment nahin — jismani aur jazbati takleef hai.
Pro tip
When you hear yourself romanticising a trip — "I just need to get away" — recall this ḥadīth. Travel is not a panacea. It's a piece of punishment, justified only when there's a purpose worth the deprivation.

The operational instruction — "hasten home"

Reason 1
Your family has rights. Wife, children, parents — your absence is a deprivation for them too.
Reason 2
Imam Khaṭṭābī (commentator) notes: extended travel means missing Jumʿah and congregational prayers (which a traveller is not obliged to attend in congregation).
Reason 3
Extended travel erodes your spiritual rhythms (consistent ʿibādah, your circle of believers, your routines).
Roman Urdu
Doosri half mein amali hukm hai — "jab kaam khatam ho, ghar lautho jaldi". Teen wajhain: (1) ghar wale tumhara intizaar kar rahe hain, woh bhi takleef mein hain. (2) Jumʿah aur jamaʿat miss ho rahi hai. (3) Apni roḥani routine bigad rahi hai. Kaam hua — ghar wapas.

The deeper teaching — this dunyā is a journey too

Insight
Classical commentators extend the metaphor: the believer's whole life in this world is a journey. If physical travel is "a piece of punishment", what does that say about this world? It suggests the same Prophetic discipline applies: don't over-romanticise dunyā, finish your purpose, don't stretch the trip artificially.
Roman Urdu
Aalim kehte hain: yeh dunya ka safar bhi qiṭʿah min al-ʿadhāb hai. Hum sab musafir hain — asal ghar Akhirah hai. Dunya mein bhi "kaam khatam, ghar wapas" wali zehniyat rakho. Yeh hadith Chapter 1 ki dunya sijn al-muʾmin wali hadith se directly jura hai.

⚠️ Landmine 6 — "Travel is haraam"

Don't let classmates over-extend. "Yeh hadith yeh nahin keh rahi ke safar haraam hai. Rasool ﷺ khud safar karte thay — Hajj, tijaarat, jang ke liye. Yeh sirf keh rahi hai: takleef ko pehchaan lo, over-romanticise mat karo, aur kaam khatam ho to ghar wapas lautho."

Why the return is rewarded

Commentary
Mullā ʿAlī al-Qārī (Mirqāt al-Mafātīḥ 7:402): the warrior returns to rest, regain strength, and see to the needs of his family — with the intention of returning to battle if needed.
Two anchors
(1) Intention to return if needed — the heart hasn't exited the cause. (2) Family rights are themselves an obligation — going home is the continuation of worship in a different form.
Roman Urdu
Wapasi ka ajr kyun? Do baatein hain — (1) niyyat qaayam hai (zaroorat parey to wapas chala jaaye). (2) Ghar walay ke haq khud ibadat hain. Jang ka safar ek shakal mein ibadat thi, ghar lautna doosri shakal mein ibadat hai. Niyyat saath hai to ajr saath hai.

The "intention chain" — generalising the principle

Principle
Intention sustains worship across pauses. As long as your intention remains aligned, the gap is not a gap — it is part of the trajectory.
Applications
A ḥāfizah taking a break to care for parents — if intending to return to recitation, the gap is rewarded. A student stepping away from study to handle a family crisis — if intending to return, the time is not lost. A daʿwah worker slowing down for a season — the season is not lost if intention is preserved.
Roman Urdu
Gehri baat — niyyat se ibadat continuous rehti hai, gap mein bhi. Agar niyyat hai "main wapas aaungi" — to woh gap "lost time" nahin, ibadat ka hissa hai. Allah ka hisaab niyyat ki zanjeer dekhta hai — sirf active moments nahin. Yeh bohot raahat wali baat hai.

💡 Teaching tip — the relief in this ḥadīth

Many classmates feel guilt over pauses — pauses in Qurʾān memorisation, in halqa attendance, in active worship. Use this ḥadīth as raḥmah: "Niyyat saath hai? Toh gap mein bhi ajr hai. Allah ne 'intention chain' ka hisaab rakha hai — sirf 'continuous active worship' ka nahin. Yeh prophetic generosity hai. Sukoon ki saans lo — agar lautne ki niyyat hai, tum mujahid ho — chahe abhi rest mein ho."

sayyidu l-qawmi fī l-safari khādimuhum

Two readings (Mullā ʿAlī al-Qārī, Mirqāt 7:465)

Reading 1: Duty
The person elected leader is responsible for the rest and should see to their interests. Leadership = responsibility, not privilege.
Reading 2: Merit
The one who voluntarily attends to their needs and serves them deserves to be the leader — even if not their equal in other respects. Service is how leadership is earned.
Both at once
"If you are leading, become a servant." And "If you are serving, you are qualifying to lead." Two sides of the same coin.
Roman Urdu
Do matlab hain: (1) Agar tum sardar bana diye gaye ho — tumhari zimmedaari khidmat hai. (2) Agar tum khidmat karte ho — tumhe sardari ka haq hai. Khidmat = sardari ka haq. Yeh formula yaad rakho.

The Prophet ﷺ's example

Khandaq
At the digging of the trench, he carried earth alongside the Companions. He didn't supervise from above.
Tabūk
On the journey, when a camel slipped, he helped push it. He took the last portion when food was distributed.
Principle
He was the leader because he was the servant. The two were not separate.
Roman Urdu
Rasool ﷺ har safar mein khidmat mein shamil rehte thay — Khandaq mein mitti uthayi, Tabuk mein oont ko dhakka diya, khaane mein aakhri hissa liya. Aap ki sardari isi liye thi — woh sab se bara khadim thay.

Application — for Ramsha and the classmates

Household
If you are head of a household — your "headship" is the work you do for them. Not the comforts you extract from them.
Class / halqa
If you are leading the halqa — your job is to serve the others. Carry the chairs. Set up the tea. The honour is the work.
Project / team
If you lead a project — your authority comes from the burden you carry, not the title above your name.
Roman Urdu
Ghar, class, halqa, project — jahaan bhi tum "lead" kar rahi ho, wahaan teri "izzat" wahi hai jo tu khidmat karti hai. Position se alag koi "izzat" nahin milti — khidmat hi izzat hai.

💡 Closing the chapter — show the arc

This is the last ḥadīth of Part 1. Notice the chapter's bookends: #31 (don't harm others) → #40 (actively serve others). From minimum protection to active offering. End class on this arc: "Yeh chapter — aur Part 1 — kahaan se kahaan tak gaya? #31 mein 'doosron ko nuksaan na do' se shuru hua, #40 mein 'doosron ki khidmat karo' pe khatam hua. Yahi pori 40 hadithon ka safar hai — minimum se maximum tak. Hum apna mom-i-bandh dekh sakte hain — kahaan hain hum, kahaan ja rahe hain?"

⚠️ Common student confusions — watch for these

1. "I haven't hit anyone" — ignoring the tongue

Ḥadīth 31 names both tongue and hand. Most classmates check the hand and forget the tongue. Push them on speech-harm — gheebat, taunts, sarcasm, undermining jokes.

2. Blurring the four definitions (#31–#34)

Muslim, muʾmin, mujāhid, muhājir are distinct nested levels, not synonyms. Draw the staircase on the board: minimum → upgrade → inner → daily.

3. "Greater jihād" framed as dismissing battlefield jihād

Ḥadīth 33 includes battlefield jihād in the broader definition — it doesn't exclude it. Both are valid; the nafs-jihād is the more constant of the two.

4. "Burden of proof" treated as a court rule only

Ḥadīth 35's principle applies in families, friendships, WhatsApp, gossip, and your own heart's suspicions. No evidence = no claim. Apply it everywhere.

5. "I'm being a mirror by criticising publicly"

The mirror metaphor demands three things: privately, truthfully, gently. Public criticism violates the first. You'd be a megaphone, not a mirror.

6. Disconnected from the umma's pain (#37)

If a classmate has stopped feeling the pain of Gaza, Sudan, or even the masjid down the road — the diagnostic is for them, not a guilt trip. The "spiritual nervous system" may need re-connecting.

7. "Travel is haraam"

The Prophet ﷺ himself travelled. Ḥadīth 38 just demystifies travel — names the deprivation, urges quick return. Halāl travel for purpose is fully permitted.

8. Guilt over "pauses" in worship

Ḥadīth 39 is the antidote. If your intention remains, the pause is not lost time — it is rewarded as part of the worship trajectory. Use this as raḥmah.

9. Leadership confused with status

Ḥadīth 40 inverts the worldly pattern: leadership = the burden you carry, the people you serve, not the title or the comfort. No separate "honour" exists apart from the service.

📿 Connect to the Qurʾān

Verses that mirror the ḥadīths of this chapter. Use 2–3 in class — don't overload.

Surah al-Tawbah · 9:71 — believers as awliyāʾ of one another

وَالْمُؤْمِنُوْنَ وَالْمُؤْمِنَاتُ بَعْضُهُمْ أَوْلِيَاءُ بَعْضٍ يَأْمُرُوْنَ بِالْمَعْرُوْفِ وَيَنْهَوْنَ عَنِ الْمُنْكَرِ

"The believing men and the believing women — they are awliyāʾ (protectors, allies) of one another — they enjoin good and forbid wrong..."

Why this fits: Establishes the legal walāyah (mutual protection / allegiance) of believers. Ḥadīth 36 (mirror & brother) and Ḥadīth 37 (one body) give the practical and organic expressions of this verse.

Urdu mein: Quran ne legal taur pe iss baat ko qaayam kiya ke momin aur mominaat ek doosre ke walī (hifaazatkaar) hain — yeh hadith #36 aur #37 ka Qurʾānic asal hai.

Surah al-Ḥujurāt · 49:12 — guard against suspicion without evidence

يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِيْنَ آمَنُوا اجْتَنِبُوْا كَثِيْرًا مِّنَ الظَّنِّ إِنَّ بَعْضَ الظَّنِّ إِثْمٌ

"O you who believe — avoid much of suspicion. Indeed some suspicion is sin..."

Why this fits: The Qurʾānic foundation of ḥusn al-ẓann (good opinion) — the inner disposition that Ḥadīth 35 (burden of proof on the accuser) operationalises. Without evidence, suspicion is itself sin.

Urdu mein: Bina saboot shak rakhna khud gunaah hai — yahi hadith #35 ka roḥani basis hai. Husn-e-zann default position.

Surah al-Anfāl · 8:46 — patience and not breaking ranks

وَأَطِيْعُوا اللَّهَ وَرَسُولَهُ وَلَا تَنَازَعُوا فَتَفْشَلُوْا وَتَذْهَبَ رِيْحُكُمْ ۖ وَاصْبِرُوْا

"And obey Allah and His Messenger, and do not dispute with one another — lest you lose courage and your strength depart. And be patient."

Why this fits: The umma's strength depends on not breaking ranks — the legal-political form of Ḥadīth 37's "single body". The same logic at the level of a community's outward unity.

Urdu mein: Yeh ayat ummah ki "outward unity" wali hadith 37 ko siyasi paigham deti hai — aapas mein jhagra na karo, hawa nikal jaayegi.
