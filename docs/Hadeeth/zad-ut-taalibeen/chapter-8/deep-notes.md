# Part A · Understanding the chapter

These ten aḥādīth (71–80) are still inside Part 1 of Zad ut-Taalibeen — short, free-standing, pithy. But notice something the book does on these very pages: it stops grouping them by *topic* and starts grouping them by *grammar*. Aḥādīth **71–76** all begin with the particle **لَا (lā, "there is no…")** — the book heads them *"Nominal Sentences Beginning With the Particle Lā"*. Aḥādīth **77–80** all begin with **إِنَّ (inna, "indeed, among… there is…")** — headed *"Nominal Sentences Beginning With the Particle Inna"*. So the chapter falls into two grammatical halves, and that grammar is itself a quiet teaching:

- The **"Lā" sayings** are *definitional negations*: *there is no real X without/except Y.* No faith without trust (71); no nobility like good character (72); no wisdom without experience (73). They tell you what a thing **truly is**, by denying the counterfeit.
- The **"Inna" sayings** are *qualified affirmations*: *indeed, among X there is Y.* Among speech there is magic (77); among poetry, wisdom (78); among "knowledge," ignorance (79); even the slightest showing-off is shirk (80). They tell you that a category you take at face value is actually **double-edged** — sift it.

Put the two halves together and a single spine runs the whole chapter: **weigh the reality, not the label.** Faith isn't a claim — it's trustworthiness (71). Nobility isn't lineage or wealth — it's character (72). Wisdom isn't bookish — it's lived (73). Piety isn't withdrawal from life (74). Obedience isn't absolute (75). Wealth isn't inherently corrupting (76). Persuasive speech isn't always truth (77). Poetry isn't always frivolous (78). "Knowledge" isn't always beneficial (79). And a good deed isn't good if it's done to be seen (80). Every single one asks you to look *past the surface* and weigh the *substance*. That is the believer's inner scale — *baṣīra*, discernment.

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter 8 ka naqsha — yeh das hadithein do *grammar* ke hisson mein hain: **"Lā" wali** (71–76, *"koi X nahin… siwaaye Y ke"*) aur **"Inna" wali** (77–80, *"beshak X mein se kuch Y hota hai"*). Magar dono ke neeche ek hi dhaaga: **asal ko taulo, zaahir ko nahin.** Īmān da'wa nahin — amānat hai (71); sharaafat khaandaan/maal nahin — akhlaaq hai (72); hikmat kitaabi nahin — tajrubaati hai (73); taqwa dunya chhodna nahin (74); itaa'at mutlaq nahin (75); maal khud bura nahin (76); meethi baat hamesha sach nahin (77); shaayri hamesha faaltu nahin (78); har "ilm" faayda-mand nahin (79); aur nek kaam dikhaawe se nek nahin rehta (80). Har hadith kehti hai: *zaahir ke peechhe dekho, asal ko taulo.* Yahi momin ki *baṣīra* (basheerat) hai.

Three natural themes — keep this in your head as the spine of the chapter:

1. **The foundations there's no substitute for** (Aḥādīth 71, 72, 73) — the first three "Lā" sayings define the believer's *inner makeup*. Faith stands on trustworthiness and keeping covenants (71); true nobility is planning, good character, and restraint — not lineage or wealth (72); and real clemency and wisdom are *forged*, by stumbling and by experience (73). These are the things for which there is *no substitute*.
2. **A faith lived in the world — within limits** (Aḥādīth 74, 75, 76) — the next three "Lā" sayings turn outward to *how the believer engages the world*. There is no monkery or withdrawal in Islam (74); obedience to any creation stops the instant it means disobeying the Creator (75); and wealth itself is no harm — to the one who fears Allah (76). Engage with life — but on Allah's terms, within His limits.
3. **Weigh what you take in — and why you give** (Aḥādīth 77, 78, 79, 80) — the four "Inna" sayings train *discernment*. Some speech is so eloquent it bewitches (77); some poetry carries real wisdom (78); some "knowledge" is actually ignorance, and some speech a burden (79); and the slightest ostentation turns a good deed into shirk (80). Sift what enters your heart through your ears — and check *why* your own deeds leave your hands.

Notice the flow: **build the inner foundations → live them out in the world rightly → then sift what you take in and purify why you act.** And the deepest current under all ten is *discernment*: the believer is trained to **weigh the reality, not the label.** Where Chapter 7's hidden thread was *muḥāsaba → dhikr* (live awake, remember Him), this chapter's hidden thread is *baṣīra* — *don't be deceived by the surface of things, including your own deeds.*

**Yaad rakho:** Teeno theme ek dhaage se bandhe hain — *baṣīra* (basheerat, discernment). Pehle *andaruni buniyaadein* banaao (71, 72, 73), phir unhein *dunya mein sahi tareeqe se* jiyo (74, 75, 76), phir *jo kuch sunte/seekhte ho use chhaano* aur *apne amal ki niyyat saaf rakho* (77, 78, 79, 80). Pichle chapter ka dhaaga *muḥāsaba se dhikr* tha; is chapter ka dhaaga *zaahir se dhoka mat khao — asal ko taulo.*

Work through each ḥadīth below. For each, ask yourself: *can I explain this hadith to a classmate in one sentence, in Urdu, without reading the book?* (*Kya main yeh hadith ek classmate ko ek line mein Urdu mein samjha sakti hoon, bina kitaab dekhe?*) If yes — move on. If not — re-read.

> **A note on order:** the book gives these in numeric order (71 → 80), and — happily — my three themes are *already* in numeric order (71–73 · 74–76 · 77–80), each theme sitting neatly inside the book's own grammatical halves (Theme 1 & 2 = the "Lā" sayings; Theme 3 = the "Inna" sayings). So this chapter needs **no re-ordering** — the book's sequence and our teaching arc are the same.

> **A note on the Arabic:** unlike Chapters 6 and 7, **all ten matns this chapter are short, well-known, and clearly printed**, so I give the Arabic for *every one* of them — read off the rendered page, harakaat and all. The one detail to watch is the idghām in #71 (*li-man **lā**…* — the book prints the shadda on the lām), reproduced exactly.

## Theme 1 · The foundations there's no substitute for

## 71 · لَا إِيْمَانَ لِمَنْ لَّا أَمَانَةَ لَهُ، وَلَا دِينَ لِمَنْ لَّا عَهْدَ لَهُ — "No faith for one who has no trustworthiness; no religion for one who keeps no covenant"

**Full wording:** *There is no faith for the one who has no trustworthiness (amāna), and no religion for the one who does not keep his covenant (ʿahd).*
**Source:** Bayhaqī (*Shuʿab al-īmān*).

### Faith is proven by trust, not by claims

The chapter opens with a hammer-blow: a person who has **no trustworthiness has no faith**, and a person who **does not keep his word has no religion**. *Amāna* is trustworthiness — honouring what is entrusted to you (a deposit, a secret, a duty, a relationship). *ʿAhd* is the covenant — your promises and agreements, with Allah and with people. The ḥadīth ties the *invisible* (faith, religion) to the *visible and testable* (do you keep trusts? do you keep your word?). You cannot inspect a man's heart — but you can watch whether he returns what he's given and honours what he promises. That is the **label vs the reality**: anyone can *claim* faith; the proof is *amāna* and *ʿahd*.

### The crucial qualification — this is not takfīr

Read the book's own explanation carefully, because the wording is alarming if taken flat. The book says: the negation here is most likely a negation of the **completeness (kamāl)** of faith and the **certitude (yaqīn)** of religion — *not* of faith and religion themselves. In plain terms: a person who betrays a trust or breaks a promise is **not thereby an unbeliever** — he is doing something *unbecoming of a believer*, falling short of complete faith. (The book offers a second reading too: a person who makes a *habit* of such treachery could be dragged, over time, into losing faith and religion altogether.) So the ḥadīth is a *severe warning to take amāna and ʿahd with deadly seriousness* — not a verdict that every promise-breaker is out of Islam.

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter ek hathaude se khulta hai: jis mein *amānat* nahin uska *īmān* nahin, jo *ahd* (waada) na nibhaaye uska *deen* nahin. *Amāna* = jo cheez tumhe sونpi gayi (deposit, raaz, zimmedaari, rishta) use sahi rakhna; *ʿahd* = apne waade poore karna. Hadith *ghaib* (īmān, deen) ko *zaahir aur qaabil-e-test* cheez se baandhti hai: amaanat aur waada. Dil to nahin dekha jaa sakta — magar yeh dekha jaa sakta hai ke banda amaanat lautaata hai aur waada nibhaata hai ya nahin. **Ahem qaid (kitaab se):** yeh nafi *kamaal* (completeness) ki hai — *poore* īmān ki — **takfīr nahin.** Jo amaanat mein khayaanat kare ya waada tode woh isi se *kaafir nahin* ho jaata — woh ek *momin ke laayaq nahin* kaam kar raha hai. (Doosra mafhoom: aisi aadat banaa lene wala dheere-dheere īmān-o-deen kho bhi sakta hai.) To yeh *sakht tambeeh* hai, har waada-tornay waale ko Islam se khaarij karne ka *fatwa nahin.*

## 72 · لَا عَقْلَ كَالتَّدْبِيرِ، وَلَا حَسَبَ كَحُسْنِ الْخُلُقِ، وَلَا وَرَعَ كَالْكَفِّ — "No intelligence like planning, no nobility like good character, no piety like restraint"

**Source:** Bayhaqī (*Shuʿab al-īmān*).

### Three counterfeits exposed in one breath

This ḥadīth takes three things people *think* they understand — intelligence, nobility, piety — and resets each to its real measure. The book unpacks all three:

1. **"No intelligence (ʿaql) like planning (tadbīr)."** Real intelligence is not quick wit or clever talk — it is *thinking through the outcomes of your actions* and *prudently working out what will benefit you in the Hereafter*. The intelligent person plans toward the right destination.
2. **"No nobility (ḥasab) like good character (ḥusn al-khuluq)."** *Ḥasab* normally means ancestral honour — your lineage, your family's standing. The ḥadīth overturns it: the real nobility is *cordial dealing with people (mudārāt) while observing the limits of Allah*. Your character, not your bloodline, is your honour.
3. **"No piety (waraʿ) like restraint (al-kaff)."** *Waraʿ* — God-fearing scrupulousness — is at its peak not in extra rituals but in *holding yourself back from everything prohibited and doubtful*. The most pious act is often the thing you *don't* do.

Three labels — intelligence, nobility, piety — each redefined by its *substance*: plan well, be of good character, restrain yourself.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh hadith teen cheezein jin ko log *samajhte* hain — ʿaql, sharaafat, taqwa — har ek ko uske *asli paimaane* par le aati hai. (1) **"Koi ʿaql tadbeer jaisi nahin"** — asli ʿaql tez zabaan nahin, balke *apne aamaal ke anjaam soch kar Aakhirat ke liye faayda-mand raasta chunna*. (2) **"Koi sharaafat husn-e-akhlaaq jaisi nahin"** — *ḥasab* (khaandaani sharaafat) nahin, asli sharaafat *logon se achha bartaao, Allah ki hudood ke andar*. Khoon nahin — akhlaaq tumhari ʿizzat hai. (3) **"Koi taqwa kaff (rukne) jaisa nahin"** — sab se bara waraʿ extra ʿibaadat mein nahin, balke *har haraam aur mashkook cheez se ruk jaana*. Aksar sab se nek kaam woh hota hai jo tum *nahin* karte. Teen labels — har ek apne *asal* se naye sire se taula gaya.

## 73 · لَا حَلِيمَ إِلَّا ذُو عَثْرَةٍ، وَلَا حَكِيمَ إِلَّا ذُو تَجْرِبَةٍ — "There is no clement person except one who has stumbled, and no wise person except one of experience"

**Source:** Tirmidhī, Mustadrak.

### Clemency and wisdom are forged, not granted

Two more counterfeits exposed — but this one carries a gentle, almost consoling message. *Ḥilm* is forbearance, the calm self-control that lets you forgive and not lash out. *Ḥikma* is wisdom, sound judgement. And the ḥadīth says: **there is no truly clement person except one who has himself stumbled (ʿathra), and no truly wise person except one of experience (tajriba).** The book explains the logic exactly: the one who has *himself slipped and made mistakes* knows what forgiveness is worth — so he is *gentler* toward others when *they* slip. And a wise person is precisely one whose decisions are *built on experience*, not theory.

There is real comfort here. Your past stumbles are not only a source of shame — handled rightly, they become the *raw material* of clemency. The person who has never failed is often the harshest judge; the one who has fallen and been forgiven becomes soft toward the fallen. So wisdom and forbearance are not gifts dropped on the lucky — they are *forged* in the fire of real life. The label "wise/forbearing" looks like a trait you either have or don't; the reality is that it is *earned* through stumbling and living.

**Yaad rakho:** Do aur "naqli" cheezein bay-naqaab — magar is mein ek narm, tasalli-bhari baat hai. *Ḥilm* = bardaasht, woh thandak jo maaf karne aur na bhadakne deti hai. *Ḥikma* = hikmat, sahi faisla. Hadith kehti hai: **koi sacha ḥalīm nahin siwaaye us ke jo khud thokar (ʿathra) kha chuka, aur koi sacha ḥakīm nahin siwaaye us ke jo tajrubekaar (tajriba) ho.** Kitaab ki daleel: jo khud *phisla aur ghalti ki* hai woh maafi ki qadar jaanta hai — isliye doosron ke phisalne par *zyada narm* hota hai. Aur ḥakīm wahi jiska faisla *tajrube* par bana ho, kitaabi nazariye par nahin. Yahaan asli tasalli hai: tumhari pichli thokarein sirf sharm ka sabab nahin — sahi sambhaali jaayein to woh *ḥilm ka khaam maal* ban jaati hain. Jo kabhi nahin gira woh aksar sab se sakht munsif hota hai; jo gir kar maaf kiya gaya woh giron par narm ho jaata hai. To hikmat aur bardaasht qismat-waalon par giri hui ne'mat nahin — yeh *zindagi ki aag mein dhalti* hain.

## Theme 2 · A faith lived in the world — within limits

## 74 · لَا صَرُورَةَ فِي الْإِسْلَامِ — "There is no celibacy in Islam"

**Source:** Abū Dāwūd, Mustadrak.

### Islam is not a religion of withdrawal

*Ṣarūra* means celibacy — the deliberate renunciation of marriage as a supposed act of devotion (the monk's path). The ḥadīth declares flatly: **there is no ṣarūra in Islam.** Islam does not consider withdrawal from married life a virtue; the Prophetic way is to *engage* — to marry, to build a household, to be in the world. The book adds a second, less-known meaning of *ṣarūra*: it also refers to *abandoning the pilgrimage (ḥajj) after it has become obligatory upon you.* On either meaning, the book's verdict is the same: **such a person is not considered a complete Muslim** — the one who shuns marriage as devotion, or who neglects an obligatory ḥajj, has stepped *outside* the balanced, engaged path Islam lays down.

This is the "label vs reality" of *piety itself*: people imagine holiness means fleeing the world — celibacy, isolation, renunciation. The ḥadīth says the real path runs *through* the world, not away from it. (Connect it to #72's *waraʿ* — piety is restraint *within* a fully lived life, not a retreat from life.)

**Yaad rakho:** *Ṣarūra* = (rahbaaniyat jaisi) shaadi se jaan-boojh kar kinaara-kashi, "ibaadat" samajh kar. Hadith saaf kehti hai: **Islam mein koi ṣarūra nahin.** Islam shaadi-shuda zindagi se kinaara-kashi ko fazeelat nahin samajhta — Nabawi raasta *shaamil hona* hai: nikah karo, ghar basaao, dunya mein raho. Kitaab ek doosra (kam-maʿroof) matlab bhi deti hai: *ṣarūra* woh bhi hai jo *ḥajj farz ho jaane ke baad use chhod de.* Dono mafhoom par kitaab ka faisla ek: **aisa shakhs *mukammal* Muslim nahin** — jo shaadi ko "ibaadat" samajh kar chhode, ya farz ḥajj ko nazar-andaaz kare, woh Islam ke *mutawaazin, shaamil* raaste se *baahar* qadam rakh raha hai. Yeh *taqwa* ka "zaahir vs asal" hai: log samajhte hain paakeezgi dunya se bhaagne mein hai — hadith kehti hai asli raasta dunya ke *beech se* guzarta hai, dunya se *door bhaag kar* nahin. (#72 ke *waraʿ* se jodo — taqwa *poori jee gayi zindagi ke andar* rukna hai, zindagi se farar nahin.)

## 75 · لَا طَاعَةَ لِمَخْلُوقٍ فِي مَعْصِيَةِ الْخَالِقِ — "There is no obedience to any creation in disobedience of the Creator"

**Source:** Baghawī (*Sharḥ al-Sunna*).

### The one ceiling on every human authority

This is the weightiest ḥadīth of the chapter, and it must be taught with care. **There is no obedience owed to any created being in something that involves disobedience to the Creator.** Islam commands obedience in many directions — to parents, to rulers, to teachers, to those over you — and it takes those duties seriously. But every one of them has a *ceiling*: the moment a created authority commands what Allah has forbidden, that command **loses its claim on you**. The book gives the exact, gentle example: one is *strongly* commanded to be respectful and obedient to one's parents — but *when they command something that conflicts with a command of Allah or His Messenger ﷺ, their command is overruled.*

Hold two truths together, exactly as the book does. First: this does **not** cancel the underlying duty of respect, kindness, and service — you still honour your parents, your ruler, your elders. Second: obedience to *people* is never *absolute* — it is always *nested inside* obedience to Allah. So this is not a charter for rebellion or rudeness; it is a *limit*. You disobey the *specific sinful command* — gently, respectfully — while keeping the *relationship* and the *general obedience* intact. The "label" is "obey those over you"; the "reality" is "obey them *within* obedience to Allah, never against Him."

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh chapter ki sab se bhaari hadith hai — ehtiyaat se sikhao. **Kisi makhlooq ki itaa'at us cheez mein nahin jisme Khaaliq ki naafarmaani ho.** Islam bohot jagah itaa'at ka hukm deta hai — maa-baap, hukmraan, ustaad, baron ki — aur in haqooq ko sanjeedgi se leta hai. Magar har itaa'at ki ek *chhat* hai: jis lamhe koi makhlooq woh hukm de jo Allah ne mana kiya, us hukm ka tum par *koi haq nahin rehta.* Kitaab ki narm misaal: maa-baap ki *taakeed* se izzat-o-itaa'at ka hukm hai — magar *jab woh Allah ya uske Rasool ﷺ ke hukm ke khilaaf kuch kehein, to unka hukm radd ho jaata hai.* **Do baatein saath rakho (jaise kitaab):** (1) Yeh izzat, mehrbaani, aur khidmat ka asal farz *khatam nahin* karti — maa-baap, hukmraan, baron ki ʿizzat barqaraar. (2) *Logon* ki itaa'at kabhi *mutlaq* nahin — woh hamesha *Allah ki itaa'at ke andar* hai. To yeh baghaawat ya bad-tameezi ka parwaana *nahin* — yeh ek *hadd* hai: tum sirf *us khaas gunaah waale hukm* mein — narmi aur adab se — naafarmaani karte ho, jabke *rishta* aur *aam itaa'at* qaayam rakhte ho.

## 76 · لَا بَأْسَ بِالْغِنَى لِمَنِ اتَّقَى اللهَ عَزَّ وَجَلَّ — "There is no harm in wealth for one who fears Allah, the Mighty and Majestic"

**Source:** Ibn Māja, Aḥmad, Mustadrak.

### Wealth is not the problem — the heart that holds it is

After a chapter (and a tradition) full of warnings about *love* of the world, this ḥadīth sets the record straight: **there is no harm in wealth — for the one who fears Allah.** The variable is not the *money*; it is the *taqwā* of the one who holds it. The book explains both directions: a *wealthy person who is pious and God-fearing* is likely to spend his wealth in the path of Allah and to promote good with it — so his wealth is a *blessing and a tool*. An *unrighteous person*, by contrast, is more likely to *squander* it — and that squandering becomes a liability against him in the Hereafter. The book then quotes the Prophet ﷺ: *"How excellent is pure wealth in the possession of a righteous person."* (*Al-Adab al-mufrad.*)

So wealth is *neutral* — it takes the moral colour of the hand that holds it. This is the perfect close to the "engage with the world" theme: just as Islam doesn't ask you to flee marriage (74) or rebel against all authority (75), it doesn't ask you to flee wealth. It asks you to hold it *with taqwā*. The label says "wealth is dangerous"; the reality says "wealth in a God-fearing hand is excellent — a means of immense good."

> ⚠️ Hold this **together with** the wider teaching (e.g. Chapter 5's #49, *"love of this world is the root of all sin"*). There is no contradiction: the danger is *love of* wealth that displaces Allah; the safety is *possessing* wealth while *fearing* Allah and spending it rightly. Don't let #76 become "money is always fine" — it is fine *for the muttaqī*, the one whose taqwā governs how he earns and spends.

**Yaad rakho:** Dunya ki *mohabbat* ke khatron se bhari rivaayat ke baad, yeh hadith baat saaf karti hai: **maal mein koi harj nahin — us ke liye jo Allah se darta hai.** Variable *paisa* nahin, balke us ka *taqwa* jo paisa rakhta hai. Kitaab dono taraf samjhati hai: *muttaqī maaldaar* apna maal Allah ke raaste mein aur bhalaai mein kharch karta hai — to maal *ne'mat aur auzaar* ban jaata hai. *Naafarmaan* shakhs use *zaaya* karta hai — aur yeh Aakhirat mein uske khilaaf bojh. Phir kitaab Nabi ﷺ ka farmaan naqal karti hai: *"kitna achha hai paak maal nek aadmi ke haath mein."* (Al-Adab al-mufrad.) To maal *neutral* hai — jis haath mein ho usi ka rang le leta hai. Yeh "dunya mein raho" theme ka mukammal ikhtitaam hai: jaise Islam shaadi (74) se bhaagne ya har ikhtiyaar (75) se baghaawat ko nahin kehta, waise maal se bhaagne ko bhi nahin kehta — bas use *taqwa ke saath* rakho. **(Ehtiyaat:** ise Chapter 5 ki #49 — *"dunya ki mohabbat har gunaah ki jad"* — ke saath rakho. Tazaad nahin: khatra maal ki *mohabbat* hai jo Allah ki jagah le le; salaamati maal *rakhna* hai Allah se *darte hue* aur sahi kharch karte hue. #76 ko "paisa hamesha theek hai" mat banao — yeh *muttaqī* ke liye theek hai.)

## Theme 3 · Weigh what you take in — and why you give

## 77 · إِنَّ مِنَ الْبَيَانِ لَسِحْرًا — "Indeed, some eloquent speech is [like] magic"

**Source:** Bukhārī.

### Eloquence is a power — and power cuts both ways

Now the grammar shifts: the "Lā" sayings end, the "Inna" sayings begin. *Bayān* is eloquence — clear, beautiful, persuasive speech. The ḥadīth says: **indeed, among eloquence there is that which is [like] siḥr (magic).** The book explains: certain speech is "magical" in that it is *spellbinding* — it can *captivate the hearts of people*, sweep them along, bend their will. And then the book adds the crucial neutral note: *whether this be for the sake of good or otherwise.* That little clause is the whole lesson. Eloquence is *power*, and power is not good or evil in itself — it takes the direction the speaker points it. A spellbinding tongue can rally people to truth and justice — or seduce them into falsehood.

So the discernment here runs *two ways*. As a *listener*: don't let a beautiful, moving speech *be its own proof* — the fact that words bewitch you says nothing about whether they're *true*. Weigh the content, not the charm. As a *speaker*: if Allah gave you eloquence, you hold a sharp tool — aim it at truth and good, never at deceiving people. The label is "what a wonderful speaker"; the reality is "wonderful speech can carry you to Heaven or off a cliff — check where it's pointed."

**Yaad rakho:** Ab grammar badalti hai: "Lā" wali khatam, "Inna" wali shuru. *Bayān* = fasaahat, saaf-meethi-asar-daar baat. Hadith: **beshak baʿz bayān mein *siḥr* (jaadu) hota hai.** Kitaab: aisi baat "jaadu" is maʿne mein hai ke woh *dilon ko moh leti hai*, baha le jaati hai, iraadon ko mod deti hai. Aur kitaab ek aham neutral baat jodti hai: *chahe yeh bhalaai ke liye ho ya kisi aur cheez ke liye.* Yahi poora sabaq hai. Fasaahat *taaqat* hai, aur taaqat khud-ba-khud achhi ya buri nahin — jis taraf bolne wala mode. Moh lene wali zabaan logon ko haq-o-insaaf ki taraf bula sakti hai — ya baatil mein phaansa sakti hai. To yahaan basheerat *do tarfa* hai. *Sunne waale* ke taur par: khoobsoorat, dil ko chhoo lene wali taqreer ko uska *apna saboot* mat banne do — baat ka *moh lena* yeh nahin batata ke woh *sach* hai. *Charm* nahin, *content* taulo. *Bolne waale* ke taur par: agar Allah ne fasaahat di hai, tumhaare paas tez auzaar hai — use haq aur bhalaai ki taraf chalaao, logon ko dhoka dene mein kabhi nahin.

---

## 78 · إِنَّ مِنَ الشِّعْرِ حِكْمَةً — "Indeed, some poetry is wisdom"

**Source:** Bukhārī.

### Don't dismiss a form — weigh its content

The shortest saying in the chapter, and a perfect mirror of #77. *Shiʿr* is poetry. People in many cultures (then and now) either over-glorify poetry or wave it away as frivolous entertainment. The ḥadīth cuts straight through both: **indeed, among poetry there is wisdom (ḥikma).** Not *all* poetry — *some* of it. The point is that the *form* doesn't decide the worth; the *content* does. Poetry that carries truth, sound counsel, and moral insight is *ḥikma* — wisdom — and is to be valued. Poetry that carries falsehood, indecency, or arrogance is not redeemed by being well-crafted.

Notice the *exact* parallel with #77: in #77, beautiful *speech* is not automatically good (it can bewitch toward falsehood); in #78, dismissible *poetry* is not automatically bad (some of it is wisdom). Both aḥādīth attack the same lazy mental shortcut — *judging by the container, not the contents.* The believer doesn't accept a thing because it's eloquent (77), nor reject a thing because it's "just poetry" (78). He opens it up and weighs what's inside.

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter ki sab se chhoti baat, aur #77 ka theek aaina. *Shiʿr* = shaayri. Log (us waqt aur aaj bhi) ya to shaayri ko had se zyada barhaate hain ya use faaltu tafreeh keh kar tha-tha. Hadith dono ko kaat deti hai: **beshak baʿz shaayri mein *ḥikma* (hikmat) hoti hai.** *Saari* nahin — *baʿz.* Baat yeh hai ke *qaalib* (form) qadar tay nahin karta; *content* karta hai. Jo shaayri sach, achhi naseehat, aur akhlaaqi basheerat rakhti ho woh *ḥikma* hai — qaabil-e-qadar. Jo shaayri jhoot, behayaai, ya takabbur rakhti ho woh khoobsoorti se "paak" nahin ho jaati. #77 ke saath theek mawaazi dekho: #77 mein meethi *baat* khud-ba-khud achhi nahin (baatil ki taraf moh sakti hai); #78 mein "haqeer" *shaayri* khud-ba-khud buri nahin (baʿz hikmat hai). Dono ek hi sست (sust) zehni shortcut par waar karti hain — *dabbe se faisla, andar ke maal se nahin.* Momin kisi cheez ko sirf fasaahat ki wajah se qubool nahin karta (77), na sirf "shaayri hi to hai" keh kar radd karta hai (78). Woh use khol kar *andar ka maal taulta* hai.

## 79 · إِنَّ مِنَ الْعِلْمِ جَهْلًا، وَإِنَّ مِنَ الْقَوْلِ عِيَالًا — "Indeed, some 'knowledge' is ignorance, and some speech is a burden"

**Source:** Abū Dāwūd.

### Not everything called "knowledge" benefits you

This is the deepest of the "Inna" sayings. **Indeed, among "knowledge" there is ignorance, and among speech there is that which is a burden (ʿiyāl).** The book explains carefully — and you must too, because this is easily misheard as anti-knowledge:

*Sacred knowledge (ʿilm)* lights the path of guidance and turns a person from sin — that is *praiseworthy*. But some "knowledge" is *not* counted beneficial by the Sharīʿa, and chasing it actually *derails* a person from seeking the knowledge that benefits — so it becomes, for him, *a form of ignorance*. The book gives a vivid image from the scholar Azharī: a person who *acquires* knowledge but never *acts on it* is like a *donkey carrying huge tomes* — it bears the books on its back but understands nothing of them; possessing such knowledge is, in effect, *not* possessing it. That image is straight from the Qurʾān: *"The likeness of those who were entrusted with the Torah and then did not uphold it is the likeness of a donkey carrying volumes of books"* (Qurʾān 62:5). And Qatāda's verdict: *anyone who disobeys Allah is ignorant* — however much he "knows."

The second half — *"some speech is a burden (ʿiyāl)"* — the book reads as: some speech becomes a *source of hardship* when it is delivered to someone *who already knows it* (so it's tedious and useless) or *who cannot grasp it* (so it confuses and burdens him). In both halves the lesson is the same discernment: a thing wearing the *label* "knowledge" or "speech" may, in *reality*, be ignorance or a burden. Weigh whether what you learn *benefits* and whether what you say *lands*.

**Yaad rakho:** Yeh "Inna" wali sab se gehri hai. **Beshak baʿz "ilm" mein jahaalat hai, aur baʿz baat *bojh* (ʿiyāl) hai.** Kitaab dhyaan se samjhati hai — aur tumhe bhi samajhana hai, kyunki yeh aasaani se "ilm-mukhaalif" samajh li jaati hai: *deeni ʿilm* hidaayat ka raasta roshan karta aur gunaah se rokta hai — yeh *qaabil-e-tareef.* Magar baʿz "ilm" Shari'a ke nazdeek faayda-mand nahin, aur uske peechhe bhaagna banday ko *faayda-mand ilm se hata* deta hai — to woh, *uske haq mein*, ek qism ki *jahaalat* ban jaata hai. Kitaab ʿAllāma Azharī ki tasveer deti hai: jo *ilm haasil* kare magar *amal na kare* woh us *gadhe jaisa hai jo bhaari kitaabein laad* kar le jaata hai — peeth par kitaabein, samajh kuch nahin; aisa "ilm" rakhna goya *na* rakhna. Yeh tasveer seedhi Quran se hai: *"jin par Tauraat ka bojh daala gaya phir unhone use na uthaaya, unki misaal us gadhe jaisi hai jo kitaabein laade"* (Quran 62:5). Aur Qataada ka faisla: *jo Allah ki naafarmaani kare woh jaahil hai* — chahe woh kitna hi "jaanta" ho. Doosra hissa — *"baʿz baat bojh hai"* — kitaab yun parhti hai: baat *takleef ka sabab* ban jaati hai jab woh us shakhs ko ki jaaye jo *pehle se jaanta* hai (faaltu) ya jo *samajh hi nahin sakta* (uljhan aur bojh). Dono hisson mein wahi basheerat: jis cheez par "ilm" ya "baat" ka *label* lagaa ho woh *asal* mein jahaalat ya bojh ho sakti hai. Taulo ke jo seekhte ho woh *faayda* deta hai, aur jo kehte ho woh *pohanchta* hai.

## 80 · إِنَّ يَسِيرَ الرِّيَاءِ شِرْكٌ — "Indeed, even the slightest ostentation is shirk"

**Source:** Ibn Māja.

### The final scale — weigh *why* you act

The chapter closes by turning the scale *inward, onto your own deeds*. **Indeed, even a small amount of riyāʾ (ostentation, showing off in worship) is shirk.** After teaching you to weigh others' speech, poetry, and "knowledge," the last ḥadīth makes you weigh the *hidden motive of your own good deeds*. The book supports it with another ḥadīth: the Prophet ﷺ said *the thing he feared most for his community was "the lesser association" (al-shirk al-aṣghar)* — and when asked what it was, he answered: ***riyāʾ*** (ostentation). And on the Day of Judgement, when Allah rewards people for their deeds, He will say to the ostentatious: *"Go to those for whom you performed your deeds — do you find any reward with them?"* — the deed done for people's eyes earns its "reward" only from those eyes, nothing from Allah.

This is **shirk aṣghar** — *lesser* shirk — and that distinction is the landmine to handle gently. It does **not** mean a riyāʾ-tainted person leaves Islam (that would be *major* shirk); it means an act of worship done *to be seen* is *hollowed out* — its reward stripped — because you've split your intention between Allah and an audience. The cure is in #69 territory: do the deed *for Allah alone.* And note the danger this verse-of-warning closes the chapter on: it's not the loud sins that worry the Prophet ﷺ most for us — it's this *quiet, invisible* one, the one that hides inside our *best* deeds. The whole chapter trained you to see past the *label* of things; the last saying turns that x-ray on *yourself*: a good deed is not good if its *real* reason is to be seen.

**Yaad rakho:** Chapter taraazu ko *andar, apne aamaal par* mod kar band hota hai. **Beshak thoda sa riyāʾ (dikhaawa, ʿibaadat mein numaaish) bhi shirk hai.** Doosron ki baat, shaayri, aur "ilm" taulna sikhaane ke baad, aakhri hadith tumhe apne *nek aamaal ki chhupi niyyat* taulne par lagaati hai. Kitaab ek aur hadith se taaeed karti hai: Nabi ﷺ ne farmaaya jo cheez woh apni ummat par sab se zyada *darte* the woh *"chhota shirk" (al-shirk al-aṣghar)* hai — poochha gaya woh kya? Farmaaya: **riyāʾ** (dikhaawa). Aur Qayaamat ke din, jab Allah aamaal ka badla dega, dikhaawe waalon se kahega: *"jaao un ke paas jin ke liye tum amal karte the — kya unke paas koi ajr paate ho?"* — jo amal logon ki nazron ke liye kiya, uska "ajr" sirf unhi nazron se hai, Allah se kuch nahin. **Yeh *shirk aṣghar* — *chhota* shirk — hai, aur yahi narmi se sambhaalne wali baat hai:** iska matlab yeh *nahin* ke riyāʾ waala Islam se nikal gaya (woh *bara* shirk hota) — matlab yeh ke jo ʿibaadat *dikhaawe* ke liye ki gayi woh *khokhli* ho gayi, uska ajr cheen liya gaya, kyunki tum ne niyyat Allah aur naazireen ke darmiyaan baant di. Ilaaj #69 wala hai: amal *sirf Allah ke liye* karo. Aur dekho hadith chapter ko kis cheez par band karti hai: Nabi ﷺ ko hum par sab se zyada khauf *uunchi awaaz waale gunaahon* ka nahin — is *khaamosh, ghaib* cheez ka hai, jo hamaare *behtareen* aamaal ke andar chhupti hai. Poore chapter ne cheezon ke *label* ke peechhe dekhna sikhaaya; aakhri baat woh x-ray *tum par* mod deti hai: nek kaam nek nahin agar uski *asli wajah* dikhaawa ho.

## The big picture — what these 10 aḥādīth are silently building

Stand back. The chapter trains the believer in **three movements**, all under one discipline — *discernment*:

1. **The foundations there's no substitute for** — faith is proven by trustworthiness and keeping covenants (71); real nobility is planning, good character, and restraint, not lineage (72); and clemency and wisdom are forged by stumbling and experience (73).
2. **A faith lived in the world — within limits** — there is no withdrawal or celibacy in Islam (74); obedience to any creation stops where disobedience to the Creator begins (75); and wealth is no harm — in a God-fearing hand (76).
3. **Weigh what you take in — and why you give** — eloquent speech can bewitch toward truth *or* falsehood (77); some poetry is genuine wisdom (78); some "knowledge" is really ignorance and some speech a burden (79); and the slightest ostentation hollows a good deed into shirk (80).

The deepest current under all ten is the single discipline of **baṣīra — weigh the reality, not the label.** Don't read faith off a claim, nobility off a bloodline, piety off a withdrawal, obedience as absolute, wealth as evil, eloquence as truth, poetry as worthless, "knowledge" as benefit — or your own deeds as good. Look past every surface and weigh the substance — *including the hidden motive of your own worship*. Chapter 7 taught you to keep the heart *alive* with dhikr; Chapter 8 teaches you to keep the heart's *eyes open* — to see things, and yourself, as they truly are.

**Yaad rakho:** 10 hadith — magar 3 harkatein, ek tarbiyat: *basheerat.* (1) *Buniyaadein jin ka koi badal nahin* (71, 72, 73). (2) *Dunya mein zindagi — hudood ke andar* (74, 75, 76). (3) *Jo lo use taulo, aur jo do uski niyyat dekho* (77, 78, 79, 80). Sab ke neeche ek dhaaga: **asal ko taulo, zaahir ko nahin.** Īmān ko da'we se, sharaafat ko khoon se, taqwa ko farar se, itaa'at ko mutlaq, maal ko buraai, fasaahat ko sach, shaayri ko faaltu, "ilm" ko faayda — aur apne aamaal ko nek — *mat* maan lo. Har zaahir ke peechhe dekho, asal taulo — *apni ʿibaadat ki chhupi niyyat samet.* Chapter 7 ne dil ko *zinda* rakhna sikhaaya; Chapter 8 dil ki *aankhein khuli* rakhna sikhaata hai.

# Part B · Teaching the chapter

Now you understand the chapter. This section is how to *deliver* it to your classmates.

---

## The 5 landmines to watch for

### Landmine 1 · #71 — "so a person who breaks a trust is an unbeliever (kāfir)"

The wording — "no faith for one who has no trustworthiness" — sounds like takfīr (declaring someone out of Islam). It is **not**. The book is explicit: the negation is of the *completeness (kamāl)* of faith, not faith itself — such a person is doing something *unbecoming of a believer*, not leaving the religion.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh hadith yeh nahin keh rahi ke amaanat mein khayaanat karne wala kaafir ho gaya. Kitaab saaf kehti hai — yeh nafi *poore* (kamaal) īmān ki hai, asal īmān ki nahin. Aisa shakhs ek momin ke laayaq nahin kaam kar raha hai — magar deen se nahin nikla. Yeh ek *sakht tambeeh* hai: amaanat aur waade ko bohot sanjeedgi se lo — kyunki sacha īmān inhi se zaahir hota hai."*

### Landmine 2 · #74 — "so marriage is obligatory on everyone, and staying single is sinful"

"No celibacy in Islam" condemns *renouncing marriage as a supposed act of devotion* (the monk's path) — it is **not** a ruling that every single person is sinning, nor a stick to beat someone who hasn't found a spouse or can't marry. Teach it as Islam's rejection of *withdrawal-as-piety*, not as marriage-policing.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh us shakhs ke baare mein hai jo shaadi ko 'ibaadat' samajh kar jaan-boojh kar chhod de — rahbaaniyat. Yeh har ghair-shaadi-shuda ko gunaahgaar nahin keh rahi, na us par taana hai jise rishta nahin mila ya jo majboor hai. Sabaq: Islam mein paakeezgi dunya se *bhaagne* mein nahin — Nabawi raasta dunya mein *shaamil hona* hai, shaadi karna, ghar basaana. (Kitaab ek aur matlab bhi deti hai: farz ḥajj chhodna bhi 'ṣarūra' hai.)"*

### Landmine 3 · #75 — "so I can disobey / disrespect my parents and rulers whenever I disagree"

This is the heaviest landmine in the chapter. The principle "no obedience to creation in disobedience of the Creator" is a *ceiling on obedience*, **not** a licence for rebellion, rudeness, or disobeying parents over ordinary disagreements. It applies only when a created authority commands an *actual sin* — and even then, you decline the *specific sinful command* gently and respectfully, keeping the relationship and the general duty of obedience intact.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Khabardaar — yeh har ikhtilaaf par maa-baap ya hukmraan ki naafarmaani ka parwaana nahin. Yeh sirf tab laagu hota hai jab koi *Allah ki naafarmaani* ka hukm de — masalan koi gunaah karne ko kahe. Aur tab bhi: tum *us khaas gunaah waale hukm* se — narmi aur adab se — inkaar karte ho, jabke izzat, mehrbaani, aur baaqi har jaayaz baat mein itaa'at *barqaraar* rakhte ho. Itaa'at-e-makhlooq hamesha *Allah ki itaa'at ke andar* hai — yeh hadd hai, baghaawat nahin."*

### Landmine 4 · #79 — "so the dīn is against knowledge / learning"

"Some knowledge is ignorance" is **not** anti-knowledge. *Sacred and beneficial knowledge is praised throughout the dīn.* The ḥadīth targets two things: (a) "knowledge" the Sharīʿa doesn't count as beneficial, which *distracts* a person from what does benefit, and (b) knowledge *acquired but never acted on* — the donkey carrying books (62:5). The cure is *beneficial knowledge, acted upon*, not *less* knowledge.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Yeh ilm ke khilaaf nahin — deeni aur faayda-mand ilm ki to deen bhar mein tareef hai. Hadith do cheezon ko nishaana banaati hai: (1) woh 'ilm' jo Shari'a ke nazdeek faayda-mand nahin aur banday ko asal faayde se hata de, aur (2) woh ilm jo seekha to magar uspar amal nahin kiya — woh gadha jo kitaabein laade phirta hai magar samajhta kuch nahin (62:5). Ilaaj kam ilm nahin — *faayda-mand ilm, jis par amal ho.*"*

### Landmine 5 · #80 — "so any good deed someone notices is ruined / I'm always sinning"

This breeds *waswasa* (scrupulosity). #80 is about **shirk aṣghar — *lesser* shirk** — and about *intending* to show off. It does **not** mean you've left Islam, and it does **not** mean the *natural* gladness that a good deed was seen, or doing good *openly to encourage others*, is sinful. The poison is *performing the act for people's eyes instead of Allah's*.

**Urdu mein samjhao:** *"Ise dil par bojh mat banao. Yeh *chhota* shirk (shirk aṣghar) hai — Islam se nikaalne wala bara shirk nahin. Aur yeh us *niyyat* ke baare mein hai jab tum amal *logon ko dikhaane ke liye* karo. Agar koi nek kaam dekh le aur tumhe qudrati khushi ho — ya tum jaan-boojh kar *doosron ko targheeb dene* ke liye khule-aam neki karo — woh riyāʾ nahin. Zeher yeh hai ke amal *Allah ke liye* nahin, *logon ki nazron ke liye* ho. Ilaaj: niyyat *sirf Allah ke liye* karo (#69 ki tarah)."*

> **Bonus watch-point · #77 — "so eloquence/persuasion is praised manipulation"?** No — the book itself says *"whether for good or otherwise."* Eloquence is a *neutral power*. As a listener: a moving speech is not its own proof — weigh the *content*, not the charm. As a speaker: aim the gift at truth, never at deceiving people. Pair it with #78: don't accept a thing for being eloquent (77), nor reject it for being "just poetry" (78) — open it and weigh what's inside.

## What you should NOT teach in Chapter 8

Avoid these even if classmates ask — say *"yeh hum aage dekhenge"* or *"yeh book ke scope se bahar hai"*:

- ❌ **The detailed fiqh of trusts, deposits (amānāt), contracts, and oaths** triggered by #71 — keep it to the *principle*: faith shows in keeping trusts and covenants; betraying them is gravely unbecoming (but not, in itself, kufr).
- ❌ **The full ʿaqīda of īmān increasing/decreasing and the categories of kufr/nifāq** triggered by #71's wording — give only the one clean point: this negates *completeness* of faith, not faith itself. Do not open the takfīr discussion.
- ❌ **The detailed fiqh of marriage** (rulings on who must/should marry, the categories of nikāḥ) triggered by #74 — keep it to: Islam rejects *withdrawal-as-piety*; the path runs through the world.
- ❌ **The full fiqh of obedience to rulers, the conditions of lawful authority, and rebellion (khurūj)** triggered by #75 — teach only the *ceiling principle*, gently, with the parents example. This is **not** a class on political dissent.
- ❌ **The detailed ʿaqīda of major vs minor shirk, and the science of rulings on the ostentatious** triggered by #80 — give the heart of it: riyāʾ is *lesser* shirk, it *hollows out* a deed's reward, the cure is sincerity. Don't spiral into waswasa-inducing detail.
- ❌ **Lists of "blameworthy sciences," or debates about poetry/music** triggered by #79 and #78 — keep it to the discernment point: weigh whether knowledge *benefits* and what poetry *carries*; don't adjudicate every borderline case.

**Yaad rakho:** Pre-loading aur tangents madad nahin karte. 10 hadithein hain — har ek apne aap mein kaafi. Aur cheezein mat daalo — khaas taur par #71 par *takfīr* ki behes aur #75 par *siyaasi baghaawat* ki behes mat khol-na, aur #80 ko *waswasa* mein mat badalna. Class ka focus = *yeh 10 hadithein dil mein bait jaayein* aur basheerat ka dhaaga samajh aaye. Bas.

Before the class, make sure:

- [ ] You can recite each of the 10 aḥādīth in Arabic (all ten matns are given this chapter) with correct meaning in English + Roman Urdu. *(Saari 10 ki Arabic di hui hai — chhoti aur strong honi chahiye.)*
- [ ] You can give the source of each (Bayhaqī / Tirmidhī / Mustadrak / Abū Dāwūd / Baghawī / Ibn Māja / Aḥmad / Bukhārī) — at the book's exact level. *(Har hadith ka source.)*
- [ ] You can name the **three movements** — the foundations / faith in the world within limits / weigh what you take in — and which ḥadīth goes where. *(Teen harkatein.)*
- [ ] You can point out the book's **two grammatical groups** — the "Lā" sayings (71–76) and the "Inna" sayings (77–80) — and why the book groups them so. *(Do grammar groups.)*
- [ ] You can state the chapter's deep thread — **baṣīra: weigh the reality, not the label** — and show it in every ḥadīth. *(Asal ko taulo, zaahir ko nahin.)*
- [ ] You can deliver #71's qualification cleanly — *kamāl*, not takfīr. *(Kamaal ki nafi, kufr ka fatwa nahin.)*

## After class — diagnostic checklist

For each classmate, watch for:

1. Did they hear #71 as takfīr, or as a severe warning about *completeness* of faith? *(Kufr ka fatwa — ya kamaal ki tambeeh?)*
2. Did they catch #72's three resets — intelligence = planning, nobility = character, piety = restraint? *(Teen naye paimaane?)*
3. Did #73's consolation land — that past stumbles become the raw material of clemency? *(Thokar se ḥilm?)*
4. Are they reading #74 as "marriage is compulsory / single = sinful", or as Islam's rejection of withdrawal-as-piety? *(Rahbaaniyat radd — ya har ghair-shaadi-shuda gunaahgaar?)*
5. **Do they hold #75 correctly** — a ceiling on obedience for an *actual sinful command*, declined gently, relationship intact — not a licence to rebel/disrespect? *(Hadd, baghaawat nahin?)*
6. Are they reading #76 as "money is always fine", or as wealth being *neutral*, safe in a God-fearing hand? *(Maal neutral — taqwa variable?)*
7. Did they grasp #77 + #78 together — judge by content, not container (don't accept for eloquence, don't reject for being "just poetry")? *(Dabbe se nahin, andar ke maal se faisla?)*
8. Did they mishear #79 as anti-knowledge, or grasp it as *non-beneficial / unacted-upon* knowledge (the donkey, 62:5)? *(Ilm-mukhaalif — ya be-faayda/be-amal ilm?)*
9. Can they hold #80 as *lesser* shirk about *intention* without spiralling into waswasa? *(Niyyat ka chhota shirk — waswasa nahin?)*
10. Can they state the arc and the deep thread — **asal ko taulo, zaahir ko nahin** — ending on weighing their *own* deeds (#80)? *(Chapter ka dhaaga aur #80 par apne aap par x-ray?)*
