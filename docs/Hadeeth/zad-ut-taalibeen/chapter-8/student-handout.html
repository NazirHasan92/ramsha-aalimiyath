Chapter 8 — Student Handout

Aḥādīth 71–80 from Part 1 of Zad ut-Taalibeen · weigh the reality, not the label

👋 Welcome — what we'll learn today

Today we cover ten short aḥādīth of the Prophet ﷺ that, read together, teach one habit of mind: look past the surface of things and weigh their reality. They move through three movements: the foundations there's no substitute for, a faith lived in the world — within limits, and weigh what you take in — and why you give.

Aaj ka sabaq — das choti hadithein jo ek aadat sikhaati hain: cheezon ke zaahir se aage dekho aur unka asal taulo. Teen harkatein: buniyaadein (jin ka koi badal nahin), dunya mein zindagi — hudood ke andar, aur jo lo use taulo, aur jo do uski niyyat dekho.

The 10 fall into three themes (and follow the book's own two grammar groups — the "Lā" sayings, "there is no…", and the "Inna" sayings, "indeed, among… there is…"):

  1. The foundations there's no substitute for (#71, #72, #73) — faith is proven by trustworthiness and keeping your word; true nobility is character, not lineage; and clemency & wisdom are forged by stumbling and experience.
  2. A faith lived in the world — within limits (#74, #75, #76) — there is no withdrawal or celibacy in Islam; obedience to any creation stops where disobedience to the Creator begins; and wealth is no harm in a God-fearing hand.
  3. Weigh what you take in — and why you give (#77, #78, #79, #80) — eloquence can bewitch toward truth or falsehood; some poetry is real wisdom; some "knowledge" is ignorance; and the slightest showing-off turns a good deed into shirk.

The deep thread beneath all ten: baṣīra — weigh the reality, not the label.

Asal ko taulo, zaahir ko nahin.

Ḥadīth 71 · Faith is proven by trust

لَا إِيْمَانَ لِمَنْ لَّا أَمَانَةَ لَهُ، وَلَا دِينَ لِمَنْ لَّا عَهْدَ لَهُ

lā īmāna li-man lā amānata lah, wa lā dīna li-man lā ʿahda lah

"There is no faith for the one who has no trustworthiness, and no religion for the one who keeps no covenant."

"Jis mein amaanat nahin uska īmān nahin, aur jo waada na nibhaaye uska deen nahin."

Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-īmān)

Faith shows in what you do, not what you claim. Amāna is honouring what is entrusted to you; ʿahd is keeping your word. You cannot inspect a person's heart, but you can see whether he returns trusts and keeps promises — and that is the proof of faith.

⚠️ This is not takfīr

The negation is of the completeness of faith, not faith itself — a promise-breaker is doing something unbecoming of a believer, but he does not thereby leave Islam. It is a severe warning to take trusts and promises seriously.

Yeh poore īmān ki nafi hai, asal īmān ki nahin. Amaanat-khori momin ke laayaq nahin — magar kufr nahin. Sakht tambeeh hai.

Ḥadīth 72 · No intelligence like planning, no nobility like good character, no piety like restraint

لَا عَقْلَ كَالتَّدْبِيرِ، وَلَا حَسَبَ كَحُسْنِ الْخُلُقِ، وَلَا وَرَعَ كَالْكَفِّ

lā ʿaqla ka-t-tadbīr, wa lā ḥasaba ka-ḥusni l-khuluq, wa lā waraʿa ka-l-kaff

"There is no intelligence like planning, no nobility like good character, and no piety like restraint."

"Koi ʿaql tadbeer jaisi nahin, koi sharaafat achhe akhlaaq jaisi nahin, aur koi taqwa (rukne) jaisa nahin."

Bayhaqī (Shuʿab al-īmān)

Three things reset to their real measure. Real intelligence is planning toward the Hereafter, not clever talk. Real nobility is good character — not your lineage or family standing. Real piety is restraint — holding back from the forbidden and doubtful. Often the most pious act is the thing you don't do.

Ḥadīth 73 · Clemency & wisdom are forged

لَا حَلِيمَ إِلَّا ذُو عَثْرَةٍ، وَلَا حَكِيمَ إِلَّا ذُو تَجْرِبَةٍ

lā ḥalīma illā dhū ʿathra, wa lā ḥakīma illā dhū tajriba

"There is no clement person except one who has stumbled, and no wise person except one of experience."

"Koi sacha bardaasht-waala nahin siwaaye jo khud thokar kha chuka, aur koi sacha hakeem nahin siwaaye tajrubekaar ke."

Tirmidhī, Mustadrak

A consoling truth. The one who has himself slipped knows the worth of forgiveness — so he is gentler when others slip. And wisdom is built on experience, not theory. Your past stumbles are not only shame; handled rightly, they become the raw material of clemency. The one who never failed is often the harshest judge.

Ḥadīth 74 · There is no celibacy in Islam

لَا صَرُورَةَ فِي الْإِسْلَامِ

lā ṣarūrata fi l-islām

"There is no celibacy in Islam."

"Islam mein koi (rahbaaniyat jaisi) be-nikaahi nahin."

Abū Dāwūd, Mustadrak

Islam runs through the world, not away from it. Ṣarūra is renouncing marriage as a supposed act of devotion — the monk's path. Islam does not treat withdrawal as a virtue; the Prophetic way is to marry, build a household, and engage with life. (The book adds a second meaning: abandoning ḥajj after it became obligatory.)

⚠️ Not marriage-policing

This condemns withdrawal-as-piety, not every unmarried person. It is no taunt at someone who hasn't found a spouse or can't marry.

Yeh rahbaaniyat ka radd hai — har ghair-shaadi-shuda par taana nahin.

Ḥadīth 75 · There is no obedience to creation in disobedience of the Creator

لَا طَاعَةَ لِمَخْلُوقٍ فِي مَعْصِيَةِ الْخَالِقِ

lā ṭāʿata li-makhlūqin fī maʿṣiyati l-khāliq

"There is no obedience to any creation in [something involving] disobedience to the Creator."

"Kisi makhlooq ki itaa'at us cheez mein nahin jisme Khaaliq ki naafarmaani ho."

Baghawī (Sharḥ al-Sunna)

The one ceiling on every human authority. Islam commands obedience to parents, rulers, and elders — seriously. But every such obedience has a ceiling: the moment a created authority commands what Allah forbids, that command loses its claim on you. The book's example: respect your parents — yet if they command something against Allah, that command is overruled.

⚠️ A limit, not a licence

This does not cancel the duty of respect and kindness, and it is not permission to rebel or be rude over ordinary disagreements. It applies only to a command of actual sin — which you decline gently, keeping the relationship intact.

Yeh itaa'at ki hadd hai, baghaawat ya bad-tameezi nahin. Sirf gunaah waale hukm par — narmi se inkaar; izzat aur rishta barqaraar.

Ḥadīth 76 · Wealth is no harm — to the God-fearing

لَا بَأْسَ بِالْغِنَى لِمَنِ اتَّقَى اللهَ عَزَّ وَجَلَّ

lā baʾsa bi-l-ghinā li-mani-ttaqā Llāha ʿazza wa jall

"There is no harm in wealth for one who fears Allah, the Mighty and Majestic."

"Maal mein koi harj nahin us ke liye jo Allah se darta hai."

Ibn Māja, Aḥmad, Mustadrak

Wealth is neutral — the heart that holds it decides. A God-fearing person spends his wealth in Allah's path and promotes good with it; an unrighteous person squanders it. The Prophet ﷺ said, "How excellent is pure wealth in the possession of a righteous person." So Islam doesn't ask you to flee wealth — it asks you to hold it with taqwā. (The danger is love of wealth that displaces Allah — not possessing it rightly.)

Ḥadīth 77 · Some eloquent speech is like magic

إِنَّ مِنَ الْبَيَانِ لَسِحْرًا

inna mina l-bayāni la-siḥran

"Indeed, some eloquent speech is [like] magic."

"Beshak baʿz fasaahat (khoobsoorat baat) mein jaadu hota hai."

Bukhārī

Eloquence is a power — and power cuts both ways. Beautiful, persuasive speech can captivate hearts and bend wills — the book adds, "whether for good or otherwise." So as a listener: a moving speech is not its own proof — weigh the content, not the charm. As a speaker: if Allah gave you eloquence, aim it at truth, never at deceiving people.

Ḥadīth 78 · Some poetry is wisdom

إِنَّ مِنَ الشِّعْرِ حِكْمَةً

inna mina sh-shiʿri ḥikmatan

"Indeed, some poetry is wisdom."

"Beshak baʿz shaayri mein hikmat hoti hai."

Bukhārī

Judge by the content, not the container. The mirror of #77: don't accept a thing just because it's eloquent (#77), and don't reject a thing just because it's "only poetry" (#78). Poetry that carries truth and good counsel is wisdom and is valued; poetry that carries falsehood is not redeemed by being well-crafted. Open it up and weigh what's inside.

Ḥadīth 79 · Some "knowledge" is ignorance, and some speech is a burden

إِنَّ مِنَ الْعِلْمِ جَهْلًا، وَإِنَّ مِنَ الْقَوْلِ عِيَالًا

inna mina l-ʿilmi jahlan, wa inna mina l-qawli ʿiyālan

"Indeed, some 'knowledge' is ignorance, and some speech is a burden."

"Beshak baʿz 'ilm' mein jahaalat hai, aur baʿz baat (sunne waale par) bojh hai."

Abū Dāwūd

Not everything called "knowledge" benefits you. Beneficial, sacred knowledge is praised. But "knowledge" that the Sharīʿa doesn't count beneficial — and knowledge you learn but never act on — is, in effect, a kind of ignorance: like a donkey loaded with books it cannot understand (Qurʾān 62:5). And speech becomes a burden when said to someone who already knows it or cannot grasp it.

⚠️ Not anti-knowledge

The cure is more beneficial knowledge, acted upon — not less. The ḥadīth targets the non-beneficial and the unacted-upon.

Yeh ilm ke khilaaf nahin — ilaaj faayda-mand ilm hai jis par amal ho.

Ḥadīth 80 · Even the slightest ostentation is shirk

إِنَّ يَسِيرَ الرِّيَاءِ شِرْكٌ

inna yasīra r-riyāʾi shirk

"Indeed, even the slightest ostentation is shirk."

"Beshak thoda sa riyāʾ (dikhaawa) bhi shirk hai."

Ibn Māja

Now weigh why you act. The chapter ends by turning the scale onto your own deeds. Performing worship to be seen is shirk. The Prophet ﷺ said the thing he feared most for us was "the lesser association" — and named it: riyāʾ. A deed done for people's eyes earns its "reward" only from those eyes — nothing from Allah.

⚠️ Lesser shirk — don't fall into waswasa

This is shirk aṣghar — it does not put you out of Islam, and it does not mean natural gladness that a good deed was seen is sinful. The poison is doing the act for people instead of for Allah. The cure: intend it for Allah alone.

Yeh chhota shirk hai, niyyat ka. Islam se nahin nikaalta; qudrati khushi riyāʾ nahin. Ilaaj: niyyat sirf Allah ke liye.

🔑 The chapter in one picture

Ten aḥādīth, three movements, one thread:

  1. The foundations there's no substitute for (#71, #72, #73) — faith = trust & covenant; nobility = character; wisdom = forged by experience.
  2. A faith lived in the world — within limits (#74, #75, #76) — no withdrawal; obedience has a ceiling; wealth is safe in a God-fearing hand.
  3. Weigh what you take in — and why you give (#77, #78, #79, #80) — eloquence cuts both ways; poetry can be wisdom; some "knowledge" is ignorance; the slightest show-off corrupts a deed.

The thread: baṣīra — weigh the reality, not the label — including the hidden motive of your own worship.

Buniyaadein → dunya mein hudood ke andar → jo lo use taulo. Dhaaga: asal ko taulo, zaahir ko nahin.

Exercise 5 · Reflection — write a sentence or two each

  1. Name one trust (amāna) or promise (ʿahd) you are carrying right now (Ḥadīth 71). Are you honouring it fully?
  2. Ḥadīth 72 says piety is often restraint. Name one thing you could gain reward by simply not doing this week.
  3. Recall a past mistake of your own (Ḥadīth 73). How could it make you gentler toward someone who makes the same mistake?
  4. Has anyone ever told you to do something that conflicts with Allah's command (Ḥadīth 75)? How could you decline gently, while keeping the relationship?
  5. Think of a speech or video that moved you recently (Ḥadīth 77). Did you check whether its content was true, or were you swept by the delivery?
  6. Pick one good deed you do (Ḥadīth 80). Honestly — is any part of it for people's eyes? How can you re-aim it for Allah alone?
